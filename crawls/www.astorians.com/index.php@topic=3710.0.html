On Mendy's
« on: October 06, 2006, 01:53:20 PM »
Or, "For the Love of a Jewish Sandwich"
Or, "Heart Attack on Rye"
Or, "Back in the New York Groove"

So I woke up this morning and read Jonathan's post about "good-smelling" short ribs.  That got me hungry.  Then on my way to the subway this morning I passed by one of the Greek restaurants (actually, it may have even been Souvlaki Plus), and the meat smelled amazing.  So all I kept thinking all morning was MEAT MEAT MEAT MEEEEEEEET.  So I decided that I would need meat for lunch.  For most people, this might not be a big deal, but because of my dietary restrictions (kosher), I hardly ever eat meat- kosher meat is way too expensive, very rarely any good, and at least in Astoria, impossible to find.

So I find myself at the New York Public Library, and decide to take a stroll down to Mendy's at 34th and Park.  Not the Mendy's from Seinfeld ("They have be best swordfish in the city, Jerry, THE BEST!"), but Mendy's Deli (Glatt Kosher).  I usually don't go in for stuff like that- anyone who has ever been to a kosher restaurant knows what an ordeal it can be, and that it's never really worth it.  But I struck gold (GOLD, Jerry!).  Maybe because tonight is a Jewish holiday, the place was empty.  And maybe because I was the only customer, and was courteous and said "please" and "thank you" (all rarities in this kind of establishment, let alone this city), the guy behind the counter hooked me up.  I ordered a corned beef and pastrami sandwich on rye, and guy lopped on a tennis-ball sized scoop of chopped liver, for free.  There must have been a pound of steaming meat on that sandwich.  The guy behind the counter pressed it down with both hands and skewered it with toothpicks- in the end, it was the size of a rugby ball- it, along with the half-sour pickle, could barely fit in the plastic container.  The whole mess ended up being only about ten bucks, which for a huge amount of kosher meat is quite reasonable (the Mendy's in Grand Central Station may be more expensive, though).

I then got my ass in gear and shlepped my three-pound sandwich to Bryant Park (which is usually infuriatingly crowded during lunchtime).  It looked like it was about to rain, so I really hauled ass, trying hard not to knock people out of the way as I barrelled down Madison Avenue carrying a bowling-ball's worth of cured and smoked meat.  I finally got to Bryant Park, which perhaps because of the weather, was totally empty.  I couldn't believe my luck.  I sat down, popped open my treasure chest of a plastic container, and the carnage began.

The meat was still warm.  The flavors all sort of melded together- the sourness of the corned beef, the spiciness of the pastrami, the pleasant smokiness of the chopped liver, the almost sweet taste a real New York bread (it's the water).  The whole thing melted in my mouth.  Like buttah (but no butter, of course, it was kosher).  Unbelievable.  As I sat there inhaling the quintessential New York sandwich, I felt like the king of the world.

And now, I feel totally gross.  Light-headed, sluggish, somewhere near the frontier of the demilitarized meat-coma-zone.  But I had just enough energy to type this note.

I also just found out that for the holiday tonight (Sukkot), we are having brisket.  Oy!

Jonathan:
 yes, the chopped liver was on the sandwich.  I know there are people out there who are skeptical that such a thing could be good.  But let me tell you, people: you haven't lived until you've eaten (good) chopped liver on a deli sandwich.  No mustard, no lettuce, no tomato, or any other accoutrements- just meat and chopped liver.  As a good introduction to such a sandwich, I would recommend a mild (and lower fat) meat like turkey breast.  The chopped liver compliments it nicely without sending you into a world of overpowering shock and awe.  But if you think you can handle it- take things up a notch and get some red meat on there.  The other nice thing about the red meat option is that corned beef, served properly, is warm- thus, you get the nice contrast of temperatures with the cool, dare I say refreshing, chopped liver. 

If you don't keep kosher, the Carnegie Deli serves a verson of that sandwich called the Broadway Danny Rose (after the Woody Allen character) that is supposed to be very good (my dad had one like 20 years ago and he can't stop raving about it- of course, he also thought he was going to die instantaneously upon ingesting the thing).

Juvenile male carassius auratus (a.k.a., Goldfish Boy): It's possible that you saw me- I was in there at about 12:15.  I didn't ask anybody about being open on Sunday, though.  I'm about 5'8'', brown hair, short beard (that probably describes 90 percent of the people who eat at Mendy's!).  I did see someone with a knish, but it was on a tray.  And then after that guy left, I was the only one there.  Maybe I'll run into you another time!

I would argue that Jewish food outside has always been updated according to regional changes. For example, I have Jewish friends from South America whose families came from Poland to Argentina and Chile, respectively, during the 19th century. When making Gefilte Fish, their grandparents adapted the dish using local fish in place of the traditional Carp. Even closer to home, the German side of my family had a different recipe for chopped liver than the Polish side. Also, Israeli cuisine is in constant change, including the fusion of different traditional foods for the holiday feasts.
