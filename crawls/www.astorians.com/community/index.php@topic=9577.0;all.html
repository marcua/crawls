I'm not objecting to the idea of universal health care, but for the record, the programs in those countries are drastically different and are often supported through everyone paying more taxes, not through tax penalties on the uninsured.

Functionally, it is necessary to make health care affordable to a majority of the population before imposing fines or neglecting tax benefits for those who do not have insurance.

While universal health care would be great and I do not support forcing the insured population to pay for the uninsured, I do not want the situation to worsen and believe that there are steps that need to be taken before entering into a system of penalization for lack of insurance. We are leaps and bounds away from being in a position to require insurance in the manner that Senator Clinton has proposed.

[OT]
My only sibling, my sister, was diagnosed with non-Hodgkins lymphoma. She was told that her only hope would be a bone marrow transplant. The papers were submitted to her insurance carrier, and 30 days later, while she was still hospitalized after 3 invasive surgeries, she was informed by regular mail that the request was being denied. Gratefully, her portion of this hospitalization left her financially bankrupt, and the social services department of the hospital applied her for Medicaid which she got 90 days later. Medicaid allowed the transplant, but unfortunately by that time she was so weakened that it was not feasible to do it until they strengthened her up. Six months later the transplant was performed. She passed away 90 days later.

Would she have lived if she hadn't been turned down by her carrier? I don't know, but I sure would have preferred to find out rather than have to live with this.
