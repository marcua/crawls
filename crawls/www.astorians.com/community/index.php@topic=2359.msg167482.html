While I'm not pregnant, I saw subway etiquette from a different perspective today.  I sprained my ankle something awful a little over a week ago and it's so bad that my friend loaned my her spiffy cane for extra support as I hobble down the street (canes really work!).  I was on the subway going home today and on the N train not one person offered me a seat.  I knew people saw the cane, and one guy looked at me before he hurried over to a seat that had just become available.  Many people were spacing out on their ipods, text messaging, or just really into whatever it was that was on their mind. 

I'm lucky that I could still manage without the cane, but what if I was someone who couldn't?  What if I was a 40 year old with MS?  Or an elderly person that got tired easily from standing?  Or a pregnant person who suffers from horrible back aches?  I think a lot of people are simply not paying attention; so often it's crowded and uncomfortable that we get used to tuning a lot out on the subways.   So, I think a lot of people are more thoughtless rather than malicious.  This is not an excuse, but more of an observation.

I would like to see more kindness and care towards the people around us.  Is that too much to ask?

An interesting story that probably hasn't happened to too many of you. Last week I get on the almost empty last car of the Jamaica bound E at Roosevelt and the smell of bums hits me right in the nose - nothing unusual I say to myself and I try to take a seat that's rather far from them. Problem is that they're right in the middle of the car so sitting or standing very far is somewhat impossible. What happened next was that one of the bum takes some stinking cigarette from behind his ear and lights it up. So what are my options at this point: to spend 6 minutes in a smelly and smoky train, to do something illegal and possibly dangerous by moving to the other car or to risk getting assaulted by two smelly old guys. I decided that there were at least a couple of guys who weren't happy with them either and would've stood up for me if something happened so I took my headphones out of my ears and said with a big smile and my thick accent (completely natural): "Do you mind putting it out?" He did, I said: "Thank you" and that was it.

So what was my point? First, people do a lot of inappropriate things in NYC Transit but smoking isn't usually one of them. Second, if you want something to happen, you have to speak up. If I'd kept my mouth shut the guy could've lit a second one after the finished the first. Of course, beware of your surroundings and don't tell a group of 15 loud high-school kids to shut up. Best you're going to get away with is even more shouting.
