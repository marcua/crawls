I usually talk about the neighborhood with pride, but when people come to visit and the station signs are unreadible because of tags--I don't blame them for thinking it's ghetto.
And like others have said it wouldn't be AS bad if this was graffiti art (like off the 7 train heading towards Manhattan or the painted mural under the subway on 31st).

I've seen them tag the sides of homes andwhen I lived on Ditmars, right next to Brooklyn Bagel we woke up to a set of tags on our front door. And the same thing happened the the 718 restaurant, which is one of the more aesthetically pleasing buildings in the neighborhood to me.  It really feels like they are trying to lower the value of the neighborhood intentionally.

I know this may strike a raw nerve and it really isn't my intention.  But...  most graffiti is done by minors.  And most of these kids still live at home.  And most of the graffiti is done in the dead of night.  So where are the parents?  If a 17 year old is hanging over a billboard at 3am, doesn't anyone at his house know he's up to trouble?  I realize that if the kid was from a stable, responsible home, then he would probably be doing more productive things with his time - and would be in bed at night.  But still.  And second, I don't worry so much about the ghetto impression.  I've come to realize that graffiti exists where ever the person can get away with it.  The only reason it's more prevalent in areas like ours is that our area does go to sleep.  In places with street traffic all night and a lot of lighting and surveillance cameras, it won't happen.  Or maybe I'm just naive about all of it.  All I know is I don't like it.  And I don't buy the art argument for a minute.  I prefer art in galleries and museums.  Not on the side of my house.  Unless I commissioned it!

I would go so far as to say that even throw ups and marker tags, could be considered graphic design in its most primary form.  I think that there is a lot more to graffiti than "losers" making a mess, and I think that things like public walls or funding for public school art programs combined with painting over illegal work are the best deterrents to graffiti and could potentially create the next Marc Ecko. 
If you're even remotely interested in graffiti culture, or simply want to figure out why kids will climb up billboards to paint an alias, I highly reccommend Style Wars, which is a 1983 PBS documentary which is a surprisingly in depth look at graffiti culture.
