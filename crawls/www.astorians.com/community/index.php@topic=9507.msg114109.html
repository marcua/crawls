merm, your idea of moderation is clearly different from a lot of people's. It's a fair difference, but I think a lot of us would prefer it if the offending posts were deleted and warnings were posted, instead of silently banning/suspending people and leaving the guilty post up for all to see. Could you explain your rationale for doing it that way? The former method allows people the chance to step back and go "Yeah, I guess that was out of line" rather than getting even more irritated that they can't even log in to explain themselves, and that they weren't given a warning that they're out of line. People who aren't trolling do tend to come around when given the chance. Suspending people is not a warning anymore than receiving a speeding ticket isn't a warning. A warning is to let people continue on their way while letting them know they're out of line and shouldn't do it again.

No matter how clear you think the rules are, what constitutes as inflammatory and insulting is highly subjective. We've lost some decent people here because of the insta-ban method of moderation. It feels very un-community-like when people who've been helpful and contributing members of the forum for a long time are suddenly banned because of one or two unfortunate posts.

What I find unfortunate is that this problem really only exists within the topic of politics (with a few exceptions). Most of the other topics on the board are discussed rationally and there's seldom the need for any moderation at all. But when it comes to politics the discussion can very quickly become heated and the conversation is derailed.

If I were to delete offending posts I know there would be those who would protest that as well (a great example of that is in this very thread). It also creates additional complications around the issue of censorship, and it lets people who make abusive or hateful comments hide behind the veil of a moderator's delete key. It's a valid approach, but it does have its own set of complications.

You are right, we have lost a few members because they threw big fits over being suspended for 24h or something equally as silly, but only a few. We've lost far more members (although still relatively few compared to the entire member base admittedly) because they've been insulted or abused by other members on the board. And of those who has left (for whatever reason), most often it has been because of political discussions that have gotten out of hand.

Another approach would be to suspend or warn people publicly (as you've pointed out), so that everyone can benefit from seeing what is and what is not crossing the line - but this too creates problems as it can humiliate the person who gets suspended. I know because I've tried this, and I agreed with the criticism that it can be humiliating. It's admittedly a very good way for people to learn what is appropriate and inappropriate behavior, but there's a cost.

The bottom line is that any action that a moderator takes that suggests someone is out of line is often met by a challenge and excuses (not always), even when we do nothing but send a PM pointing out an area where someone has crossed the line. Many people are unable to see or admit where they've created a problem, even where it's obvious. After four years of running this site, I can say I've tried a lot of different moderation styles with different members and while it's a great idea to think that I can be a kind of benevolent administrator that comes behind and reads every post and gently reminds people when they get close to crossing the line, it just doesn't seem to work that way I've found (to be fair, I used to think this was possible.) All this being said, I don't think my moderation style is that different than you'd see on other successful moderate to large sized discussion forums on the net.

Troll

1. (n.) A supernatural being, often represented as of diminutive size, but sometimes as a giant, and fabled to inhabit caves, hills, and like places; a witch.

2. (v. t.) To move circularly or volubly; to roll; to turn.

3. (v. t.) To send about; to circulate, as a vessel in drinking.

4. (v. t.) To sing the parts of in succession, as of a round, a catch, and the like; also, to sing loudly or freely.

5. (v. t.) To angle for with a trolling line, or with a book drawn along the surface of the water; hence, to allure.

6. (v. t.) To fish in; to seek to catch fish from.

7. (v. i.) To roll; to run about; to move around; as, to troll in a coach and six.

8. (v. i.) To move rapidly; to wag.

9. (v. i.) To take part in trolling a song.

10. (v. i.) To fish with a rod whose line runs on a reel; also, to fish by drawing the hook through the water.

11. (n.) The act of moving round; routine; repetition.

12. (n.) A song the parts of which are sung in succession; a catch; a round.

13. (n.) A trolley.
