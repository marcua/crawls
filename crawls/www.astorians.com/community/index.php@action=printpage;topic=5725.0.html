ps: Sabeers, here's my 2-cents on gear shifting, tho i'm no lance armstrong: staying in the same gear just gives you a lot more work to do.  what i've been taught is that irrespective of terrain, you want your legs moving as steadily as possible (knowing that you'll obviously work harder on uphill and less hard on downhill ... it's just a general idea) - so, switch the rear gears (right gear shift) in order to keep a fair tension on your legs.  just takes practice and often, a lot of unecessary peddling till you get a good rhythm.  and there's something  to be said for that cliche about riding a bike ... it'll all come back to you!

Post by: Christine on April 29, 2007, 07:48:43 PM
Let's see if I can explain.......

Here's how I do it: Left = Front, Right = Rear.

Meaning, the brake and shifter controlled by your left hand is for the front brake and the three cogs between the pedals. The right hand controls the rear brake and those 7 gears in the back.

By shifting with the left hand, you can choose from those three front cogs:

Big ring = flat terrain, going fast (more rotations of the tire per pedal)
Middle ring = gradual hill, moderate speed
Small ring = steep hill (gotta pedal fast just to stay upright!)

Those are easy. The rear ones work in the same way (the bigger ones make you go faster.) Think of the rear ones as fine-tuning the front ones. For example, when you want to go balls-out fastest possible, biggest cog in front and biggest cog in back.

Want to go pretty fast but not too crazy? Use your right hand to shift to a smaller cog .

See a hill coming up while you're going fast? Use your left hand to quickly shift to the middle ring as you get to the base of it.

Review: Left hand/front three cogs = Fast/medium/slow (instant speed change); Right hand/rear seven cogs = fine tuning the fast/medium/slow speed you're comfortable with.

As for the brakes:

Front brake has more power BUT if you grab just the left brake while going fast, you'll tumble over the bars. So it's best to "feather" the brakes (squeeze gently then let go over and over in quick succession.)

When going downhill and you need to brake or slow down, put more pressure on the rear brake (right hand) than the front (left hand) and get your butt off the back of the seat. Don't be afraid to stand in your pedals (3 o'clock and 9 o'clock positions) while absorbing shock with your knees as you do this.

Hope this helps!

Randall's Island/Ward's Island is actually very easy...I do it most mornings (at least 3 a week right now) in my commute to Harlem. At about 28th St on the North side of Astoria Blvd. there is a stair to the pedestrian bridgeway. It has a rail for pushing your bike up the stairs, but I find it easier to just pick up the bike & climb. There are 2 of these easy stairs going up and 1 going down. It takes me about 7 minutes to cross the bridge to Randall's.
