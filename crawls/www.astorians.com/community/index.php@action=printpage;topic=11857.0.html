Title: Changing the Definition of Abortion in Medical Standards
Post by: paratactical on August 18, 2008, 10:48:26 AM
I know opinions on abortion are heated, personal and religious.

I'm not trying to open up that can of worms. I've got pretty mixed views on it myself.

But yesterday I got a petition from MoveOn.org, a group that I believe has the best intentions but often doesn't understand what they're doing and promotes sensationalist responses to things. So when I got their announcement that there was a draft circulating that would include contraceptives, including the birth control pill, as part of the blanket term "abortion", I did some research.

I managed to find a copy of the leaked draft here. (http://www.rhrealitycheck.org/emailphotos/pdf/HHS-45-CFR.pdf)

Upon reading it, it seems that the real issue here goes back to the pharmacists that refused to dispense birth control for their religious reasons.

I'm torn on this issue because on one hand, I do not want to force people to violate their religious beliefs. On the other hand, I'm not sure that I want the people who disperse my medication to have their actions dictated by their personal beliefs above my and my doctor's choices based upon medical decisions.

I guess what confuses me the most about this is that I'm surprised there are that many people opposed to birth control and contraceptives. I know part of the problem is a reluctance to distribute the morning after pill, but the draft, were it taken as written above, would include all contraceptive devices and not just the controversial RU medicine.

The discussion that I'm curious to is how do you think we can balance these varying opinions in our country? Is there a way to protect a religious pharmacist without harming the future availability of contraceptive devices? How should we draw lines between appropriate religious refusal and a violation of the duty of one's profession?

I guess I've been mulling this around in my head and I was interested to see what others think.

From a philosophical standpoint, it depends on whether we think of pharmacists as merchants or as people performing some sort of civil service.
