You can get individual insurance directly from the insurers.  One of the best deals for decent insurance when I signed up was HIP.  Unfortunately, they're up to $408.90 a month now.

The deal with the Freelancer's Union isn't bad, actually.  I'm in the process of signing up now myself.  There is a $25 fee to join and a $25 yearly fee.  The health insurance itself is through HIP and is as little as $186 a month (if you don't mind a $30 standard/$50 specialist office copay and generic-only prescription coverage) or $288 a month for a more standard plan (comparable to what I'm paying over $400 for now).  There's one in between as well.
http://www.workingtoday.org/productsservices/healthdental.php

The requirements for membership aren't a hard dollar figure.  They just want to see proof that you're working for yourself as a freelancer.  You need to show that you've earned $10,000 in the last six months *OR* billed for 20 hours per week for the last 8 weeks.  So, you could do it on the billable hours.  Even if you need to bill yourself out cheap for a bit, it could be worth it.

Another thing people might consider is checking with your college's Alumni Association.  They sometimes have health plans for alums.  My alma mater CalArts has a health plan ($100/month for health care, a bit more than I can afford) but dental is a mere $13.25/month!  Check with them - you might find something that works.  CalArts is a young institution so I wonder if older institutions have more flexible deals.  Anyway, it's worth inquiring.

Another dental option is Northeast Dental Plan of America.  For less than $100 a year (I think it might be $84) you get discounted dental work.   I saved 50% on my wisdom teeth extraction - instead of paying $1,000 for both teeth, I only paid $500.  Insurance, of course, is better, but NDPA is not a bad option.

One might also consider medical savings accounts in lieu of insurance.  If one doesn't need prescription medication or have any known health issues requiring regular doctor visists, opting out of insurance is probably a wise choice if you have no assets that need protecting.  Set aside a fixed amount that you had been willing to spend on insurance in a bank account and don't touch it except when you need to visit a doctor.  If you have no house or cash savings, in the unlikely event of a young healthy person being in a serious accident simply run up those bills.  If they get too bad, bankruptcy is an option down the road.  In the meantime, those hundreds of dollars you save each month will go a long way toward paying the occasional office visit.

Also, doctor/hospital bills can be paid off in small increments over time.  As long as you keep making payments, your credit won't be destroyed.  Just never miss a payment and only pay what you can afford.

Not an option for everyone I realize, but it's there for a young broke healthy person struggling to make crippling insurance payments every month.
