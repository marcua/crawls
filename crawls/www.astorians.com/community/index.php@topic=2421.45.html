I certainly hope that ‘megc’ and friends will report with glowing tribute to the location and quality of the fireworks. That someone would choose to enjoy the Fourth at on the East River simply underscores the importance public access to our waterfront.

And, although I certainly hope this year will be different, I recall hearing and seeing the same things that ‘daisy’ experienced: the security guards only allowed those with passes to get onto the piers.

I wish that ‘Spokesmancycles’ was actually there! I do not recall any security guards on potty patrol. They did not resemble high school monitors giving hall passes. 

It seemed as if the piers were teaming with a large portion of Queens West.
 
But ‘Spokesman’ did have a good point. Even if a ‘first come first served’ policy was in place, crowd control would seem like a logical reason to exclude people.

And thanks to the insightful point raised by ‘the.’ Public property can be leased.

‘Public access’ can be something slippery and fleeting, as just about anyone who has fallen in love with our waterfront, and spent time around it, will testify.

There will be discussions like this on every inch of ground on the waterfront, especially when more of us will get into the other borough waterfront parks and have a chance to see the realm of possibility.

At Gantry Park last year, we are certain that loads of people had fun. However, we found the line of security grim, the plaza hot, and after a few hours, our group retreated back to the old rooftop.

There, for years, a ritual repeats itself. We could see hundreds, mostly immigrants, commonly, if only for a moment, sharing an uniquely American experience. 

The official fireworks  began. Citicorp partially blocked one of the barges. We wondered what the waterfront would look like someday, how many more years before view would be forever blocked.

A Guyenese buddy came over and we talked as old friends and took turns watching each others stairs. There were bonfires in the streets. Cases of rockets went off. ‘Private displays’ went up around us.

After a while, in the distance, the fountains of shooting lava ceased. “I guess its over,” I said, and we shook hands. As he walked away, he smiled and gestured to the private displays firing over our heads unabated, “then the block has won.”

Discussing beach access is a topic that has been kicking around ever since the first towns were set up on Long Island 350 years ago. Library bookshelves are groaning with dozens of books written about it.

The issue of NYC's waterfront is slightly different. NYC is planning for real parkland on most of the waterfront: not only BOTH sides of the Hudson, but along the East River for the wealthier areas in Brooklyn and Manhattan.

In other words, most of the city.

In direct contrast, it seems like those private development projects that are cutting the public off from the waterfront (and reserving it for the rich) is just planned for a handful of poorer areas: Red Hook, Greenpoint/Williamsburg, and Queens.  

Why? Look it up on zoning studies dating back to the early 1990s.

Is this fair? Ask our elected officials. Ask our community boards. They voted for it

The folks in Greenwich Village (http://www.gvshp.org/) have been doing marvelous work in trying to gain back their waterfront. Check out their work.

Unlike the 70s, funding is found to maintain these places. Go ahead, walk along the existing waterfront parks in Brooklyn and Manhattan. They are stunning!
