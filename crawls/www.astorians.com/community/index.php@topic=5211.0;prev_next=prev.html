January 01, 2007

Vowing to move forward and lead New York into a "more vibrant future," Eliot Spitzer was sworn in as the 54th governor of New York Monday afternoon at a rainy inauguration ceremony at the state Capitol in Albany.

"New York has slept through much of the past decade while the rest of the world has passed us by. Today is the day when all of that changes - when we stop standing still and start moving forward once more," said Spitzer in a 20 minute speech that followed his swearing-in ceremony, as well as the ceremonies for new Attorney General Andrew Cuomo and Lieutenant Governor David Paterson.

"I stand before you to announce that Day One of our time for change has arrived," he said.

Revoking his campaign slogan – "On Day One everything changes" – throughout the speech, Spitzer called for an end to "the politics of cynicism and division" witnessed under 12 years of Republican Governor George Pataki and pledged to clean up state government and revitalize the economy.

"This election was not about electing one person as governor. Rather it was about what we the people collectively elected for the future of our state," he said. "We chose pragmatism and ethics over partisan politics and dysfunction, and we demanded an end to gridlock."

Gridlock that Spitzer said has blocked much needed reform in education, job creation, taxes, health care and governmental ethics.

Spitzer has tall challenges ahead of him. He will face the same divided legislature as governor's before him.

The outgoing attorney general took the official oath of office in a private ceremony at midnight at the Executive Mansion with his wife and his parents standing by.

"What I want to observe just very briefly is not only what an honor it is to have so many friends, family, those who have been supporters, critics even, to have you here tonight to join in this excitement, but to look around this room at the portraits of those really remarkable individuals on whose shoulders we all stand," said Spitzer shortly after midnight.

The governor has a busy first day. Despite the wet weather, he started the day off with an early morning jog. Governor Spitzer also met with lawmakers this morning and attended an interfaith service.

Following the inauguration, Spitzer and his wife Silda received the public at the Executive Mansion.

From cheese to wine to Buffalo wings, around thirty of New York's most beloved foods were served as part of the inaugural lunch.

Brooklyn's own Junior's Cheesecake made the cut, as did Gus's Pickles, and Yonah Schimmel Knishes, which are both from the city.

Also on the menu: Rochester's famous "Garbage Plate," which is served at Nick Tahou Hots restaurants. The signature dish combines macaroni salad, hash browns, and your choice of meat, which is topped with special sauce, which also contains meat.

The new governor will host a free concert from 5-7 p.m., featuring James Taylor and Natalie Merchant.

'RIGHT' ON, GOV
CONSERVATIVE BOSS BACKS BUDGET IN STUNNER

By KENNETH LOVETT in Albany and CARL CAMPANILE in N.Y.

 March 16, 2007 -- Saying he's horrified by the huge spending increase sought by Senate Republicans, the leader of the state's Conservative Party grudgingly announced yesterday that he backs Gov. Spitzer's budget.

Conservative Chairman Michael Long, in a letter offering unprecedented support for Democrat Spitzer, said he hoped "we can work together this year."

Spitzer said he welcomed Long's support.

Long had been sharply critical of Spitzer during last year's election. But in his letter, he wrote: "William Shakespeare wrote in 'The Tempest,' 'Misery acquaints a man with strange bedfellows' and in 2007, I find myself in the same position."

While Long noted that he didn't like Spitzer's record-high $120.6 billion budget proposal, he also said Bruno's alternative - which aides to Spitzer say adds as much as $3 billion - is even worse.

"As difficult as your budget is, what the Republican-controlled Senate is proposing forces me to re-evaluate your proposals," wrote Long.

"We support your efforts to cut health care. And we support your efforts to increase the number of charter schools in New York state . . . Hopefully, we can work together this year."

Spitzer, acknowledging that he and Long don't usually agree on many issues, said he is "thrilled" to have the Conservative Party chairman's support in the budget battle.

"People have to be real about what we are doing," he said. "That's why Mike Long's observations today are so interesting and important. They show that this isn't a partisan issue. This isn't an issue of placement along a political spectrum.

"This is an issue of reality versus unreality."

Bruno again defended the Senate Republican budget plan, saying Long should recognize that it would provide needed tax relief for property and business owners.

"These are the people Mike Long should be speaking for, instead of echoing the sentiments of a governor whose budget increases taxes," Bruno said.

Senate Minority Leader Malcolm Smith (D-Queens), who in the past has been endorsed by the Conservative Party, said Long's comments strengthen the arguments against Bruno.

Smith laughed off Bruno's comments on Wednesday to Spitzer during a private screaming match that Smith and Assembly Speaker Sheldon Silver "are so far up your ass it's ridiculous."

"I told Joe there's room, he can come on up, too," Smith told WROW-AM in Albany. "He can come on up there."

Spitzer said he told Bruno that "I thought it was not the proper way to talk about some of his colleagues."

(9) Take a Step to Reduce Your Carbon Footprint: Buy Compact Fluorescent Lightbulbs
- A message from DFNYC's Teresa Weber

Compact Fluorescent lightbulbs (CFLs) may be more expensive at first glance. However, they last much longer than incandescent bulbs (average lifespan of a CFL is 8,000 hours), and they use less energy. This means that over the long run, you'll spend less on quantity and your electric bill. DFNYC Linkup host Dan Jacoby recently ran the numbers on this, looking at the price of compact fluorescent light bulbs and his Con Ed Bill, and found that he will save about $53 over the life of the CFL bulb.

A few things to note before buying CFLs: the bulbs typically come in two shades: cool white or warm white. Cool white bulbs have more of a bluish tint than those of your everyday incandescent bulbs. Warm white bulbs are closer to what you may currently be using. You will also note that the wattage for a CFL is very different from that of an incandescent bulb. An 11W CFL is roughly equal to a 60W incandescent bulb.

You can often find CFLs in your local hardware store. If they are not there, try other retailers. Buying in bulk will certainly save you money. You can check out http://www.1000bulbs.com/ to begin your search. Join us at Linkup tomorrow night for more tips on reducing your carbon footprint.
