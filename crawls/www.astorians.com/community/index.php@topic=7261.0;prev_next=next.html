Some establishments may not have made the switch, but is it also possible that, in some of these readings, the trans fats that were discovered are not coming from the frying oil used by that specific restaurant, but from the fry itself?  Or, more specifically, since most places use frozen french fries instead of fresh-cut fries (including Uncle George's, for instance), most frozen fries have a coating outside that contains hydrogenated oils, which are trans fats.  Just go to any supermarket's frozen food section and look for the bags of frozen fries and read the ingredients.  Most brands still include partially hydrogenated oils. 

So, in other words, even if the oil is trans fat-free, the fries may still have trans fats as a result of their coating. 

Also, thanks to FDA regulations and loopholes, something can be labeled "zero trans fat" even if it has, say, 0.49999999999 grams of trans fats per serving, since the FDA only requires food manufacturers to round figures in the nutrition facts up or down, which is why you never really see anything have, say, 87 calories per serving, but rather, 85 or 90.  It's a pet peeve of mine to see products in stores labeled that way, when a look at the ingredients reveals that there are, indeed, trans fats in the product.

Finally, I believe that frying at a very high heat can also alter the chemical bonds in any frying oil, even those that are truly trans fat-free, to form new trans fats during the frying process. 

I do support measures to reduce as much as possible trans fats and hydrogenated oils from foods, since they really, truly are toxic, and there's no real reason, from a taste or nutritional standpoint, why foods have to be fried/prepared with them when there are other, better alternatives.  However, they would be impossible to eradicate completely.  Some dairy products and meats also have natural trans fats within them as well.
