Title: Driving in Manhattan? You Pay, Under One Idea
Post by: megc on November 11, 2005, 12:02:28 PM
I find this (http://www.nytimes.com/2005/11/11/nyregion/11traffic.html?ex=1289365200&en=cbfe92f02c7ca75b&ei=5088&partner=rssnyt&emc=rss) intriguing:

It is an idea that has been successful in London, and is now being whispered in the ears of City Hall officials after months of behind-the-scenes work by the Partnership for New York City, the city's major business association: congestion pricing.

The idea is to charge drivers for entering the most heavily trafficked parts of Manhattan at the busiest times of the day. By creating a financial incentive to carpool or use mass transit, congestion pricing could smooth the flow of traffic, reduce delays, improve air quality and raise the speed of crawling buses.

To be sure, it is far from being a reality, or even a complete proposal - when pressed, the mayor's spokesman said it was not on his second-term agenda. Yet the Partnership's work suggests a plan that, if carried out, could profoundly alter the way New Yorkers and those visiting the city use their cars.

Title: Driving in Manhattan? You Pay, Under One Idea
Post by: lanseaux on November 12, 2005, 08:55:40 AM
I do not think all cars should be banned from Manhattan.  

I agree that the current system is unsustainable, but there are a few things to consider.  I have a friend who cannot go up and down stairs, so she cannot take the subway.  The bus is similarly impractical (she can walk, so trying to convince the bus driver, even with ID, to activate the kneeler is almost impossible.)  She lives in Manhattan.  The only way she can commute or get around in general is to drive.  (Disabled drivers are exempt from the congestion toll in London, btw.)

I bought a washing machine from a guy who lived in Chelsea.  The only way I could get it home was by car.  (Timarimba, I hear you!  I've shlepped drum kits to and from gigs on the subway - NOT fun.)

Bloomberg proposed this four years ago as well, and it was shot down.  I think one reason for that was his proposals were not sufficiently researched or based on needs of New Yorkers.  There are valid reasons to have cars in Manhattan.  There is also a very serious need to change the current system, but advocating a complete ban will not work, nor should it.  I'm not offering a solution because I think the only tenable one will require a great deal of urban research and planning.
