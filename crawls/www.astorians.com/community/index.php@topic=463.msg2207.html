Quote from: KilDaraSidhe

***iPods can also have the battery changed by oneself, but this is not an option for people who aren't good with sharp objects or patience.  The cost is significantly less this way.  As the owner of an iPod for several years, I have yet to change the battery and have had little degredation of play time.  I can't imagine needing to do this more than a couple times over the life of the player.

***iPods don't convert anything at all.  They simply load whatever the person has in iTunes.  iTunes can be set to rip songs as five different formats currently.  (as a side note, you can use whatever third party rip tool you like to format the music whatever way you desire for the iPod as long as it is iPod compatible with mp3 format, wav, or Apple, but not custom Sony or Microsoft formats)

***There are free utilities on the internet to move songs from the iPod back to a computer.  However, I would strongly encourage all readers to BACK UP EVERYTHING on your computers.  One can purchase customized programs to do the same thing as the free ones but with some more flexibility in what they do.  The extra tools are not probably going to be useful to people not so technologically savvy.

Not to start a flamewar, but in the matter of the ipod, "you get what you pay for" is not entirely accurate.  In my experience, I got a dead ipod after a year.  :)  But first, some gripes:

The ipod has an obfuscated filesystem - requiring a 3rd party program to take your music files off of an ipod is odd to me, and a bit shoddy of apple.

The ipod supports AAC, MP3, and Apple Lossless - all proprietary encodings.  But what if I want to rid myself of digital rights management?  I can't because the ipod does not support ogg or FLAC encodings.  Nitpicky, but with apple attempting to appeal to geeks, this oversight is inexcusable.

The ipod 'photo' apparently has sound problems ...
http://forums.macrumors.com/archive/index.php/t-121067.html

My ipod died a bit over a year after i bought it.  My wife's ipod is always on odd behavior (skipping, not turning off ...) . I would argue (and I think i would have a pretty strong case - remember the battery debacle?) that Apple makes their ipods to break.

I have a new player now (a cowon iaudio X5).  It uses open music encoding formats, has an FM radio, lasts about 10-14 hours on the battery, displays pictures, plays AVI movies (overkill, but interesting), and costs about the same as an ipod.  I can browse the filesystem with ease.  Plus, IMHO, it sounds better than my old ipod did.  Do I recommend it to everyone?  No, because Itunes makes organizing music a snap - but I would argue that the iaudio X5's quility is a few notches above the ipod's.  I miss Itunes a bit, but that's not a huge loss for me.

I think the ipod wins in pure convenience, not quality.  Hooking up an ipod to a mac with itunes is about as brainless as breathing.  Which is great!  I just wish that apple could concentrate on making the ipod a little more rugged, a bit more friendly to the power user, and less entrenched in proprietary audio formats.  It seems that the newer ipods have better battery power, and they now support podcasting.   Unfortunately, apple has already lost my faith and my dollar.  I'll give them another look in a few years.
