OK, I'll grant you that the bagel is essentially a New York invention (at least I think it is), but I beg to differ that the pizza of Chicago or other cities is merely a "rendition" of New York pizza.  It's its own thing - it's not trying to be thin crust pizza.  And thin crust pizza in Chicago probably tastes just as out of place as deep dish pizza tastes in New York, I imagine.

You missed my point.  I'm not addressing the location of the invention of either pizza or bagels -- in fact, both have their roots in Europe.  I'm talking about foods that are in the soul of New Yorkers, and what New Yorkers think about those foods in other cities, regardless of who did it first.   

That said, however, the first pizzeria in the United States is considered by many to be Lombardi's (opened in 1905), and the style of pizza they serve is, of course, thin-crust, which is itself a rendition of Neopolitan pizza, whose roots stretch back to Roman times.  Chicago-style pizza was first created in 1943 by Ike Sewell, the founder of Pizzeria Uno.  Sure, it's its own thing, but it is an adaptation, a rendition, of a more traditional form of pizza (which is basically what New York pizza is).  Here's an interesting article that includes the origins of Chicago-style pizza:  Sewell eventually agreed to forgo enchiladas for pizza, but not until he’d inflated the thin-crusted Neapolitan recipe to make it more palatable to Americans. “Ike tasted it and said nobody would eat it, it’s not enough,” Evelyne Slomon, author of The Pizza Book, said. “So he put gobs and gobs of stuff on it.”
