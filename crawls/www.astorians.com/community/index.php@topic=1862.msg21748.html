Goldfish Boy, I think you're right about London.  London is funny because there's a part called Westminster (and the postal address actually says Westminster, not London) that includes the Buildings of Parliament, Buckingham Palace, and other such places.  The rest of it is called London, and the further you go in any direction starts to be called by their local names, like Wimbledon, Motspur Park, Morden, Fulham, etc.  I'm more familiar with the southern part of "London" (in quotes because I'm really not sure where the cutoff is) having spent 3 months there a few years ago near the Raynes Park, New Malden area.  It seems like it's similar to here nomenclature-wise, not that there are boroughs, but the outlying areas aren't really suburbs, and they are referred to by a name and have a specific mailing address (like Astoria), but they're not truly part of "the city."  I don't think people who live in Wimbledon, for example, although they are only a 20-minute Underground ride away from Central London, would call themselves Londoners.  But for the most part, I don't know jack about squat; I just felt like throwing in my two-cents.

Yes, that's a time-honored outer-boroughs term for Manhattan.  I first learned it from a Brooklyn friend, when I lived in Manhattan, and when I became a Queensite by adoption I started using it too.  Alfred Kazin named his boyhood memoir A Walker in the City, and if you read it you find he means a walker in Manhattan--Kazin was born and raised in Brooklyn.

My mother, a native of the Bronx who lived for a time in Brooklyn, remembers another term.  If she was going to Manhattan she said she was going uptown.
