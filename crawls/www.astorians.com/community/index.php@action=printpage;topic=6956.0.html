Title: Re: So Long JackFM, welcome back Oldies 101.1
Post by: neo11 on July 12, 2007, 12:13:33 AM
I'm entering this discussion a bit late but I am a bit of a radio enthusiast, albeit one that agrees with the common sentiment around here that the New York City radio landscape is more of a tune-out than anything else.

Count me in among the people that's going to miss Jack, if only because of the music they played, which as far as commercial radio goes, was pretty diverse and interesting.  I especially enjoyed the excellent 80's weekends and hearing all my favorite 80s new wave classics.  I can understand why so many people hated Jack, because it replaced the beloved CBS FM, but I also think that in the two years since the original CBS-FM was "jacked," nostalgia and a form of romanticism set in amongst its former listeners.  I've been following the radio landscape for a number of years, including the New York Radio Message Board that was referenced in the first post of this thread, as well as other radio message boards, and I remember that in the couple of years before CBS-FM flipped to Jack, most of what I read about CBS-FM came in the form of complaints.  Complaints that the playlist was too limited, that there were too many ads, that they turned off the reverb, that they turned the reverb back on again, that there was no 50's music, that there wasn't a lot of 60's music, that there wasn't enough 80's music, that there was too much 80's music, and so on.  And for all the crying about the station changing formats, a lot of people seem to have also forgotten that many legendary CBS-FM jocks were let go in unceremonious fashion long before the format switch....which, of course, led to more complaining.

There's really nothing here to indicate that the "new" CBS-FM is going to be much better.  It's still going to have a 300-400 song (at most) playlist, just like pretty much every other commercial station, and you're still going to hear a lot of the same burned out songs.  There's not going to be much 50's music except as an occasional "oh wow" type of moment.  There's surely still going to be a lot of ads, and ultimately, in the end, it's still going to be a radio station run by a huge corporation in which profit is the only bottom line.

Don't get me wrong: oldies deserves a spot on the NYC dial.  NYC radio is so bland and limited it's absolutely mind-numbing.  Aside from oldies, there's so many other formats here that are missing: a good triple-A format, some modern rock (not the dino-rock with a couple of new Linkin Park songs per hour that K-Rock...also a CBS station...features), definitely a real dance station (not the sappy disco and 80s ballads that KTU passes off as "dance" music), a station with that classic alternative/new-wave sound, and, god forbid, even a country music station.  Unfortunately though, in a corporate-run radio climate, with CBS and Clear Channel and all those other large companies putting millions at stake, the common thinking is that only a certain amount of formats work.  That's why oldies was taken off the air in the first place, except that Jack largely failed...so CBS is going to give it a whirl with an "updated" classic hits station which might buy it a few good years of revenue and profits.  But I wouldn't be surprised if one day, a few years down the line, when the next format of the moment appears, CBS jumps at the opportunity and ditches its "aging" classic hits format.

By the way...it was awesome to see a few other people refer to the old 92.7 WLIR.  Now THAT was an awesome station.  I was too young to catch the station's new wave heyday in the 80s, but I listened to the station throughout my teenage years in the 90s and all the way up to January 9th, 2004 when the station was sold.  WLIR still exists out on the East End of Long Island, on 107.1, but the signal doesn't come close to reaching New York City, though I do believe there is an online stream.  In my opinion though the station doesn't sound as good as it did when it was on 92.7, even in its latter days.  The station had personality and great music and played a ton of stuff no other commercial station ever touched.  I remember LIR After Hours with all the dance remixes of the new wave and newer stuff.  I actually was able to get the last few hours of WLIR on 92.7 on mp3, recorded right off the air from Astoria...I listen to it sometimes when I want to remember what real radio (in my subjective, humble opinion) sounded like :)

As for the overall radio landscape in NYC...pitiful, for all the reasons stated above and more.  But again...it's the corporate thinking.  A full power FM station in NYC is worth upwards of a $100 million.  Even WLIR, with its lower-powered signal coming from the Queens-Nassau border, sold for $62 million in 2004!  They don't want to take perceived risks, and they play it ultra safe....though we've seen that even with that, they fail and fail bigtime.  I don't consider Jack a big failure, though it was under a dark cloud from day one with the way CBS-FM was thrown off the air.  Now...Free FM....now *that* was a failure, especially when the same format was tried, and failed, only a few years before, by the same company, on 102.7.  You can also thank the FCC...for never updating its FM allocations from the early days in the 1940s and 50s, leading to a limited amount of stations.  Travel to Europe, and FM frequencies are separated by 0.1 or even 0.05 mhz instead of 0.2 here, and in some countries, like Italy and Greece, the FM dial is crammed with stations literally 0.2 mhz apart.  I always found it fascinating that I heard more and better American music on the radio whenever I traveled to Greece, than I did in New York  :-P

Post by: DJArturo on July 18, 2007, 05:39:21 PM
I'll stick with the bottom of the dial: WFMU, WBGO, WSOU, etc... There's something for everyone on these stations and they're not ruled by corporate sponsors or underwriters as to what content they can or can't play. In fact, WFMU is 100% listener-supported and doesn't take a dime from the government or any corporations. Granted, you might hear Kenny G (the DJ, not the sax "player") play a baby crying for three hours in the middle of the day, but you'll also consistently hear and be introduced to a lot of great music--both new and old.

I listen online to all of these stations since they lack the giant megawat broadcast towers as their commercial counterparts:
WFMU - wfmu.org (http://wfmu.org)
WBGO - wbgo.org (http://wbgo.org)
WSOU - wsou.net (http://wsou.net)

Of course, there's always WWOZ (wwoz.org (http://wwoz.org)), New Orleans' jewel of a radio station. And then there's my podcast, Crate Digger's Gold (http://djarturo.podomatic.com)--one hour of scratchy old 45rpm records from all walks of life!

The non-commercial stations are quite an oasis in the sea of mediocre commercial radio, but I think even here, there's programming missing.  WBGO is jazz 24-7.  WNYU is free-form but with a weak signal and is only on part of the day...the other half is WFDU (Farleigh Dickinson University in NJ) with an even weaker signal over NYC.  WSOU is pretty much just hard rock and metal.  WKCR is great for certain, specific genres of music.  WFUV always came across as too holier than thou to me.  WFMU is a great station and truly follows the free-form philosophy, but the signal is bad in Queens (they do webcast, as most of these stations do, however).

My alma mater, Stony Brook U., has a great free-form radio station that you can catch over the air if you're out on Long Island, or online...WUSB 90.1 FM.  Everything from polka to techno to rockabilly to alternative news/talk to showtunes.  I was program director there as a student for a year and a half...great experience even if some of the staffers there were....interesting, to say the least.
