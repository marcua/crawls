Somebody e-mailed me to grumble about the above post.  I stand by my words.

Getting a district landmarked is a lot of work; please believe me.  There are doors to knock on, petitions to draft, meetings to attend and arrogant opponents to deal with. Folks from outside of the community can help a great deal, and some will. Still, the primary stakeholders for landmarking will always be the residents of the community itself. When a sizable number of residents gets on board,  City agencies and politicians will start to take notice.

That's how Sunnyside Gardens got landmark status. People in the community WANTED it!  And they pushed hard. Sure, there was some opposition, mostly from a strange coalition of "My Home is My Castle" types and hot-heads who tried playing the racism card.  But their "grass roots coalition" collapsed as quickly as it formed.

Residents of OAV, the ball is in your court.

The problem is likely a combination of apathy, ignorance of the facts and a "my home is my castle, so I can do whatever I want" mentality. Here are some things for locals to consider:

1.  Landmarking helps protect your investment by keeping the area attractive.
2.  Help is available for homeowners who need to make expensive repairs in landmarked areas.
3.  Landmarking actually increases property values. Look at places like Cobble Hill and Brooklyn Heights.

Sunnyside Gardens residents wanted the advantages of landmarking, and that is why most were in favor.  Folks from outside the community who wanted to preserve this special area gave valuable support, but in the final analysis this was a fight won because so many locals put many hours of hard work into the process.  They collected signatures.  They did research. They sent hundreds of letters and e-mails.  All of this was more than idealism; they knew that landmarking works.

Hi everyone,

Some of you may be aware of Council Member Vallone's ongoing efforts with the Department of City Planning to have a comprehensive zoning study done for Astoria.  His goal is to target certain areas in the neighborhood that are appropriate for larger developments and preserve those that aren't, such as Old Astoria Village.  Our office has had several meetings with City Planning and it appears as though the study will be happening shortly.  To that end, City Planning is asking for pictures where buildings have gone up that are detrimental to the character of the neighborhood.  For example, just on my block alone a 3 story 6 apartment building sits right next to a older wooden frame home.  We have submitted many examples and pictures of this and we are looking for even more to show.  If anyone would like to submit a location that they feel would help strengthen the case for our neighborhood's rezoning please feel free to post it and we will go out and take pictures.  If you have pictures you would like to email I can be reached at cathleen.sims@council.nyc.ny.us or cathleensims@hotmail.com. 

The Council Member also wanted me to extend his thanks to all who attended last weeks meeting and he is looking forward to more in the future!
