This thread has actually gone in the direction I had anticipated.  I'm not so interested in the actual show as I am the underlying subjects we've been discussing.  And I'm also trying to relate some of my own personal experience as a musician to it.  For example, I have no false hope of fame or wealth, or even the notion of making a living playing music.  I do it because I love it.  I don't feel as though I'm entitled to anything aside from what I've worked for.  And I'll fight tooth and nail to get gigs and perform, and meet new musicians, and network, etc...  But I don't expect, or anticipate anyone will magically give me all these things.  It's my responsibility.  And what I see with this show, is a general sense of entitlement from most, but certainly not all, of the contestants.  For example, there was a girl on last night with a really nice, big voice.  She was a crack baby, and had a tough upbringing.  She was just glad to be given the opportunity, and didn't expect to necessarily go through.  I'll be rooting for her.  But I find she is the exception to the rule with the contestants, and with a lot of the youth I see around NYC.  I agree that Myspace and other social networking sites are a symptom, and not the cause.

My overall point in this particular post, is that a sense of entitlement is BS.  And most of the kids that have a sense of entitlement need to be slapped silly and given a solid dose of reality.  Life ain't easy, and you don't always get what you think you want or deserve.  Instead of treating kids with kid gloves and trying too hard to protect their feelings, perhaps we should go back to teaching that life isn't always easy.  Sometimes it sucks.  And maybe, just maybe, you aren't going to be famous.  So what are you going to do now that your fame is nonexistent?  And while opportunities are given from other people, it's our own work that determines whether those people give us an opportunity.

At the advice of a friend, I tried watching "American Idol" in the second season. I watched the first or second episode. Two things bothered me. One was the people who were going on these auditions and were clearly pulling the leg of the producers. People were faking a belief in actual talent just to get on TV. It seemed to me that they knew they were horrible, they were consciously trying to be even more horrible and they were looking to get famous by being horrible.

But while I watched audition after audition, something else bothered me that I couldn't quite put my finger on. Then a very stylishly dressed pretty blonde gave a thoroughly bland performance of Stevie Wonder's "Superstition". Did she have a better voice than me? Yes. She even added some of those popular vocal trills meant to approximate emotion in so many songs these days. The judges, of course,  loved her! One even complemented her on the "cool boots" she was wearing. That's when I realized what the second thing that was bothering me was. We don't need to be looking for another stylishly dressed pop tart simulating emotion to sing Stevie Wonder. We need to be looking for the next Stevie Wonder! So I turned the show off and haven't watched it since.

P.S. And yeah, seriously, what the hell is up with Jewel?

What Jtrane says makes me think of a story on NPR about a middle eastern woman (didn't catch the country - came in at the middle of the show) who was in a horrible marriage but did not leave because making a decision based on her own needs rather than the needs of her family and community was completely at odds with her worldview. I was struck at how wrong this would seem through american eyes. We would say - and I think in this situation it would be true - "You have to do what's best for you."

I think the behavior and attitudes we are discussing here reflect a basic value of "Doing what's best for me" that goes beyond simply seeing after our basic needs and happiness and on towards ignoring the needs and happiness of others to get what we want. Look at other threads about people who dump trash on the subway, play their music so loud that their neighbors can't sleep, etc. Message: My momentary pleasure and convenience is more important than the cleanness of the subway or the comfort of my neighbors.

From American Idol to neighborhood issues may seem like a stretch, but I think there's a connection between the narcissism Jtrane mentions, the lust for fame and money, and a complete disinterest in the needs or well-being of those not intimately connected with us. I think there's a connection between individualism taken to an unhealthy extreme, consumerism, and lack of caring for others.
