Here is a different perspective -- that of the student, specifically a non-Catholic sent to private (not parochial) Catholic school starting in 7th grade.  As I am also now a parent, it is a relevant issue to me as well.

Why do parents send their kids specifically to parochial schools? I would imagine that some like the fact that parochial schools are Catholic - and some like what they perceive as a more "old-fashioned" way of education than the NYC public schools and don't care about the religion aspect.  Since a parochial school is affiliated with a specific church (I think), parents who attend that church may want their children to go there - they may think there is more accountability since they are members of the affiliated church.  Parochial schools tend to be less expensive than private Catholic schools.  There may be a minority who don't like the religion aspect but consider it a valid trade-off for what they think is a better education.  I don't know.  Unless the religion matters to the parent, I don't see the appeal myself, and I also always had a similar impression that they weren't stellar academically.

As a non-Catholic, my own personal experience of Catholic private school was horrendously miserable.  I think if you are not Catholic you are at a distinct disadvantage if you are sent to a Catholic school, whether it is private or parochial.  It wasn't "discrimination" or bias against me in any way relating to the schooling - it was a good school academically and I did well.  It was things like - being required to take the religion classes, and being made to go to Mass - and even confession, which completely flummoxed me as a concept.  I was expected to know religious concepts I had never even heard of, and they didn't particularly care if it put me at a disadvantage.  It's also a little disconcerting to be taught that you are (probably) going to Hell, even if you don't believe it.  Some of the nuns, especially the ones who taught religion, clearly had a disdain for me.  I was never outright teased or taunted about not being Catholic by the other students, but it was definitely an underlying issue and I felt singled out and uncomfortable a lot of the time.

Kind of a loaded question, don't you think?

But seriously, the schools in Astoria are, for the most part, very good.  It's easy to find out which ones are good on Insideschools.org and greatschools.org, and on the NYC Department of Education website.  Personally I don't know why you'd forego a really good and free public school for a parochial school, and with few exception there's no evidence that parochial schools are, individually or on the whole, better academically.  I do agree that the parochial schools have some serious advantages for working parents who can't pick up their kids right after school.  Some public schools have afterschool programs, but not all, and programs that are available aren't necessarily available for every grade.

I think one component of this issue is the question of what schools are supposed to do.  In my view, along with the three R's, kids need to learn citizenship and socialization.  What better place to do that than in a public school, where people come from varied social, economic, and ethnic backgrounds? I moved to NYC because I wanted to be in a place that has everyone and everything.  I love looking at the diversity in my sons' classrooms, and I just don't think you get that in parochial schools because the student body tends to be from a much narrower swath of the general public.

This is all aside from the issue of whether parent want their kids to get religious education -- that seems to me to be a personal preference issue.

I wanted to start this conversation for not so purely selfish reasons (but somewhat selfish)

As a strong believer in public schools I want to send my son to one. (I also don't see a private option in the vicinity that I could afford or that interests me).  I think the best way to make sure that the local public schools are great is to make sure that local active parents are very  involved in their community's school.  It bothers me to see (read) about active parents who have their kids in private schools (schools where my child and many other local children wouldn't be welcome - although they might be allowed to attend) because these are community activists who could be helping to build great schools for all children (okay - maybe very selfish).

Through reading your responses I certainly understand your choices.  Especially thoughts that the public system can be daunting and the Catholic schools are "known" vs public "unknown" entity. 

To respond to a couple of questions people asked. 
What happens when a student misbehaves in the public schools:  it depends on the infraction.  But no student is thrown out without a hearing.  Even once a student IS thrown out of public school - he/she is taken in by another public school.  (unless the infraction is extremely severe).  I believe the tolerance of misbehavior is MUCH higher at the public schools (because it has to be - students are guaranteed their right to an education).

As far as civics .... I don't remember the exact question.  All students at my school (and I think all NYC public schools) are required to complete a certain number of community service hours each year.  The older the student, the higher the commitment.  Also the mayor's office has started an outreach program this year to help make sure that all schools complete at least one community service project each year.  So far this year our schools has held 3 different Special Olympics sports events, a Special Olympics Halloween party, worked in a soup kitchen, held a food drive (City Harvest), a book drive (Project Cicero) and a $$$ collection for Clinton's Haiti Relief Fund.   (These are just the events I know about - not every event/drive is well publicized).

10 active families with the ability to fund raise $2000 each = an after school program for a public school.   10 families putting their kids into the local public school rather than the private one could make a world of difference for 150 families.
