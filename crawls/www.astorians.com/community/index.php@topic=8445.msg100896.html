Quote from: Debbie on November 08, 2007, 08:52:08 PM
This is my question, and no one can really answer it, but we can conjecture - let's say that we were simply not here, that we never evolved.  What would the earth be like today?  Would the hole in the ozone be the same size?  Would waters and air be polluted?  Would the arctic ice still be melting at this rate?  What would it be like?  Would the weather still be the same?  Let's use our imagination, just for fun!

It's a good question, Debbie, and you're definitely right that we can only imagine the answer.

I think the world wouldn't be as polluted with all of the man-made toxins and waste that we've added, that's for sure.  But, who is to say we wouldn't have the same issues?  If we're talking a similar universe, with the absence of the human race as the only instance, then you could probably take out the pollutants and hole in the ozone, but who knows? 

There would be thousands, maybe millions, more of species like buffalo, whales, and dodo birds wouldn't be extinct.  There wouldn't be RATS in, what is now, NYC (they are purportedly from the Fertile Crescent and spread with human civilization), or pigeons (they were brought to Nova Scotia ~400 years ago by Europeans).

The temperature could still be warming, the weather patterns could be just as wild and, hell, we could be WARMER (if you can go along with the Global Dimming pollution theory).  The warmer oceans might have melted the frozen methane in the sea floors, releasing all of the toxic greenhouse gases that are trapped in there and turned us into Venus.

Or, weather could be drastically cooler (ice age, even), or more violent storms, since the large amounts of trees would have, undoubtedly, created different weather patterns than we know of today. 

Maybe lightning would have struck an area under drought conditions and full of dry foilage.  There could be HUGE wildfires, much more massive than we know, since we can combat them now and there are, for the most part, fewer large forested areas.  These fires could have blacked out the sun, created their own weather, and spewed enormous amounts of carbon and CO2 into the air. 

But, if we're talking the exact same events happening, it would likely be a different temperature; warmer or colder depending on what would happen with all of the pre-civilization foilage and it's effect on temperatures and weather.  Of course, if you think of the large amounts of trees that were here previous to humans (or, more accurately, civilization), then there would be MUCH less CO2. 

Are we ruling out the occurrence of global events like the Siberian, Deccan, Emieshan Trap eruptions that spewed out toxic gases, or any of the meteorite strikes, that helped cause previous extinctions from happening (or happening again)?  If these didn't occur, we could still have dinosaurs, trilobites, or many other types of plant life that rule the planet.

Of course, there's always the chance everything would be in perfect harmony...

...just waiting for the aliens to land and take over with their new civilization!  :-P

What do you think the world would be like, Debbie?  You posed the question, but didn't give your thoughts.

But it was the miraculous size and quantity and variety of things - the sheer prodigality of life - that left the most impression.  Travelers spoke of vast meadows of grass "as high as a mans middle" and forests with towering stands of walnut, cedar, chestnut, maple, and oak.  Orchards bore apples of incomparable sweetness and "pears larger than a fist."  Every spring the hills and fields were dyed red with ripening strawberries, and so many birds filled the woods "that men can scarcely go through them for the whistling, the noise, and the chattering."  Boats crossing the bay were escorted by schools of playful whales, seals, and porpoises.  Twelve-inch oysters and six-foot lobsters crowded offshore waters, and so many fish thrived in streams and ponds that they could be taken by hand.  Woods and tidal marshlands teemed with bears, wolves, foxes, raccoons, otters, beavers, quail, partridge, forty-pound wild turkeys, doves "so numerous that the light can hardly be discerned where they fly," and countless deer "feeding, or gamboling or resting in the shades in full view."  Wild swans were so plentiful "that the bays and shores where they resort appear as if they were dressed in white drapery."  Blackbirds roosted together in such numbers that one hunter killed 170 with a single shot; another bagged eleven sixteen-pound gray geese in the same way.  "There are some persons who imagine that the animals of the country will be destroyed in time," mused Van der Donck, "but this is an unnecessary anxiety."

I always wonder that too.  Just think about the fact when you're standing under the Empire State Building that that used to be REALLY far north 200 years ago.  Even a hundred or so years ago, you could still find open spaces on the island; they feature some pictures in the real estate section in the paper of interesting buildings and areas that always amaze me. 

Just realizing that Washington Square and Bryant Parks both used to be potter's fields and Randall's Island used to be a prison, leper colony, and mental institution when I look at them today is hard to believe.  Reading the stories about those places and seeing the abandoned hospital at the southern end of the island is such a trip when they're compared to the places today.

The amount of great figures in history that have stood on Manhattan Island alone is enough to inspire awe.  The amount of ambition, hopes, dreams, and intelligence it took to build it to the place it is today is absolutely astounding.

I really have never been to another city that has just felt so alive with history, character, and life.  All it takes is popping out of the subway station in Herald, Times, or Union Squares (just to name a few) and looking around to amaze me.  The fact that so many races, religions, and backgrounds have worked together, even if not only in perfect harmony, to build this city to what we know it as today is a testament to humanity.

I definitely love New York.  Although, I'd be lying if I didn't wish it was somewhere with a little warmer climate.  I like being outside and exploring and it isn't as much fun when the weather sucks.  If NYC had San Diego weather, I'd never leave. :)
