It's obviously a complicated issue, though.  Yes, these people are someone's child, and they are human beings whose plight we cannot ignore.  On the other hand, NO ONE has the right to make you feel unsafe on the street or in the subway, be they mentally ill or in the throes of substance addiction (as many homeless are), or for any other reason.  The streets (and the subway) belong to all of us, and we all have the right to feel safe. 

Further, it's not morally valid to "let" people exercise the "right" to live on the streets, because in most cases, they are not of sound mind to make that decision.  Our city has perhaps more available services for the homeless and less fortunate than any other American city.  And yes, that is part of what we all pay for with our taxes.  The trick is to get the homeless in contact with the system where they can get the help they need.  That is the most important thing that MTA outreach workers, or any of us, can do.  By giving short term handouts, (and we have all done that, myself included) we are actually hindering the real work that needs to be done.

But unfortunately, it's beyond difficult to get people to engage with the system.  I spoke with a woman a few months ago who puts homeless people in touch with services on Skid Row in Los Angeles.  Her frustration is that advocates tell her that sometimes it takes six months, sometimes it takes six years, but eventually, you will be able to break through to people and they'll seek the help they need.  But, as my friend on Skid Row pointed out, while we're waiting for people to be "willing" to engage with the system, it's not like they're deposited in some leafy meadow owned by the state.  They're in front of businesses, using drugs, hassling people on the street,  etc.

My personal stance is that we can't ignore the homeless by letting them exercise the "right" to be on the streets.  Because there is no such right.  And because they are not of sound mind to make that decision.  So to help them in the short term with sandwiches and small cash donations is just contributing to the problem.

So, yes.  Call 311.  Repeatedly, if need be.  I still hope to run into the members of the Broadway subway station's famous "Bum Haven" soon, so I can call 311 on them.  No.  Call 311 FOR them.
