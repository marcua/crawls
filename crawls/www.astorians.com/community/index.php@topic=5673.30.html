Do they still release Riker's Island prisoners at Queensboro Plaza?   I think they probably get discharged in the early morning hours because their sentence would end at midnight.   They spend the overnight hours getting processed.   By the time that happens hours have gone by.

I bet they will find a new place for that once those new buildings start to open and the area continues to gentrify.   I remember that before the Time-Warner Center was built on Columbus Circle the old New York Coliseum stood there.   Since prisons are about the only industry left in Upstate New York many New York City prisoners end up getting sent way, way, way upstate.   An entrepreneurial sort started a bus service so the lprisoners' women could visit them.   The guy would pick up the women (and their children) at the Coliseum.   However, once Time-Warner and the upscale mall (called an "urban shopping experience" by the complex's PR flacks) the guy had to find a new spot to load his passengers.   They are now picked up at 58th Street and Broadway.   The same thing will no doubt happen in Queensboro Plaza if it hasn't happened already.

To my knowledge, inmates take the Q 101 R bus off of Rikers Island.  The bus terminates in Queens Plaza.  The city bus doesn't run at all hours, so DOC buses the recently released there as well.

The Queens Plaza renovation got a mention in the Times last weekend.

Quote
Swaled Streets

In Seattle, greening the street with landscaped areas called swales has cut the total volume of storm water runoff from the street by 99 percent. Swaled streets also cost 20 percent less to build than conventional roads. But swaled streets also have aesthetic benefits.

Birds are going to love this place, said Michael Singer, an artist who is part of a team helping the city to reinvent Queens Plaza, in Long Island City. The plan, for which construction will begin this fall, will replace soul-sapping asphalt with bird-friendly turf, put layers of plants around the skeletal infrastructure of the elevated subway lines, and tie those planted areas into landscaped areas with cisterns to collect storm water.

To gauge the effect of the plan, Mr. Singer said, imagine avenues lined with blocklong patches of rustling leaves and singing birds. All of that pulls attention away from things you want to put out of sight and out of mind when youre in a dense urban area, he said.
