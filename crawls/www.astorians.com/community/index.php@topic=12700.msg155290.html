The Colin Powell endorsement and the Republican party
« on: October 19, 2008, 12:40:35 PM »
This is pretty sad. Colin Powell just endorsed Obama on Meet The Press, which isn't the sad part. I wanted to look around at the news that's going up about this to see what people's responses are, mainly in the comments sections because that's where you get an idea of each party's talking points. Of course, the Obama supporters are happy that he endorsed Obama, as McCain's supporters would've been if he'd supported McCain. Despite the Iraq war, Powell is still seen as one of the more respectable figures in politics. More of my concern was if the crazier Republicans would start discrediting Powell's views and opinion immediately.

I'm not seeing a whole lot of people saying things like "Well that's too bad. I generally respect Powell's opinion, but I disagree with him on this." What I am seeing is stuff like "Obama and Powell are Black. Is this NEWS," "Had Powell endorsed McCain he would be accused of being an Uncle Tom by the left wing lunatics, many here assembled," and "Shame on Colin Powell for endorsing a Chicago thug with ties to Ayers, Pfleger, and Rev Wright--American haters." Those are exact quotes. This is the stuff that's destroying the Republican party at this point, and I blame McCain for this with the tone of hate his campaign took on a couple weeks ago. It's going to be hard for them to shake that.

While I admittedly don't have a whole lot of Republican friends and family members (not on purpose, mind you), the ones I do have are not happy with McCain and especially don't like Sarah Palin. In their attempts to paint Obama as a Muslim terrorist as well as their going right along with all these bank bailouts and buyouts, the Republican party doesn't seem to have much of a defined platform anymore aside from this relatively new "family values" thing that lures in the evangelicals, and the idea of small town people being superior to those elitist coastal liberals. How do the Republicans here feel about this? Do you truly like McCain/Palin and the direction the party has taken in recent months/years, or do you feel like the party is losing it?

I think it's less a question of the direction the party is going in and more a question of defending my values and my way of life.  Why on earth would I be excited at the prospect of a presidency that will jeopardize the things that I stand for?  Is this the ideal Republican ticket?  No.  But they are much more in line with what I stand for as compared to the Obama/Biden ticket.  Unless Obama starts talking across the board tax cuts, education choice and the other issues I hold dear, he will never win me over.  What scares me particularly is that with a so called "super" majority, there will be nothing to moderate Obama's legislative agenda.

And the McCain campaign proper is in no way trying to paint a picture of Obama as a Muslim terrorist.  Wacko right wing activists, maybe.  But not the campaign.  But there are real, legitimate questions about his associations which, yes, the campaign has raised.  Obama attended Wright's church for 20 years and didn't pick up that Wright's rhetoric was out of line with what mainstream Americans believe?  It's not problematic that his education adviser stands by his past acts of terrorism?  There is a questionable track record of judgement here.  Personally, I think that Obama, a Hawaiian with a Harvard pedigree had to leave Barry behind and become Barack to make it in Chicago politics.  He had to establish the right identity.  That comes with the price of having his associations questioned.  And while I won't necessarily say that some of those people in question are America haters, they certainly don't believe in an America in accordance with what the founders envisioned.

As far as small town America, the point, or at least the perception, is that the coastal elites don't have any idea what life is like there.  You just said yourself that you know only a handful of Republicans.  What do you mean the direction the party has taken the past few years?  I'd say that the Bush presidency, on the whole, has not been good for the party and that once the party is past that era, it will be better off.  I'd also say that absolute power corrupts absolutely.  The Republicans held both houses of congress for a time, and the White House.  They forgot about the values of the conservative revolution.  It's nothing they can't be reminded of again.  I reject this notion the McCain is an extension of Bush, or that all Republicans are like Bush.  I understand that it's a politically expedient argument to make, but it's a load of crap.  Did McCain and other Republicans vote with Bush a good percentage of the time?  Yes.  He is the titular head of their party.  That is how politics works.

Regarding Powell, his past service to this country is inestimable, and he is entitled to his opinion as is any private citizen.  I place about as much stock in his Obama endorsement as I place in Lieberman's position on McCain.
