I know Reagan said it but I didn't realize it was a fundamental conservative approach. Do you mean that it's important to modern conservatism or that it has always been in the U.S.?

There are many strains of conservatism today, but an underlying philosophy of most is that people are happiest when they are free to choose their own course.  Basically conservatives are in favor of small l, or classical liberalism.  Of course things get a little more wonky when you look at what extreme cultural conservatives, or the Christian right are interested in.  Sadly, personal liberty actually seems to be less important to some "conservatives" now than in the past.  If you look at the philosophers and economists that conservatives revere (Lockes, Milton Freidman, Hayek, etc.), they're all about people having the maximum individual freedom, and the government simply providing the most basic maintenance of the playing field, so to speak.

Many conservatives believe (myself included) that anything the government can do, the private sector can do more efficiently and with better results.  I don't understand when someone says something like "I am ok with higher taxes so long as it goes to worthy causes."  I believe that if you want to help worthy causes, you can and should freely give to them.  If I give a dollar to the public schools in my city, it helps a lot more than the government taxing that dollar out of me, because then the dollar will just pay for government overhead.  Also, if I give that dollar freely, it is a morally good thing to do, but if the government takes it from me, and I have no say in the matter, it doesn't have much moral value.

I used to have a copy of Dinesh D'Souza's wonderful book, "Letters to a Young Conservative" and it did a great job explaining the basic tenants of the conservative movement.  But, alas, I gave it back to the friend who lent me her copy.

Thanks, that is very nice of you.   :oops:

The basic answer to all of your questions is that I'm not a pure libertarian.  Many people who subscribe to pure, theoretical libertarianism, live in a fantasy world where they believe it is possible to eliminate all taxes and government intervention into our lives.  I do not subscribe to this belief.

I believe that taxes are necessary to provide for the common defense, and for certain social aims (helping very select people who would otherwise keel over and die, education, etc.).  I also believe that taxes should be minimal, and that private, individual solutions should always be supported, and valued above government solutions.  So, for example, I am in support of initiatives like the school choice movement (vouchers), and Clinton era and beyond efforts to move people off of welfare rolls and onto work rolls.  But I bristle when the government establishes new regulations, or tries to use our tax dollars to expand government involvement in our lives.  So I would not be in favor, for example, of the government paying students for good grades.

And lastly, it would be cowardly of me not to address the big issue here:
First off, I am for same sex marriage if approved by the bodies that approve such initiatives. I oppose any judicial branch (or executive) coming down from on high with their edict.
But as a libertarian I am against government recognition, or benefits for any marriage. But I live in a society and I understand that the majority of people disagree with me. I suspect it is because they have the utilitarian belief that marriage and family is a net good for society. Certainly the numbers bare this out that two parents raising children in a stable environment is best for society. So I accept this, but now my conservative side kicks in and says that as long as it does the least to produce the greatest good, and is set up in a way that is the same for everyone I am OK. So the rules get written: must be of a certain age, not so close in family, one man one woman etc etc. And so it was for hundreds of years in this country. But societies will change, as all of them do, and age old ways of doing things get questioned. So when people are given a chance to say "how about we change it two people instead of one man one woman" most of them say "nah" let's keep it this way. But many also say "yeah sure great idea". Now there are ways to handle these disputes. And I will predict that at some time in the not so distant future, the population will tilt to the side of "yeah sure great idea".
