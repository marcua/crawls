I have found that powerpoints are actually helpful for students to take better notes. As much as high school students are pushed to take notes, they don't get to college and graduate school knowing the ropes and very few people teach note-taking. Handing out the lecture word for word is inappropriate, but handing out the notes is only fair. If I am going to show a powerpoint on a projector, I would rather encourage meaningful discussion than have my class furiously note-taking...It might decrease some note-taking, but the proof is in the pudding when they do well on tests and assignments.

You have a point, but it's impractical to put my entire powerpoints online for several reasons.  I don't have direct access to my course website; there is an employee in the slide library who works Wed-Fri on the course websites.  I teach Mon and Wed, so for anything to be placed on the website before lecture, I'd have to work a week or more in advance, and that's just not possible with everything else I have to do.  Also, we are talking about powerpoints with 30-40 frames of images - jpegs of up to 1mb or so each - unlike Alexa's and Tumnus's powerpoints, which I'm assuming are text outlines.  There would be issues of taking up too much server space at some point, and I can't drive the slide library employee crazy by asking her to put things up and then take them down, especially after I give her the edited version of my powerpoints to place on the course website for students to study before exams.

The bottom line is that students *are* getting images to study from on the course website, not to mention that many of the most important images are in their textbook anyway.  I've only had this request from one student, who has been very demanding in other ways - what I don't want to encourage with this particular student is her midguided sense that her success in this class depends on *me* rather than on her own effort.  There is a difference between students asking for tools so that they can do the work for themselves, and students who want teachers to take responsibility for them.  Yes, it takes time to develop a visual memory, but that's a skill they'll learn to develop in the class.  And the point is not to have them memorize what I say, but for them to learn how to look at objects.
