Whenever the Straphanger's Campaign conducts its surveys of the NYC subways the N/W always seems to fall at or near the bottom of the rankings.   I have so many stories of shoddy service and certainly others do to.

I remember one night -- on the 30th Avenue platform -- one wag said to me after an interminable wait and the passing of a train that was running "express" that the N stands for "Never" and the W for "Whenever."

I had a funny story this a.m. when I was riding on one of the new Ns with the automated voice announcing the next station stop.   The only trouble was the voice had us going into Astoria -- where the next stop was Astoria Blvd. -- when we were actually pulling into Broadway.   I was half asleep and half paying attention when I snapped to attention thinking, once again, I got on a subway the wrong way.   I gathered my wits and the conducter corrected the announcement before we pulled into 36th Street.

Another little torture, besides frequently running trains express, is when in the evenings I switch at Queensboro Plaza getting off the 7 and switch to the N/W.   I like to count how many 7's come before one N/W arrives, packed to the gils of course.   The 7, by the way, always ranks at the top of those Straphanger's Campaign surveys.

More follies this p.m. and no rain in sight.  Maybe the MTA can blame the heat.  I get to the 30th Avenue station at 1 p.m. and see a train about to pull into the station and say "great" because I was, as usual, running late, late, late.   Well, it seems the train was just sitting there, outside the station Manhattan bound.   And I looked to the other side of the station and there is a train stopped just outside of the station.   And I turn to the Ditmars bound side with trains just outside and before the station.   All just sitting there.   And I can see the lights of a train sitting in the Broadway station on the Ditmars side and one other behind it between 36th Avenue and Broadway.  It seems our friends from the MTA were having another bad day.   Signal problems according to the conductor.

The W that was sitting just outside of the station came and went with no one in it; it didn't even stop.   Finally an N came and then just sat in the station.   And we sat in the Broadway station.   And we sat in the 36th Avenue station.   And we sat in the 39th Street station.   I don't think we sat in the Queensboro Plaza station, but it must have taken us 20 minutes, starting, going about 10 feet, then stopping, staring, going another 10 feet.   In any event another adventure on the N train.  It took close to an hour for me to get from 30th Avenue to 59th & Lex.   It then took me 10 minutes to get between 59th & Lexington and the Brooklyn Bridge on the 4 train.

Boy did N service suck this weekend!   I got on the train going into Manhattan Saturday about 12:30 and while I couldn't complain about my wait, there seemed to be an awful lot of people on the platform for a Saturday afternoon.   Coming home was a different story.   I must have waited 20/25 minutes at 57th Street and two R's came before one N and the train was rush-hour packed.   No chance of getting a seat.   I even moved up to the first car in hopes of nabbing a seat.   No luck On Sunday I didn't have any problems getting into Manhattan, but at 57th Street two R's came before one N and again it was jam packed at 5:30.   No seat again.

I remember -- more and more fondly -- the days of the 1980s and the early 1990s when you may have been taken your life into your hands when you rode the subway at night, but at least you could get a seat.  I remember when the MTA set up "off hour" waiting areas in the subway because so few people rode the trains.  No need for that anymore, particularly on the N train. I know coming home at night 11/12 o'clock if you get on after Times Square chances are you are not getting a seat and even some people who get on at TS don't get a seat.   It is getting that way more and more on the N with the trains always rush hour full.   The crowded conditiions are the ramifications of the changing demographics of Astoria.
