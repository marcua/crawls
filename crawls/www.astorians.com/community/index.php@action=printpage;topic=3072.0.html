Inaction On Noise

Dear Editor:
   As many of our fellow residents in South Ozone Park are aware, summer here signals party season, with outrageously loud music, DJ mikes, drums, port a potties on the sidewalk, and huge crowds packed into small driveways and backyards, often for days and nights on end. I was curious about just how noise complaints are handled by our local precinct, and so wrote to Police Commissioner Ray Kelly.

   I received a call from a sergeant at the 106th Precinct and she answered my questions and assured me that the noise situation was a priority here under Operation Silent Night and that port a potties on the sidewalk are blatantly illegal and will not be tolerated. As a follow up, I was visited at my home by the local beat officer and I provided him with a list of problem homes, where the partying goes on every weekend, and where summonses have been issued before. I was told that this area would be patrolled and that situations like the ones described would be taken care of.

   Only a few days after that visit, two parties kept us all awake on both a Friday and Saturday night. The Friday night party was at one of the problem houses I alerted the officer to. It began at 1 a.m. and blasted us until nearly 3:30 a.m. The party was filled with drunken young men who after throwing up on the sidewalk in front of the house, got into their cars and drove away. The 106th Precinct was called and did not respond.

   On Saturday night, there was another party complete with the illegal port a potty on the sidewalk, loud DJ music, loads of double parked cars, motorcycles and a huge crowd that spilled into the street. The next morning, I don’t want to tell you what the block looked like. The 106th Precinct was called at the numbers provided to me by the precinct and no one even picked up the phone. On the fourth try of calling the regular precinct number, an officer picked up and hurriedly said he’d send someone over. About 10 to 15 minutes after that call, a police van did a drive by, whooped its siren and kept right on going, past a loud party that had spilled out onto the street and sidewalk, with young people drinking and sprawled over double parked cars, stoops, as well as other people’s property. Needless to say, the port a potty stood until the next morning, and the party went on until 5 a.m.

   My family and I are great supporters of the NYPD, I myself am a 9/11 survivor and will never forget what I saw that day and the sheer heroism of New York’s Finest. I am fully aware that police resources are stretched thin, but I can’t help but feel that there is plenty of political correctness going on here, spiced with a healthy dose of ethnic sensitivity. It is no secret that these parties are almost exclusively held at the homes of immigrants from Guyana, Trinidad and India (as evidenced by the blasting Bollywood and reggae music).

   I myself worked for the federal government and for a member of Congress and I well know the pressure to be culturally sensitive and aware of diversity. That is all well and good, but the law is the law, and should be applied equitably.

   There can be no real quality of life when behavior like this is allowed.

   Ann Rychlenski,
   Ozone Park
