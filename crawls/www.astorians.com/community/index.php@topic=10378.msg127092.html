Quote from: FZ on May 07, 2008, 11:29:01 PM
I think a healthy fear of elite is what  this country was built on, after all, the British knew 'better'  than  the 'colonists.'

That's a pretty didactic way to phrase the argument and a pretty false comparison.

First off, "elite" isn't bad. I don't know what dictionary you're using, but while "elitist" can have a negative connotation, "elite" is

Quote from: Merriam-Webster Dictionary
1 singular or plural in construction : the choice part : the best of a class
   singular or plural in construction : the socially superior part of society
   a group of persons who by virtue of position or education exercise much power or influence
   a member of such an elite usually used in plural

In fact, "elite" is often a positive connotation. Elite colleges, elite honors, etc, etc.

But, regardless of the problem wherein you mis-define elite, you're also missing a large part of the argument.

The problem with one country ruling another is that a will is inherently put upon the controlled country. The ruling country's party have little reason to care for the territory's best interests if it does not also serve theirs. Thus, a cross purpose creates the situation where the British "think they know better" but do not do "right" by their territory.

With a President and advisers, you have a completely different situation. The goal of American economists and an American President when looking at how to help the American economy should be the same purpose. Whereas Britain did not have the same goal as the American Colonies, economists and the President should.

That metaphor is really ineffective and elucidates a lack of understanding.
