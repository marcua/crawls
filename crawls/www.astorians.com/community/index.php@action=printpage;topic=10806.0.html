Obama: Peacenik or untested warmonger?
By JEREMY LOTT | 6/4/08 9:29 AM EST     
 
 
 
Barack Obama declared his opposition to “a dumb war, a rash war, a war based not on reason but on passion, not on principle but on politics” at an anti-war rally in early October 2002. From the time of the joint congressional resolution that made the Iraq war possible, through President Bush’s 48-hour ultimatum to Saddam Hussein, Obama, then an Illinois state senator, warned that his country was making a horrible mistake that it would soon regret.

That noble declaration has drawn throngs of supporters to the young and untested freshman U.S. senator. Even his most enthusiastic fans will grudgingly admit that experience is not Obama’s strength in this race. But while Sen. Hillary Rodham Clinton was busy justifying her vote in favor of the joint war resolution — and Sen. John McCain’s impatient message amounted to “Faster, pussycat! Kill! Kill!” — Obama made what a majority of Americans now believe was the right call. Surely that has to count for something?

In a just and rational universe, it might. However, a look at America’s past peace candidates should give voters pause — or at least cause for despair. These purported peaceniks have a disturbing tendency to go from the most subdued, peace-loving candidates to war-waging presidents.

Woodrow Wilson ran for reelection in 1916 on the slogan “He kept us out of the war.” Note the past tense. Once he was back in the White House, Wilson plunged America into World War I and locked up political dissidents on a massive scale.

Wilson was far from a rare example. Franklin D. Roosevelt promised to keep American soldiers out of foreign wars during his 1940 reelection campaign and then was forced to preside over World War II — and the Japanese internment. Lyndon Johnson painted Barry Goldwater as a dangerous, unstable warmonger who would blow up little children and flowers. Then Johnson escalated U.S. involvement in Southeast Asia into the Vietnam War.

Republicans are missing from the list because the party doesn’t tend to run out-and-out peace candidates. Instead, it runs “win the peace” candidates, “peace through strength” candidates, “peace with honor” candidates, “keep the peace” candidates, etc.

In fact, the closest Republicans have come to a peace candidate in a long while was George W. Bush. He was far less hawkish than Al Gore, didn’t seem to know or care much about the rest of the world and wanted a “humble foreign policy.” Gene Healy, author of “The Cult of the Presidency,” a history of the growth of executive power, recently wrote of our current president, “Part of me thinks there’s an alternate universe somewhere where the Twin Towers are standing and George W. Bush became the sort of president about whom you could say, as [H.L.] Mencken did of [Calvin] Coolidge, ‘He had no ideas and was not a nuisance.’”

That Bush presided instead over two wars points to one fatal flaw of peace candidates, at least from a pinched, pacifist point of view: There are some wars presidents simply can’t stay out of, and most Americans — even otherwise anti-war ones — wouldn’t want them to.

To wit, during World War II, the isolationist America First Committee disbanded after Pearl Harbor, and anti-war stalwart Charles Lindbergh requested that Roosevelt let him be part of the war effort by reinstating his military commission. After the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks, even Reps. Ron Paul (R-Texas) and Dennis J. Kucinich (D-Ohio) voted for the authorization of force in Afghanistan.
