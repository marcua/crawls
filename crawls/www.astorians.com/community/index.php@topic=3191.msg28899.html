Eggplant "Sandwiches"!

3 Medium To Small Eggplants (or 1 huge one from your CSA!)
1 Pound Fresh Mozzarella ball
2 Eggs, beaten
1 1/2 Cups Seasoned Bread Crumbs
Fresh Basil Leaves
Vegetable Oil For Frying
Salt & Pepper
Lemon wedges to finish

Cut the eggplants into round slices, about 1/4 inch thick. Cut the cheese into thin
slices to match the size of the eggplant rounds. Place the breadcrumbs
in a shallow tray. Take two equal sized slices of eggplant, and place a slice of cheese
and a fresh basil leaf between them. Carefully dip the "sandwich" first
into the beaten egg, and then into the bread crumbs. Heat the oil in a large deep skillet
about 1 inch deep. Cook the eggplant sandwiches until they are golden brown. Keep
them warm in the oven, while you continue to cook the rest. Serve warm, or at room
temperature with a little squeeze of lemon (or topped with bruchetta!). Smile.

Bitter Melon! (I grew up with this stuff in Manila)

It must be such a weird veggie to people who haven't encountered it before. This is what I remember when I ate it as a kid: (still do, if I am ever home)

1 bitter melon
1 egg
some ground chicken, pork or beef (not much needed)(optional)
1 clove garlic
Ginger
S&P
Cooking oil


Cut bitter melon in half length-wise (I haven't see this variety with all the pointy extaraneous bits) and remove most of the rind. Cut bitter melon into slices about 1/8 inch thick. Julienne some ginger (to taste) and put aside. Crush garlic.

In a sauteeing pan, add 1/4 cup of water. Add bitter melon and bring water to a boil. Stir for 7 minutes or until water evaporates, which ever comes first. Drain water if any left. On medium high heat, add oil, garlic, ginger and sautee for 5 minutes.

Add egg and meat (optional) and stir until egg is cooked. S&P to taste. If you are bold, add a splash of Thai/Philippine fish sauce.

Serve with rice.

YUM!

Your friends have much truth when they say that. The durian's imported here are frozen before shipping and kept that way until they are thawed in market. The reason for this is to prevent its rather pungent aroma from permeating during shipping, whether it be on cargo/pax planes. As a matter of fact, SIA (Singapore Airlines) prohibits the transport of durian in/out of Singapore. This is largely due to the fact that it would basically smell-up the plane/airport/passengers!

The durians we get here in the U.S. are primarily from Thailand. The Filipino durians are bigger, and, IMHO, much tastier. The more pungent they come, the "creamier" they taste.

I've never had a durian here in the U.S. (they are available in Chinatown (Queens/Manhattan)) I am so used to the unfrozen ones. But, there is always room for curiosity.
