I don't have an actual GPS Navigator.  I have a Verizon cellphone and I have added the "VZNavigator" service for $9.99 per month.  It's very easy to use, and I believe it updates automatically.  My phone will show the map of where you are and speak turn-by-turn directions, just as a GPS system will.  Added benefit is that if I'm out walking and am not sure what direction to go, or I'm trying to get to a new place, I can take out my cell phone and use this feature.  It also has the "local search" like regular GPS so that you can find nearby places of business.  For example, yesterday I was in Chinatown and wanted to find a vegetarian restaurant, so I did a seach for "vegetarian" and all the restaurants nearby came up and I could navigate to them.  I also use the feature while driving all the time, and it operates the same way as a regular GPS and also gives traffic updates and there is a "detour" option to get around the traffic (or if you just want to go a different way) and it also will reroute you if you take a wrong turn.  It keeps a record of "recent addresses" where you have gone before, although looking at my list, this "recent" listing includes every address I've navigated to since I got my most recent Verizon phone over a year ago.  It's nice to not have to re-input the addresses.
Oh, and you have not lost your mind!  Once you go GPS, you will never go back!  I don't know how I ever survived without it...

Any of the newer GPS units by Garmin (I have a c550), TomTom or Magellan are generally easy to use. Spoken street names are the most important feature, I my opinion. In places like Boston, Pittsburgh or even Lower Manhattan "turn in 500ft" is useless with all kinds of weird intersections. And here's a tip- I see that unit has a 5"(!!!) screen. DO NOT PUT IT ANYWHERE ON THE WINSDHIELD. It will draw police to you for 'blocking view' and is considered a dangerous condition. New Jersey Troopers are big on that one; THEY WILL stop you, and it's not a cheap ticket. Depending on the car, it can be put on the dashboard in a spot that will not block your view out the window.

Programming a multiple stop thing is simple. On mine, I put in the furthest stop first. then add the others 1 at a time and it optimizes the route among all the stops. But that works best for a delivery type of system. If this is a semi-pleasure trip, don't get locked into a particular route. Unless you are really pressed for time, slow down, take a back road and enjoy. If you wind up somewhere lost, flip on the GPS and tell it where you want to go. I've "found" the best things while "lost".

Another tip. first thing you do when you get it set up in front of the house. SET CURRENT LOCATION AS HOME!

Well... two things to consider. Do you have traffic on it? it may have been routing based on traffic flow. OR- do you have it set for "Shortest Route" or "Fastest Route". There is a difference. When I go downtown to Mercer it constantly tries to get me to take Steinway to Northern, then Vernon to the Tunnel, down 2nd to 13th then over to Mercer. Or sometimes down the BQE to the Willie B over to Bowery and over that way. So it depends on a few things. Some newer units remember your preferences and will take your favorite routes into account and use those instead, but will warn of traffic.

To the unit, the upper deck may seem to be going "out of the way" or "the long way".

I use a combination of radio traffic reports and the Unit's traffic service. You can't go wrong with more information.
