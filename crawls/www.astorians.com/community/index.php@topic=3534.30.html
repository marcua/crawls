Quote from: lanseaux on September 25, 2006, 12:02:08 PM
Generally, a vegetarian diet is more carb-heavy than an omivorous diet, and while some people can handle that physiologically, other people find it more difficult.  (I'm not commenting at all on personal reasons that people have for choosing a vegetarian or an omnivorous diet -- those are completely different and not on topic for this thread.)  For someone who has some insulin sensitivities (not necessarily diabetes), a vegetarian diet has to be very carefully calibrated to contain sufficient protein.  I know vegetarian low carbers, in fact.  I've experimented with higher carb diets (and I don't mean a Standard American Diet, I mean one with less meat and more whole grains, all whole food), and I instantly start to gain weight, become sluggish and sleepy after meals (the effect of insulin), and generally have less energy.  I have a family history of type II diabetes on both sides of my family, and based on personal experience and extensive research, I am convinced that by avoiding or minimising my intake of foods that cause consistent insulin spikes (I also pay attention to the glycemic index/load of foods), I am lowering my own risk of developing it later in life.
Brava, Lanseaux. You've found the perfect combination and I am truly jealous of how successful you have been. And congratulations on you weight loss and you ability to keep it off. You look wonderful.  :mrgreen:

I mentioned earlier in this thread that merm might consider seeing a physician and a dietitian/nutritionist primarily because, as he has mentioned here many times, he lives a vegetarian lifestyle. I simply thought it would be in the best interest of *his* health to rule out any possibility of organ-, lipid- or insulin-related problems and assure the success of whatever endeavor he chooses, but also in the hope that his choices were sustainable, like yours, for the long term. I, too, have a family history of both Type I and II Diabetes. My sister, who was a vegetarian, had no idea that her lifestyle was contraindicated with our genetic background. It led to her becoming a Type II Diabetic before her 45th birthday. Once she worked with a nutritionist, and accepted Lanseaux's method of (mostly) planned meals and paying attention to the glycemic index/load of foods, she was able to live without any medication to adjust her insulin levels and she enjoyed life to the fullest.

Not to be be redundant, but 25 pounds *is* an appreciable amount of weight to lose. It is always in your own best interest to be sure that the weight gain was not only from poor nutritional choices or larger portions. It is better to rule out any clinical and/or pathological (READ: medical) health issues before starting a weight loss regimen.

I've been going at this more seriously than i have ever done in the past.
so i've been reading as much as i can, and picked up what i can.
one of the first things is that you can change your eating habits all you want (i refuse to use the word diet) but you have to excersise. which is sort of where i'm at now. i've been able to adjust my crap eating habits slowly (i believe that if you try to make a MAJOR change too quickly you're setting youself up to fail, and then you reach for the carton of donuts) and now i have to incorporate a more routine schedule to go to the gym (its around the corner & i can't drag my ass over? what's wrong with me?) cardio burns up your calories and weight training build your muscle to help burn fat. right now, i'm thinking of focusing on the cardio to get a rhythm going then start to add toning.

although everything-calories, fat and carbs should all be accounted for and balanced, i think the easiest thing to do is count calories. this of course doesn't mean if something has low calories and high fat it's ok all the time, you've got to be aware and factor all of it in.

speaking of being aware-something that has been helping me is a food log. not simply a list of everything i've eaten, but rants about how i'm hungry, or i'd love a cookie but i shouldn't have one blah blah. and how i feel about progress or lack thereof. but in the back of my notebook i made categories(fruit, veg, dairy, chicken, fish etc) and wrote my faves and looked up their nutritional value and marked them in the notebook. (there are numerous calorie counter books, can't remember the one i used as it's my roommates) this way when i eat, i can easily look it up. I usually bring my lunch to work and try to stick to a salad, or a half a homemade sammy and a small salad. i'll also bring in weight watchers et al pizzas and have that with a salad also. today is a veggie burger on whole grain english muffin and a salad. since i pretty much eat the same salad all the time, i figured out the nutritional value and keep that in the back of the notebook.
i've found that writing all of this down has made me accountable for everything i eat. you'd be amazed at what you could put away in a day without realizing how many calories you've injested. it's wild. but if you make it a point to write everything down you realize, you don't really need that cookie.
i've got a major sweet tooth, and thats been kind of hard. i've just avoided the snack aisle at the market all together. started buying more fruit and yogurts. but when the weekend rolls around, or if i go out to dinner with friends, i'll happily share a dessert. if you don't give yourself the tasty treat once in a while you're bound to go overboard and inhale the carton of donuts(i have to stop using those as a reference since i know have visions of powdered donuts dancing in my head). i usually try to save it for the weekend.
but i've noticed 2 things.
1) I've become ridiculously obsessed with counting calories. i'd rather obsess over this than what cookies are on sale at Pathmark (nabisco, half  price)
2) i seem to eat a crazy low amount of calories durning the day.

for instance,
i've been making sure to eat breakfast every morning (which is another good habit to get into for losing weight, you jump start your metabolism that way)
then later i usually make a cup of tea so that i don't reach for the snapple we have in all the fridges here.
lunch time for me is 1:30, since i cover phones on the early shift. like i said thats usually a salad or something with a salad.

i do find myself, in general, more hungry during the after dinner hours. but then i feel like i'm over snacking.

Thanks, NYCMacUser.  It's still something I work at - almost every day.

Meg - as far as calorie intake goes, there really is no average, which is why the old "calories in, calories out" is not helpful for many people.  My best advice would be for you to track it for awhile, and you'll start to see a pattern.  And for maybe the first week or so, don't try to change your eating habits - just observe and take notes.  Once you've established what you do eat and what your typical intake is, then you can start figuring out what to change.  I think the governmental "average" of 2000 calories per day is too high for many women - I'm not talking about serious athletes, but I am including people who exercise regularly.  Personally, I can't go over about 1500 calories consistently without gaining, even when I am jogging regularly.

Oh, and Fitday tends to overestimate caloric output.  I say this from my experience, as well as experience from many other people I know who use it or have used it.

p.s.  I agree that on Fitday, "nfs" does mean "not further specified."  I think a common default is one cup.

Well, if you're working out, you might need more calories than you think, otherwise your body might go into "starvation mode" and slow down your metabolism no matter how much you work out. Your body will start to hoard calories because evolution programmed our bodies to believe that less food consumed = less food available. I've seen it happen to friends of mine who were on 1,000 - 1,200 calorie/day diets, no matter how much they worked out. This is why things like breakfast and 5-6 small meals a day are good ideas - your body is constantly getting food, so your metabolism is constantly running (obviously its always "running", otherwise we'd be dead, but I mean running at an efficient level). It never feels the need to hoard energy.

This is also a common reason why people who have very strict diets gain weight even when they finish their diet and return to what would normally be a healthy way of eating. Our bodies are programmed to believe that a sudden increase of food won't be permanent, so the body better hold on to what it gets. Again, this is why I think its important to make gradual healthy changes that you can keep up for years as part of a healthy way of living as opposed to a "diet" to lose X pounds in X time.

Anyway, at the very basic level, you need to use more calories than you take in to lose weight. Fitday can track an estimate of this for you - just make sure your height, weight and basic activity levels are accurate, and you should be fairly close to your daily calorie use with no exercise, depending on your metabolism. Also enter any additional exercise you do, they have a pretty good selection (although upon completion of this post, I do tend to agree that they overestimate some activities).

If you want to be really geeky about it, do this - say you're aiming to lose 2 pounds of fat a week. In theory (and we all know that nothing in real life equals the theory, so take this with a grain of salt), a pound of fat is 3,500 calories. So daily, you need to burn 1,000 more calories than you consume. Its not perfect, and there's no easy way to know exactly how many calories you're burning because everyone's body and metabolism is different, but its a decent guideline to follow. If you start going into wanting to lose 5 or more pounds of fat a week, you run the risk of going into that starvation mode because you're suddenly using A LOT more calories and probably eating less than you usually do, so your body will be all, "OK! TIME TO HOARD THE FAT!"

But you also have to remember that you'll be building some muscle (even if you're not weight training), so the scale shouldn't be the ultimate barometer of your fitness for you, since lean muscle is denser than fat, so your body can get smaller, but the scale won't move much. Plus, as you lose weight and build muscle, your body will be more efficient at burning calories, something that a website can't really tell, you know? This is why journalling is helpful - if you're not a runner, the first few times you go running might be hell. But if you keep it up for a few weeks, that same hellish first route is a piece of cake. Chances are you won't remember just how completely tough that first time out was. Its always nice to go back and see something like, "OMG THAT WAS HORRIBLE" a few weeks later do the same thing and feel comfortable with it.

I went on the first diet of my life a couple of months ago when I realized that (almost) middle-age spread would be creeping up on me before I knew it.  I signed up for Weight Watchers online and my experience has been the same as schmennifer's.  I've lost 8 1/2 pounds in about 2 months (though I may have gained some back after the Ali's outing :-D) and have about 7 more to go to reach my personal goal.  I feel healthier and all my clothes are getting baggier (not necessarily the greatest thing as I'll have to overhaul my wardrobe).

I've always known the most sensible way to lose or maintain weight are: 1) eat less--American portions are too big; 2) eat better--less processed glop, more organic and farm-fresh food; 3) exercise more; and 4) cut way way down on sodas, juices, and alcohol.  However, with WW's points system, you really notice how the little things add up.  I'm trying not to keep cookies in the house anymore and have been fanatic about measuring portions.  I never knew exactly what 1 cup of cereal was until now, for instance.  Also, with the points system, you can plug in the components of a recipe and specify the # of portions it makes, and the computer will automatically calculate the points per portion for you.  You can also have the occasional splurge for a special occasion, and not spend Thanksgiving or Christmas or a barbecue picking at your food.
