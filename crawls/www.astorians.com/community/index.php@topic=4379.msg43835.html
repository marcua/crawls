Well, I was upstate and the components were surprisingly inexpensive:

3 black freshwater pearls
6 small sterling silver beads
3 sterling silver wires to make them into earrings and pendant
= less than $15 with tax

The owner helped everyone put their creations together. Here in the city, not only is everything is more expensive, but I found the craft places unfriendly -- I wouldn't want to ask for help putting a piece together. And some places have classes and charge a lot to attend them, so I'm sure they wouldn't want to give away their help for free.

Then again, when you buy handmade jewelry, you are paying for the supplies and the labor. Just as when you go to a restaurant, you are paying for the cost of the food and the cost of preparing, serving, and cleaning up afterwards!

I think that the final cost really comes down to the quality of the materials - there's a big difference between, say, gold-plate and solid gold.  Likewise, a big difference between someone who buys beads at their local bead supply store or someone who travels abroad to buy gemstones and has them cut to specifications.  And factor in specialized techniques like soldering and setting stones rather than simply stringing beads on a wire...

I imagine that the markup in jewelry might be higher than for other products anyway, because for most people, it's not exactly an everyday purchase.

Many years ago, when I was raising babies, I used to make myself safety-pin necklaces to go with special outfits. On one occasion, at a fancy affair with my late husband, this very elegant lady admired my necklace to the point that I was embarrassed. I took it off and gave it to her. She wrote down my name and address and a week later I got a letter and a check for $50.00 from her. She was a buyer for Lord & Taylor. She offered to buy 200 pieces for $50.00 each to sell at the store. They sold for $175.00 on the first floor. The money was the beginning of college funds.

It took me months to get them all done, but every one of them was different. I later went on to do other crafts and also sold them at many different locations around the city. It doesn't need to be a business, there will always be a vast market for hand crafted jewelry. The more you do your craft the better you get at doing it. Good luck.
