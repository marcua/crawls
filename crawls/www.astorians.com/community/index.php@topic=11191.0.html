Astorians,
  Something has been bothering me since I moved to Astoria from the deepest, darkest prairies of the American northern midwest so many years ago. 
  When did the "average" Astorian decide that the "garbage food" proffered by multinational corporations was superior to the local version of the real thing?
  I really don't want to open another Applebees debate here, but I am concerned about our neighborhood and its local businesses.
  Why?
  Starbucks on every street corner--you need a coffee fix?  Didn't the deli across the street provide it in the past?  Why should coffee cost more than gasoline?
  Pizza--Why would an Astorian buy a pie from Papa Johns, Uno or Dominos, when there are far superior products available from local vendors--on almost every corner for a smaller price?
  Heros--Every deli in LIC makes a better sandwich for about the same price than Subway or Blimpies.
  Why are these companies thriving in Astoria?  That's my question.

In some cases it's unique flavors or textures.  For instance, before Blimpie's people could get a sandwich anywhere, but I personally had never heard of putting on oil & vinegar or oregeno.  Any place could put lettuce and tomato on a sandwich, but I'd never seen a place shred the lettuce or get the tomatoes sliced so thin.  It made the lettuce and tomato easier to eat.  Originally it was part of Blimpie sandwich making to put on so much shredded lettuce that it extended way beyond the sandwich and covered your tray.  Oddly enough they didn't have any forks for you to pick up that extra lettuce, though.  To this day, in New York City, I've had only one Blimpie experience that was below grade.  Once the franchise spread around the city, you didn't have to guess what was a good place to eat at, if you saw the Blimpie sign, you knew you could get a quality sandwich, and you knew what was going to be on the menu.  They always had your favorite.  I've had debates with other people around the US and Canada.  About 50 percent prefer Subway, but I've almost always had disappointing experiences with them (in New York City).

Prior to Starbucks coffee pretty much came in one flavor, "coffee".  You could have it hot or you could have it iced.  The only other coffee products were Manhattan Special, if you in an Italian neighborhood, and Coffee Time Syrup, available at the regular supermarket.  For the most part, coffee was either percolated or dripped.  Starbucks uses (or used) a European coffee press, and offered many flavors.  Dunkin Donuts and 7 Eleven followed long later.  As for the price of Starbucks coffee, I'll give an analogie - I think I heard it on This American Life.  A store had tie-dyed sox that weren't selling because seemed so out of date.  The store lowered the price to get rid of them, but nobody bought them.  On a crazy whim the store raised the price to double the original price and they sold out rapidly!  "If it costs more, it must be better".  Starbucks is built on snob appeal.

Uno's is a Chicago style specialty, so that's a cultural import, but the other pizza chains you mentioned arose at a time when many people outside the major cities had never tasted pizza, and being stuck in the suburbs with little kids, it was much more convenient to have pizza that came to you instead you packing up the kids and traveling miles to dinner.  I was surprised when they came to New York City.  To paraphrase Gloria Steinem, New Yorkers need a Pizza Hut like a fish needs a bicycle.

As for McDonald's, personally I don't regard the Big Mac as a hamburger, to me it's a Big Mac - if it didn't have that sauce, than bun, that dried texture, it wouldn't be a Big Mac, but it still wouldn't be a hamburger.  One day a few years ago I realized I was walking right by Bel Aire to go to McDonalds, and I asked myself "What am I doing?????"  If I want a burger, I should get a burger, even if it was going to cost more.  It was delicious, and I swear, I can still taste that icey cold root beer in a real glass glass.

I think this is a fairly typical and, in my opinion, bad argument to make.

First of all, the worst way to start a discussion with anyone is by attacking their habits.  All you do is put them on the defensive and you're left with a stalemate.

If you want to get to the bottom of things, you could start by asking who shops at these particular stores and why.  Maybe those answers will offer you some insight into the reason such businesses are flourishing in Astoria.

I think you'd be better off educating people as to how other choices are better (i.e., healthier, cheaper, etc.) for them than simply telling them that what they're doing is wrong.  We vote with our wallets every day, and our choices (to some extent) reflect our values.  If you value mom & pop salumerias, then you should only buy your sandwiches and cold cuts from those establishments.  When they raise their prices due to the relative smallness of their business, your loyalty will be tested.  Certainly, in your heart you'd love to keep shopping there.  But what about in your wallet?

A lot of decisions people make these days are for convenience.  There are numerous threads on this board complaining about children in restaurants.  Well there are at least two restaurants I can think of where that is probably NEVER a complaint: Uno's and Applebee's.  They serve a purpose, just like the euro-style cafes up and down 30th Avenue.  They are targeting a particular group of consumers, and it's working.  Their target isn't you (obviously) and it isn't me, as I don't go there either.

I just think people should do a lot less complaining and a lot more educating about what you like and why you like it.  That's the point of this board.  Tell us (your community) what's good and worth keeping so we go there too and help it flourish.  I think it'll take a little more than your ranting and raving to prevent further encroachments by the likes of Starbucks and Applebee's into Astoria (and I think Coldstone is an even bigger plague).

What a lot of people don't realize (and this can be researched in the internet) is that the less processed foods are the more filling it is.  Hence, if you're paying more for something at a Mom and Pop chances are that you'll get filled up a lot faster and won't be able to eat the complete portion, whereby you'll have the portion  you didn't eat for later. 

Processed foods lack the natural nutrients (i.e. nutrients are added) and that is why you can eat the whole thing and be full for a while.

This is a major reason why the U.S. ranks high in obesity.  In other countries (i.e. France for an example) people have bread on a daily basis, eat butter, etc., etc. and THEY'RE NOT OBESE (at least not like here) and that's because in France and most of Europe (look at Spain) food are still very natural with no additives.

So even if it's convenient to eat from food chains and fast food...we're not doing our bodies any good.

I'm by no means a skinny person but that's because of the fact that my ancestors were mountain people in the middle of Italy somewhere...so I, like the rest of my family on that side of the family have very thick bones.  When I lived in Miami you don't have Mom & Pop's like you do here so I used to frequent food chains more and I was always sick.  Ever since I moved back (8 years ago) and eat stuff from the Mom & Pop's and buy fresh made mozzarella, etc., etc. I've only been sick 3 times in all of 8 years.  It does make a HUGE difference.

Plus...it tastes better.

My two oldest kids moved back to Miami to go to college and one of the things they miss the most from here (and mind you, they're both Florida born) is the food that you're able to get from small establishments.
