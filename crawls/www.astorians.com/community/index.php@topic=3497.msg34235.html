Quote from: wasabisam on September 21, 2006, 02:01:26 PM
Yeah, but what gives us the right as Human Animals to kill other animals? Look - my view is skewed and will never reflect yours because I am a life-long vegetarian, and over the last year or more have become more ethically interested in the treatment of animals no matter their human-imposed category (food or pet). So it is difficult for me to say, "yeah your argument makes sense because cows really are just beef that haven't been killed yet." To me cows are cows, not meat (and on some farms are very large pets).

You'll get no argument from me about humane treatment of (living) animals! As for vegetarianism, I applaud your decision to live your values. This said, I don't think it supports your argument about the right to life of animals when you explicitly state that your conclusions are due to your lifestyle, and not to any sort of reasoning. Also, if I may be cynical, I must point out that if you elected to eat beef, that more cattle would be raised to meet demand, and another cow would have the opportunity to have at least some life. If you bought free-range beef, it would even be fairly pleasant, if a bit short. In other words, by your actions you are implying that your answer to my question is "(b) to never have lived". If you're comfortable with that, that's fine. I don't think there's a "right answer" here, but I do ask that you have a coherent argument...

This said, there are other interesting and more coherent arguments for vegetarianism, or at least restricting what you eat. Let's get past cows and cats, and talk about people. Suppose I had a farm where I raised people for food. (Soylent Green, yum!) Clearly, those people only existed because I put their parents in cages with a bottle of cheap wine and Isaac Hayes on the stereo, so by my previous argument, we should feel comfortable slaughtering them when they're nice and tender and eating them. Clearly, I don't want to take this position, however, so I need another principle! So let me take the principle that eating things that are too similar to me is bad. We can all agree on some boundary conditions (eating people = bad, eating lettuce = good), so where do you draw the line? Are other primates OK? Monkeys? Mammals? Chordates (things with backbones)? Animals? By this argument, you can choose to draw the line wherever you like, and I can't really argue with you. So for you, eating animals is bad. And for me, mammals are tasty, but monkeys are not to be eaten.

Quote from: wasabisam on September 21, 2006, 02:01:26 PM
While I don't think that merm is saying this guy should be roaming the streets, I do think that his point is valid - why treat any animal different than another animal? Saying, "because that's just the way it is and the way humans have set up the cycle of life - cows=beef, cats=pets" isn't much of an argument, IMHO.

That is indeed my thought. I'll say again I'm not entirely against eating animals, but I find the "why" killing "which" animals is okay and not others interesting. I personally find these social rules are not based in science, but just based entirely on human convenience, not necessity.

There's no scientific or rational reason imho that:
- Killing a pig is okay as long as it ends up in a happy meal
- Killing a pig is okay if it is for sport
- Killing a pig is not okay if it is done in anger (but not eaten)

I think it's sad that this cat was killed, and I most certainly think the guy needs help. But I'd also argue by extension that it's no worse than those animals that are killed to end up in a happy meal. Just because there's a "system" in place to kill animals and put them in pretty packages doesn't justify it for me.

I've also spent quite a bit of time in the last year or so learning about food production in the United States and this has influenced my views quite a bit. I don't think you can effectively argue these days that a) meat production is necessary or that b) animals that are raised for meat are privileged. Most of these animals spend their lives diseased and in constant discomfort, only to be slaughtered for no real reason other than the fact that we like their taste. We don't even need them for nutrition. Some could even argue that animals that are domesticated and then killed in one violent rage by a human actually live a more fufilling life.

On one hand we have unnecessary killing as a result of the whim of a violent man,
and on the other hand unnecessary killing as a result of the whim of a violent society.
It doesn't seem like apples and oranges to me.

The "how" and "what" makes the killing "justified" isn't scientific or rational, it's just a social construct of pure convenience. Most people have no idea where or how their "food" got to them - I think this makes it easy to keep eating meat. But I think that any person here who finds this story upsetting, will feel equally so if they take an unbiased view of food production in the U.S.
