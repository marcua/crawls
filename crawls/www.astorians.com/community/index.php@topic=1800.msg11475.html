Greetings all. Having a history of being raised in a somewhat conservative Christian family I don't think Betty is too far off by saying that the general understanding of athiesm is associated with hopeless and depressed people. (I make this assertion because the majority of people in this country are Christian or connected to it in a significant way, and I think that the Christian paradigm is strong enough that many of these people will feel the same way I did in the past.)

I believe the reason for this is because Christians are taught from a very early age to put all of life's hopes, dreams, and day to day trust in Jesus. While this might sound strange to an outsider who was not raised in this paradigm I want to assure you that this is as real as the child's view that their parents are all-powerful as well. A child's entire life revolves around that of their parents and one's sense of security, of hope, love etc are all wrapped up in them. [I should insert here that I still consider myself connected to Christianity in some ways, and I don't believe that humans are the highest of all beings. So I do identify to varying degrees to what various religious folks feel. I'm just trying  to explain in a clear way the feelings I've felt, I don't want to say that all religious people are children.] I think everyone these days experiences this when we invest our hopes in a relationship, business, a home, a friend etc. For Christians though I think that "Jesus" is a huge part of their identity though, past, present, and future. And it absolutely is very real.

It seems perfectly reasonable that taking away something (physical or imaginary) that someone has put all their hopes and dreams into their entire lives would lead to a period of depression, this is easily seen when people's homes burn down or they lose a loved one. For Christians, Jesus is, at a minimum, just as powerful as this. So then, from the Christian paradigm, to come across someone who doesn't believe in such a figure (or a higher being etc) falsely means to them that this person has no place to put their hopes, dreams. Christians live day to day for a vision of a future utopian society created by Jesus, and a mansion on a hilltop built by Him - everything physical is linked through the Jesus paradigm. So from the perspective of a Christian you can easily see how they misunderstand athiests as being spiritually bankrupt and depressed people. If they can't place their trust in Jesus, and Jesus is the highest thing that someone can think of, then someone who doesn't believe in Jesus must be sad and we should feel sorry for them.

I don't think in most cases it's because they seek to stereotype or mistreat people, but simply because for most it's too difficult to try and think out of their own paradigm.

Sadly however, difficulty to think outside one's own paradigm seems to be status-quo these days no matter what religion, nationality, or gender you are.

Anyway, I just thought I'd chime in and say after reading the entire thread straight through just now I didn't think Betty was trying to piss anyone off. I just think a couple of her posts were a little dry and needed some back explanation behind them so they wouldn't come across as trying to start a fight.

This is a really interesting topic though, I hope it doesn't die over what I perceive as just misunderstanding.

Atheists have maintained that the existence of evil makes the existence of God improbable. In particular, Atheists assert that theism does not provide an adequate explanation for the existence of seemingly gratuitous evil, such as the suffering of innocent children. Theists commonly defend the existence of evil by claiming that God desires that human beings have the freedom to choose between good and evil, or that the purpose of evil is to build human character, such as the ability to persevere. Atheists counter that justifications for evil in terms of human free will leave unexplained why, for example, children suffer because of genetic diseases or abuse from adults. Arguments that G-d allows pain and suffering to build human character fail, in turn, to explain why there was suffering among animals before human beings evolved and why human character could not be developed with less suffering than occurs in the world. For Atheists, a better explanation for the presence of evil in the world is that G-d does not exist. Simple. No? I can see no depression or sadness in that.
