How important is the roll in a cheesesteak?  My friend from Philly told me that the restaurant "99 Miles to Philly" used the same bread as the cheesesteaks he grew up with.  He told me that a lot of places outside of the Philly area get "most of it right, except for the bread.  Before I could ask him what kind of bread he was talking about, he started complaining, again, about the size of the cheesesteak and the price.

The roll is crucial, and it's the hardest thing for out-of-towners to get right.  It's got to be an Italian roll, with lots of air on the inside and crusty on the outside.  It takes some effort to tear the crust, it shouldn't be soft.  I went to the other 99 Miles location once, and the roll was totally wrong.  It was more like a hot dog roll, with a soft crust and wonder bread-y insides.  Wrong.  But from your post it seems they may have rectified that.

(Here I must point out that in the AC area, they use a completely different kind of roll, but they're forgiven because it's amazingly good.  Many have tried to duplicate those rolls but it seems they can only be baked in the AC area.)

If there isn't enough meat on those steaks, that's a problem too.  If you want grilled cheese, you can get that for a lot less money.

I was at Steve's Prince of Steaks in Philly and my friend and I were talking about the thinness of the meat, and the guy behind the counter explained how they do it. They freeze the ribeye and slice it paper thin on a slicing machine and put papers between each stack so that all they have to do is pull out the next paper and there is all the meat they need for each sandwich.
