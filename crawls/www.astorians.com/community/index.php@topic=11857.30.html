There really are two discussions going on here.
One about what takes precedent over a pharmacist decision (or any health care provider) to sell a product or provide a service. I think that the constitution is quite clear on this matter.

The other is this Draft "CFR" which reads like no other CFR that I have ever read. It doesn't say anything about what a health care provider can or can't do. It says that any entity receiving federal funds can not discriminate against a health care provider because that health care provider refuses to perform certain services that they find morally objectionable. More to the point, it just reinforces the current law by attempting to establish reporting guidelines to ensure that the law is being followed.

Yes, this. Sort of related, what about the cab drivers in Minnesota who refuse to allow people carrying alcohol into their cabs because of their religious beliefs? These drivers go to the airport but refuse passengers who have wine or liquor from the duty-free store. Their religious beliefs tell them the transport of alcohol is a sin. But a cab could rightly be considered a civil service rather than a merchant with the rights to sell what he or she wants.

I'm all for the right to believe whatever you believe, but not to enforce it on others, including through your job.

If a pharmacist is an employee of a pharmacy which carries birth control and is licensed by the state, in my view, they are in fact morally obligated to fill any valid prescription even if it violates their religious beliefs. Likewise, a pharmacy owner is free to refuse to stock, carry, or order birth control or any other medication he or she finds objectionable--and I am free to take my business elsewhere.

The argument of "what if you're in a small town and can't get it anywhere else" hold little water with me. I'm from one of those small towns, and it's not really true. There might be only one pharmacy in town (as there was in mine), but there's probably one within an hour's drive, and the internet actually does reach most of the country. In a town small enough, you'd rather drive an hour than see someone you know in the pharmacy anyway.

Now, I'm sure eventually with research I could find something smaller, but I think the impact would be felt.

Yeah, it's a very sticky situation. In my small town, the pharmacy was privately-owned, so there was definitely the risk that they could decide what to sell and to whom. I have to hope that most national chains would let go of moral considerations since they're in it for profit, but chains like Chik-Fil-A demonstrate that even hugely-grossing businesses will continue to hold to certain ethics of the owner/founder. (For those who don't know, Chik-Fil-A is a regional chain restaurant that is closed on Sundays.)

I think the bigger problem at this point is not the threat of chain pharmacies deciding not to carry the pills, but of individual pharmacists using their religious views as an excuse not to do their jobs. Perhaps all pharmacies should require applying pharmacists to agree to fill any valid prescription, or state that they have a religious objection and then require that they share shifts with pharmacists who WILL fill the prescriptions. If the pharmacy does not always have two pharmacists on duty, it would require an extra expenditure on their part, and they might decline to hire a pharmacist who would act this way, thereby weeding out the ones who refuse to fill the prescriptions. This is a dangerous ground, too, as it might become religious persecution of a sort. I was raised in a religion in which the believers keep the Jewish Sabbath and do not work from sundown Friday to sundown Saturday. I got passed over for waitressing jobs as a teen and I felt it was unfair, but I was essentially refusing to work the busiest shifts and my prospective employers had the right to decide not to hire me at all. I was not being prevented from working entirely, it was just harder to find a job.
