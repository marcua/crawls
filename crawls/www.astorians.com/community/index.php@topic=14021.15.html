Quote from: 28Grand on February 19, 2009, 08:55:44 AM
Harlan I agree with you that everything is inter-related and that you can't necessarily treat cases in isolation and I'm not necessarily arguing that individuals shouldn't be helped out but the trigger that pushes people into insolvency is losing their job.

I see the problem more as an unemployment issue than a real estate issue so why limit any help to mortgage holders?

I'm not sure that this is correct. Unemployment usually lags changes in the economy, and this recession is no exception. The problem started when the housing bubble collapsed. All of the consequences, both direct consequences as people stopped being able to refi their property and went into foreclosure, and indirect consequences as large banks' balance sheets went negative as MBSs collapsed, freezing the credit markets, preceded the unemployment rate increase. Usually the economy starts growing again before unemployment starts to fall. Growing unemployment makes everything worse, in a disasterous spiral, but I'm pretty sure full employment wouldn't fix the problem. Instead, housing prices have to hit bottom and start (slowly) growing again, and banks have to be stabilized, and the economy won't recover until those two things happen.

This said, I certainly wish the stimulus package had larger transfers to states to support unemployment payments!

Also, there has to be a certain amount of responsibility placed on the people.

Quote from: From the Article:
As the mortgage crisis unfolded, observers of all political stripes repeated a boilerplate line: the "affordability products" that have flooded the lending market in recent years--from subprime to interest-only loans--have done more good than bad by fueling a surge in black and Latino homeownership. But while minority homeownership may have grown in the short term, the long-term outlook promises quite the opposite, as southwest Atlanta painfully illustrates.

While there should be grave amounts of responsibility leveraged upon the bankers and brokers and the credit rating agencies for perpetuating a system that allowed families that could not afford homes to purchase them (regardless of race), there has to be a certain amount of personal responsibility as well. Bailing out foreclosing homes is only going to allow fasely inflated property values to exist and the problem will never be solved in the long-term without a market correction.

Quote from: From the Article:
First-time homebuyers have originated less than a tenth of all subprime loans since 1998, according to a 2007 Center for Responsible Lending analysis. As recently as 2006, just over half of all subprime loans were refinances of existing home loans.

This is another part of this mess that I can't understand. You already had a mortage or home loan. You've (hopefull) done the research and invested proper time before the first purchase. You've been making payments, receiving paperwork and paying taxes on your home for years and when you refinance you don't even bother to do accurate research and investigate the current situation? What? It just strikes me as incredibly irresponsible.

I know that there are some honest-to-goodness cases of people who did everything right and got screwed anyway. There's a few, sure. There always will be. But the bulk of this is just a large blanketing of irresponsibility from Moody's and S&P down to the Seattle condo owners and the large Atlanta neighborhoods refinancing their homes.

It seems to me that it all boils down to one thing: Over the next decade we are going to see more homeless old ladies wandering the streets of North America than ever before in history. All those widows and single mothers who lost their homes, savings, jobs and stability, without any bailouts lined up for them. While we are bailing out banks, mortgage lenders, and irresponsible home owners and major corporations, lining up construction jobs for the healthy and strong, the weakest in our society are going to go by the wayside without any hope or help.

I am not proud to be an American at this time.

Whatever happened to the means test to receive benefits?

Right here in Astoria elderly folks—and since most women outlive their male counterparts—women have lost their meals-on-wheels and now must fend for themselves. Or, if they are lucky enough, become a burden to their families. Tell me, are you in a position to buy food for the elderly in your families? Most people are just eking through themselves.

If I am sounding like I am terribly afraid of the future, I am. I really am. I am terrified that the day will come that I will no longer be able to afford my rent and basic sustenance. Perhaps that's why I cannot pass an elderly woman of the street without opening my wallet and sharing with her. I always wonder if she was just like me . . . struggled her whole life while raising her family . . . and suddenly finding herself without the means to support herself.

Screw the banks, the mortgage lenders, the car manufacturers, the irresponsible people who put this country—the whole world—in this position. Let's just help people, on a one-by-one basis with a means test that shows that we value the American spirit of survival, not the greed.
