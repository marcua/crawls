The paragraph above the chart provides some explanation.  The US Census provides population & income data by ZIP codes.  So these researchers looked at where recruits were coming from, by ZIP code.

They then made three calculations.  First, they figured out what share of recruits came from each ZIP code (if the armed forces recruited 100,000 total new recruits in the US, & 50 of them came from ZIP code 11105, then their share was 50/100,000 or 0.0005).  Second, they figured out what share of the total youth population (ie., potential recruits) lived in each ZIP code (so if there are 1,000,000 recruit-age people in the US, & 1,000 of them live in ZIP code 11105, then 11105's share is 1,000/1,000,000 or 0.001).  Then they took the first number & divided it by the second number to give them a ratio of how many people were recruited from a given ZIP code over how many people were available in that ZIP code to be recruited.  Using my fake number, 11105's ratio would be 0.0005/0.001 = 0.5.

A ratio of 1 would mean that a given ZIP code is sending exactly as many recruits as you would expect for the number of potential recruits that live there.  A ratio above 1 means that ZIP code is sending more recruits than you would expect, & less than 1 means it's sending fewer recruits than you would expect.

The researchers then looked at the median household income of each ZIP code (this data is collected by the US Census).  They divided the incomes into ranges (listed on the bottom of the chart) & figured out the average ratio for all the ZIP codes in each income range.

So, looking at that data..... ZIP codes with median household incomes below, at, or just barely above the national median ($47,837) send more recruits to the armed forces than you would expect based on their population of potential recruits.  Really rich ZIP codes (further right on the graph) send fewer recruits.  Strangely enough, so do the poorest ZIP codes. 

Now zip codes are not representative of population of that area (as for example congressional districts) are they?  I don't think they are, but I may be wrong  I would assume richer zip codes have less population, because they have larger property tracks, and the converse would be true (not accounting for farm areas).  I know this is a big generalization, and I am taking a lot of wealthy zip codes in the city out of the equation, but I think overall I am fairly correct in my assumption.  Therefore, these numbers are skewed because more densely populated zip codes would have a large discrepency in income, thereby changing the mean. 

I think this chart would be more accurate if it was by congressional districts -- or maybe there would be the same problem?  Any thoughts?
