Just for reference, here is an article about "trusted computing" from the EFF:
http://www.eff.org/Infrastructure/trusted_computing/20031001_tc.php

Daveed, you can get GIMP (the gnu image manipulation progam).  It doesn't look as pretty as Photoshop, but it is extremely powerful.  Also, there are some very nice GUI's that come with a reasonable Linux distribution.  But in general, the vast majority of commercial apps are not available for linux.  Below, I'll get into the why.  Perhaps this will open up some further discussion.

I agree with windowsrefund on principle. We should all be using Linux, Free/Open/Net BSD, or any other OS variant that promotes either open source or free software.  I use Linux on my desktop myself (yellowdog, running on a Mac g4), and I find Windows to be bloated, slow, and generally difficult to work with.  Of course, I'm a programmer, and my entire day is usually spent typing into a terminal window, or pouring over a technical resource - not typing into Excel, munging Word documents, or making presentations in PowerPoint.

Unfortunately, the problem remains that Linux cannot survive in a groupware/office application environment.  And it's in this groupware setting where Linux is going to have to gain public approval.  If I were an admin about to deploy a network for office workers, I would curse several times, resign myself to rebooting the Exchange server every week, and then place my order for Windows Server 2003, and a whole bunch of Windows XP licences. :(

I hate saying this, but Outlook is great at what it does, and its the best out there.  Ximian (from Novell) looks like its catching up, though from what I can tell, its not as mature.   Plus, M$ Office, with all its crashes, is pretty darn good.  Open Office, the comparable open source product, just doesn't look and feel as slick as M$ Office.  Windows, Exchange, Outlook has the fastet and easiest path for deploying machines, networking them, and getting users up and running quickly.  Plus, every office employee has used M$ office.

No, I don't like it at all, but that's the current score.  In order for Linux to be more relevant in the business world, it needs an easy deployment path for admins who are either short on time, experience, or talent. And Open Office needs to look and feel more professional.  It needs to appeal to the office workers - more so than the techies.

I think it was Eddie Izzard that said it: "70 percent is how you look, 20 percent is how you sound, and 10 percent is what you say."

From reading the Novell website, it looks like they are trying to compete with M$, and that is excellent.
http://www.novell.com/products/desktop/
If Novell can make their desktop look good - if they can make good officeware, comparable to Outlook/Office - and if they start pushing it during the commercials of Law and Order, they might have a chance.  I'm eagerly awaiting the day I see a "Linux on the Desktop" commercial, but I'm not holding my breath.

And that, my own little nutshell,  is why we don't see Adobe developing for Linux.

But anyway, I got a bit sidetracked from the original thread.  To me, this whole "trusted computing" thing is just another thing, in a laundry list of things, that Microsoft is doing ensure their domination of the desktop, and the money in our wallet.  Should you use GNU/Linux or another open OS?  Well, I think so.  But it boils down to using the right tool for the right job.  If Linux can't provide what you need to do your job, what can I possibly say to you to make you switch?  Well, nothing really! In Daveed's case, I would probably recommend that he doesn't switch - his software requirements may be too specialized, and retraining him on Linux with a suite of unfamilliar applications would probably be a monumental task.  (I think that web development tools are widely available for Linux, though I'm not sure if Linux has a reasonable answer to Dreamweaver -please correct me if i'm wrong.)  If he felt strongly about moving away from Microsoft and was ready for a bumpy, but extremely rewarding road, then I think he (and anyone else) should go for the switch.  GNU/Linux and the BSD's are better operating systems than Windows, plain and simple.

Unfortunately, at the end of the day it depends on getting the job done, and the applications are what allow the user to get the job done, not necessarily the quality of the OS.  99% of computer users want (and probably need) a black box, where they can forget about the details of a computer's inner workings,  and concentrate on their job.  Windows (and the Mac) provides this, and Linux is maybe 60% there.  I sincerely hope that Linux can keep up the fight, and that more large companies will continue to throw their support behind it.  Until then, we're going to be stuck in Windows Purgatory.  

Note!  I just looked at openoffice.org - looks like they have a release candidate for Open Office 2.0.  Exciting!

Just for fun, here's my setup:

Machine: Apple g4 733 Quicksilver
OS:  GNU/Linux (Yellowdog 4.0)
Window Manager: XFCE
Email Client:  Pine, (if Thunderbird were readily available for my architecture, I would use it)
Browser: Firefox
Office Suite: Open Office
Music Organizer: Rhythm Box
Music Format: OGG/Vorbis

I'm happy with it - I get to use a geeky OS, plus I get to say "screw you DRM!!" by using OGG/Vorbis.
