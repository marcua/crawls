Okay, so now I am interested in what your definition of overcharging is.  As a business owner, you must know that overcharging cannot be construed to mean that some customers must pay for the services that customers as a whole require.  Advertising and ancillary services/products are all founded upon such.

My business sells freshly baked organic dog treats.  In order to create the feel of a real bakery, I have all of the treats displayed in a pretty expensive glass case, which sits atop a pretty expensive counter top.  I then handle all of the treats with food service tissue and package them in white bakery boxes, slap a company logo sticker which I ordered from California on the box and then put it into a white paper bag which I had delivered from Pennsylvania.   Its rung up on a touchscreen computer and if a customer wants to pay by credit card, they may.  All of this costs money, including the credit caqrd machine and the percentage I have to pay on each transaction.  This money which is worked into the price of the treats.  Am I overcharging some cash paying customers because they would have been happy if I displayed them in a cheap plastic container and packaged them in a crappy little white plastic bag?
