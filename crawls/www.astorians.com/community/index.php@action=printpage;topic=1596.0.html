Title: Re: the secret cause of flamewars
Post by: sorabji on February 14, 2006, 07:14:41 PM
I actually think that  the ambiguity of online communication is one of the things that makes it so amusing. But what do I know: I was surprised that the "I'm sorry" thing was being read as a flame thing. I found it positively mild.

It's aggravating at times to say things in what I think is the clearest, plainest possible English, only to have it interpreted as snide, ignorant, bilious, or any other random sentiment that has no bearing on the reality of what I said.

I, for one, write letters. Stamps, paper, pen... It just feels better than pecking out an e-mail. It doesn't solve the problem of being mis-interpreted, which is nothing new to e-mail.

I've been running message boards for over 11 years (which is nothing to brag about), and lately I'm getting a lot of requests from people who ran vanity searches on themselves and found that stupid or somehow embarrassing things they posted many years ago are now surfacing #1 on some search engines when someone looks up their name. Guess it pays to be careful with the invective online, especially if you have a distinctive name. I happen to have a very common name so it's unlikely anyone would confuse me with the identically-named white supremacist in Pennsylvania, the British comedian, or the car salesman in Oregon. But you never know, because like the Wired story says, believe whatever's most convenient for themselves.
