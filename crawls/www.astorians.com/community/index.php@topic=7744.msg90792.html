Good point, Chris, but I thought that it was a lot worse in the 90's.  Perhaps, it's escalating again, which would be unfortunate.  Just a quick story about gangs in this area.

In the mid-90's, two or three rival gangs were fighting in the western Queens area, over the usual things that gangs fight over.  In fact, a friend of mine from Mexico had explained that they had come up from villages in Mexico and were now continuing their rivalry in Queens.  The Mexican owned and operated bars in the area were fairly dangerous places to be around and one of them on 36th Avenue was even used a hang out spot for one of the gangs.  It culminated in the summer of 1997 with the double homicide of two gang members on the corner of 36th Avenue and 32nd Street, which I unfortunately witnessed.  I knew both of the victims as they used to go to my friend's bodega on 36th Avenue.  The average resident didn't even realize what was going on before the violence erupted on the street that night.  You would be surprised how prevalent and pervasive the gangs were back then.  Anyway, after the shootings and several other murders in the area, which by the way, barely got any English language media attention, the police stepped up their presence and most of the leaders, feeling the heat, fled back to Mexico.

Again, I hope they catch these killers and hopefully this young man's family will get some sort of closure.

Anytime a young person is targeted like that, the media automatically assume it's gang related. This may very well be a gang-related attack, but it could also be that these guys had a beef with this kid for some other, non-gang related reason. Look at the violence that happened at The Quay -- because it didn't involve Latino or black youth, the assumption wasn't that it was gang violence, though it was an attack on one person perpetrated by a group.

This type of random -- yes, random -- violence occurs anywhere and everywhere. This kid worked in Astoria -- if he had worked on Park Avenue, it would have happened there. He was shot and stabbed -- which suggests that the attackers knew their victim. Most stabbings aren't random. It wasn't a robbery, it happened too quickly. This group wanted THIS guy dead and knew his travel plans.

If you look at the police blotter in the Gazette (thanks to whoever posted that in another thread), Astorians have more to worry about when it comes to drunk drivers in our neighborhood than we do about random shootings.
