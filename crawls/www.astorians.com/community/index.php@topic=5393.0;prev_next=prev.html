Featuring
Peter D. Salins

New York is a city of renters: more than two-thirds of Americans are homeowners, but only one-third of New Yorkers are. A majority of NYC’s renters live in units subject to some form of rent regulation. The largest program, rent-stabilization, has been modified so that more and more units are being decontrolled. With the booming real estate market pushing up market rate rentals, and big developments like Peter Cooper Village and Starrett City changing hands, many are calling on the Governor to reverse the deregulation trend. Who are the beneficiaries of rent regulation? Of deregulation? What are the consequences of the deregulation trend and what would happen if it were to be reversed? Can New York be an affordable city? Should it?

Monday, March 26th, 2007
6:30 P.M. Prompt
(Free and open to the public - Reception to follow)

Fordham Law School
140 W 62nd Street, McNally Hall
(Corner of 62th Street and Columbus Avenue)

Quite frankly, I believe rent control is a necessary evil.  If rents were to be completely deregulated a lot of people would lose their homes because they would not be able to afford living here.  The economic situation has changed substantially.  People are not making the kind of money now that just a couple of generations ago were making, inflation, etc. 

The amount of people applying for unemployment in the past 10 years is staggering. 
As companies become more automated, less people are needed. 
Salaries have not kept up with inflation.

What about those persons that have lived in the same apartment for over 30 years and are retired and on a fixed income?  What would they do?  New York does not have sufficient housing for them and Plan 8 would increase substantially costing the City even more money, costing tax payers even more money that already have to deal with paying higher rents. 

Does it make sense?  No, the chain reaction would be catastrophic.

Unfortunately, these Management/Realtor companies only have one thing in mind...making money.  I feel that if rent control were eliminated there would be nothing to protect the Tenants.  They would just increase the rent to whatever they feel is the market value and renters would have no choice but to move out, but not only that...move where? 

It would be disastrous for a lot of hard working people (of which some work two jobs to cover expenses). 

It's not to say that there are those that abuse the system, but deregulation would not only affect them but also those that are honest hardworking individuals.

Rent control needs to stay.

Quote from: lizluv43 on March 02, 2007, 07:11:18 PM

Rent control needs to stay.


lizluz, think of any market in which the presence of more and more competition results in higher priced goods for consumers.

Inevitably, unfettered markets produce more for less. What we need in NYC is more market forces influencing real estate development and less government interference.

Rent control actually deprives consumers of an array of options that naturally arise in a truly competitive market. How does this happen? Well, put yourself into the position of the real estate developer. In NYC, the best bang for your investment dollar comes in higher-end markets because, at the lower end, the market is saturated with rent-controlled buildings AND because taxes on property and costs associated with unionized construction work are obscenely high. Ergo, ROI (return on investment) is extremely difficult at the mid- and low-end. We see plenty of competition in luxury buildings, which is where money can be made, and then a huge portion of the population is underserved, stuck in increasingly poorly maintained buildings, paying rent instead of mortgages.

To create a vibrant market in real estate development and home ownership for people at all income levels, the government has to get out of the F-ING way and make it profitable for developers to meet the demands of consumers. But this won't happen if only rent control is ended. The state and city overburden developers with red tape, fees, taxes, concessions to builders' unions.... they've made it too costly to develop real estate for the lower and middle income folks. This is infuriating to me. Shakes me to the core.

You're probably right that some/many people who have grown accustomed to subsidized housing would be inconvenienced and/or experience significant distress, at least initially, when rent control is ended. But make no mistake that the institutionalization of rent control has created this fiasco. The problem isn't that market prices would be too high, it's that we've had government interference for so long, it's disincentivized the development of a market to address the demands of lower- and middle income consumers. So would the transition be rough? Yes, but markets are quick to form where demand is high and it sure is high here.

What if the person paying $700 a month for a 2 bedroom has a net worth of 10 million dollars and annual income of $500,000? Should those who can afford to pay more continue to receive the benefit of a market distortion intended to benefit the poor? This was a frustrating unintended consequence of rent control that continues to this day.

How about this? End rent control tomorrow with something like the following stipulations: (1) anyone drawing less than $50,000 a year in income whose net worth is under $250,000 continues to have the benefits of rent control for as long as they stay in their apartment; (2) rent control will cease to be attached to specific apartments when all of the people covered by the above either move or die. It's an approach worth considering. Still, I tend to favor voluntary charity (actually, that's all I favor). The truth is that whatever solutions are implemented at this stage are going to be frustrating to many people. Transitions from government control to freedom often appear to be "heartless" or "compromised", it just goes with the territiory. Purists

Again, the arguments against rent control are irrefutable in theory. In practice, there are many factors that combine with rent control to artificially inflate prices and ending rent control without addressing them would be irresponsible and ultimately counterproductive in that doing so might lead to a backlash against what is otherwise a smart reform. What am I talking about?

What transpired in the former Soviet Union is instructive. After the communist system collapsed, the government did not move to establish laws protecting private property and individual liberty, to ensure due process rights. Of course, free enterprise relies on these institutions and they were never present there and still aren't in Russia. So the immediate post-communist regime in Russia was an experiment in what happens when people's rights are protected by groups other than the government, like, for example, organized crime syndicates. "Vee'll 'protect' you, vether you vant it or no!" Might made right. People lived in fear. Then, playing on people's response to what was described as a failure of the free market, which was in fact the failure of the government to pass and enforce laws protecting property and individual liberty, Putin moved in, and now Russian is essentially a mess again.*

The danger in ending government control a little at a time is that the half-measures still inhibit the free market response we're counting on to remedy situations. It is an illusion to believe that there is a perfect solution. Promises of perfection only come from politicians seeking to take more of your money and more of your freedom, too. In the absence of perfect solutions, we have to pick the ones that are most excellent.

The problem with saying that you want to preserve the situation for the poor dude paying $700 a month is that it assumes that the market won't provide him with a more affordable option. I mean, imagine a world in which the cost of a cell phone is reduced to zero as long as the buyer signs a 2-year contract? This is not a world in which the government runs the cell phone industry.

-Adam

*Fortunately, many of the satellite states (Lithuania, Latvia, and especially Czech Republic and Estonia) fully embraced freedom and they are truly thriving.
