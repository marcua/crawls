City Hall News

Conservative Democrat Enters Race For Gianaris Seat
By Chris Bragg
 
Add one more candidate to what at first seemed like it would be a sleepy race for the Assembly seat of Mike Gianaris.


John Ciafone, an attorney and former school board president in Western Queens is getting into the race—and he hopes to appeal to conservative Democrats with a platform which includes support for charter schools and opposing gay marriage.

Ciafone, who is Italian-American, officially announced his candidacy at a meeting Tuesday night the Long Island City-based Aldos Democratic Club, which he heads. The meeting was also attended by his two Democratic opponents in the race, civil rights attorney Jeremiah-Frei Pearson and corporate attorney Aravella Simotas.


Gianaris is running for the Senate seat being vacated by George Onorato, who is retiring.

Ciafone, who opposes same-sex marriage, said he would run to the right of the two other candidates, who are pro-same sex marriage and who so far have been jockeying for the liberal primary vote. The district has a heavy Latino, Greek, Irish and Eastern European populations that can be more socially conservative than the normal Democratic primary electorate.


The district largely overlaps with that of Council Member Peter Vallone, Jr., one of the most conservative members of the City Council, and is squarely within the Senate district held by Onorato, who voted against the same-sex marriage bill.

Even though he is a former school board president in the area, Ciafone also said he was strongly in favor of lifting the charter school cap, which he said also said differentiates him from his two opponents.


“I saw first hand how afraid everyone was of the UFT,” he said. “Most politicians are afraid of them.”

Ciafone said he hopes charter school supporters will provide him funding in the race. He has hired consultant Michael Tobman, who is working with several pro-charter candidates this year.

Ciafone was complimentary of Frei-Pearson—whom Ciafone said had encouraged him to run—but bashed Simotas for her close ties to Gianaris and other members of the Queens Democratic Party, which last Monday voted to officially endorse Simotas.


“All the politicians have already gone with her,” Ciafone said. “They’re like the political mafia.”


As a conservative Democrat, Ciafone could cut into the base of older, more conservative voters in the area that typically back the county candidate, in this case Simotas. Frei-Pearson, meanwhile, is trying to make inroads with the younger voters that have moved into Astoria and Long Island City.

Simotas’ campaign, though, in a statement to City Hall said that, “It doesn’t matter who runs because Aravella is the best candidate and is going to win.”

That sentiment was echoed by Vallone, who beat Ciafone in a heated 2001 Council campaign, and has endorsed Simotas. Vallone noted that Simotas, who is the first Greek-American woman to run for Assembly, already had lined up the support of the biggest Democratic club in the area, the Taminet Regular Democrat Club.


“They’re the older voters who supported George Onorato for so many years,” Vallone said. “They have a very large membership, and they love Aravella.”
