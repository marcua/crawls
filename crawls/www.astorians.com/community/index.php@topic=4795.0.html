Your question is "Should religious beliefs directly related to issues and controversies be addressed by political figures?"

You're asking whether political figures should address "religious beliefs directly related to issues and controversies".  I'm really not sure what this means.  It sounds like you mean that there are certain issues and controversies that have religious beliefs attached to them.  I agree to the extent that there are certain issues (for example, a woman's right to choose, gay marriage, the death penalty, the right to die) to which some people with religious beliefs react strongly, but the issues themselves have no inherent religious meaning. 

Next, what do you mean by "addressed"?  I can think of a bunch of interpretations of it -- do you mean should they

- say that yes, one's religious beliefs directly relate to issues and controversies?
- say this is my religion and it influences my take on issues and controversies?
- say I will take into account the religious beliefs of my constituents in my approach to issues and controversies?

Basically, though, I think you are questioning the role of religion in politics; is that correct?  What role should religion have in politics, if any, and what role should a politician's own religion and/or the religious belief of his or her constituents have in the politician's approach to his or her job of representing that consitutency?

Quote from: Alexa on January 20, 2007, 04:10:11 PM
As for the insufficiency of the poll, what options would be appropriate to fit your needs?
It shouldn't be about my needs. I think your clarification makes it more meaningful. I do think the poll has too many choices. Necessary evil is just a subcategory of need to be addressed. I will offer up my suggestion that I think it would be a better poll if it were yes or no. Unsure category is wishy washy and doesn't really measure anything other than the percentage of people who haven't spent any time thinking about it, and honestly, who cares about that? I'd do the agree/disagree options and let the textual debate give the flavor and nuance to what the poll results mean.

I'd add that religion should be a part of any politician's approach and dialogue. If those beliefs are important enough, they ought to carry over to their private and public lives. The trick is to not mandate who can believe what or place any one religion's belief sets above another, including atheism.

I believe it is necessary from a marketing perspective. If a Democrat expects to win they have to pander to the religious right in this country, and if that means that they acknowledge God or that they go to a specific church then so be it. I don't love it myself, but I do see the value of it to try and win a significant number of votes that they wouldn't get otherwise. Some may see that as sleazy but campaigning basically equals marketing and vice versa.

As far as specific political issues go - no I don't think that they should directly mix. I'm not a big fan of Bush saying that he feels God supports his war in Iraq, for example. Once you are in office I think that you can still be a religious person and demonstrate that, but if you're directly mixing your views with your job it's like me going to church on the weekend, then going into work and saying, "I'm sorry - God told me that I can't work on your website. It just isn't gelling with my religious beliefs" or "Let's pray about this marketing initiative. I feel that we'll succeed only through the grace of God." It's just weird, it divides, and it make for one hell of a work environment.
