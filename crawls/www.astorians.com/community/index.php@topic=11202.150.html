Bill -- you've mentioned good outcomes for some government subsidies and that's what I meant by some I agree with and some I don't. But welfare is just another word for subsidy and the argument can be made that a university, with a billion dollar endowment and highly paid management team, could afford to fund their own research -- ditto for a pharmaceutical company -- especially when that tax seed money earns those organizations patents and hefty profits.

In regard to social benefits I'd rather someone work than pay them welfare and I think it's best to encourage and conjole people to find ways to earn a living but I also rather pay them welfare than have to step over them while walking to work every day, especially when our tax monies also provide a very good living for many people who have means without that tax money.

Para -- I wasn't assuming anything about you or your opinion personally and I'm sorry if it appeared that way but I think you understand that I why I reacted as I did.

Here's where I'm coming from: there seems to be something stirring in that I read more and more articles where the opinion rages about not paying for people's lifestyle choices, mainly when it comes to healthcare, but it's creeping into other areas and it has me worried. We won't pay for health problems of the smokers, the fatties, the drinkers...where does this go? That's what I mean that we draw these lines just beyond our doorsteps. Who will be next? Do we only care for people who are deemed worthy and throw the rest on the scrap heap? That happens too much already.

Consider this: what about people who engage in rough sports? Do we not treat them when they break an arm or a leg beacuse it was their choice to play that sport? Unlikely, yes but you can't say that will never happen. Maybe it's me but I don't believe in never.
