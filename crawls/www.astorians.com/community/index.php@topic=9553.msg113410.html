I thought I posted my Obama endorsement here, but I think it got lost in the outage. This is why I think that we actually can:

Here you have a politician who not only talks about acting to cross the party lines, but actually has a history of doing so. He's also made friends across the aisle that have helped move forward on nonproliferation incentives. He has a vested commitment to transparency in government, both in funding and spending. He's written bills to make a searchable database of who receives federal grants and contracts. He's been preemptive about avian flu before it was a major health concern but not about war.

And you can talk about whether or not he's experienced, whether the viral internet, massive money raising campaign he's working is prompt and effective, but you can't argue with the enthusiasm and hope that he's given to people my age all around this country. We're paying attention to politics. Disillusioned kids who've seen our parents' sorry attempts at "revolution" and mock them for it and here is this guy with views we like and a voice that sounds like ours and he's not playing the game dirty.

This is someone I would vote *for* and not against. What matters right now is that so many people who didn't care before, care now. Whether or not this man has the skills to be a better President than the brand name that is Clinton is immaterial. Even if Clinton were to make a better President, to see Obama lose to a creation of the political machine would further alienate an entire generation that is already dangerously close to throwing up hands and giving up on American Brand Politics altogether.

I will fully acknowledge that I'm probably too young to know better; that I'm a hopeless idealist and that, when I hit 30, reality will most likely shoot me in the face like I metaphorically went hunting with Dick Cheney and all these silly things like passion and inspiration and worthless catchphrases like “change and hope” will ring as hollow to me as they do to you. But right now, they don't, and I'm clearly not alone.

Yeah, I think we actually can.

It doesn't matter what Obama or Clinton have done (or haven't done); despite the rhetoric, the policy documents, and assorted hoo-ha that makes its way into the papers, politics is an intensely emotional process. And people vote largely on the basis of emotion.

Look at Bill Clinton. He was in effect a nobody from a minor state most Americans dismiss out of hand. (Although he was extremely effective establishing a base w/in the Democratic Party at this time -- that man was always thinking ahead to the White House). And in 1991-92, he managed to knock out an incumbent president who had had the highest approval ratings ever.

Remember the swooning over Bill's persona? The sax playing. The rock 'n roll. His youthful exuberance. The boxers(?) That's what got him elected; not a "what has Bill done for me" rational calculus.

Hillary simply does not have the emotional draw for people. She is a technocrat. A policy tinkerer. And a full-on establishment politician. These traits have served (and will continue to serve) her well as a senior Senator from a state that values these things. New York likes its machine politicians, no matter which party they're from. Because politics are more institutionalized here than in other states, what with unions, public-private entities (e.g. The Port Authority), a strong, entrenched legislature, powerful  lobby groups, etc. Moreover, she is a strong supporter of regulation and government intervention, which a lot of New Yorkers want from their politicians.

Then there are the negatives. And her detractors (in both parties) are legion. The primary results clearly lend weight to the fact that nationally, most people just don't want her.

It's not her time. Probably never was.

But New York will still accept her (I presume she doesn't have to relinquish her seat to run for president) and most likely will remain its senator for a good dozen years or more. Her local supporters at least have that to look forward to.

The other point I want to make is that the President doesn't write laws! In some sense, it hardly matters what Obama wants to do with Medicaid. He's not the decider (on new laws). He only gets to negotiate and threaten vetos.

Clinton has good ideas and is a clear master of the system, but if she alienates people, she won't be able to inspire solutions.

Popularity matters for Presidents.
