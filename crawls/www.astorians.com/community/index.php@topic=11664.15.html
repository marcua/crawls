care to share that vegetarian chili recipe?

All right - it's not very specific, but here are the things you'd need for vegetarian chili a la essen:

vegetable oil
2 cloves garlic, minced
1 medium yellow onion, chopped
1 green pepper, chopped
1 package white mushrooms, cut into wedges, 4 or 6 wedges per mushroom depending on size
1 large can whole peeled tomatoes (whatever the biggest standard size can is)
1 can black beans
1 can red beans
approx 1/4 cup of bulgar wheat
approx 2 TBSP Badia chili powder (I use Badia brand, but you may want less depending on what brand you use)
approx 2 TSP cumin
some ground black pepper
dash of cinnamon

Get out a very large pan or pot. Cut the green pepper first and sauté it in the vegetable oil, and as it's cooking cut up the garlic and onions. The pepper should sauté on low-medium heat for at least 10 minutes before adding the garlic and onions, and those should cook for a few minutes. Before you add anything else, the peppers should be starting to look cooked and the onions should be softening.

While that's cooking, cut up the mushrooms and then add those at the appropriate time and mix it all around. Open up the tomatoes and break them into the mix, including the juice from the can, and then add the two cans of beans. (You may want to drain the bean juice, but I like leaving in some of it. I like the organic beans from Key Food; the juice in the other stuff skeeves me.) Then add the spices. Then add the bulgar wheat. Add as much of the bulgar as you think you want - for me it serves to provide a meaty texture without being "fake meat," but the chili shouldn't be overwhelmed by it, so if I were to measure it I think it's around 1/4 cup.

The wheat will absorb the juice from the tomatoes and bean cans. This should all simmer for at least 20 minutes. Stir it occasionally and taste it to make sure you like the spice level. Add whatever other spices you want; keep in mind that the chili that results from this is not spicy at all, but it is tasty. If you like spicy chili you will need to add your own spicy things. Simmer until it's not so liquidy and the mushrooms have shrunk. I like serving it with rice, and I cook the rice in a method inspired by Chipotle, which is to cook it with fresh cilantro and lime. But that's optional.

Served with rice, I would say this makes 8-10 servings.

I found myself spending loads of money on basics like shampoo and conditioner, so I started reading the Rite Aid ad online every Sunday morning.  If you're disciplined about submitting your receipts either online or through the mail you can get insane deals like free organic shampoo and conditioner.  I've gotten shaving cream, shampoo, conditioner, and other useful stuff for free and other things like deodorant and hair color for a few dollars.  You have to wait for the rebate check and you can only submit for your rebate once a month (I think they hope that you'll shop in the beginning of the month and forget to request the check because you don't want to be locked out of good deals), but its a reliable program and I've saved a lot of money while actually getting to use better products.  The best deals are for newly introduced brands; you can still save a few dollars on established brands.

I agree on the cooking at home for friends rather than dining out: I spent less than $20 to make BBQ chicken (thighs and legs are good deals and its even better if you cut apart a whole chicken yourself), veg kabobs, and feta/porch produce flatbread, and brownie sundaes for three and we're still eating leftovers.  I think that the key to staying on a food budget is to limit feelings of deprivation by occasionally making really wonderful things that you otherwise couldn't afford.  When I want something that looks (and tastes) like haute cuisine I make pork tenderloin medalions with red onion compote.  When I want really delicious Italian I spend an afternoon churning out goat cheese and mushroom ravioli.  For just a few dimes you can make the famous (and insanely awesome) no-knead bread that will make the local bakery loaves taste like paste.  And if you need to impress someone special just roast a chicken--you can get them for under four bucks at Trade Fair: stuff it with a lemon and bit of sage, salt and pepper liberally. 

Oh- and nstead of skipping the bar, I just order Diet Coke instead of cocktails.  Yes, I hate paying $2.50 for each soda, but after two you really can't take any more and it costs so much less than booze while still getting to see your friends look foolish.  AND it saves on a mandatory, stumble-induced cab fare home.

My red onion compote goes something like this:

1 tbsp olive oil
1 red onion, finely sliced
½ tsp soft brown sugar
1 tbsp balsamic vinegar
½ lemon, juice only
salt and freshly ground black pepper

Heat the oil in a pan and add the red onion. Fry for 5-8 minutes, or until just softened but not too browned.
Stir in the sugar, vinegar and lemon juice and season, to taste, with salt and freshly ground black pepper.

(From: http://www.bbc.co.uk/food/recipes/database/frenchonionsoupwithc_87385.shtml)

Sometimes I like to trade honey for the sugar--just add to taste.

Enjoy!
