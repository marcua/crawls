Hard Labor
« on: November 27, 2006, 12:58:18 PM »
I recently read this Nation article about organic farming.  I like to buy organic food for various personal reasons, some of which I'm sure I share with many people reading this.  I've been aware that on some organic farms - usually the very large ones like Earthbound Farms - it is not the utopian atmosphere that one often associates with organic farming.  Workers are not necessarily treated better (apart from lacking in the direct contact with pesticides, although there is pesticide drift) at some organic farms than at conventional farms.  However, circumstances behind the treatment can be different.  A question:  should this matter to us as consumers? 

This is not to say that all workers are mistreated on organic farms.  See Swanton Berry Farm (most amazing strawberry jam I've ever had).

Some of us are becoming more concerned with animal treatment, perferring to buy a "humanely grown" chicken or beef.  Labeling helps us in knowing how an animal was raised before slaughter.  One suggestion in the article has to do with labeling an organic product in a similar vein, with regard to the workers.  From the article:

Quote
In the spring of last year, researchers at the University of California published a study showing that organic farmers widely oppose requirements that they pay benefits and allow farmhands to organize.

Nonetheless, there is a small but growing campaign, backed by some of organic agriculture's staunchest supporters, for a new kind of food labeling, one that would guarantee that food is produced in ways that benefit workers as well as the environment.


Anyway, it was an interesting and throught-provoking article.  I'd love to know what others think, too.

Sadly the thing about organic is that it isn't always sustainable - and I would think that this would include unsustainable labor practices as well. You can't always be entirely aware of farming practices, and it does seem like bigger companies like Earthbound would have less than ideal practices.

That's why it is good to try and buy local (and in season) as much as possible, and to check with organizations like Just Food to verify that local farming practices are up to par. Farm Aid is probably another good resource (and one that I think I heard about from you Meg :)).

What is interesting is that until a year or two ago I didn't really think about organic not always being sustainable. It adds another layer of personal challenge for me because it means that when I go to the store and look at the organic Earthbound lettuce I have to ask myself - Was this farmed satisfactorily? Should I support an organic product that traveled thousands of miles to get to me or should I try to eat a salad that was farmed closer by? And to be honest, I don't always address those things, but when I do - oh frustration ensues! :-(
