Bloomberg's RPP Plan
« on: March 12, 2008, 02:37:27 PM »
There was another press conference today that had nothing to do with prostitutes.  Mayor Bloomberg and DoT Commish Janette Sadik-Khan revealed their residential parking permit (RPP) plan in Boerum Hill.  It was the result of several neighborhood parking workshops the DoT hosted from the end of 2007 to the beginning of 2008 (I had attended the most recent one in LIC).  The up side is it will prevent park and riders from, well from parking and riding, and that residents can request the local community board to hold a public hearing on RPPs for the 'hood.  The downside is it will only go into effect if congestion pricing is passed.  You can read the Streetsblog report on the press conference here.

Well this is all well and good....certainly having people come into your neighborhood to take up parking spaces can be quite a nuisance (though I've also been guilty of doing it!).

However, there's some practicalities that would need to be ironed out:

Many people "park and ride" because they are coming from areas that do not really have adequate public transportation.  I know that's hard to imagine in Astoria, where almost all of the main thoroughfares have a bus line and there's also the N, R, W, V and sometimes G trains, but not much further out in Queens, you have neighborhoods that have very inadequate bus service, and no train service.  Not everyone works 9-5, but bus/train schedules in the outlying areas and suburbs are completely geared to the 9-5 commuter, even in a changing economy.  Thus, some people park and ride.  By creating residential parking permits, a lot of these people might just be forced to take their own cars all the way, instead of relying on public transportation at some point during their commute.

For example, the city of Athens, Greece (which is quite large in its own right, with a population of 4-5 million) during the past decade, has built a brand-new subway (metro) system.  Most of the stations except the ones in the very center of the city, have park-and-ride facilities.  Why?  Because even with the extensive growth of the city's metro system, and with a very strong network of bus lines, public transportation routes still do not go everywhere and are not always convenient for everyone, in terms of the routes or the schedules.  Apparently, the park-and-rides are a great success, as has been the metro, as traffic circulation has improved, ridership is high and air pollution is on the decline.

Additionally, many people park in residential areas because they are visiting businesses in that neighborhood.  Certainly, there are not enough metered parking spaces on the main thoroughfares to accommodate demand for parking.  Reducing the supply of parking spaces even more might have the undesired consequence of more people circling around for fewer parking spaces, adding to traffic and exhaust pollution. 

Yes, I know the point is to have more and more people rely on public transportation.  However, a lot of people come to Astoria and other neighborhoods like it from far away....outlying areas of NYC, the suburbs, even other states to visit. 

Depending on where you are coming from, Astoria is not always the easiest place to get to.  I recall going to school out on Long Island, only an hour's drive from the city during non-peak hours....and the only public transportation out there being the LIRR, which ran every 2-3 hours at best, and took two hours to get to Penn Station.  A lot of areas are even worse off, as my school was right next to the train tracks.  Hence, a lot of people just go with their cars.

My point, to summarize, is that such measures are really "quick fixes," at best, and only solve a small part of the problem while potentially creating new ones.  To truly encourage the public to rely on public transportation completely and abandon their cars, infrastructure needs to improve, especially in the many areas where it is almost non-existent.  Until that happens, people are going to go with the convenient choice, and that is their car.  It's not be being in favor of cars and exhaust...I see it as the reality of how most people will choose to commute.
