Well to continue:

Actually the THREE things that most rankle me are:

1) They often don't have the advertised sale prices entered into their system.  You have to pay attention (and know the sale prices), or you get charged the regular price.  For me, yesterday, it was exactly twice the sale price.  This seems to happen again, and again.  The weekly advertised specials pull people into the store, but you will often have to put some work in to actually get the sale price - either get help at the self-check stations, get the checker to make the change, or haul you tail back in with your receipt to get the refund.  Who manages this store?  Why would it be so hard to go through the store each week to make sure the price changes are posted, and to go through the system to make sure they've been entered?  Pretty fundamental quality assurance.

2)  I've particularly noticed in the produce section, especially in the high-priced organic produce, that there are frequently items without any display of unit price whatsoever.  Apparently this display of prices is legally required - does anyone know more about this?  You get to find out the price when you check out, or if you can use the price scanner provided for shoppers, BUT...

3) They've no longer got a price scanner there for shoppers.  It's place is memorialized by a dangling cord right now.  In the past, when I've spoken to KF staff about how frequently the prices aren't posted, they've pointed to the scanner and said that I can just use it to find out.  That's of course not too convenient, and at least for, it was often hard to use.  I could imagine lots of consumers, particularly older and disabled people, might have some trouble using it too.  Maybe they'll replace it with a better one, but that's kind of beside the point.  It shouldn't be such a project to make a consumer decision based on pricing.

Does anyone have any ideas about consumers can have influence on their local grocery chain store?  I realize that there are many people on limited and fixed incomes dependent on however Key Food chooses to run their store.

So I went back to Key Food a day after the sale price error I wrote about above, bought something else, and checked to see if they'd corrected the price in the computer system for the sale item.  They hadn't.  I asked the same staff person who I'd reported it to, and she essentially said it was not her job, as did her supervisor, and they said that I should come back the next day and report it to the produce manager.

I found this on the Key Food websites, a page with a form for submitting messages about specific stores:

http://www.keyfoods.com/ContactUs.aspx

I received an emailed reply from a V.P of Merchandising, cc'ed to six other people, thanking me for my message, and requesting that I contact him directly with future issues/questions.  He described how even if they were 95% correct with their sale pricing, that could leave 7 or 8 incorrect items, and that in the produce section, they receive so many items new to the system, that it was harder to ensure that they were in the system with correct information.  He said that the supervisors would be in the store checking that the signs and labels were correct in the produce section.

I'm not clear why the work process can't include these steps:

1) Each week, take the sale ad and just go through the store, checking to see that the sale signs are up.  Then, check in the system that each sale price has been entered.  Simple, logical quality assurance process.  If you thrust advertising of sale prices into each home, you have an obligation to have those prices for the consumers you have lured with them to spend their money in your store.

2) Whenever you put an item on your shelves, check that the signs and system labeling are in place - clear, visible and correct.  Is there not a law requiring this?

The next day after I'd double-checked to find they had not corrected the price of the first incorrectly priced sale item, my partner was in Key Food to buy something else, also organic produce, that did have a sign for its sale price.  Again, incorrect price in the system, which he caught at the register.  He was not terribly comfortable while coordinating the correction, with a line of increasingly impatient customers waiting behind him for it to be figured out and corrected.  Corrected for him that is - it doesn't seem like the staff understands any responsibility to follow up to see that corrections happen in the system or with the shelf signage.

If you've been frustrated with Key Food pricing problems, I'd suggest copying the weblink above into your favorites and sending in quick reports each time.  It's clear from looking back the several years through this thread that they have not seen cause to be much concerned with putting procedures in place to fix this problem.  In this regard, they take their consumers for granted.
