Your statement made me realise that I really know nothing about how green or not Starbucks is.  So I hunted around a bit.  I'm not trying to promote or bash them, I just thought some facts would be in order.

According to the EPA:

Quote
Using the Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol, Starbucks calculated its total GHG emissions in 2003 to be 254,000 metric tons of carbon dioxide (CO2) equivalents. . . . Starbucks established an emissions reduction target and detailed appropriate metrics to measure ongoing performance in 2005. As a result of the Company's initial inventory, Starbucks also joined the Green Power Partnership and committed to purchase enough green power to cover 5 percent of their retail energy needs in North America, cutting CO2 emissions by 2 percent. Expanding on the success of its commitment in 2005, Starbucks has increased its green power commitment to 20 percent in 2006.

The EPA's top 25 corporations in terms of purchasing green power is here.  Starbucks is tenth on the list.  (Here is the definition of green power as it is used by the EPA.)

Here is Starbucks' Corporate Social Responsibility 2006 Annual Report.  Of course, some may discount it because it comes from Starbucks itself, but the report details the manner in which the data it contains is independently verified.

Here is some information about Starbucks' paper cups.

You can also pick up used coffee grounds at any Starbucks for composting purposes.

Here is a link to a site advocating a boycott against Starbucks based on organic issues - organic/rBGH milk and fair trade coffee.  It notes that 3.7% of the coffee Starbucks purchases is fair trade.  (Note:  Starbucks is apparently in the process (yes, I know, nebulous term) of switching to artificial hormone-free milk.)

lobber, thanks for your clarification.  From your posts, I assume that you're primarily concerned about social responsibility and consumerism.  My biggest concerns are with waste and recycling.  That is why I was glad to discover that I could just get an iced drink in a paper cup rather than a plastic cup, if I didn't have my commuter mug handy.  I think that there is a lot of invective against multi-nationals, I'm sure a lot of it well-deserved.  I don't like being told what to think about big business and find it difficult to access comprehensive, unbiased information.  However, I do know that discarded plastics take much much longer to degrade (if at all) than paper, and currently only plastic bottles and jugs can be recycled in NYC, not the plastic cups that iced drinks come in.

If I drank coffee more than once a week or every other week for three months in the summer, I would consider investing in a coffee maker.  But as it is, I'm satisfied with the 6 or so iced coffees I've had this summer from Starbucks.

One thing I did recently which I felt pretty good about was getting my leather boots fixed. Sure there is the ethical question of whether I should have bought them in the first place, ok I accept that.  My girlfriend got me these boots about two years ago for my birthday.  I wore them a lot and the soles wore down and some of the stitching had come loose.  On rainy days my socks were getting wet so I was going to throw them out and buy some new ones.  However, I dropped by my local shoe repair man (Raphael on 28th between 43rd and 42nd St - nice guy) and he fixed them up for me for a mere $28.  I got new soles, a new heel, and the stitching was all fixed.  These boots are like new!

So in one simple action, I prevented those boots from ending up in a landfill, saved some money, and responsibly reused the leather, plus the boots feel great and keep my feet dry now rain or snow! :mrgreen:
