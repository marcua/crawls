My interest is that I think many people think that purchasing medical insurance is the same as purchasing medical care -- it's not. When you say '...sticking an insurance company with a bill or 'redistribution of wealth' it make me wonder that's all.

First, a medical insurance company does not pay the bills for medical care. The insured and everyone else who pays into the plan pays for it. Healthy people subsidize those who are sick so if any one is being stuck it's healthy people.

Second, the whole concept of 'insurance' is socialism at it's best. Groups of people come together and pool their resources for the good of the group. Resources contributed by the healthy are redistributed to those who are sicker. In most insurance plans today, if you need ongoing care for an illness it most likely it comes on someone else's dime.

That's why I asked if you have insurance. This 're-distribution' is the price of admission of having insurance. If you feel so strongly on not having someone else pay for the services you receive, I would think you would opt out entirely so as not to put yourself in that position.

I have medical insurance by the way. I pay for it and am relatively healthy and rarely go to the doctor. I feel many people go to the doctor unnessarily, and that is a legimate complaint. But in general I don't mind paying in more than I take out. But if I pay in, I expect to be covered in the event I fall sick or am injured in the future.

In Debbie's case, you made an assumption about 'pre-existing condition'. It wasn't clear to me what came first the insurance or the illness. It sounded like the child had insurance, was diagnosed with juvenile rheumatoid arthritis and then was denied coverage. If this is the case, it's not right.  

If you paid into an insurance plan for years and relied on it to help defray medical costs for yourself and/or your family and then god forbid developed a chronic condition, would you be willing to sit back and let the insurance company say f--off?

Not exactly.  Insurance, properly understood is "shared risk," and those who are demonstratively more at risk should pay more up front, no?

Quote

Resources contributed by the healthy are redistributed to those who are sicker. In most insurance plans today, if you need ongoing care for an illness it most likely it comes on someone else's dime.

Not really.  Insurance, when adequately structured, has already extracted that money from the patient in premium payments and grown it through investment to pay for subsequent care.  Now, on a microscale, that may not be how it works out for a particular individual, but that is how it works across the group.  Medicare, Medicaid and Social Security are better examples on socialism, were one looking for some.

Quote
That's why I asked if you have insurance. This 're-distribution' is the price of admission of having insurance. If you feel so strongly on not having someone else pay for the services you receive, I would think you would opt out entirely so as not to put yourself in that position.

Quote

No, my problem with this whole pre-existing condition thing in the bill she lauds, is that it requires insurance companies to accept those with pre-existing conditions without allowing them to price that risk into the policies.  Which means those without those pre-existing conditions will subsidize them, especially in 2014 when they will be required to purchase insurance.

If Debbie's sister's insurance company tried to pull a fast one after they contracted coverage, sure, screw that company to the wall.  But what if they didn't?  What if pre-existing condition is in fact a pre-existing condition, they refused to cover it, charged a premium based on the assumption they would not be covering it, and are now compelled to do so?  Are you OK with that?
