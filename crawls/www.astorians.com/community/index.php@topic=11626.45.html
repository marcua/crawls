Quote from: CountingZestfully on August 08, 2008, 10:27:17 AM
As an "expert" in the field of online communication, I haven't seen a specific piece yet about men and women trolling differently because it's way too difficult to study accurately. It's hard to contact trolls and be assured that what they are telling you is the truth. There are case studies here and there (as in the NYT piece), but it's difficult to get enough people for a testable sample.

The closest thing I can think of are some qualitative studies with adolescents that examine the types of bullying each gender tends to carry out online. It's a lot like it is offline--boys go for the random ad hominem attacks (sort of equivalent to a fistfight or scuffle offline), but girls are vicious such that they systematically attack the target in such a way that undermines their relationships, pretty much dismantling someone's self-esteem and confidence bit by bit. A boy might simply flame someone, but a girl will pretend to be someone's friend, have a long and intimate chats or email exchanges with someone, and then save the deep dark secret parts and show them to everyone else or otherwise use them against the person. It's much more devastating than someone simply calling names (see: Meghan Meier).

CountingZestfully, I would love to meet you offline and discuss this more in depth.  I manage online communities for a living, and deal with trolls on a consistent basis.  In fact, this week, we did a big troll purge!  The community was thankful. Still, more will surface.  And I don't really believe in "harmless trolling".  The object is always to disrupt the community. 

I agree that the way girls bully and the way boys bully are different.  I disagree that it stops in adolescence, though. In my 20s I had two female friends (also in their 20s, K and G), and one (G) did exactly to her (K) what you outline above.  I'm glad I had my head screwed on straight, so that I didn't fall for the attempted manipulation by G.  I am no longer friends with G, and K has gone on to do amazing work in poor and ravaged communities in Uganda and Chad.  I consider her a role model and am thankful I stuck with my friend K.

It's harder for adolescents to resist the manipulation, I imagine.

Sure, that would be super cool to get together offline. A good deal of my graduate work was on computer-mediated communication, and although I do engage in some research in that area in my current job, I still would love to scratch that itch a bit more. How does one get into managing online communities for a living? I wanna do that!

I'm not saying that that behavior stops after adolescence. There's just not a lot of research available on that kind of behavior in adults. With kids it can be easier because parents can give permission to monitor the online activities of their children. Adults who volunteer for that sort of thing are a self-selecting group, introducing all kinds of bias. There's still some bias with the adolescent data but it's lessened in comparison. I can completely believe that there are adults out there who do this. There's just not a whole lot of them who have opened up to researchers about it. Again, there are one or two case studies, but not any large-scale studies.

Personally, I subscribe to the belief that life is pretty much just high school over and over again, in different contexts. There are always cliques and crowds and always the "cool kids". College is a continuation of the high school crowds (did some grad work on this one too). At work there are the "IT guys" and the "HR girls" and the "cool" table in the company cafeteria. And the natural division between management and "regular" employees, or union and non-union members. Same sort of thing in apartment buildings or neighborhoods (e.g., that mommy clique vs. the other mommy clique). Most discouraging, I've had friends tell me about their relatives in nursing homes or senior communities and the cliques that spring up there! It never stops. And bullying, gossiping, and other sorts of undermining are just the natural outcome of all these group divisions. It's kind of depressing.

Sorry for the possible thread hijack here.
