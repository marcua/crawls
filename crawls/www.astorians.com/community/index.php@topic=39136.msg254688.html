When I saw this question originally, it made me think of the Time Out NY cover in 1999 that announced that Astoria was the next place to be. It took 16 years for me to meet a couple who lived in Brooklyn at the Ditmars Astoria Bier and Cheese (locally owned), who told me that they had come to Astoria because Lonely Planet had told them that Queens was the #1 destination for 2015. I was barely able to contain my laughter as I gave them directions to SriPraPhai, the next place on their list to visit in Queens.

Williamsburg really isn't a good comparison to Astoria in any sense. Large swathes of Williamsburg were abandoned or gutted, unlike Astoria. Astoria has always had a strong middle class population, even in the '70s, unlike Williamsburg, or even parts of Park Slope (remember when 9th St. in Park Slope was edgy? The abandoned buildings along 6th Avenue? Beyond that -- remember when Pierrepont St. in Brooklyn Heights had SROs??). (Aside - Brooklyn Kitchen, which would seem to be an uber-hipster kind of joint, has a really passionate owner who lives in and is invested in his community. I don't find that sort of commitment to be "insanely dull".)

Like CJ, I'm not an Astoria native, but I've lived here for over 16 years, and have been spending most of my time here for over 23. I'm part of a family that has been here since the 1920s, some of whom have moved away, but many of whom have lived here all along. Most of them are pretty happy with Astoria these days. A few years ago, everyone was bemoaning all of the nail salons and banks, which I get. Now, restaurants have been added to the list. Of course, there are long time stores, more and more, it seems recently, that I miss. Delis, hardware stores, pork stores, and more. There's a picture of me standing on Ditmars and 35th in about 1994 -- I think the only place that hasn't changed is Ferrari cleaners. My mother-in-law remembers King Penny. It may be the only store left on that stretch of Ditmars from 60 years ago.

But, I agree with Eamonn - there are also lots of new places to shop as well, and more interesting places all around - far more than there were 5 or 10 years ago. And I'm with megc -- soundbites can make for interesting reading, but they don't convey what's really happening. I know of quite a few new places that have been opened by Astoria natives or long-time residents who care deeply about their neighborhood. And even some places opened by newcomers who think it's a great neighborhood and want to contribute to it.

But there's this -- Astoria's a real neighborhood. Walking down the street on the weekend, I revel in how many people I see that I know, how many friends I run into. On my block, I know my neighbors. This hasn't lessened as years have passed; it's grown. I meet people on the streets and in restaurants and in shops. It's pretty great. It's not dull, pretentious, or gentrified. It's life.

Total aside - AlexNYC -- a great local place to shop for shoes (and fun accessories) is the Brass Owl, on Ditmars between 36th and 37th. I have absolutely no connection to it aside from being a very happy, satisfied customer.

Once upon a time you could go clothes and shoe shopping on Ditmars and most of the other avenues in Astoria. You still can on Steinway, though by some of the opinions voiced on this forum in other threads, what’s on offer may not suit everyone’s taste.

Who remembers Weber’s, Famous Fashion/Susan Terry, Astoria Bootery, Genovese, Woolworth’s?

Most of these types of stores closed way before, many of you were born. It wasn’t because of rising rents or gentrification it was because most people didn’t like what the mom and pop stores had to offer and started to shop in national chains, in shopping malls, which they could drive to.

So it amazes me when people today complain about not being able to buy or a shirt or pair of shoes (or a pot or pan for that matter) on Ditmars. You could at one time but people didn’t so the stores went out of business.

If you live long enough, and live in one place a long time, you can see changes as part of life. You may not like them but they are a part of life and one can accept and adapt, or not.

For some businesses, the owners are older, ready to retire and find themselves with either no one to take over or sell the business to, so they shut down. Yes, rising rents/costs are a major factor but not always. Some also can’t adapt or compete to a new demographic or today’s retail landscape but often it’s just a case of being tired.

How many of you want to work 12+ hours a day, seven days a week? It ain’t easy.

For anyone who makes a go at it whether it be a coffee shop, restaurant, or salon (because cash businesses are the only ones that can survive) I salute them and can’t understand frankly why people complain about the type of business opening because the alternative would be empty shops.

My family has been in Astoria for a long time. We owned several businesses. My parents, aunts and uncles worked like dogs so that we didn’t have to. None of the businesses were taken over by the next generation. Some were sold and continued on for many years under new owners. All have since ceased operation. I can tell you right now, it wasn’t gentrification, or at least how I define it.
