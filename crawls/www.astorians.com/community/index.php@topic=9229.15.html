I was just going to post the same thing.  If you are talking about a 401k I assume you mean you own mutual funds in it.  The biggest selling point of this type of investing is dollar cost averaging.  Buying small amounts over a long period of time.  Now that the market is down, this is a great time for you.  You are buying fund shares cheaper then they were a few months ago.  You have only lost money now if you sell and put your money in a money market fund, then buy back in when prices go back up.  It's the biggest mistake small investors can make.  In terms of a 401k...forget about it.  Keep putting your money in and cheer when the market tests its highs again.  This assumes you are in a good mix of funds and you are not near retirement.  If you are near retirement or for some other reason need your money soon for other reasons, then that changes things.

I'd be careful with doing that.  The way inflation is going, that might not be worth much by the time the government is done with it.  If the government keeps printing money at will to pay off debts and fund itself and the Fed keeps lowering interest rates, inflation is going to run rampant (more likely stagflation) and the dollar is going to lose value.

I'm a bit more of a pessimist; maybe because I'm shorting quite a few stocks and the market as a whole.  I think we might be in for a pretty painful recession.  The housing market is way over-priced and over-saturated and, in all likelihood, will not be even beginning to recover until sometime in 2009.  This means people will be making payments, that are still going up as rates reset, on homes that aren't worth what they're paying for them. 

Consumers are more in debt than ever and having to pay even more for their necessities (groceries, gas, utilities, etc.), while the seemingly unending flow of cheap credit is drying up.  No longer can they tap their "home ATMs" for more cash and, even the ubiquitous credit card offers, are starting to dry up as delinquencies in credit card payments increase.  What's going to be more important; paying for food, gas, and healthcare, or paying credit card bills and mortgages on a house that is now worth 10+% less (and still dropping)?

Even in the seemingly invincible microcosm of New York City, things won't be pretty.  The housing crunch has been abated somewhat by foreign investors, a still reasonably healthy job market, and one other major fact: New York State has the longest foreclosure time period of ANY state in the country.  It takes, on average, 450 days for foreclosure in New York State, compared to an average of 120 days in the "troubled" states we hear about (FL, CA, NV, etc.) and 300 days in the second longest state of Illinois (http://www.mortgage-investments.com/borrow-money/foreclosure-laws.htm). 

As Wall Street bonuses dry up, foreclosures begin rising, and companies begin downsizing in a recession, things could really get ugly here, too.

Another concern is the astronomical growth that China has undergone over the last year.  Will it be sustained?  They have a very serious bubble, so much so that there are kiosks installed everywhere at which anyone can invest their money in the stock market.  People that have no idea about stocks just randomly buying because the market has gone up so drastically.  But, there's a reason behind this: The Chinese can't SHORT (bet on something to drop and making money if/when it does) their market.  It's not even available.  So, they have no interest in allowing the market to go down, especially before the Olympics come to Beijing and they get their chance to impress the world with their growth.  After that, I think we might see things begin to hit the rocks there.  If they go there, they'll spread rapidly.

But, those are just my opinions and I am no expert.

However, there is a caveat.  Investments, such as 401ks, IRAs, and any other retirement/long term plan, are just that; LONG TERM.  While it might be rather painful to watch it and, what you consider now, your future lose value, there are two important things to remember:

1. The market has increased thousands of times over since 1900.  The direction of the market is up and, while there might be a few months/weeks/years of pain, the market has always recovered and went on to new highs.  Even during the crash of 1929 and subsequent depression, stocks rose dramatically above that level.  It took 20 years, but it still occurred. 

2. (Most important) Because you are investing over a long term and have many years till you are going to be using the money in your 401k, you WANT the market to go down.  When the market goes down, you are getting more for your money.  That same amount you have always aside monthly from your earnings is now buying MORE shares than it was when the market was up.  It's called Dollar Cost Averaging and, in down markets, is a great thing. 

So, don't drive yourself nuts worrying about where your 401ks are now, because it'll only make you crazy.  Personally, I think we'll see the market drop down quite a bit more over the next year or two.  If that's the case, your balances are only going to shrink more.  BUT, remember that you are also adding MORE to your position each month at a lower price, which lowers your overall buy-in average, and only means that you'll make even more money when the market goes back up.

If you're not currently investing in the market and you're considering it, holding off might not be a bad idea.  It's never good to try to "pick a bottom" and invest when you think something has gotten "cheap," because, inevitably, it will drop further right after you by.  I think it's a stock investment axiom. 

Much like the subway axiom: Whichever train you are waiting for, two will come that are going the other direction or pass the station going express before the one you need shows up.

No problem, Daisy.  While investing can be very volatile in the short term, it has been an absolute boon for the long term.  The earlier you start in life, the better you are...EXPONENTIALLY.  If you kept all returns the same (ie, 8% per year every year), you'd have more money when you retire at 65 if you were investing for 10 years between age 25-35 than you would investing the same amount of money each year between 35-65.  So, if you invested 2k per year, you would invest 20k total between 25 and 35 and, when you're 65, have more money than if you invested 2k per year between 35-65, which would be 60k total invested.  Time and, through time, compounding interested (look that up if you don't know what it is) will provide you with MUCH more more money.

Another example: I have a guy that I talk stocks with that invested $300 (in 1967 dollars, so factor in inflation) in what's called a Dividend Reinvestment Plan (look that one up, too) with General Mills (the cereal company) in 1967.  General Mills isn't a big growth stock, but just the time and dividend reinvestments over a 40 year period turned his $300 (again, in 1967 dollars)       into $160,000 today. 

Now, growth in the stock market was pretty enormous over that time and it MIGHT not be quite so large over the next 40 years, but still...shows how worth it even simple investing can be.

Good luck with your research and post up any more questions that you may have.
