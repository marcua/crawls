The problem is not so much getting the dog exhausted, but having the dog get dehydrated . . . even in the winter. If a dog is on leash, hydrate as soon as the dog slows, or in the case of a dog not obedience trained, as soon as the dog stops pulling. Most dogs will tell you when they are exhausted by by either stopping or laying down and panting. Once the panting begins the dehydration is in full swing.

As far as tempered pads are concerned, most smaller breeds tend to have non-leathery pads which are not conducive to long hikes on rough terrain. As part of normal grooming, small dogs have softening lotion/salve applied to their pads to keep them comfortable. When the pads begin to crack—get leathery—they are said to be tempered. For large dogs this is to be expected. For small dogs this should be done very gradually to prevent discomfort for the dog.

Please understand, dogs are not hikers. Owners should not assume that what they like to do, their dogs will also like. They can usually have a good time on a comfortable terrain for about an hour at a walking speed. Depending on the age and physical condition of a dog, they can also be good for running up to 20–30 minutes at a clip. Domesticated dogs, in particular, can find longer times exhausting, but are obedient or submissive enough to continue in the face of their discomfort to please their owner.

Before you take any dog into a wooded area, please check with your vet to be certain that your dog has all their vaccinations and, if you plan on the dog being off leash, that the dog will stop on a dime if you call him to either STOP, STAY or COME. Remember that if you are allowed on a trail with your dog, then others are also. They may not have the most friendly dog and you need to be certain that your dog is protected by understanding their commands. I generally recommend a short obedience reinforcement training prior to trail hikes.
