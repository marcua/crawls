Quote from: Billz1981 on January 09, 2008, 03:58:48 PM

I feel like there should be some sort of moral cost versus moral benefit calculus, which I can't put my finger on how to define.  Wantonly hurting people is out of the question, but we do need to get information from terror suspects because it saves lives.

The problem of using waterboarding or other methods of torture is that it gets people to offer false information in order to get the torture to stop, thereby undermining the reason for using it in the first place. People's bodies can only withstand so much, and even less one's mind under that kind of extreme stress and torture. Even if the "terrorist" means to tell actual valuable information at that point, their memory is not reliable.

There is an actual reason why the Supreme Court banned the use of third degree interrogation tactics (those involving physical "persuasion") - it makes it more likely that innocent people will confess to things they did not do as well as negatively impacting the reliability of actual perpetrators' memory. It just doesn't make sense to use these kinds of tactics when they reduce the diagnosticity of whether the information we receive is good or bad.
