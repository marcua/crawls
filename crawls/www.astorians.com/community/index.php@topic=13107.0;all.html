Oh, I read it.  The first one, anyway, that was all I cared to read.

I'm an avid reader of young adult fantasy, because there is some high quality young adult literature coming out these days (in addition to Rowling, I'm a big fan of YA authors like Scott Westerfeld, Cinda Chima, Francesca Lia Block, and in non-fantasy, Sarah Dessen).

Twilight I put in the same category as the Christopher Paolini books - very little craft, and I could see exactly where the story is the author's personal escapist fantasy rather than a well-formed tale, which makes me very uncomfortable as a reader. 

But obviously a lot of people like the books - I just hate to see people reading this and thinking that's the current state of young adult fantasy post-Potter, when there's so much great stuff out there beyond it.  :)

the fact that you did read the book makes me respect your original comment a whole lot more.  as you can probably imagine, there are a lot of people out there who would make a judgement call on the books based on the movie trailer.  

as with everything else, it's all about personal taste.  for example, i also like the christopher paolini books (as well as harry potter, the sisterhood of traveling pants, and the more "childish" series of unfortunate events and spiderwick chronicles...just to name a few).  as for the twilight series, contrary to you, i did feel that the book was going somewhere, and for me, it made a very comfortable read.  what unnerved me (simply because i NEVER expected it when i picked up the first book) was that i did end up getting so involved with wondering what was going to happen next.  i simply couldn't get enough, and when i finished book four, i wanted to more.  when i couldn't because it was the last one, i wanted to read the whole series again (i didn't though).  but again, to each their own, right?!

as for paolini and meyer, when they wrote their first books, they were rather "young."  paolini was only 15 when he wrote eragon, and meyer, although an adult, wrote twilight on what sounds like a whim (she will even admit in interviews i've read, that she doesn't consider herself a "writer").

when there are comparisons to harry potter and the twilight series, the critics are referring to the passion that the fans have for the series, not so much the writing or the storyline.  and talk to anyone who loves these books...there is certainly a "passion" for it.

oh i didn't see it this way at all.  actually bella turns out to be a VERY strong character as the series progresses.

remember, she's a teenager, and i think her character (especially in the first book) is an accurate portrayal of what's it's like to be a teen girl, in a new town/high school.  there aren't too many young girls out there with the confidence of an adult woman.  if anything, buffy is the fictional character in that sense.  you can't really think of it from the perspective of a mature woman, but from the way it might have been before you knew any better.

in the series, bella comes into her own (as girls do in real life), and becomes the strongest of all the characters.

plus, edward is her first love, and she is his.  think back to your first love...it's all encompassing.  i think this is what made these books for me.  it's like reading a modern, vampire (teen) version of a jane austen book.  <swoooon>
