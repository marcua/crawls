Title: Queens and Brooklyn comfort food
Post by: astoristani on August 03, 2014, 10:10:03 PM
I find it so amusing that Brooklynites and Astorians alike are willing to pay a huge premium for "comfort food" at their local hip eatery.Comfort tends to be the catch phrase for food that the poorer classes eat at an exaggerated price.
My particular restaurant scam is is Southern dish of Biscuits and Sausage gravy that is so popular down in Williamsburg and seemingly now here in Astoria.
As a an ethnic Hillbilly on my mother's side (believe it or not, I'm a WV Hatfield), I've been eating this dish since I could dunk a biscuit into gravy and preparing it since I could turn on a stove.
This mystical dish, the storied breakfast meal of Hipsters and Yuppies alike is an extremely easy and very inexpensive dish to prepare. It's Hillbilly soul food. My grandmother could feed an army for the price of a B&SG entree either here in Astoria or Brooklyn.
Borough Nosh features the dish for about $6.00 but let's take a look at the price of the ingredients before I share the recipe with you.


Ingredients:
!lb pork sausage 3.99 at any supermarket
2 cups (1 pint) of milk $1.00 at Deals
A few table spoons of Flour to thicken negligible
Salt and pepper negligible


Total price of gravy is about $6.00
Divided into six portions: Total cost $1.00 per portion.


Now, let's be generous with the price of biscuits.
4 for a dollar seems to be about right.


The total price of a portion of this extremely exotic comfort food is $1.25. You might say that the mark up (no pun intended that you are an easy mark) is alright because it is such a difficult dish to make. It's not worth my time.


See Ingredients above:
  • [size=78%]Cook the sausage and render the fat (discard if you are politically correct). While frying the sausage use the spatula to to chop the sausage into smaller and smaller pieces.[/size]
  • [/size][size=78%]Once the fat is rendered and the sausage is shredded add the milk[/size]
  • [/size][size=78%]Increase the [/size]heat [size=78%]
  • [/size]Add[size=78%] flour as need to thicken the milk[/size][size=78%]
  • [/size][size=78%]Bake your biscuits and you're done (Grands or Jiffy Mix)[/size][size=78%]
[/size]Six home-made servings for your own brunch vs. one bad restaurant experience.[size=78%]

Honestly, I would say the rise in upscale "comfort food" restaurants stems not from demographics, but rather from a greater societal emphasis on healthy eating. Healthy eating shopping and recipes are so ubiquitous that people who are cooking for themselves and seeing what actually goes into a dish they are less likely to amp up the oil, cream, butter, lard, etc.  I would say this is particularly true of NYC in the past 10 years or so with Bloomberg's personal crusade against fat and sugar. When you go out, however, you want to have something you wouldn't make for yourself that is nostalgic and comforting. You can also turn a blind eye to the mountains of cheese and butter that go into it. Hence the success of such restaurants. It's not about the "hipsters" or the "1%", it's a shift in our culture's relationship to food.
