I've been lurking a few weeks now, finally working up the nerve to post.  :mrgreen: I graduated from college this year and have been looking for work in the city pretty much all summer. I'd say 80% of the stuff on craig's list is pretty crappy.  Alot if it is what daisy said: bait and switch.  But there are some gems out there and I found my apartment through it. 

Since there are thousands of folks looking at craig's list and only a few good listings, its my suggestion that you are all ready when you go and look at a place.  I'm talking credit checks, ID, deposit in hand, even references.  I lost out on a great loft in Greenpoint because I waited a day to get back to the landlord (luckily, I learned my lesson and scored my place).  Also, try and call and get as much info as you can out of the person.  Landlords/brokers who have a good place on their hands are usually only too happy to give away details, because the place can sell itself.  Its the more cryptic ones which have something to hide.

I've found that roommate situations on Craigslist are mostly legit (I searched 2 years ago), but if you're looking to live in Astoria, give yourself a lot of time to find a place and meet with people - most people who are looking for roommates are meeting with a bunch (I've heard as many as 10-15) of other people as well. I set it up so I had at least an appointment or two on evenings when I didn't have to work late (it worked out that I would go out to apts 1-2 times a week). You may get lucky and find a place right away, but don't get frustrated if it takes a few visits with people.

I was actually offered a couple of rooms in other places before I found my current place, but I didn't take them because I didn't feel like I "jived" well with the roommate, or the place was icky or whatever, you know? But I had the luxury of time (about 2.5 months between when I started looking and the absolute last day that I definitely had to be moved in). I ended up finding my place about 1.5 months in, and I'm still here, over two years later.

Go with your gut when it comes to the ads - if its super short (like one line) and just gives a phone number, I didn't bother.

Oh, when responding to ads, tell the person a bit about yourself, esp if they've taken the time to describe the apt and themselves a bit. I have a new roommate now, so I've been on the other side of this, so I can't tell you how much I hated when I got one-line responses like "hi, I'll come over, what time?" after I had taken the time to write a fairly detailed ad and posted pictures. Plus, if you take the time to introduce yourself (you don't need a novel, but a few lines), it's more likely that they'll invite you over to see the room. (I don't think this will be a problem for you based on your original post.)
