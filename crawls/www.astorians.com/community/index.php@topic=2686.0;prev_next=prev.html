I've always found that any place worth returning to, is well worth a letter, or making a phone call to the owner describing the problem encountered.

Here's just a couple examples from the past year:

Eighteen of us (old hags) made reservations and had a 60th birthday celebration for one of our clique at Uncle Jacks' Steak House. The wait staff was miserable to us. Everything seemed to be a problem. We asked for a family style meal. They served everything on individual plates making it difficult to pass around the food. When I made mention of our request, I got toxic attitude. Someone like that can really ruin a fun time. The following week I wrote a letter describing the entire situation. I named names, dates, and even dollar amounts. I got a phone call a few days later inviting the birthday girl and myself to a free meal, and the assurance that our service would be impeccable.

I generally shop at Associated on 21st Street. When I have a big order I have it delivered. Twice in a row the delivery person plopped the bags down on the floor *after* I asked him to please put them all up on the counters in the kitchen. Each time I had to strain my back to pick them up and I had to chase one or both of my dogs around the house after they stole something from one of the bags. I am a fairly decent tipper, but this time I really got pissed. I just gave him a couple of bucks and showed him out the door. I called the owner/manager. I explained the situation to him. I told him that I was standing there on 2 forearm crutches and his delivery person arrogantly defied my simple request. I politely told him that if it happens again I would take my business elsewhere, as Astoria provides me many other options for grocery shopping especially since I have a car. My next order was placed on my counters by a different delivery person who smiled and said thank you when I tipped him.

My point is this: most places survive on word-of-mouth and repeat customers. If they have a problem serious enough to keep regulars from returning, management recognizes that something needs to be done. If they don't know there is a problem—it can never be resolved.
