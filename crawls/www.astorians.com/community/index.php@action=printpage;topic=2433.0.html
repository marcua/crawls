Title: Re: Books, bookstores, book-swaps, and books!
Post by: enigmacat on July 05, 2006, 10:40:59 AM
Instructions for a book swap:

1. Bring books that either you want to give away or really want to share. You can request that a book be returned, but it's best not to swap the signed first edition of "The Sun Also Rises" which is a family heirloom. If you can't replace it, or buying a new one would be a hardship, better not swap it. OTOH, if you want to share a great book and want to get it back, exchange phone numbers and email addresses with the swappee and make sure they know you want it back. Mark it clearly with your name and contact info.

My personal preference is to bring books prepared to release them into the great unknown and request only that the swappee enjoy them and pass them along when finished. However, sometimes there's a book so great you just have to share it and so great you just don't want to give it up forever. That's understandable :)

2. Bring 2 or 3 books (or more, if you want).

3. Unwanted books can be donated to Seaburn books. (if no one takes them and the owner doesn't want to take them home again).

That's all I can think of. I think there will just be a few of us, so this can be relaxed and informal.

Title: for all you LOTR fans
Post by: enigmacat on July 19, 2006, 10:00:34 AM
from today's Writer's Almanac:

It was on this day in 1954 that the first part of the Lord of the Rings trilogy was published—The Fellowship of the Ring. Seventeen years had passed since the publication of The Hobbit (1937), to which The Fellowship of the Ring was a sequel. The Hobbit had gotten a great review in The Times Literary Supplement, and it went on to become a best-seller. So J.R.R. Tolkien (books by this author) began working on a sequel, about the nephew of the hobbit Bilbo, the nephew being named Frodo. He decided that the story would center on the magical ring, which hadn't been an important part of The Hobbit.

Tolkien spent the next seventeen years working on The Lord of the Rings. He was well into his first draft by the time World War II broke out in 1939. The book became more complicated as Tolkien went along, and it was taking much longer to finish than he had planned. He went through long stretches where he didn't write anything and considered giving the project up altogether. He wanted to make sure all of the details about the geography, language, and mythology of Middle Earth were consistent. He made elaborate charts to keep track of the events of his story, showing dates, days of the week, the direction of the wind, and the phases of the moon.

Finally, in the fall of 1949, Tolkien finished writing The Lord of the Rings. He typed the final copy out himself, sitting on a bed in his attic, balancing the typewriter on his lap, and tapping it out with two fingers.

The Lord of the Rings turned out to be more than half a million words long. Tolkien wanted to publish it in one volume, his publisher wanted to divide it into three volumes and so the first volume, The Fellowship of the Ring, came out on this day in 1954.

Only about three and a half thousand copies were printed, but it turned out to be incredibly popular, and it went through a second printing in just six weeks.

Title: New York Book Fairs & Festivals
Post by: duluoz_cats on August 25, 2006, 10:10:30 AM
Thought folks might be interested in some of these upcoming literary events and festivals (and wouldn't it be nice to have one of our own? ... there is actually one in Queens (http://events.aalbc.com/queensbookfair2006.htm) but it was last weekend ...):

Brooklyn Book Fair (http://www.publishersweekly.com/article/CA6327163.html/)
September 16 at Brooklyn Borough Hall (no direct link on the event, just a PW article about it.)

NY Times Great Read in the Park (http://209.131.117.124/greatread/)
October 15 in Bryant Park. Featuring: Well-known authors and new writers; A children's area with books, readings and live entertainment; panel discussions, book signings and special events; on-site bookstore; used books sale with net proceeds donated to the Brooklyn Public Library, New York Public Library, Queens Library and the Fund for Public Schools.

87th Annual Children's Book Week (http://www.cbcbooks.org)
November 13 - 19: The theme this year is "More Books, Please." From its beginning in 1919, Children's Book Week introduces young people to authors, illustrators, and books in schools, libraries, homes, and bookstores. Sponsored by the Children's Book Council in New York City.

New York Book Fair (http://www.goddard.org/events/bookfair.htm)
The Goddard Riverside New York Book Fair (one of the best book fairs the city has, bar none); Saturday, November 18, 10-6 and Sunday, November 19, 11-6. All the books, CDs, calendars, cards, videos and other gift items sold at the New York Book Fair are generously donated by publishing houses, editors, literary agents and other members of the book industry.All proceeds support Goddard Riverside Community Center, which provide services annually to 16,000 people from Manhattan’s Upper West Side and Harlem. The agency manages 600 units of affordable housing. It advocates for justice and enriches community life with recreational, cultural and arts activities. Core programs reach out to children and youth, homeless people, and older adults.

Small Press Book Fair Events (http://smallpress.org/events/bookfair/2005/events.asp)
The Nineteenth Annual Independent and Small Press Book Fair - December 2 & 3. The Small Press Center is awesome, great independent publishing and interesting authors.
