I'm not a smoker and have never been one in my life--the only time I ever smoked tobacco was as a rite of passage, and once I experienced tobacco rejection I was completely satisfied.  I have enough unhealthy, expensive vices without smoking.

BUT THAT BEING SAID:

I'm with you, Frappe; I'm aghast at this new rating standard.  What will young people watch--legitimately, that is?  How are they going to learn what a good movie is?  I'm no fan of the tobacco habit or the tobacco industry, but the era when people smoked routinely just happened to be the era when moviemaking was at its best.  I'm not saying that one caused the other, but that's just the way it was.

Furthermore, young people should have the opportunity to see the excellent and important movie Good Night and Good Luck.  One of the things I liked about that film, aside from its political content, was George Clooney's sheer refusal to turn a 50's story into a pseudo-2000's story.  For one thing, people smoked in the workplace--that's what most public places were like during the 50's.  It was well known that Edward R. Murrow smoked like a furnace.

Not to mention the sheer ridiculousness of such priorities:

"One can, on film, depict violence, brutal violence--mass murder, torture, beatings--and still walk away with a PG 13 rating.  Isn't murder and this glorification of violence a little more offensive then some guy lighting up a smoke?"

Amen.


What really gets a "yuck" from me, tho, is the scene in It Happened One Night where Clark Gable smoked IN BED!  That can kill you more quickly than a lifetime of mere smoking.
