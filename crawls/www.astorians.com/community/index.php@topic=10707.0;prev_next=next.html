plastic made from corn
« on: May 26, 2008, 01:16:50 PM »
Has anyone looked into this stuff? I thought just the idea of it was kind of strange, that it felt like plastic and yet was compostable, but I figured maybe I don't know science all that well and I should welcome myself to the future. So I started doing a little research on it last night, and I came up with stories about how it's really not compostable in the average person's compost pile. It has to be done in industrial composting facilities where the temperature reaches 140 degrees for ten consecutive days. Also, these facilities generally do not accept household waste for composting. Some of the companies that sell products in corn plastic, like some Wild Oats stores, accept the packaging back to send it off for composting, but most don't. I wonder if anyone's actually tried composting these things and wondered what was going on when the cup was still sitting there in perfect shape after a year.

What's worse is I looked at the bottom of my corn plastic iced coffee cup yesterday to see if it was recyclable, and it had a 7 on it. I'm not finding much info on this, but I take that to mean it's not at the point where it's recyclable either, and the little info I am finding supports that it is not currently recyclable. So the producers of corn plastics advertise their product as being recyclable and biodegradable, but it is really neither for the general public. The only positive I'm finding with this product is that it is easier to make, and they say it doesn't leach toxins into food like traditional plastic does. That's nice, but it's still meant to be disposable and it creates waste that doesn't simply go away, which is the problem with our use of plastic and why people want an easily biodegradable product to begin with.

Well, it does seem that the corn-based products are still better than the traditional ones in that they use less energy to produce and don't do the chemical leeching. Plus, when they do finally break down by whatever method, they (supposedly) don't leave whatever toxins behind that the other stuff does. I'm just concerned that people are going to go around guilt-free throwing out the corn plastic, thinking it's going to decompose in a timely manner, and then we'll just end up with landfills filled with this stuff instead of the other stuff. I went into Fresh Start today and noticed the trash can was filled with these corn plastic cups from whatever smoothies people were drinking out of them. I'm sure Freezepeach's trash cans look the same. I know they mean well, so it's kind of sad.

What's worse will be if this results in yet more unnecessary packaging. The problem is not what we package things in but that we go through so much disposable packaging altogether. This may be the conspiracy theorist coming out in me, but I think this is just a way to get corn into more things we don't need it to be in. As if someone was like "Need a substitute for something? Let's see if we can make that out of corn."

Quote from: Handyman on May 26, 2008, 06:31:21 PM
To the best of my knowledge, NY only recycles numbers 1 and 2.

That's what it was when they first started recycling, but at some point it changed and they no longer refer to the numbers, but rather the shape of the plastic container.  I know this because we got a ticket for recycling something that had a 1 or a 2, but was the "wrong shape".  I fought the ticket, arguing that the regulations were unclear, and got it dismissed, but I haven't seen any clarification on it.

Here is the Sanitation Department's rationale for the reason they don't rely on the numbers any more.

I think the idea behind using corn for plastic is that you don't need to recycle it because it's biodegradable and from a renewable resource.  It's good on the front end because it is not made from petroleum.  However, if you look into it, it's not as good on the back end.  It turns out that it is biodegradable -- but only in specially designed facilities.  If you throw it into an ordinary landfill, they think it will take as long as a regular plastic cup to degrade. Corn Plastic to the Rescue (Smithsonian magazine - some background);  recent critique of bio-plastics from The Guardian.

There's a good article about sustainable packaging in the May 2008 issue of "Acres USA" (a magazine I highly recommend if you're interested in farming, government involvement in farming, farm politics, etc).

It says:

"Biodegradable plastic will degrade due to the action of bacteria, fungi, etc, but there is no set time period, and the final degradation can leave some toxic residue.
"Compostables adhere to a higher standard. These products will biodegrade 60 percent in a commercial composting facility within 90-180 days and ultimately leave no toxic residue. Look for an American Society for Testing & Materials (ASTM) 6400 certification on the materials to be sure that 'compostable' is not just a vendor's marketing claim."
