I'm not sure which way I go on this bag check issue.  As most of you who are regulars here know, I am somewhat conservative, and I strongly support law enforcement.  However, as a conservative, I am an ardent defender of individual liberties.  And I am not a big fan of government intrusion into our lives.

What people miss is that the cops are not literally trying to catch someone in the act of holding explosives.  They're creating a disruption and monitoring the behavioral cues of people in the area of the check point.  Also, they make every effort to be as noninvasive as possible.  I have been checked I believe twice since the program started.  The most recent time, the cop ran a swab over the outside of my bag and ran it through a machine to check for the presence of explosives.

While searching through a bag might be a violation of my civil liberties, swabbing my bag for explosives is not.  There is no defensible reason why I would have been handling explosives without a good explanation.  Being swabbed slowed me down, which was an inconvenience, but convenience is not a civil liberty.  Also, as has been said above, there is a real demonstrated security rationale for such a program.

I am surprised that no one on this site has commented (to my knowledge) on Operation: Lucky Bag.  (Which you can read about here: http://www.villagevoice.com/news/0751,gardiner,78677,2.html)  Again, I am a big NYPD fan, but even I see this program as a civil liberties problem.  This is a much more clear cut violation of civil liberties than the bag search program.  Though I am still undecided on that one.

I'm not saying this to be sarcastic, but I wish that those of you who defended personal liberty were also interested in the second amendment.  That one tends to get lost here, and we are the poorer for it.

Re: Bag Checks on the Subway
« Reply #20 on: December 20, 2007, 04:02:13 AM »
Easy: because *every* passenger boarding the plane is being checked, for starters, and also because there's *no other way* to board the plane.  Same with a concert or sporting event.  Everyone entering the facility is being checked, and all the ways to enter that facility are secured in that same way.  There's no picking and choosing.  It's wildly different from random checkpoints in the subway, where someone who is hell bent on causing harm can easily avoid the checkpoints and walk to another entrance or another station with no security checkpoint.

Ethically I don't think it's bogus, and I never said it was ethically bogus.  My position has been, and is, that it is not an effective deterrent because it is so easy to bypass, and that as a result, the amount of law enforcement resources that are dedicated to it by the police is probably better used in other ways.  Instead of sitting around and sorting through people's backpacks, those officers could be on active patrol instead, and actually serve as additional eyes and ears against crime of any kind.  I think that would be far more effective, and if you want to bring intrusion into people's lives into this, yes, that would be less intrusive as well.

See, this is not a black and white issue.  Being against random checks doesn't mean you're against law enforcement.  In my case, I feel it's ineffective law enforcement and a very ineffective use of resources, that just merely serves as "reassurance" to the public that something is being done to protect them, even if the benefits that arise from it are few, if any, and even if they are not really any more secure as a result of those measures.
