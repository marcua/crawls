Yuppies in Astoria?
« on: August 06, 2006, 09:43:17 PM »
I find that when I tell people I live in Astoria, I often get the reaction "Oh that's where all the yuppies are moving."  I have been here 6 years, and I haven't really noticed many "yuppies."  I find Astoria to be a very diverse place.  There are many rent stablized apts and there are also expensive apts.  From what I can tell, many people in the expensive apts actually have roomates.  I see mostly working class people and some middle class people.  I just haven't seen many yuppies.  I wonder why this perception exists about Astoria.   Have you heard this before?  Do you agree or disagree.  If you disagree like me, why do you think this perception about yuppies in Astoria exists?

They've been saying Astoria's up and coming, trendy, etc.  for about 5/6 years now.  Astoria's been stubborn though, and thankfully hasn't turned into another Williamsburg yet.  I like the diversity here, and the home-town feel, and I hope it stays that way for a long time to come.    For a long time even the rents held on, but that's been changing.  The last 2 years have seen a significant increase in rents, and the arrival of chain restaurants and stores.  I assume that many locals looking to open their own  businesses will find it hard to afford the rents, which is probably why the chains so easily get the storefronts.  I do like that astoria is more popular now, i just wish that this whole yuppification/gentrification/whatever process didn't push everyone else out, because usually the rents and prices in the area for food/etc. just rise too much and things just aren't affordable anymore.   I specifically cannot afford Brooklyn which is why I've always stayed on in Astoria.  I've come to love it though and can't imagine leaving now.

And prime example of rising prices:  Beer Garden is now officially $14 per pitcher, and overly crowded every weekend.   I remember when those pitchers were $10, and when it was actually a relaxing way to spend your evening.  We're apparently trendy enough for non-Astoria folk to come here on a regular basis.

I think the upward trend is really obvious east of Steinway and Broadway.  In the past few years more bars and restaurants have opened.  Some are obviously geared towards certain communities (like Albona is frequented by some flavor of former Yugoslavian) but others seems geared towards new residents such as Le Sans Souci (a French Bistro w/ Garden) or Thai Chili, now under it's second or third management and doing well.  Or how about the quite popular Dandanna Egyptian Cafe right on my block!  And accross the street the Greek Social club has been replaced by an Acupuncture Clinic.

The tenants in my building have gotten much younger too.  When my neighbors who had been in their place since the early '60s moved to the Long Island I tried to convince them to keep quiet and let me pay their rent ($300 for a 2 bedroom!)  They didn't go for it & the place was gutted, turned into a 3 bedroom (and let me tell you, those bedrooms are tiny) and rents for about $1700 now.  last time around the Leases all had riders stating that preferential rent would now longer be given.

I don't think the upward pressure is that severe yet though.  I posted before about an attempt to raise rent way up in one unit.  It turns out the increase was the difference between preferential and the 'rent stabilized' rent.  When the tenant decided they weren't having the rent increase they went ahead and found something in Spanish Harlem and gave notice.  In the meantime the owner has come back with offers that split the difference.  So there is still some hope!

I am rent controlled. Something seems wrong to me about your numbers. Let's say your neighbors moved into this 2 bedroom apartment in 1960 and that their original rent was $50. That's fair, isn't it? In 1970 when rent stabilization was approved, rent control increases were mandated at 7.5% per year. Some years, however, it rose to 9% and last year 8.5% and then 13%—that's per year—no lease. How could their rent have only been $300? Not possible. The landlord cannot just raise the rent of a newly registered rent stabilized apartment to anything he wants. There is a formula that *must* be proven with receipts for the work done. One other thing that rent stabilized tenants should be aware of, *no* rent can ever end in an exact dollar amount. The nature of the formula will always have cents. In June 1997, Governor George Pataki, as a part of his efforts to destroy rent regulation, forced changes that gave landlords large vacancy bonuses. Provisions of his Rent Regulation Reform Act of 1997 allow the rents of apartments to rise by a statutory percentage.

My friend's mother lives in a rent-controlled (not rent-stabilised) apartment in Clinton Hill, and has been there since 1968.  Her rent is $150/month.

According to the NYC Rent Guidelines Board, the maximum change per every two years for a rent-controlled apartment is still 7.5%, according to the Fact Sheet #1: Rent Control and Rent Stabilization; however, that 7.5% raise is only up to the Maximum Base Rent limit.  The Maximum Base Rent Program that is used to calculate rent-controlled rents is explained in Fact Sheet #22.

In general, that website has a lot of good information, although it can be a bit tricky to navigate.  There's even a stabilised building list by zip code, although the site warns it is not complete.
