Restocking fridge after blackout
« on: August 16, 2006, 10:08:40 AM »
This was in the Splendid Table newsletter today, and I thought you guys might be interested.

We received a listener question recently that I answered in my newspaper column, Ask the Splendid Table®, but I thought it would be handy for newsletter readers, too.

The listener lives in St. Louis, where a recent storm knocked out power for several days. He and his wife had to pitch nearly everything in their refrigerator. Aside from the usual foods, they wanted to restock with "new and exceptional things" and asked for ideas. These were my thoughts:

Organic whole milk yogurt stands in for sour and heavy creams in soups, dips and dressings, and is lush on its own.
Premium Japanese soy classes up take-out sushi and rubs for grilled foods (refrigeration keeps the sauce in excellent shape for a year).
Cultured butter tastes richer than regular butter.
Cold-pressed almond and walnut oils make everyday salads taste new, but they must be kept cold once opened.
Thai curry pastes (green and red) need a short simmer in coconut milk with your choice of chicken or fish, vegetables, a shot of lime or tamarind, and a finish of fresh herbs for a big bowl of eye-opening, sinus-clearing stew.
A brigade of fresh basil, rosemary, coriander, sage, thyme and marjoram mean you are never at a loss for a fast, fresh tasting sauté. Pitted Kalamata olives could go into those sautés along with zest from an orange, lemon or lime.
There always seems to be that citrus triumvirate of lemons, limes and oranges in the vegetable drawer. If they don't go into savory dishes, they end up squeezed into icy pitchers for summer coolers. And fresh lime juice, tequila and sugar shake into the lazy man's very fine Margarita.
I keep whole-grain flat breads (they keep longer in the refrigerator) like Caribbean-inspired lentil/legume roti and whole wheat tortillas for quesadillas, bastardized pizza, and for cutting up into bite-sized pieces and toasting with garlic and olive oil. Here is where that whole-milk yogurt with fresh coriander and a dash of Thai curry paste enter the picture as a topping for the perfect small bites with drinks.
Vastly, consider the foods which are meals unto themselves, as in a collection of keeping cheeses to serve as a main course with a few interesting condiments and a salad. You could stick to non-fragile American artisan cheeses and pick from Grafton cheddars, Vella Dry Jack, vacuum-packed fresh goat cheese, California's blue Humboldt Fog, Van Gogh from Roth Kase, Wisconsin's Mt. Pleasant cheese, and that state's Carr Valley cheeses. Group together a trio, flank them with condiments of dried fruits, salted nuts, or spiced chutneys and bread for a company supper.
I should add that nearly all the jars of condiments in the cupboard, once opened, end up in the refrigerator—so tamarind concentrate, Spanish pequillo peppers, Chinese Hoisin sauce, Romanian eggplant relish, Italian mustard fruits, and tapenade from France nearly crowd out the fresh items coming in every week.
Take your pick of what sounds new and interesting. But beware—you, too, will join the ranks of those who've not seen the backs of their refrigerators since the Clinton administration.
