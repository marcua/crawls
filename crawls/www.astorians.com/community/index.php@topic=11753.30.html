Well I asked what was the goal that one wishes to attain by making English the official language and I didn't get an answer.  That's cool and I really don't expect an answer, because I have never gotten one other then "hey man, we live in America.  We all should speak English." 

1.  I thought it did make a difference to you when it was crossed out.
2.  People push for laws when a group or the establishment feels threatened.  Example: America existed for years without laws which defined the chromosome make up of two folks who wanted to get married, but now with the logical path America took after the sexual revolution of the 60's and with homosexuals increasingly unwilling to take the short legal shrift, a particular segment of America feels threatened and therefore is pushing for a law.  Language laws are pushed when a particular culture feels threatened.  In some cases this can be a very real threat and in other cases, like ours, the threat is a phantom of paranoia and media hype.  I mean, c'mon, really, to your last blurb about being coherent and understanding each other, do you really feel that a monoglot anglophone would have have trouble getting around, even an extremely diverse neighborhood like Astoria?  Do you honestly think that for one second, that the making English our national language will improve the understanding and coherency between the groups in this country.  Whites and blacks both speak English and have almost no understanding or coherency between the two.  I'd rather bring those two groups together rather then divide folks with a national measure that can't hope to accomplish anything aside from being divisive and get a few votes for the panderers.

3.  In some instances, a culture is strongly expressed in the the speaking of specific language (similarly, by the practice of a specific religion).  In Quebec, the act of speaking French flexes the muscles of a specific culture with a specific history.  In the mish-mash of the Caucuses, Balkans and central Africa, speaking a language and having it taught in the schools and used in the courts denotes the people of an area.  This can be very important when the folks from the UN come in and figure out a national boundry line.  Even here in Astoria, the abililty to speak, read and write in Greek goes a long way in identifying one's greekness.  Even more interesting were the riots which took place as the demotiki replaced the karthevousa (too long to explain).  I can really go on all day with this:  Basque in Spain/France, Nahualta (spelling?) in Mexico, Dutch/French in Belguim.  Even the nearly identical languages of Serb and Croat, which differs only in the scripts used, show how important language is to the culture (and national identity) to these folks.

America, on the other hand, does not have this issue.  While English does operate as a defacto national language, one does not identify speaking English with being American here.  Our heritiage and culture and our national boders are not dependent on such.  We do not have breakaway republics or autonomous regions.  Even the part which does not speak English, Puerto-Rico, would crap themselves if they were ever given their independence.  On the contrary, America is all about taking in folks from other lands and accepting them for who they are, especially in New York, especially in Queens and especially in Astoria!  Folks who speak no English, but believe in the American ideal, are Americans.  Abroad, naturally, English is more closely tried to being American, but official language laws have nothing to do with that (unless you'd like to do it Russian style).  American culture, to the chagrin of many folks around the world, is expanding across the globe, into lands which do not speak English at all (though the embattled French have come up with some cute ways to avoid using English languiage roots!)
