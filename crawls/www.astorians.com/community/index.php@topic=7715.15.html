This is not directed towards any specific person or comment made but the point I was trying to make in my previous post was that the number of complaints about ‘development’ seem to far out number and are disproportionate to the actual increase in population leaving me to wonder what exactly is bugging people?

To frame the development discussion strictly in terms of lack of infrastructure as compared to growth just doesn’t quite fit and my feeling is that Merm has a point that it can feel like “people-phobia” at times.

Is it “anydevelopment” or “overdevelopment” that is the problem?  Because for some it seems no amount of growth or change is acceptable and I would like to ask what would you like this city to be – Detroit? Buffalo? St. Louis? – cities which essentially have been depopulated?

New York once teetered on the edge of an abyss and younger or newer resident may find it hard to fathom but once upon a time, one not so long ago, no one wanted to live in this city.

Some of the changes I see in Astoria make me very anxious. It’s as if my history is being erased but at the same time I would much rather see a thriving, dynamic neighbourhood/city with more buildings going up than ones sitting empty and derelict AND at the same time growth that is managed and focussed on the quality.


By the way QueensCrapper – I read and really enjoy your blog and I say keep on complaining. I agree with you on many of the issues but the problem of unregulated basement apartments is not one of overdevelopment it is of illegal development, which to me is quite different and you are right that the city often does nothing.

Thank you 28Grand!  Some great points you made there.  

I tend to define overdevelopment in terms of what was there before and what is there now structurally.  The building that is being promoted we are told is to prepare for "1 million more people" yada yada.  How they determined there will be or should be 1 million more people is something I'd like to know.  From what I understand, the 1961 zoning was done in anticipation of having some ridiculous number like 30 million people by the year 2000.  We fell far short of that number, and I can't imagine what this city would be like if we had that many people living here.

There's not much more space left, which is part of the reason for the recent sewer problem - no place for the ground to do the absorbing.  What I do on QC is research what was there before vs. what is there now.  If I see a one-family or open space was replaced with a 3-family or larger, then I tend to post about it.  

I also tend to post about just really freaking ugly houses.  You can tell what the government's agenda is for your neighborhood by what's being built there.  These days, it's either high rise luxury condos for the upper echelon, or Fedders crap for the lower echelon.  Real quality middle class housing is not being built anymore, although I guess you can make an argument that McMansions fall into that category, where you have a lot of house taking up a little bit of space.  Still, they add to the infrastructure problems of electricity and flooding.

Time Out New York actually recently did a feature on racial views in the city and it revealed that ethnic populations tend to segregate themselves intentionally for a number of reasons and racism among them may actually exceed that of the "Archie Bunker" types QC readers are accused of being.  I've found most people in Queens  really don't care where you're from as long as you don't them any harm.  People warm up to each other eventually.  Of course, if you don't speak English then it's kind of hard for most people to get to know you or for you to accept me into your world, for that matter.  That's just reality.

In my opinion this infrastructure problem is something thats happening around the country.  For the last 30 years or so politicians on both sides of the fence both federal and local have been falling over themselves to get votes by cutting property taxes, tax rebates etc.  And local budgets have compensated by making cuts where they can, including things like roads and bridges, mass transit and power.  Remember the big black out of 2003?  That was because the grid got hit by lightening and the whole system got fried.  And if I remember right, the whole thing was blamed on our aging power system, which hadn't been updated.  Same thing with the Western Queens black out.  The trains are too crowded, I agree.  Well when Koch was mayor the MTA got 200 million a year from the city/state for its operating budget.  Over time its been cut down to 70 million a year.  Thats a lot less, particularly when you figure the dollar isn't worth as much as it was a few decades ago.  Either way our infrastructure is going to need improvements whether or not the hyper development continues.

Certainly guys like ConEd and MTA need to get their act together in many regards, but at the same time the money to maintain and improve the infrastructure needs to be budgeted from somewhere, either in cuts to other parts of the budget, or from property/income taxes or from pricing plans and sale taxes, but at the end of the day, the money comes from us.  And no one wants to pay for anything  (Tax collection is screwed up too and favors the rich now more than ever but that a whole other rant).

As for over development, I moved here from PA almost 7 years ago, not sure if I count as one of "those people" or if I've been here long enough to be one of "them", but I try and be involved in the hood.  You can't just put a moratorium on development, in an area like NYC where people come to live here, all that'll do is drive the rents and apt prices up even more, and the only thing to stabilize it would be folks no longer wanting to live in NYC, which would be bad.  On the other hand, I don't want to see towers blocking the river either.  The city needs to work out a better system, to keep some housing affordable, still allow the developers to make some profit and start to bring the infrastructure up to snuff, none of which really seems to be happening enough.  I don't have an answer for that one, just stating the obvious.
