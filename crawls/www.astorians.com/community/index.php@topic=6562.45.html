Quote from: Harlan on June 08, 2007, 10:03:48 AM
It's not very complicated... The congestion fee is per-day, and any tolls you pay that day are subtracted. So if you drive over the TBB on a Friday afternoon and stay overnight, you pay $4.50 + ($8.00 - $4.50) = $8. If you drive round trip, you pay $9.00 + |$8.00 - $9.00| = $9.00 (vertical bars meaning "but not less than 0"). And you're right about the Hudson crossings. They'll pay $6.00 + ($8.00 - $6.00) = $8.00.

Harlan...sorry for being dense and I admit math talents are sorely lacking but your numbers are screwy.

I get $3.50, not $4.50 when subtracting $4.50 from $8.00.

And for a roundtrip of $9.00 + |$8.00 - $9.00| or $9.00  doesn't make sense (or cents for that matter) because this is the same amount of money paid by drivers now. How will this discourage them from coming into the city?

And why is it fair that drivers from New Jersey would only pay an extra $2 but outer borough drivers would pay an extra $3.50.

The pricing scheme in London is a success because the original zone was relatively small and there was one governmental agency responsible for the it's administration. Plus London is not an island city like New York, which has existing toll structures administered by competing government agencies. It's going to be much, much harder to get going here and I expect much more expensive to operate.

I'm open to the idea of congestion pricing as a way to limit the number of cars coming into the city...but as Astoria residenta, we live on the edge of the zone there's a strong possibility of creating more traffic in our neighborhood. We're already saddled with pollution spewing power plants. We don't need more car pollution as well.

Currently Astoria residents get a lot of thru traffic from outside residents who drive through our hood to take the free QBB instead of the toll Tri-Boro or Midtown Tunnel.  What this does is no longer provide motorists with an incentive to avoid the toll bridges and tunnels because none of them are free.  This also goes for people upstate and from CT that drive through the Bronx avoiding their toll bridges as well.  So there won't be more traffic through Astoria there will be less.  This has already been covered a couple times in this very thread.

And it won't require getting all these agencies involved, the city will set up the system and the money goes into the SMART fund which is used to pay for MTA and DoT transit improvements.  Something interesting Bloomberg said at the hearing today was that the Sec of Transportaion has bascially given him a verbal promise that if NYC puts in place congestion pricing we will get between 400-500 million of this 1.1b one time grant the federal gov't is given to cities that are engineering new ways to reduce auto traffic.  It will take about 280 million to get the plan in place leaving between 120-220 million to spend on transit before the plan goes into effect (plus the mayor has pledged about 225 million before the plan goes into effect on top of that).  So even if this plan doesn't work, doesn't reduce traffic, and doesn't pull in the 600 million gross (I think they quoted operating costs at about 200 million) in the first year they are aticipating, after the 3 year pilot plan is over the city still got the system for free (with fed money) and extra left over for transit.  And I think it will turn a profit anyways.

As for everyone that would like more acurate census figures.  They don't have more recent census figures until the next census.  Every city agency uses this figures as their base, both proponents of congestion pricing and opponents.  I believe the make allowances for changes between then and now, but thats what the government has to work with as an accepted neutral base for stats, any other stats would accused of being biased for the study. Aaaaand most of the statistics came from the 2000 census and a study put together by T.A (which I had linked).  So the numbers are more recent than that anyways.
