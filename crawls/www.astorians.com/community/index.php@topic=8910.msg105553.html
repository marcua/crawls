The only thing that surprised me about the mitchell report was how few impact players were named.  Out of the impact players that were named (the Clemenses, Pettiteses, etc) only Tejada is really an A list player who isn't at the tail end of his career.  It's obvious that more guys were doping than the 88 players that were named, and it's a shame that Bud Selig opted to be a spineless weasel in this instance just like he's been the rest of his tenure as comissioner. 

As far as the numbers, the report says it estimates 30% of the players, which would be around 255. So he got what he believes were 1/3 of them. You also have to keep in mind that Mitchel didn't name any players as using when teams they were on won the World Series. That's why all the Yanks mentioned talk about post-2000 incidents and the BoSox guys are pre-2003/4 guys.

I think this happened for two reasons. First, and most obvious, is that the MLB and player's unions are not likely to be able to reach agreement on any level if all the WS titles end up being contested. Enough records are going to have trouble holding after this report. But second, that Mitchell's attempts to dig up evidence probably found people more willing to dish on guys who aren't playing any more or just plain aren't playing at the top of their game for the best ball park they played for. It's one thing to out Gagne when he's gone and another to trash guys who are currently in the game.

While I wish it would have approached more of the current players, I think it's important to keep in mind that at least this is a first step for the game and hopefully not the last.
