Anyone out there who rents an apartment in a private home...from a landlord...or else who rents in a small old walk-up building?

I am wondering in these types of buildings, what people's norms have been over the years with rent increases.  Obviously the larger and newer buildings are run by property managers/developer types, who are just plain greedy, and jack up the rent every year.  And many of these start out as 'rent stabilized' because the developer got a tax write off (421-a), but eventually all these apts will become market rate.

I'm more curious about the 'average' old-school rentals across Astoria.  If you live in such a building or house, what has the pattern been with increases?  Have you seen any change in the rent increase as Astoria has gotten more popular?

I rent in a small apt bldg. where the LL lives with his family, and he loves to talk about how this is a 'family' building but yet, my strong sense is that he's going to raise the rent every year, 'just because'.  I just received my second renewal lease since I've been here, and just like my first renewal went up $50, to 1800 (from $1750), so too is the rent going up again, this time another $55.

I know Astoria has gotten more popular and so property values have increased, and perhaps property tax, but I can't imagine it's been so much to warrant yearly rent increases, and of almost 5%.  I think it's just pure greed.  So I'm curious how many others in 'old-school' apartments are seeing the same thing.  My strong sense however is that there ARE some old-school homeowners out there who are not greedy, and simply charge reasonable rents, and raise them only when they need to.  (It's  just the brokers who, once they get involved, ruin everything...cuz they inflate the market because of course their commissions % are all based on the price.)

Yipee, just FYI the consumer price index is a number created by the bureau of labor statistics that reflects any increase or decrease in cost of living for a given US geographic area.  It takes into account the prices of many different everyday items.  Many pensions, union contracts and other similar contracts use these values to determine rate adjustments.  Just because your employer didn't give you a raise doesn't mean that your landlord doesn't have the right to ask for one from you (don't worry many, many people are in the same boat as you with stagnant wages over the course of this decade).

One of the problems is that because the neighborhood has become "cool", the tax assessors have been dramatically increasing assessed values (and property taxes) over the past few years on anything bigger than a single family home (which have legal rate increase limits).

Since you live in a rent stabilized apartment, your landlord can't ask for any increase for a one year lease extension and a 2% increase for a 2 year lease extension.  If your landlord tries to charge you more, you can theoretically sue and get triple damages for every dollar you overpaid.

Here are the rate increases as per the NYC Rent Guidelines Board:
http://www1.nyc.gov/nyc-resources/service/2069/new-york-city-rent-increase

It seems that what your landlord is doing is illegal.  It is up to you how much of a fight you want to put up.

I didn't specify in my original post but... while my apt. is rent-stabil, he was not charging me the full Legal (allowable) Rent in the past.  Until now.  Now with my new lease, my rent will be AT the Legal (maximum allowable) Rent for my Rent Stabil apartment.  So from this point onward, indeed, any increases he wants for my renewal leases can only be for any amount determined by the rent board guidelines.
