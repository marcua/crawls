Quote from: jayme on April 25, 2007, 11:14:54 AM
Does anyone know if you can wash sponges? 

By "wash" I assume you mean "throw them in the wash." That, I'm not sure. But you can wash the heck out of cellulose sponges using some white vinegar and then steam-sanitize them the way you would steam broccoli. Just squeeze as much water out of them as you can (don't use dry sponges) and place them in a steamer for ten minutes, allowing them to cool naturally. Then just ring-em out and let em dry.

Quote from: Sambarella on May 02, 2007, 01:05:40 PM
I look forward to the day that consumers become as interested in the environment as in the human factor and labor in the goods they buy. 
[...]
Sometimes I wonder what is taking so long for people to be motivated to change these conditions, and why sometimes the fair labor struggle is overshadowed and regarded as not as important as the environment by consumers?

I don't believe that the two are mutually exclusive, nor do I believe that the majority of consumers are "interested in the environment." Just look at how much waste we produce and how much focus there is on developing products based on "convenience through disposability" (plastic razors, Swiffer dusters or Pledge wipes, for example). I would also say that I think there's been LOTS of progress in monitoring and changing the habits of corporations that have exploited the poor masses for centuries. However, there's obviously a LONG WAY to go before true fairness in the workplace is achieved--if that's even possible in a capitalistic system. Just look at the seemingly endless supply of Chinese peasants flooding the cities looking for manufacturing jobs that pay pennies a day...

I think we're pretty much in agreement, Sambarella. My wife is a social worker and I hear about the labor/imigrant side of things on a daily basis. I also feel that human beings, i.e. "labor," have probably always been seen by big business as a resource (like water or trees) or even as a commodity to be traded. Being in the line of work you are, I imagine you've seen Stephanie Black's film, "Life and Debt" about Jamaica and the free trade zones. I was floored when the local workers complained about poor working conditions and low pay and then the manufacturers (which were producing Haynes underwear at the time) fired them all, locked them out and began importing workers from China. Because the manufacturers had free trade zone status (a requirement for Jamaica being granted an IMF loan) they could only stand at the barbed wire fences that surrounded the factories and watch as busload after busload of Chinese laborers poured in to take their jobs. I guarantee you that those Chinese people were NOT paid a humane wage. Oh, my head hurts... :yuck:
