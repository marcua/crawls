Astorians.com DNS Problems (status updates)
« on: September 07, 2006, 10:06:52 PM »
During Tuesday evening (Sept 5) astorians.com began experiencing trouble with it's domain record which was registered through enom. During the night hours users reported problems getting to astorians using that .com domain, and the problems got worse through the night. What I didn't realize at that time was that somehow the domain name (the "address" that points to the server) had been changed (though at this point we are still looking into how that happened - it may be nothing). Working with my domain registrar and server support on Wednesday we tried to pinpoint the problem, but realized that it was further up the chain, not something we could deal with directly, so we had to contact people that have access to the root domain registry for the entire internet.

By Thursday morning the domain astorians.com had been deleted from the majority of the internet, which is why most of you were unable to reach the site.

It's important to note that the server is just fine, we've experienced no downtime related to the server at all, just the domain name (which basically tells your browser how to find the server) has gotten corrupted somehow.

The technical support at enom are looking into the problem and I hope they'll be able to get us back to normal soon. In the meantime, you can use our astorians.net domain to reach the site (since the server itself is fine).

You can check on the status of the problem yourself by checking to see if the "world" domain servers are recognizing astorians.com yet: Check Astorians.com Domain. You will be looking at the root domain servers, if they are still saying "astorians.com doesn't exist", then you know that they haven't fixed the problem. yet. Until the root servers think we exist astorians.com won't be seen as a valid address.

I'll continue to post updates here for anyone interested in the status of the domain. And I'll try and let people know the root of the problem once we figure out what might have caused the probelm in the first place.

Re: Astorians.com DNS Problems (updated)
« Reply #5 on: September 09, 2006, 01:36:45 PM »
Since it will have been nearly a week before our domain has the possibility of being reactivated I think it's fair for me to pass along what's been going on, and how I believe we have been subject to a very creative (and maybe legal) kind of "Denial of Service" attack. I've learned some really interesting things through this process.

Long version starts here:

On August 24 the moderators and I received a "reported post" notification for this thread. The email came from a person representing the website astoria.org and this person asked for the immediate removal of the thread. I let the individual know that it is not our policy to censor members unless they violate board policy (posting personal contact information, or posting explicit or clearly offensive material). Instead of censor, I encouraged the person to introduce themself in the thread but they declined.

On August 25 I received notification from my domain registrar, sitelutions.com that they had received a complaint from the person representing astoria.org regarding the whois information on my domain astorians.com, and that I should update it to make sure it was accurate per ICANN standards. I updated the information and emailed sitelutions to ask if whois record was within the guidelines as updated, and they said it was. I emailed the person representing astoria.org and let them know I had updated the record and that if they needed to contact me they could do so directly (since they had my contact information). I didn't get a response.

On August 28 I received another notification from my domain registrar, sitelutions..com, that they had received another complaint from the same person again regarding the whois information on the domain astorians.com. Since sitelutions had advised me that my record was ok, I did not make any changes. I emailed the person who made the complaint and advised them that the whois information had already been updated per their request, and asked them to contact me directly if they had any complaint or issue with my domain. I again gave them contact information. I also called the person on their telephone of record and left a message letting them know that they were welcome to contact me directly if they have any problem with my domain. I didn't get a response.

On September 4 I received another notification from my domain registrar, sitelutions.com, that they had received another complaint from the same person, again alledging that my whois information on the domain astorians.com was inaccurate. Sitelutions told this person that if they have an issue with the domain that they should contact me directly and let me know that this third complaint had been made. I again emailed the person, and let them know that the information was indeed accurate, and that I was happy to speak with them should they have a complaint against my domain. I didn't get any response.

On September 6 our domain began experiencing problems resolving to the server when people tried to access the site using astorians.com. I worked most of the day with support trying to figure out the problem, and with sitelutions support to try and determine the cause of the problem. I wasn't able to find the root of the problem this day. And still believed the problem was technical in nature.

September 7: I finally became aware that the problem was originating with the company that sitelutions works with, enom. eNom is the company that manages massive numbers of domains, and companies like sitelutions resells those domains to people like me. Sitelutions submitted a trouble ticket to eNom and advised me to wait for a response. We waited all day - no response.

September 8: After waiting 36 hours for a response from eNom, I decided to contact them directly myself to see what the problem was. The tech that I spoke with on the phone told me that my domain had been suspended because someone had made a complaint about the whois (contact) information for my domain. I explained that the information was indeed accurate and that this was in error. But the tech said there was nothing he could do. I had to email the "whois department" at enom and prove that I was the owner of the domain. Which I immediately did on my own. After waiting several hours for a response I contacted sitelutions and asked them to petition on my behalf. They had me write an email which they passed on to eNom. eNom has so far provided no response whatsoever.

My whois (contact) information on my domain is accurate, and should that person (or eNom) wish to contact me I can still be reached through the contact information on my whois record, through this website, or through any of the emails I have sent to this person directly. I am, and always have been willing to discuss the matter with this person, but I remain committed to not censoring non-explicit, and non-privacy-invading discussion on this board.

Summary:

At this point we are waiting for my letter to be reviewed by eNom, and for them to release the domain back to me so that astorians.com will work once again. The person who has made the complaints has not contacted me directly at any point, though I have repeatedly responded to their complaints, and made myself available to them to resolve their alleged dispute in good faith. Sadly, eNom never contacted me to ask me to validate my identity or contact information before  suspending my domain.

This makes for a very interesting effective way for someone to exploit the trust of a domain registrar to wage a creative DoS attack against any domain. In these days of spammers, fraud, and phishing it seems that some domain registrars will simply suspend your domain and let you deal with the aftermath. Insulated from any consequences by the legal agreement you must enter into by purchasing a domain from them (which says basically that they can do anything they want with your domain at any time for any reason - why waste time "verifying" every complaint?) In this case the easiest way to exploit someone's domain isn't by brute force at all, but just by some creative talking.


Recommendations:

Finally, I should mention a few things to anyone who owns a domain name as this scenario represents an interesting risk to anyone who depends on the reliability of their domain name:

1. Make sure that you keep your information up to date. When this person originally complained it was true that my information had not been updated (though this person knew my contact information anyway, and I updated it immediately). Even if you use your initials for your "name" (as I did), eNom may suspend your domain without notice.

2. Check to see if your domain is registered through eNom. If it is, just know that it appears that in practice their policy to shoot first and ask questions later (or not ask questions at all actually), and that if they suspend your domain they don't seem to be in a hurry to restore it. Your domain can be disabled at any time, for any reason, and you have absolutely no recourse (read their legal agreement). It appears that by simply claiming that you have inaccurate information someone can effectively wage a Denial of Service attack on your website. Even if that complaint is patently false, eNom may still deactivate your domain without contacting you. This may even be legal, though you might be able to do something if you can prove that the complaining party acted in bad faith (unknown). Essentially all it requires is that you socially engineer the domain registrar (eNom) to do the DoS for you!

3. Consider using a service that guarantees your privacy. Registrars (including eNom itself) now have a feature that allows you to hide your private information (name, address, phone etc) from the public whois record. This makes it much harder for an individual to harass you or attempt to bring down your domain by claiming you have inaccurate whois information.

4. Make sure that the email address on your domain registration record is hosted at SOME OTHER DOMAIN (@hotmail, @gmail etc). If eNom does suspend your domain they will require you to contact them from the email address on record. But if they disable your domain guess what, you can't get or send email from there.

Incidently there's a very interesting ongoing disucssion about whois records right now going through various government agencies. Read about it at EPIC. For people like me that don't want their home address in their whois record (which is publicly accessible to everyone: spammers, identity theives, bulk marketers, and the average person) it looks like very promising legislation.

I'll continue to update this thread as more information becomes available. [Discuss]
