Actually. They changed the rules back in the 90's. Now there are only three classes of licences. Technician, then General, then Extra (in that order.)

The Tech licence doesn't require any morse code at all, then the General and Extra both just require 5 words per minute on morse code.

However, with the basic level one license it lets you use quite a few of the frequencies, and play with the small mini ham radios as well as some of the bigger ones. And of course you can participate in any amateur radio events.

The basic license isn't hard to get, just answer 30 questions.

A couple people have suggested that I post a little information about Ham Radio and some more information about getting your licence.

The process is very simple. It entails taking a 35 question test and scoring above 74%. There is a question pool of about 400 questions that they will randomly pull the 35 questions from, so before you take the test you can study all the questions and answers ahead of time. The test costs $12. This is the only fee you pay to enter the world of Amateur Radio.

Ham radio is free. You can use it to make phone calls (like a cell phone,) talk to others locally that you know (other Astorians or New Yorkers,) or contact people around the world. There are no costs involved except purchasing a radio.

There are many different kinds of radios that you can buy. Some of them are less than $50, and some can run you into the thousands. These days they make very small ham radios that are smaller than most cell phones. With these units you can talk up to several hundred miles, you need a bigger radio in your car or at home to talk around the world generally speaking.

Ham's also own several satellites that have been launched into space that you can play with. In other words, from your little radio you can talk to someone in another country using the satellite. This is all free.

The reason ham radio is free is because regular people are the ones that are building, buying, and maintaining the equipment that you are using. If you make a phone call on your radio, that phone line is in someone's house and they are letting the public use their phone for calls. If you are using a satellite or a repeater to boost your signal, that too was put in place by volunteers. Ham radio is completely open-source, and free.

It is also useful in emergencies. For example when an earthquake, tsunami, hurricane, terrorist attack, blackout, or other emergency happens traditional methods of communication are typically the first to go down. Cell phones, landline phones, electric, internet all are the first networks to go down in an emergency - ham radio never goes down. It's a good way to get information about what is happening from others that are seeing/experiencing it, and to help by relaying information out of the area or call for help, you can also help out yourself by getting involved in helping emergency communications proceed in the area.

Overall - ham radio is just fun. It's a cool way to speak with others who live in your town and have a "conference call" type conversation with many people, or just a few and not have to all be in the same place.

For anyone who is interested in getting there licence, there are currently 6 of us that are going to go for it. There are several books I recommend for studying for the test. Now You're Talking is a book that covers all the topics that need to be learned for the test in a well written format. This is a long format that describes everything you need to know. Technician  Class Preparation is a great "crash course" that quickly takes you through all you need to know, then presents you with all the questions that may appear on the test. Along with the questions is an explanation as to which answer is correct and the reasoning behind it. This is a great book.

There are other books you can buy, but these two are my personal favorite. No matter what you get, you need to get one that covers all the qeustions and answers. The above books do that.

If you have any questions please feel free to message me on this board. Our group will probably start studying in the next week or so, and then we'll pick a date to meet and discuss the topic, the test, and help each other pass.

We'd love to have you join us.

Hi Zeno,

I'm glad to be finding others that are interested in the hobby as well. It's a lot of fun when you have a few local people to talk to about the topic.

First off, there's no need to learn morse code for your Technician licence. Code isn't required for this level of the license. It's only required if you want to go for a General, or Extra licence. However, the FCC just last week has asked the body that regulates ham radio to drop the requirement for learning morse code al together. That's good news for me because I'm a technician who is wanting to upgrade to General.

I'm happy to be of help in any way that I can in your getting at least a technician license. I have the "Now You're Talking" book, and you are welcome to borrow it from me. I also highly recommend the book by Gordon West. It has the entire pool of questions, and all the correct answers explained in it. If you study that book you're guaranteed to pass because you already know all the questions and answers. He does quite a bit of explaining through the entire process.

You can find this book for pretty cheap, I think it's like $12 or something. I might be ordering it from amazon.com. If you want I could order two of them and once you hit $25 you get free shipping. Let me know if you (or anyone else) wants a copy of the book.

I'm thinking that it might just be good to work towards our licence over a period of a couple months, and then have us all go take the test together. It might be a lot of fun to meet once every couple weeks or so and help each other with any questions that might come up.
