It's true. In NY you have to be a member of the party in which you're voting for the primary.

From the Board of Elections:

"Because a primary is strictly a party election, only voters registered with one of the parties conducting a primary may participate in that party's election."

I'm registered as "no party" and when I looked up how to switch so I could vote today, I discovered that the change wouldn't go into effect until after the General Election in November.

Right, the deadline to change parties for this primary was (if I recall) at the end of December or something like that.

Different state parties do it different ways. In California, people who do not state a party can vote in the Democratic primary (which is open), but not the Republican primary (which is closed)!

And then of course, there's the many states with caucuses and conventions, and no election at all.

It's a funny issue. If I were a Republican (which I'm not!), I'd be annoyed if some Democrat lied about their party status in order to cancel out my vote. Why should anyone but actual party members be able to chose my candidates nominee? It would be perfectly reasonable, in some sense, to restrict primary voting to those people who (say) donated to the Democratic National Committee or Libertarian National Committee or what have you.

Quote from: Harlan on February 05, 2008, 02:07:27 PM
Different state parties do it different ways. In California, people who do not state a party can vote in the Democratic primary (which is open), but not the Republican primary (which is closed)!

Yep. California is a perfect example, where the Dem primary is open and the GOP one is closed.

State parties have historically engineered their nomination process to strike a balance between state party power and the influence of candidates. That's why you have the lop-sidedness of some primaries being open, and others closed, even w/in the same state. A closed primary usually gives the state party more political pull, while open primaries allow states to canvas and gauge support early in the process with the aim of securing it later in November.

Then there's the whole superdelegate thing which is like an open primary within a party's closed primary. I think... :|
