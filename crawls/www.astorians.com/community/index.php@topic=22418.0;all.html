Here is the NYC bike map.  (link to 9MB PDF)  Quick legend key - green is a protected bike path, red is a separate bike path, and orange is a shared bike path.

You can also pick one up for free at bike shops, libraries and schools.  It's your best bet for comprehensive information on bike riding.

Here is more info about the map, as well as a link to an online interactive map.

Vernon does not go all the way to Astoria Park.  The bike path goes up 8th St., jogs right at the top of the hill onto 27th Ave., and then then is a shared path down 12th St. to the park.  It continues along Shore Blvd.  Astoria Park isn't large enough to have a separate network of bike paths, but you can ride along the perimeter on the streets.

But if you want to do a lot of bike riding, you'll need to become more accustomed to riding in traffic and over bridges.  Your best bets for less congested riding are, as Jennifer mentioned, on Randall's Island or Roosevelt Island.  Your riding will be very limited otherwise.

Nearly all bike lanes in NYC are on NYC streets.  There are a few exceptions - in parks or along rehabilitated parts of the shoreline, for instance.  But yeah, you're not going to find separate road-like lanes like in Amsterdam or anything.

Protected means there is a buffer, usually a strip of roadway painted to keep cars off, between cars and a dedicated bike lane.  Separate is a dedicated bike lane indicated by solid white lines, the inner of which separates the bike lane from car traffic - like along Vernon Blvd., or along 35th and 36th Sts., and cars are not supposed to drive, park or stand in those lanes.  Shared is indicated with a silhouette of a bike followed by a series of carats and means that you share the physical lane space with cars.

As far as 31st St. goes -- my husband commutes to work by bike from Astoria (near Ditmars) to Brooklyn.  After years of experimenting with different routes, he now often chooses to ride on 31st St under the train.  He has found it less congested and annoying than some of the streets with official bike lanes.

You are legally permitted to ride on any street in NYC, with or without a bike lane.  The addition of bike lane markers is supposed to help cars as much as bicyclists, I think, to let anyone who uses that street to expect bikes and to give them some buffer if possible.  But yes, dotted lines indicate officially proposed or possible bike paths.
