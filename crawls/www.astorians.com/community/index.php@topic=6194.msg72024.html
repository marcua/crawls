Quote from: Frappe on May 03, 2007, 02:59:08 PM
This might seem is naive, but what exactly qualifies a veggie or meat to be organic?

There are USDA standards now. For veggies, briefly, you can't use chemical fertilizers, pesticides, or herbicides. "Natural" fertilizers (manure) and pesticides (Bt) are fine. Meat is complicated, but I think it involves feeding mostly organic feed, and no prophylactic antibiotics or hormones. Note that certification is expensive, so most labeled organic is from medium-sized producers and larger. Small producers, such as those at farmers markets, often grow organically but are not "Certified Organic."

Quote
Does organic farming generally involve an increase or decrease in costs as well as production per acre?

Usually, but not always, organic farming costs more and has lower yields. Nitrogen fertilizer made from oil is really, really cheap. (Thank you Green Revolution.)

Quote
And what makes organic products healthier than non-organic.

Usually nothing, except that some of the pesticides and herbicides have residues that may be harmful if ingested regularly. Oh, and organic produce has more bugs, which are high in protein. Personally, I think most of the benefits of organic are environmental more than medical, especially if you're careful to wash produce before eating it.

And that's not even always the case. For example, with row crops like corn, you have to till the soil regularly to keep weeds down, resulting in more runoff of topsoil into rivers. That's not sustainable. With non-organic production, you can dump broad-spectrum herbicides that degrade harmlessly in 24 hours onto the soil, grow GMO corn that's resistant to the herbicide, and use no-till methods that don't waste topsoil. I think it's a toss-up which one is worse.

Oh, and note that you literally can't grow organic apples in the Northeast. The bugs here are so nasty that you have to use insecticides if you want more than two apples per tree.

Personal Short Story:
In 1979 one of my children developed flu-like symptoms. As an intractible asthmatic he was quickly treated with a broad-spectrum antibiotic. After 5 doses he ended up being hospitalized. Intubated and in the PICU, nothing seemed to alleviate his breathing and he was dependent on the machine to breathe for him. I was scared to death that I was going to lose him at 9 years old. It took 8 days to discover that the reason the antibiotics weren't working was because he was immune to their effect because of the antibiotics used in producing meat and poultry. The second, concurrent, problem was the nitrites used in keeping green produce green. He is currently 37 years old and has to carry an EpiPen to inject himself if he somehow ingests nitrites, or for that matter nitrates, as he instantly has an anaphylactic reaction.

Neither the FDA nor other agencies responsible for our food supply can be certain that our food is safe until offending products are removed from the food supply and not allowed for sale in the country.
