I lived in Japan for six years. Like you, I just wanted to live in another country for a while. I didn't have any specific interest in Japan before. I picked it because I knew it was one of the easiest places to get a job and a work visa. I also wanted to learn more about Asia firsthand, but knew that most of the other countries would present too much of a culture shock for me. So I just needed a place to live and work that gave me easy access to the other areas.

A friend from a previous job had worked at an English school in the countryside near Hiroshima, and he highly recommended it. They hired me and I moved without knowing a word of Japanese (other than the obvious--sayonara, arigatou, etc.). I picked up a lot more while living there, of course. I don't remember how much my expenses were, but I had a lot of money from the Internet bubble, so it wasn't a worry. However, many of the other teachers at the school were able to borrow against their salary to move there.

I was there for a year, and then I moved to Tokyo. I knew I wanted to experience a Japanese city too, so it was either Tokyo or Osaka. I interviewed for four jobs and accepted the first offer I got. That employer renewed my visa.

I didn't get too homesick because, like I said, Japan wasn't as big a culture shock as, say, Thailand would be. I do notice that among the foreigners in Japan, they tend to love it for the first 3 or 4 years, then they resent and even hate it for a few years. Eventually, they go back to loving it. (Except for my friend Ann, who loves and hates it at the same time.) The most frustrated people there are the ones who can't accept that they'll never be fully embraced by Japanese society. You'd always be an outsider in Japan, no matter how long you lived there or how good your Japanese was.

I have to admit that one of the best things about living in Tokyo was getting to know the foreign community. Tokyo's foreign community is no longer the wealthy clique it once was. A lot of companies have been pulling out of Japan or re-establishing their Asian bases in Hong Kong or Singapore. So I mean the large variety of people, from ambassadors to businessmen to artists to strippers. You get to know all these people from different walks of life simply because you have something in common. You're all outsiders. I don't know if foreign communities in other parts of the world are like that.

I was 25 going on 26 when I moved over to teach. Not exactly just out of college. And I knew a lot of people who went there looking to do something different, including couples. Which companies are you considering? People tend to hate the large companies like Aeon and Nova, but those schools do take care of their teachers as far as getting settled is concerned. I don't know what they're like on day-to-day stuff, but I do know that people who are really serious about teaching tend to hate them. You have to know what you can live with.

One option is to sponsor your own visa. My husband did that because he had several teaching gigs around the city in addition to his children's show (remind me to tell you about that one later). You just need one employer to confirm that they're paying you at least 200,000 yen/month. It doesn't matter how much the rest of them pay.
