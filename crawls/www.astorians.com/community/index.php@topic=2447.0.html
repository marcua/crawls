911 vs. the 114th precinct
« on: June 25, 2006, 10:04:44 AM »
So over on the anti-hate crimes thread we were talking about 311 vs. Vallone's office, but I witnessed an incident yesterday that made me wonder what to do in more pressing emergencies.

Back when I was mostly lurking on the forum here, someone helpfully posted the 114th precinct's direct phone number (which I noted as 718 626 9311).  I decided to save it into my phone in case of emergency, and didn't think another thing of it.

Then last night my friend and I left my apartment to head into Manhattan for a night out.  We entered the subway at 36th Avenue but before we could go up to the Manhattan-bound platform, we saw a crowd gathered at the foot of the stairs on the Ditmars-bound side, and could hear a commotion going on in the stairwell.

A couple of adult men had a young boy in a headlock (who was screaming "I'm twelve years old! Somebody help me!").  It was a little odd because it looked more like they were trying to hold him than actually hurt him, but regardless, child screaming for help = many bystanders calling 911.

My friend immediately dialed 911 and gave them the information, as many people around us were doing.  It didn't seem like another 911 call would really help, but I decided to dial the precinct just in case.  It took about 7 rings for someone to pick up, and when she did, I gave her the information as well as noting that others were also calling 911.  She told me that she would check and make sure that the 911 information was coming through and that they were on it, and got off the phone.

Since the scene didn't appear immediately violent, we stood back with a few other women at the bottom of the stairs to watch and make sure nothing escalated.  Turned out to be a good idea, because what we figured out after watching for a few minutes was that the little boy and his friends had apparently stolen a Playstation off of one of the men, and we weren't clear on the details but it appeared that the Playstation had fallen and been destroyed.  (Line of the night went to the little accused thief himself: "Can't you just let it go, man?  What are you doing with a Playstation at your age, anyway?  I'm twelve!  What's your excuse?"  Hell no they couldn't let it go, but I did have to bite my cheek to restrain my inappropriate amusement.)

The MTA ticket agent was also calling 911, but she couldn't see what was going on, so she wound up waving me back through the turnstiles for information and to find out if medical help was needed.  After she buzzed me back through, we exchanged a few shrugs as time went on and we waited and waited and waited for the cops.

The response time, which my friend and I figured out afterwards by looking at the call times on our phones, was about 12-14 minutes.  It seemed like a long time to me, but my friend pointed out that nobody was injured so the cops probably didn't use sirens (we didn't hear any).  By the time they showed up, the men had released the boy from the headlock and were holding him firmly by the wrist to wait for the police and make sure he didn't escape.  Once the situation was under control, we headed on up to the platform.

So the point of relaying this whole strange tale is, under what situations do you all think it's helpful to dial the precinct directly?  Is it possible that all our confusing calls slowed the response time, or is that response time normal for a non-life-and-death situation?

The only time I've ever called 911 was two years ago when there was a stalled boat floating downstream through Hell Gate, and it stopped underneath the Triboro Bridge.  Here's how I described it at the time:

I discovered that I have the power to summon an NYPD helicopter, patrol boat, FDNY fire and tow boat, coast guard cutters, and an assortment of land-based FDNY engines and rescue units with a few carefully chosen words.

We were in Astoria Park, just under the Hell Gate Bridge, with a cousin of mine from San Diego and her family.  Her husband is a fire chief.  As many of you have witnessed, the tide runs pretty quickly through the Hell Gate -- a boat going with the current looks like it's going extremely fast, and against the current, you often have to measure the boat against the far shore to make sure it's really moving.

So we're looking out over this current - the water level's high, and it's running fast south. I point out a boat under the Hell Gate bridge that is being swept along south towards the Triboro Bridge by the current - you can tell it's not moving under its own power, because it's going backwards. I briefly think, how stupid can you be? You don't mess with this area of water. Then someone says, look, someone on the boat is waving. Not just waving, but standing on the front deck and waving both arms. Another person in the back is also waving. Maybe they're stalled, someone suggests. Do they need help? Should we call 911? I ask.

We watch. Another boat is approaching it, moving fast with the current. As it pulls abreast, it turns slightly and comes behind the stalled boat. They move downstream in formation for a bit, and then the drifting boat comes to a stop directly under the Triboro Bridge. Apparently, it has cast its anchor. The other boat hesitates near by, a bit south of the Triboro Bridge. Just south of the Triboro, there is a sharp bend in the river, and large boats navigating that area have to turn sharply when they are positioned just about where the stalled boat is.

We suddenly hear several sharp blasts of an air horn. We look upstream. There is a loaded garbage barge approaching, being pushed by a tug. The stalled boat is in the middle of the shipping lane. The barge is moving at a good clip with the current. Blast, blast. The two pleasure boats don't move or do anything. We watch, in fascinated horror, as the garbage barge approaches. It swings a bit left, and clears the stalled boat to its right. However, this manoeuvre has brought the barge closer to the inside of the sharp curve, and the tug immediately has to turn sharply right, swinging wide and carried along by the current nearly broadside before it rights itself and continues downstream.

The other pleasure boat takes off. We wait. Another pleasure boat passes. I say, I think we should call 911. We have a bit of discussion, someone says, someone else will take care of it, and I say, isn't that what we always say? Jim hands me his phone (I've left mine at home). I dial 911. "Please state your emergency." "Hi, we're in Astoria Park, and there's a boat that's stalled directly under the Triboro Bridge, and it looked like the people were waving their hands for help." She took down certain details (which included asking me whether the boat was in Manhattan or Queens), and that was that.

Within 2-3 minutes, we heard the beat of a helicopter. NYPD. It circled the bridge and went away. Came back, circled again several times, and going under the bridge on one pass. Then we saw a Fire Department boat speeding downstream. It passed the stalled boat, turned around, got out its bumpers, and slowly made its way alongside the boat. We saw them toss the stalled boat a line.

Jim's phone rang. He answered it. He listened. No, no I didn't .... // .... oh, yes, oh, that was my wife. // No, no, we didn't see any bodies in the water. Ok. Thank you. He hung up.  Jim said that they identified themselves as "EMS", and Jim thought it was "Eastern Mountain Sports", not "Emergency Medical Services"! They were calling to find out if we had seen any bodies.

While the phone call was going on, we saw a fire rescue unit suddenly appear down the street from us, near the Triboro. Then, a full FDNY engine pulled off the road, onto a park path, and positioned itself carefully at the base of the Triboro Bridge. We watched as another unit did the same at the base across the water. Then my cousin's husband said, maybe they're checking to see if it could be terrorism.

A large NYPD boat made its way downstream, but retreated shortly thereafter. Two Coast Guard souped-up Zodiacs appeared, one from each direction, and converged on the knot of boats. They stayed behind after the FDNY started towing the other boat (agonizingly slowly upstream), slowly circling around where the stalled boat had been.

When we finally left, all the officials were slowly dispersing. It was a rather impressive display. When I called, I had had no thoughts of terroristic plots, but it was rather sobering to see the whole thing play out. How effective it would have been in a more serious situation, I don't know, but I am glad they responded quickly.
