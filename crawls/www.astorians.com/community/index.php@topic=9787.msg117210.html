If you haven't played it since you were 9 and the idea of moving and having it is stressing you out, may I gently suggest that you not keep it?  Unless, of course you plan on playing it again.  If it's sentimental but not useful, perhaps you can take a picture of it and frame it nicely so you'll remember it always.  Take pictures from all angles and make an awesome collage.  If you can't sell it and don't want to keep it, can you donate it to a child who yearns to play?  Perhaps a church could use one for choir practice?  Maybe a local bar could use one. 

In my line of work, I have a lot of experience with the sentimental-but-unusable-and-creates-undue-stress category of items.  It's a tricky one.  But usually, the item is smaller than a piano.

I wish you luck.

I actually have some recent experience with piano moving. Definitely have it tuned after it's moved, and I'd recommend waiting at least a couple weeks to get it tuned once it's in its new environment, because the wood needs time to adjust. Do not by any means attempt to move it yourself. If any stairs are involved, be expected to pay more, but just the moving of the piano will be pricey.

I have to ask though, where are you moving it from/to? Is someone keeping it for you or are you putting it in storage? Will you or anyone else actually be playing it?

Depending on the type of Piano, it isn't that hard to move it.  My parents had an upright piano, and when it came time to sell it / move it, my dad rented a piano moving jacks (for small jacks that fit around the corners of the piano), we used a ratcheted nylon clamp, pushed down on the jacks lifting it up on its wheels and rolled up the ramp into the moving truck.

It isn't nearly as daunting as it appears, you just have to make sure you get the right tools.  I believe my dad rented the jacks from Home Depot... but I could easily be wrong there.
