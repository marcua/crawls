I just glanced over at the little table next to my computer desk.  Yup, on it, among a few other things, is my copy of Little House on the Prairie.  I got it out a few weeks ago because I was talking to my dad about the book Quinine: Malaria and the Quest for a Cure That Changed the World.  He commented that the book mentions that malaria used to be called fever and ague.  I said, "I know that!"  and pulled down Little House on the Prairie and read him the section where they get fever and ague, and Dr. Tam comes and gives them quinine.  (I also love the section in one of the later books where Laura describes overhearing her Uncle Tom talking about being part of a group of settlers that first saw the Badlands, challenged the Sioux, and were (amazingly, given subsequent history) eventually forced by the US government to abandon their settlement stockade and turn back.  I remember glossing over that part when I was little, but now it's one of the most interesting parts to me.)  Actually, it's fascinating to read the books as an adult with an eye towards how much history of the settling of this country - good and bad - they recount.

My Little House books are on the same shelf as all of the Anne books, about two dozen Madeleine L'Engle books, James Harriot, and, er, Harry Potter.  I love Madeleine L'Engle, and although I've never met her, I've been lucky to know two people who worked with her or worked on writing with her.  My two favourite Madeleine L'Engle books are The Small Rain (her first published novel), about Katherine Vigneras as a child, and A Severed Wasp, about Katherine as a woman in her 70s - and which ties Katherine with the Austins.  One of my favourite things about her books is the interweaving of stories, times and places that happen not only within individual books, but among books and even series.  She has written many different types of books, including adult novels (not like that, but not aimed at the under-15 set), non fiction including memoirs (my favourite of which is Two Part Invention), philosophy/religion books, and poetry.

I read Betsy-Tacy as a child, but no longer have them.  I still have a few Judy Blume, but I think I got most of those out of the library - except Forever, of course, which my friend lent me and which I hid under my mattress.
