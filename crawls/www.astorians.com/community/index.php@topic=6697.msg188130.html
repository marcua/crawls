Does anyone here ever notice the old phone numbers still seen on some places of business? "Old phone numbers" means exchange-name formats, from before there were area codes and you picked up the phone and asked the operator for "AStoria 4-5500" or "NIghtingale 9-3839".

I started noticing these many years ago always thought they were cool, and a few months ago I started pointing them out to people. I was surprised to find that almost no one under the age of 50 knew what I was talking about, and that folks were looking right at these old numbers not knowing what they were.

The most common exchanges seen in our area are AStoria, STillwell, and RAvenswood, but among other pre-occupations I am on the hunt for other exchange name sightings. A rather large one which I bet everyone here has seen while entering Queens via the 59th Street Bridge (upper level) is the Steinway Storage Warehouse, which has had its "AStoria 8-9090" phone number on display since 1926.

I started a photo set of telephone exchange names sightings in Astoria and anywhere else, including old magazines and other old photos I have around. The real prizes, though, are the "live" ones, where companies in business since the 1920s or 1930s still display their old exchange name phone number on their signage.

Has anyone here seen other exchange names around AStoria? Here's the set.

That is kind of cool, like the yellow "fallout shelter signs" that remain or when they knock down a building and reveal the facade of a neighboring building that has an old ad painted on it for some store or brand that is long gone.

Meanwhile, I moved to my current block in Astoria in '01 and there are four small stores on the corner of 34th Street and 30th Avenues.   They have all changed since I moved to my current located in March of '01.  So it hasn't been quite nine years.   The first store, closest to 30th Avenue, was a cell phone store that looked to be on its last legs when I moved it.   It was the first of the four to close. It then became a sari shop.  That closed and it is now a travel agency that caters to South Asians.  3 businesses there.

The second store, if memory serves me correctly, was an old barber shop that I actually patronized.   The barber told me he had been there since the 1970s and it was quite the place.   The barber was getting old and couldn't see well.   I like to get my hair cut short and I'd get home and have to shave off the spots he missed.   There is now a different barber shop in the space, but I think he actually moved to the store next door and then relocated to the current spot.  Two businesses (three if you count the barber shop) in this spot.

The third store was a shoe repair place.  It was the second business, after the cell phone store, to close.   Then I think the barber shop moved it and he in turn moved next door to make way for one of those stores where you can go into a booth and make telephone calls.   I noticed that the business hasn't been open recently so I don't know what its status is.

The forth store was a beauty salon.   Then it became a business that sold DVDs and CDs for Balkin countries (don't ask me which ones).  I rarely saw anyone in there.   It lasted about a year or so.   Then an insurance agency moved in and it lasted about a year before closing in recent months.  The site is currently vacant.

Quote from: Mckenna797 on January 17, 2010, 12:45:52 AM
Whitey Fords dad's bar was on 34th Ave between 24th Street and crescent it was called the Ivy Room
That's right. I remember the place quite well because my father was a regular there. In fact, I met the gentleman in question many times. His name was Jimmy Young, and he was actually Whitey Ford's step-father. I remember him being a very tall and broad man - he looked HUGE to me at the time, but I guess the fact that I was just a child had a lot to do with it. Other than that, what I recall most about him was that he was a very nice man with a very sweet and quiet demeanor. Many times when my father had to watch me because my mother had to do something away from home, my father would take me with him to the Ivy Room. I would get my choice of either a Coke, a Seven-Up, or a Shirley Temple (which was really just a Seven-Up with a little grenadine and a couple of maraschino cherries added). I then had to sit on one of the sofas off to the side and be quiet. It seemed like most of the time, the Yankee game was on the TV. Mainly, I found having to sit on a sofa in the bar while my father talked with his friends extremely boring. Even to this day, I find bars boring, and could never understand the appeal they have for so many people.

One other thing I remember about Jimmy Young took place when I was still in kindergarten. We had to bring empty eggshells in to school which we then painted to make Easter eggs. I had about two or three of them. That day, my father picked me up from school and we stopped off at the Ivy Room for a few minutes on the way home. I gave Jimmy one of my Easter eggs which he stood in a shot glass and placed it on one of the glass shelves that ran along the wall behind the bar. It stayed there from then on until the Ivy Room finally closed about nine years later, which would have been around 1972 or 1973.
