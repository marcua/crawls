It's from 1999 and the NWU has been trying to get B&N to re-negotiate their accounting practices since then, but have not been able to receive audience with the conglomerate. Small publishers have been put out of business in addition to small bookstores because of this practice.

Holy hell there was a lot of big words in that letter!!!! I couldn't read the whole thing, I didn't have patience for all that language and random \ but I did find the part you were talking about, luckily that was written in plain english for the most part. Here it is:

So, what happens in the real world? Unless a book gets attention within a few weeks, chain bookstores will return books to the publisher, usually getting full credit if they return the books within a certain period. Indeed, the rate of returns for trade books has risen dramatically in the past few years.

The role of the chain bookstore is even more insidious. Chain bookstores negotiate deep discounts with publishers. As a result, books are sold for less and, by extension, some authors receive a smaller royalty. In other words, the very power of bookstores to demand and receive deep discounts has resulted in a depressing of the per copy royalty received by some authors.

As the FTC may know, independent bookstores, via the American Booksellers Association, have sued Barnes & Noble and Borders, alleging that the chains are receiving illegal terms of sale, including extra discounts that give them an unfair competitive advantage3. The NWU has notified the plaintiffs that we are prepared to file an amicus brief on its behalf in the action.

Furthermore, there is a direct relationship between publishers and the chain bookstores when it comes to the decisions about what books are published. Publishers\' representatives regularly assess with chain bookstores what will sell in the current marketplace or, more precisely, whether a chain will commit to pushing a particular book (e.g., featuring the book in prominent displays or scheduling author readings in its stores). In particular, the coordination is most intense around the selling of books for which a publisher has paid a large advance-a publisher wants to guarantee that the chains will flog those books because of the huge financial investment at stake.
