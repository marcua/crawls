There is a distinction between movies made in Astoria and movies made and set in Astoria.  Under the Hellgate Bridge, Astoria and  A Guide to Recognizing Your Saints would fall into the latter category.

I'm surprised no one has mentioned Kiss of Death, from the late 1940s that featured Richard Widmark as the pyscho of all pyschos.   In the film's most famous scene, Widmark throws a woman in a wheelchair down a flight of stairs.   In any event, Victor Mature, the film's star, lives in Astoria in the movie.   They filmed some exterior shots of Mature's apartment building and they used an apartment on one of the streets just south of Astoria Park because you can see the park and the bridge in the background.

Title: Re: Past Movies that were filmed in Astoria
Post by: billyboy on March 24, 2008, 10:08:08 AM
Need to chime in from lurkdom here for a moment: I finally saw Guide to Saints this past weekend on the Sundance Channel.  Man, this movie was awful, haven't had such a negative reaction to a movie since I saw Kids.  Dimestore Scorcese packed with "histrionic guido screaming" method of acting that Painco unfortunately wrought (although he's otherwise a great actor).  The few times I've taken acting classes, there's always one young Italian guy, the Pacino acolyte, whose every scene was reduced to virulent nut-grabbing and neck-straining over-emoting.  Last time I saw bad, phoney "Italian over-acting" on this level was The Pope of Greenwich Village, which also ironically featured Eric Roberts.

How do you set a movie in 1980s Astoria without any Greeks?  This is like making a movie about Harlem without any black people.

Maybe that was the problem.  The film homes in on this kid and his violent/lost buddies as they plunder through neighborhood life, and our world is reduced to theirs ... the only adults morons or objects on the street to be mocked ... or flighty gay guys who walk dogs.  In other words a passing joke in their faux macho world.  I wouldn't make note of this save the movie positions itself as a "neighborhood" study, and this was hardly Astoria in the 80s.  You can find lost kids like this in any decade, in any neighborhood in New York.  You can surely find them now in Astoria.  And I guess my main qualm is everything is reduced to histrionic, bad melodrama, in this case coupled with a constant stream of foul language, which normally doesn't bother me, but when I sense it's laid on thick to intimidate me in some sense, it gets annoying.  (True, you'll find countless teenagers like this, but why would anyone want to watch them do it for over an hour in a movie?  Generally 15 seconds is all I can stand of a kid pulling this number on the street.)

Chazz Palmienteri was the soulful heart of the movie as the ailing father?  To me, he seemed like a complete moron who didn't recognize how far his son and his loser friends had slipped in life ... or thought it was cute?  Why the film maker didn't chose to have him do every scene with an accordion and a spider monkey in a bellboy cap with a tin cup, I'm not sure ... that's how he felt to me.  And god bless Dianne Weist, but she does a bad Edith Bunker.

Queens Logic was a bad movie.  (Compared to Guide to Saints, it comes off like Citize Kane.)  At least with Queens Logic, you had some great actors (Montegna, Kevin Bacon, John Malkovich ... even Tom Waits in a fun turn as Monte, the sleazy neighborhood hustler) having fun with a very bad script.  It also had a better sense of Astoria, which was roughly the same time period portrayed in Guide to Saints -- chew on that one, as Guide to Saints seemed to delight in that faux ghetto vibe that I know for fact wasn't the case circa the late 80s here, at least in the Ditmars area portrayed.  The opening montage of Queens Logic is actually pretty good in displaying snapshots of how Astoria is.

You know how I know a movie is really bad?  I hope the same ending happens in each: that all members of the cast assemble in a house, and a bomb is dropped on the building, wiping out everyone inside.  That's pretty much how I felt after Guide to Saints, and surely with the Montegna character in Queens Logic, although the others weren't quite as bad.  Queens Logic, I can wrap my mind around as a pure goof, but Guide to Saints was lowest common denominator stuff: hateful faux tough-guy crap with bad writing, bad acting and endless second-hand Scorsese directing techniques: how it garnered awards and good reviews, I have no idea.  And I'm not even an Astoria native!  I can guarantee you, my reaction would have been even more harsh if that were the case.  Once the novelty of "Astoria" of the movie wears off, it's just a bad, bad movie, no matter where it's set.

Need to chime in from lurkdom here for a moment: I finally saw Guide to Saints this past weekend on the Sundance Channel.  Man, this movie was awful, haven't had such a negative reaction to a movie since I saw Kids.  Dimestore Scorcese packed with "histrionic guido screaming" method of acting that Painco unfortunately wrought (although he's otherwise a great actor).  The few times I've taken acting classes, there's always one young Italian guy, the Pacino acolyte, whose every scene was reduced to virulent nut-grabbing and neck-straining over-emoting.  Last time I saw bad, phoney "Italian over-acting" on this level was The Pope of Greenwich Village, which also ironically featured Eric Roberts.

How do you set a movie in 1980s Astoria without any Greeks?  This is like making a movie about Harlem without any black people.

Maybe that was the problem.  The film homes in on this kid and his violent/lost buddies as they plunder through neighborhood life, and our world is reduced to theirs ... the only adults morons or objects on the street to be mocked ... or flighty gay guys who walk dogs.  In other words a passing joke in their faux macho world.  I wouldn't make note of this save the movie positions itself as a "neighborhood" study, and this was hardly Astoria in the 80s.  You can find lost kids like this in any decade, in any neighborhood in New York.  You can surely find them now in Astoria.  And I guess my main qualm is everything is reduced to histrionic, bad melodrama, in this case coupled with a constant stream of foul language, which normally doesn't bother me, but when I sense it's laid on thick to intimidate me in some sense, it gets annoying.  (True, you'll find countless teenagers like this, but why would anyone want to watch them do it for over an hour in a movie?  Generally 15 seconds is all I can stand of a kid pulling this number on the street.)

Chazz Palmienteri was the soulful heart of the movie as the ailing father?  To me, he seemed like a complete moron who didn't recognize how far his son and his loser friends had slipped in life ... or thought it was cute?  Why the film maker didn't chose to have him do every scene with an accordion and a spider monkey in a bellboy cap with a tin cup, I'm not sure ... that's how he felt to me.  And god bless Dianne Weist, but she does a bad Edith Bunker.

Queens Logic was a bad movie.  (Compared to Guide to Saints, it comes off like Citize Kane.)  At least with Queens Logic, you had some great actors (Montegna, Kevin Bacon, John Malkovich ... even Tom Waits in a fun turn as Monte, the sleazy neighborhood hustler) having fun with a very bad script.  It also had a better sense of Astoria, which was roughly the same time period portrayed in Guide to Saints -- chew on that one, as Guide to Saints seemed to delight in that faux ghetto vibe that I know for fact wasn't the case circa the late 80s here, at least in the Ditmars area portrayed.  The opening montage of Queens Logic is actually pretty good in displaying snapshots of how Astoria is.

You know how I know a movie is really bad?  I hope the same ending happens in each: that all members of the cast assemble in a house, and a bomb is dropped on the building, wiping out everyone inside.  That's pretty much how I felt after Guide to Saints, and surely with the Montegna character in Queens Logic, although the others weren't quite as bad.  Queens Logic, I can wrap my mind around as a pure goof, but Guide to Saints was lowest common denominator stuff: hateful faux tough-guy crap with bad writing, bad acting and endless second-hand Scorsese directing techniques: how it garnered awards and good reviews, I have no idea.  And I'm not even an Astoria native!  I can guarantee you, my reaction would have been even more harsh if that were the case.  Once the novelty of "Astoria" of the movie wears off, it's just a bad, bad movie, no matter where it's set.

I spent a lot of time out here late 87 through 1991 or so -- dated a woman who lived just off Ditmars and had two friends in the 30th Ave and Broadway areas.  Granted, Astoria was a good bit more gritty than it is now, but again, not quite how Guide to Saints portrayed it.  Harlem isn't "all black" either, but to make a movie about Harlem and not have one black person in it, even in a background shot, would be very strange.  Astoria is known the world over for being a Greek stronghold -- I think second only to Melbourne, Australia in terms of population density outside of Greece itself?  Again, the film maker should feel free to make any movie he wants, but I found that sort of cultural narrowness stifling and odd.

I don't have problems with Italians per se.  I do have problems with people acting like stereotypes, and I surely have problems with people acting like stereotypical Italians in movies.  Surely isn't the first time.  Pope of Greenwich Village takes the crown for me, but there are others.  A great one that's hard to find is Blood Brothers, from the late 70s, starring Richard Gere as a young Italian guy in the Bronx trying to find his way while dealing with his insane construction working father.  While Gere himself doesn't go as overboard, the supporting cast, which inclues Paul(ie) Sorvino, presents one grievous, chest-thumping shouting match after another, clutched rosary beads, mamma mia, f word, fungoo, whassa mattah wit you, f word, etc.  Enough, man.  I've seen this used to great effect in movies -- think Moonstruck or Good Fellas -- but far more often it's hammy and cliched beyond belief.

I didn't like it simply because it felt like a bad movie to me, for the numerous reasons noted.  I did like certain things about the movie (the kid's relationship to his girlfriend was sweet, as was his new-found friendship with the Scottish kid), but everything else overwhelmed that, I felt zero empathy for any of the characters after awhile.  But, I wouldn't mind checking out Dito's book the movie's based on.  Why?  Because it seems to me like he couched a lot of his vibe and style from Jim Carroll, whose book, The Basketball Diaries, is one of my favorites about New York (it actually made me want to move here when I read it in the 70s), about him growing up in an Irish neighborhood in Inwood in the 60s (and it gets much more lowdown than Guide to Saints in terms of bad behavior).  If you've never seen the movie version of The Basketball Diaries (starring Leo DiCaprio) ... don't.  It's even worse than Guide to Saints.  Which leads me to believe Dito's book could be much better than the movie he made from it.  I'll give it a shot eventually.

BigB, I'd watch Under Hellgate, same from what I've heard of it, I'm afraid my head my explode.  I tried watching Boondock Saints for the first time the other night, and I suspect both are equally bad with Boston and Astoria being inter-changeable.  I broke out my bad VHS copy of Queens Logic last night for old times sake, and once again found myself disappointed when Joe Montegna's character didn't slip on the rope on the Hellgate Bridge and plummet into the East River at the end.
