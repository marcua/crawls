Syracuse, being pretty much smack in the middle of NY State picks up culinary delights from all over NY State and canada.  Here's some of my personal favorites:
Chicken Wings: I've never found wings outside of central / western NY that can compare.  I've been known to eat 30 in a sitting.
Coneys / White Hots: These orginated in Rochester.  Basically they're an uncured pork hot dog, which has a white color.  I have never met a woman who has liked these.  I think that every guy I know loves them.  They're spicier and far more delicious than your run of the mill hot dogs.  You can order them, but it's expensive.  I usually put in requests with my family when they're coming to visit, which is why I have like 5 lbs in my freezer.
Speedies: A speedie is a grilled chicken (or pork) sandwich with a white BBQ sauce on it, regional to the snow belt.  The sauce is like a vinegary mayo based sauce.  I'm not a huge fan, but what can you do?
Gianelli Italian Sausage: really, really, really good Italian Sausage.
The Dinosaur BBQ: There's one in Harlem, I've never been, but every time I hit Syracuse I have to eat at the Dino at least once.  So good...
The New York State Fair: The NYS Fair is located in Syracuse.  While not primarily a culinary treat, each year "The Fair" tries to outdo itself with the crap that is deep fried, grilled, and prepared in other deliciously unhealthy manners.  In the last three years I've gambled on shark, gator, deep fried mac n cheese, deep fried cookie dough, and countless cups of labatt blue and a cranberry wine slushie or two.
Oh, and Canadian beer pretty much dominates the market up there, since cheap Canadian beer is head and shoulders above cheap domestic brew.  Well, there's Genesee, but there aren't a whole lot of Genee drinkers...

When I was growing up in central Minnesota, we ate a lot of traditional German & Polish food with an American twist (ketchup on bratwurst  ).  I don't know how many of you grew up with community goulash (it was a variation of the Hungarian goulash, but with every participating member contributing some sort of ingredient to a big stew) and potluck dinners (everyone in the church brings a large dish of some sort of food), but that's how we got down in the CM (central Minnesota, for all of you who didn't know, now you know!)  Actually, they stopped the community meals a long time ago due to the incredible risk of mass food poisoning!  Oh well, they were fun while they lasted.

It's obviously not unique to my area, but I was lucky because my mother's family owned a huge farm in northern Wisconsin and provided us with fresh farm products every few months. In addition, we used to have well attended fish fries in the summer, after a successful day of fishing by the menfolk!  It was very old-school, but I sort of miss those days!

In New Orleans, there are alot, but the big ones are:
1) Oyster Dressing (a stuffing that gets served alongside the fried turkey)
2) BBQ Shrimp (which is not barbequed but sauteed with a pound of butter and rosemary)
3) King Cake (a glazed breadcake that's served between Three King's Day and Fat Monday and has a baby jesus hidden in the cake.  Whoever gets the baby has good luck for the year)
4) Abita Beer (I can't find it anywhere around here, if anyone knows, please let me know!)

I grew up in Slidell (born in NOLA) and would probably add to that list:

5. Jambalaya (a local pallea-type dish that usually contains the leftovers from every other dish from the previous week)
6. Red Beans and Rice (with or without sausage is just as nice. Generously apply Tabasco.)
7. Pim's Cup (locally famous Summertime drink consisting of equal parts Pim's and ginger ale/sprite/7-up w/ a splash of sour mix. Garnish with a cuke slice.)
8. Hot Boiled "_____" (fill in the blank: crabs, crawfish, shrimp and don't forget to throw loads of new potatos, corn on the cobb and sweet onions into the "berl" water, dawlin'.)
9. Chicory Coffee (aka: Cafe Du Monde; or brew yer own Community (brand) Coffee. That's what coffee shoud taste like!)

The list of famous Creole, Cajun and New Orleans cuisine is endless. Remember those Saturday night fish fries? As for the Abita search, I've usually seen it from time to time at the Blind Tiger and d.b.a. (they have a sister in NOLA on Frenchmen St). Also, I'd try looking on Citysearch for "New Orleans" and calling the places that seem relevant:

http://newyork.citysearch.com/search?query=new+orleans

Laissez le bon ton roulet! (local spelling!)

I grew up in Oklahoma.

1. Cornbread and beans. Cornbread made from scratch buttered and smothered with slow-cooked pinto beans. In the spring, this might be served with poke salat. Pokeweed grows all over the place (I've even seen it up here) and in the spring when the leaves are tender people pick it in the woods and fields and cook it. Sort of like spinach but kind of bitter. Yum.
2. Fried chicken with mashed potatoes - for special occasions
3. Biscuits and gravy - breakfast for the poor. Yum.
4. Barbeque.
5. Sassafras Tea. Sassafras is a fragrant tree with mitten-shaped leaves. In the spring, people dig up the roots and boil them to make tea.
6. Eggs and grits.
