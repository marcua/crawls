I'd say it could be kind of nasty digging out and shuffling to the other side of the street when it snows, especially if it snows more than a few inches. As far as how often that happens, it really depends on how bad of a winter we get.

4WD/AWD rocks.  We've got a '96 Subaru Impreza - nothing fancy, but last winter I had a photo shoot that required me to drive all over Astoria the day after the one major storm we had.  I happily drove over drifts of packed snow and ice to settle into several parking spaces without one skid or spin-out, while people up and down every block were plying their shovels to the sound of wheels spinning helplessly on ice.

And yes, the plows angle to the right.  So park on the left side of a one way street for better access after a snowstorm.  And move the car or at least clean the snow off of it as soon as possible afterwards (or clean your car regularly during the snowfall), especially if the weather report calls for a warming trend (which is actually very common after snowstorms here).  That will help minimise the icy snow that becomes one with your car that barrister15 is referring to.  You'll still have work to do but it will make it easier to get your car out in the end.

You know, it's really not too horrible having a car in the winter. If we have an inch or two (which is what a lot of the "storms" are), you can just sweep off the car and go, it's no big deal.

Like others have said, if you park on a one way street, stick to the left side (it's worth it to drive around and park a few blocks away if need be just to get a left-hand spot). Also, even if you don't plan on driving the car during the week, keep an eye on the weather - if your street has alt parking on Wednesday and Thursday, and it's supposed to snow Wednesday evening, move it as early as possible to the Wednesday side (like, immediately after alt side parking ends - it might even be worth buying a friend a couple beers to move your car if you're not around at that hour) so you won't have to worry about getting up early on Thursday morning to dig your car out.

This might seem like overkill, but if you can notice when and where the sun hits, and try to park in that area, it really does speed up the melting and makes cleaning it off easier. If you can leave your car buried for a day or two (like if it snows on Monday and you have to dig it out for Thursday), it won't take too long if some sunny melting has happened. You should at least go out and sweep off the windows though so it doesn't look like the car is abandoned, and it'll let sun warm up the interior, which will help melt whatever is on the roof.

I've been here two winters and I can only remember maybe two or three times that it's been a real pain, and even then, I just had to budget in some extra time or go out in the afternoon to clean it off for the next day. (Of course, I'm from New England so I'm Little Miss Champion snow shoveler.)
