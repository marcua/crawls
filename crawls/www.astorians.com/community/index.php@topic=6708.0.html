From the NoiseOFF.org site, I handle a lot of messages from people dealing with neighbor noise.

People dealing with noise problems should not be invalidated as being extra-sensitive.  If the neighbor took the effort to notify you there is a problem, take the high road and negotiate a solution that you can both live with.  I have seen too many nasty neighbor disputes over noise and it poisons the peace and happiness of both parties.

Apartment buildings, at least in Astoria, are generally not designed for noise mitigation, but there are steps to prevent noise from disturbing others, and steps to mitigate neighbor noise from affecting you.  Every building is different and acoustics are unique so this can be done with trial and error.

Furnishings such as heavy furniture, cushions, curtains, can absorb (and help reduce) noise.  Other furnishings such as bare floors, metal and glass furniture reflect noise (and worsen the problem).

You should use rugs and carpeting with extra padding underneath (especially if you have pets or toddlers).

Desk chairs with casters on a bare floor can be unpleasant to downstairs neighbors.  There are mats that can be used under the chair that can reduce the noise (you can find them at Staples).

A big problem neighbors deal with is stereo / television volume noise.

The use of subwoofers (on the floor) should not be used in apartments.  The low frequency noise easily travels into other people's apartments.  Also, satellite speakers placed near the ceiling also create noise problems for neighbors.

For apartments, bookshelf speakers are better suited.  They should be placed at about ear level and directed toward the center of the room.  With proper placement, you can better enjoy the sound of your speakers with less volume.

Speakers and television sets should be placed in the wall sharing your bedroom (not the shared wall nearest your neighbors apartment).

Some furnishings vibrate because of appliances (kitchen appliances, or telephones) or computer equipment (printers).  You can use padding underneath the item that can absorb the noise.  Not only does this reduce noise for your neighbor, it makes your living space more livable.
