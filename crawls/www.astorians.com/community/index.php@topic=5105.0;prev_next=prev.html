Re: Startling stats about books in the US
« Reply #11 on: February 02, 2007, 01:35:23 PM »
I find these statistics highly questionable and misleading.

For one thing the 70% who have not visited a bookstore? How are they determining this? I'm one of the biggest book-buying fiends I know and I went to a bookstore maybe three times last year. I did however visit Amazon, bn.com, powells.com, and abe.com hundreds of times and made dozens of purchases through these outlets.

Second, are they tracking sales only? And frontlist sales only? Or have they included one of the largest (and growing markets) which is used books? Used book sales data isn't as easily tracked. And are they including library book data? There's no way to determine where they're getting their info.

Third, they are some kind of self-publishing/marketing group (I don't know how much weight I'd give to statisics coming from a site that is run by a so-called "self-publishing guru"). My guess is their statistics are being used in order to sell their services to desperate writers, who have tried every possible way to get published, with promises of making their book sell. There's nothing like preying on hope (I'd like to see the statistics on companies that do that in this country ... but I digress ...).

Also, historically, the U.S. has never really been a country of readers, to put blind data out there with no context renders it rather meaningless. Is the so-called 80% they are reporting here more or less than 50 years ago? My understanding is that book sales are actually rising in this country--whether or not it's the same people buying those books I'd have to research, but, my guess is there has to be some percentage that are new consumers to the market. In 2005 book sales were over $25.1 billion, that's a lot of money being spent by 20% of the population.

Anyway, I'd stick to more legit sources like the AAP, BISG, ABA, and Bowker if one really wants to hit hard on the data. 

There may not be more cheerful news to be found on any of those sites as far as the state of readers in this country, but, my guess is you'd find some significant differences and, at the very least, more detailed analysis.
