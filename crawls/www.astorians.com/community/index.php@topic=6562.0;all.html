Since Debbie asked me me to elaborate, I'm going to do the best I can, bearing in mind that I'm a supporter and not actually working for PlaNYC or the city.

The concern about B'land, Queens and upper Manhattan being used for park and ride is valid.  Most likely when the Mayor puts an official bill together for congestion pricing it will include provisions for resident parking.  Its what they use along the Jersey side of the Hudson to keep people from park and riding there.

Daisy said that while she doesn't drive, many do.  Incorrect.  Many people take public transit, walk and ride their bikes to work, a few people drive.  And while some people that drive may be low or middle income, most aren't because owning a car in the city is expensive.  Our streets are currently geared toward serving the auto and neglecting everyone else, and only a minority of people own a car and a smaller minority use their car daily.  What do we call it when a system that is paid for by everyone, but is used by a small, mostly affluent, population?  I think Debbie used the right word, elitist.

And who pays for public transit, used by the majority and definitely used by the majority of lower income people?  Increasing it has been the straphangers and not property owners or out of towners.  If you saw the news today you know that the MTA is bracing everyone for another rate hike in the next 3 years.  That's because the MTA surplus (which is from real estate sales and not from the transit system itself or tax payers) is going to be spent of previously floated bonds and the 2nd Ave line.  Our last two mayors and Gov Pataki have had no problem cutting the transit budget to provide tax benefits, but our system needs money to run and needs money to expand.  You think its bad now?  Try in 20 years when NYC adds another 700k newcomers to our fair city.  That money has to come from somewhere, and while congestion pricing isn't going to provide all of it, it will provide more than we straphangers are currently getting.

Of course there's also the concern that our government won't spend the money on transit at all.  Not sure where this fear is coming from, just because education doesn't receive enough money in the budget (once again tax cuts) doesn't mean that the money from the lottery isn't going there.  It just means the money from the lottery alone isn't enough.  Corruption and inefficiency in gov't is nothing new and is a real problem.  But that just means that there should be greater citizen involvement to make sure gov't works for you, the same way you watch the cash register at the grocery store to make sure the checker doesn't ring up you green peppers as Holland red peppers.  What I can say, is that in the current plan the money from congestion pricing would go into a smart fund.  This smart fund would be run by appointed non government board (meaning it won't have the head of DoT on it), selected by the current Mayor and legislature.  They have a mandate to spend the money on public transit and will make decisions based on that.  Similar to a community board, except with actual power.  Some people think there should be more community involvement in the smart fund, and on a more local level.  They're not wrong, and perhaps you'd agree more with that. 

Our city is changing, just like it changed when the auto was introduced.  One thing I think we'll see more of and start using more is bus rapid transit, BRT, which a basically more express buses to get you a farther distance, rather than just cross town.  Maybe even dedicated bus lanes.  Some light rail and some new lines and extensions, but buses are gonna be the first noticeable change.  We can't afford to continue catering to cars, as firelight79 pointed out, wouldn't it be nice to travel through Manhattan without worrying about being hit by an aggressively turning auto?  I may be a little tubby, but one of me takes up a lot less space than a thin guy in a car.  As George Constanza once said, "We live in a society!"  That means we have to try and do what's best for the whole, not the minority, particularly in a place and crowded and diverse as NYC.  We're not getting anymore land, so its important we starting thinking about how we use the space we have.

So if you read this whole thing, thanks.  Hope this answered a few questions.  I'm not an expert, just an enthusiast and someone that believes change starts with our pocket books.  I was at the last congestion pricing panel in Sunnyside, it was a good discussion, the people who came (about 45) got to ask questions of the panel at the end, and the rep from the DoT stressed that this was an initial plan, there was still room for tweaking.  I personally think that congestion pricing will pass in the long run, and I think its important if you want to have a say to do so now, while there's time.  So maybe I'll see you Thursday.

For the occasional commuter I think its important to keep in mind some of the parameters of the proposed plan.  The charge is in effect from 6a-6p, Mon - Fri.  So a sunday drive through the city does not pick up the charge.  Neither would entering the city after 6p to go to dinner or see a show.  Also tolls you'd have to pay anyways, like through the midtown tunnel and the Lincoln tunnel are deducted from the charge, so if you are cutting through the city to go to Jersey, in effect you'll be paying the same tolls post congestion pricing and pre congestion pricing.  And lastly for the occasional commuter its an occasional charge.  Odds are its not going to put a serious dent in your pocket book, regardless of income level, if you are only driving into the city say once a week. 

What is interesting about Barrister15's story and is typical of many of the auto commuters in Queens is that his daily commute is inside Queens, from Astoria to Jamaica, and therefore is not part of the congestion charge.  What I also find interesting is that Jamaica has decent public transit access and in theory (not knowing exactly where in Jamaica he's headed) Barrister could take the subway to there, but he elects to drive.  Which is why we need to promote deterrents to driving and not, as some groups would promote, incentives to not drive, because people often do respond better to a deterrent than an incentive.  I'm not trying to pick on Barrister15 or tell him to change his commute, but it is a good example.

And Alexa is right as well, while it may seem to benefit only Manhattanites, it really will have global benefits that will spread out as less people drive all over.

Here's my driving story.  I used to drive commercial vans as a production assistant and a carpenter's assistant all the time from Chambers St to all over NYC.  I would spend forever sitting in traffic trying to get from the rental place to downtown and then going back uptown again to our client's location.  For the carpenter this was often on the Upper West Side and the Upper East Side.  We'd use all sorts of routes, the highway, not the highway, through the park, etc., and he had lots of traffic theories, but the fact was most of the time we sat in traffic.  On some days this clearly set us behind and cost him money to pay me to sit in traffic by the hour and to take longer to finish projects.

Sorry for the gender mix up, those little gender signs are small and easy to miss.  Your commute is your commute and you obviously feel strongly about it, however it won't be affected by congestion pricing.  Your occasional trips would, and if you enter the city enough for the price to become burdensome than maybe your occasional trips aren't so occasional after all.

As to congestion pricing, like a lot of opponents say that $8 is too much but at the same time that it won't deter many people from driving anyway.  But how can it realistically be too much for people to pay if it's not going to act as a deterrent?  I think of course that the first part, its too much, is accurate while the second part, it won't work is not.  Yes there are people who love their cars and can afford the $40 a week it would take to drive in and probably will continue to do so, but I think this is going to be a financial pinch for a good chunk of drivers and it will force a lot of people to rethink how they get into the city.  And yes, most of those people who need to rethink their commute are not going to be wealthy.  But congestion pricing isn't meant to fix the harsh realities of economic disparities, its meant to reduce the amount of traffic.  And in exchange the money from people who insist on driving, and who will be largely in the upper tiers of income, will go to public transit, already used mainly by people in the lower tiers of income.  And most of those public transit benefits are going to be seen in Queens, B'land and the Bronx, where public transit is need, initially in the form of BRTs.  This is not some sort of Flintstone's vitamin that tastes like candy and does nothing good for you, this is a nasty tasting pill that will make NYC's traffic better.  Once we swallow it we will feel better, I feel that very strongly.

I don't really understand what the lottery or A.C. casinos have to do with congestion pricing.  Beyond the fact that some of you don't believe the money will actually be spent on transit its not relevant.  And to answer your question Debbie, tolls already paid, as long as they are paid via EZ Pass (which I believe is free to set up an account) are deducted from the $8 charge.  So if you were to take the Midtown Tunnel both ways you have already paid the congestion fee, making it $9 (4.50 each way).  Same is true for all toll bridges and tunnels.  The idea is to get people to stop driving through neighborhoods like Astoria to avoid the toll bridges, jamming up the free bridges like QBB, and instead spread the traffic out where it should be.

Anywho the meeting was good, a nice showing and very informative.  We spent about 45 minutes answering questions, people had a lot of concerns but beside one woman who felt that since the QBB is free Astoria should get more of the money (even though currently the B'land and Billyburg Bridges are also free) everyone seemed to support the idea. 

Some interesting stats is that only 4.6% of New Yorkers commute by auto into the proposed congestion pricing zone. (This stat is from the 2000 census)  Of Queens and B'land residents that enter the zone, 3.7% drive and take a toll road (and therefore are already paying to enter the city) or have access to transit and choose not to take it.  0.9% drive and are too far from transit to not drive to the zone.  The remaining 95.4% take transit. (Those stats are from the 2000 census and from a Schaller Consulting report for T.A. Free Parking, Congested Streets.pdf)  Out of all the auto commuters that enter the zone (no transit commuters in these stats) 12% are from Manhattan, 49% are from the suburbs (not NYC) and 39% are from B/land, the Bronx and Queens. (same source as previous).

Looking at the amount of people that drive vs the amount of income they make and that enter the zone, Under 25k, about 35,000 take transit while 18,000 drive.  From 25k-49k about 45,000 take the subway and 25,000 drive.  From 50k-75k about 12,000 take the subway and 16,000 drive.  From 75k-100k about 3,000 take the subway and 6,000 drive and finally looking at the big boys who make plus 100k, about 2,000 take subway and 5,000 drive.  (These stats are from the 2000 census)

Here are some of the specific plans the city has for local transit in Astoria under the PlaNYC.  A QBB Bus/HOV lane, improvements (safer bus stops more buses) to the Q18, Q1010 and the QM22.  The Q19A will be extended across the QBB, using that bus/HOV lane.  Bus stops under the N/W will be made safer and generally more inviting.  Improved ped and bus access to the 36th Ave N/W station.  New bike lanes and a congestion study on the Northern Blvd corridor which will then lead the DoT to a plan of action to address the congestion there.  On top of that there's plans to extend the bus and light rail lines back into eastern Queens, as well as into B'Land and the Bronx.  (This is from the official PlaNYC)

Whats been preventing this from happening is that there simply hasn't been the money.  The city is budgeting for 50 billion (thats billion with a b) over the next 30 years for these improvements and many others citywide, and the city has 20.1 billions.  So they're short 30.9.  The idea is that congestion pricing will provide a healthy chunk of this, projecting a NET income of 400 million the first year.  The city has $220 million in the NYC capital budget to start with the immediate improvements that would go in effect before congestion pricing starts.  Mayor Bloomberg will commit $220 million per year starting in 2008 rising to $275 million per year in 2012 if the state provides matching funds.  And there is currently $400 million in federal funding for NYC to pilot congestion pricing, so putting the plan into effect shouldn't cost the tax payers anything and shouldn't take away from funds promised for transit improvements.  I've already explained the SMART fund, that will most likely go into effect with or without congestion pricing, but congestion pricing should provide a healthy chunk towards it.  (also PlaNYC).

So there you have it, a handful of stats and info to mull over.  Hopefully this will answer some of the questions out there, if not, I recommend you read the transportation section of the PlaNYC report (you can even order a hardcopy delivered to your home free of charge) or attend one of the information meetings yourself and ask your own questions.  I can't read minds but I'm guessing the mods here won't be nuts about us continuing this discussion on the calendar forums after the event is over so you might want to move this discussion back to where it began here.  Or not, I don't care, I've done my part for now.  Everyone that supports congestion pricing would do well to write, email or call your state reps and let them know you live in Astoria and you support congestion pricing.  Everyone else should just keep it to themselves.

I'm open to the idea of congestion pricing as a way to limit the number of cars coming into the city...but as Astoria residenta, we live on the edge of the zone there's a strong possibility of creating more traffic in our neighborhood. We're already saddled with pollution spewing power plants. We don't need more car pollution as well.

Currently Astoria residents get a lot of thru traffic from outside residents who drive through our hood to take the free QBB instead of the toll Tri-Boro or Midtown Tunnel.  What this does is no longer provide motorists with an incentive to avoid the toll bridges and tunnels because none of them are free.  This also goes for people upstate and from CT that drive through the Bronx avoiding their toll bridges as well.  So there won't be more traffic through Astoria there will be less.  This has already been covered a couple times in this very thread.

And it won't require getting all these agencies involved, the city will set up the system and the money goes into the SMART fund which is used to pay for MTA and DoT transit improvements.  Something interesting Bloomberg said at the hearing today was that the Sec of Transportaion has bascially given him a verbal promise that if NYC puts in place congestion pricing we will get between 400-500 million of this 1.1b one time grant the federal gov't is given to cities that are engineering new ways to reduce auto traffic.  It will take about 280 million to get the plan in place leaving between 120-220 million to spend on transit before the plan goes into effect (plus the mayor has pledged about 225 million before the plan goes into effect on top of that).  So even if this plan doesn't work, doesn't reduce traffic, and doesn't pull in the 600 million gross (I think they quoted operating costs at about 200 million) in the first year they are aticipating, after the 3 year pilot plan is over the city still got the system for free (with fed money) and extra left over for transit.  And I think it will turn a profit anyways.

As for everyone that would like more acurate census figures.  They don't have more recent census figures until the next census.  Every city agency uses this figures as their base, both proponents of congestion pricing and opponents.  I believe the make allowances for changes between then and now, but thats what the government has to work with as an accepted neutral base for stats, any other stats would accused of being biased for the study. Aaaaand most of the statistics came from the 2000 census and a study put together by T.A (which I had linked).  So the numbers are more recent than that anyways.
