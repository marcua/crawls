In this case I have to agree with 28Grand. People have been complaining for some time about Steinway Street. Despite this being a working class community of immigrants, it was an excellent shopping area since the building boom of the 20s and even before that, with the trolleys running down to the piano factory, the backbone for the northern part of Long Island City.

Scheutzen Park, a beer garden like Bohemian Hall that was at Steinway and Broadway was the premier gathering place in this part of Queens - presidential candidates (like Wilson, for example) would stop by there and hold rallies at the park when running for office.

Yes, you talk to old timers, and Steinway and Broadway was loaded with stores carrying merchandise like the type sold by Raleigh's. The furniture stores (remember Rose or Sig Stern?) were big there too.

A number of movie theaters (and ice cream parlors) made it the entertainment location of choice for generations.

The problem, however, is that the street has taken a sharp turn for the worse in the past decade.

Block after block of Middle Eastern places in the north, and Spanish places in the south, although catering to their clientale, limits the broader appeal of the street, and frankly, for whatever the reason, turns off lots of people. Now you may argue the merits of such thinking, but to turn off people is a dangerous thing to do for a retail area.

The quality of merchandise, and type of stores, has both changed sharply for the lower end at a speeded  pace in the last decade or so. If someone has one the posters of Streinway Street that was done in the 80s, and compare the listing of stores then, with today's fare, it is not a pretty sight.

Add to it the stores pumping out music, cell phones, 99 cent stores, hip hop merchandise, and rats (who is that guy at 34th Avenue that leaves a big grease spot where his cart is?) and you get a shopping experience that turns off lots of people. Again, you can argue the merits of their thinking, but a shopping area's reputation is important. I have heard from many people who spent their entire lives in this community in the past year suggest they are not going back to Steinway.

I go back to El Mundo Discount. Yes it is crowded so it must satisfy some need. But I walk by it I keep seeing flashbacks of the Lower East Side where stores were dirty and crowded, too. Was that something good? All I can say is if it was, why did LaGuadia, a product of that evirnment, do everthing he could to clean it up?

The point is just because people are immigrant, or poor, does not make it justified for them to shop in a pig sty. Working class communities always had Woolworths or Kresge's or Lambston. They did not look like dumps. A pig sty insults the people that shop there and the community around it.

Post by: neo11 on January 27, 2008, 01:25:18 AM
The thread started with a discussion about Steinway Street, and its fitting that Steinway get this sort of attention, because it is still the main shopping strip of Astoria, or at the very least, that's what it is known as being.

Regarding mom-and-pop shops, which many people lament the loss of, I think that what most people seem to miss from these shops, more than anything else, is the quality of the merchandise that was sold there.  There were truly many stores that you could compare to the boutiques that are on Austin Street (which is often used as a yardstick against which Steinway is measured on this board).  It didn't really matter that they were not affiliated to national chains, but what matters now is that the stores that have moved in, both chain and independent, have dramatically lowered their standards and the quality of the product that they offer.  The upkeep of many of these stores is bad, the presentation is bad, the service is often bad, and the appearance outside, from the garbage to the merchandise thrown all over the place at stores like El Mundo, really cheapens Steinway even more so than it already has been.  Appearances count for a lot, and with many people moving to Astoria in recent years from other parts of the city and the country, let alone the immigrants that continue to move into Astoria, their first appearance of Astoria's "main" street is probably, quite often, not a positive one.

Regarding the high rents, which someone brought up elsewhere in this thread: yes, there still seem to be many stores on Steinway that are able to somehow make ends meet with the high rents, and yes, that probably means that they are generating enough business to survive.  But take a closer look.  It seems that in recent years, more and more stores have been going out of business on Steinway Street, and many spaces have remained vacant for months, even years.  Look at the space where Kay-Bee toys used to be.  A nice, prime retail spot at the heart of Steinway Street, with two floors.  Nothing has been in there for over two years, and only recently did a (you guessed it) discount store open in part of the first floor space only.  The rest remains vacant.  And that's not even counting the many other stores that, in recent years, seem to be cycling through different ownership, opening and closing and re-opening again as something else.  That, to me, is not the sign of a healthy shopping district, and it's come at a time when Astoria has otherwise been booming...not just the housing boom, but truly a huge growth in many other parts of the neighborhood, with many new businesses, restaurants, etc. opening elsewhere in Astoria.  It seems that most of Astoria, economically, has been moving in one direction, and Steinway, which is supposed to be the center of economic activity in Astoria, has been moving in the opposite direction.

Even 10 or 15 years ago, it was much rarer to see a vacant storefront on Steinway Street, and vacancies did not stay that way for as long as they seem to in recent years, even before the recent economic crises.  Here's another indicator that things seem to be going bad: parking.  I recall that even a decade ago, it was almost impossible to find a parking spot on Steinway Street.  Now, there always seems to be spaces available, at times where you would expect it not to be so easy.  That to me is a sign that people in Astoria are taking their cars (and their business) elsewhere, and people from elsewhere are not coming to Astoria to shop.

As far as Astoria's affluence, or lack thereof, certainly it is something that major chains look at when deciding whether a certain area is worthy of a franchise.  But there's less affluent areas that *do* have the likes of Trader Joe's, or B&N, or Borders.  And, there has been strong growth in Astoria in the number of college-educated professionals that live in the neighborhood.  Certainly, the area doesn't seem to have a problem supporting two Starbucks, or the many other independently owned cafes (which are not any cheaper than Starbucks in most cases!), or Best Buy, or indie organic and health food stores.  If anything, I've noticed that many "lower-end" (for lack of a better term) chains seem not to prosper in Astoria.  Just among fast-food chains alone: KFC has closed twice on Steinway, and once on Ditmars.  Ditto Burger King.  Taco Bell on Steinway closed.  Starbucks, on the other hand, seems to be thriving in Astoria.

People keep pointing out Forest Hills.  Personally, I think that while the numbers show that Forest Hills is a more affluent area, those numbers are skewed.  Forest Hills does have a very affluent area, you know, with the beautiful, large old homes, the old tennis center, and so forth.  However, much of Forest Hills is comprised of old and large apartment complexes and tenements, and features both a very high immigrant population, as well as a very high percentage of senior citizens, many of whom are presumably living on fixed incomes.  The rest of Forest Hills, as you go south towards Metropolitan Avenue, closely resembles the residential areas of Astoria.  So, my feeling is, if it wasn't for that wealthy region in one part of Forest Hills, you have an area that is not much different from Astoria in many other ways, including the fact that, like Astoria, it is also easily accessible by subway, and as such, has become attractive to more and more people who have moved out of Manhattan in recent years.  And, that being the case, there's no reason why Astoria can't support the same type of stores that Forest Hills seems to have gotten (in the case of Trader Joes and B&N) or has retained (in the case of the many independent boutiques and other small, "quality" establishments that still line Austin Street).  Sure, rents may be expensive on Steinway, but I doubt they are any less expensive on Austin Street, and that shopping strip, compared to Steinway, is thriving.

Actually, another thing to observe is that while Astoria's restaurant and cafe scene is quite diverse and dynamic and has really grown a lot in recent years, none of that progress has made it to Steinway Street.  If anything, there's fewer food choices there now than there were several years ago, despite no shortage of vacant stores where a restaurant or cafe could open.  At least some old establishments, like Rizzo's or Villa Gaudio (okay, that's right off of Steinway) are managing to hang on.  But this is another area where the growth and development of Astoria is not reflected in the main shopping street of Astoria. 

Before anyone points out the Middle Eastern places on Steinway north of 28th avenue, allow me to point out that the shopping strip on Steinway has always been centered between 28th and 34th avenues, and especially between 30th Avenue and Broadway.  Anything north of 28th and south of 35th has really been the periphery, as far as Steinway is concerned.  I'm not taking away from the importance of those areas, especially now, but I'm just trying to keep the discussion focused on the central part of Steinway Street, which is what I think most people have on their mind when they discuss or debate Steinway's decline.

So, when pointing out the many problems that seem to exist on Steinway Street and the many changes for the worse that it has taken, I think it's unfair to blame "dirty" immigrants, because Astoria has always been a community that has seen a great influx of immigrants, from the Germans and the Irish way back, to the Greeks and the Italians, to the Bangladeshis, Pakistanis, Hispanics/Latin Americans and Eastern Europeans that are coming to Astoria today, not to mention the increasing number of folks from elsewhere in the United States that now reside in New York and call Astoria their home. 

I think it's also unfair to shrug off any talk of a decline on Steinway Street on some sort of misguided feelings of nostalgia that some people may be having.  I think that there's many valid points to be made about the direction that Steinway Street has gone towards, in relation to the rest of the neighborhood, and it's better to debate those points on face value rather than to dismiss them.

Finally, to simplify the argument into a debate as to whether one supports chain establishments or not is also misguided, in my view.  I think that taking a "you're with us or against us" type of view of chains is wrong, because, simply put, there's levels of quality when it comes to chain establishments.  If someone doesn't like Applebee's for their mediocre, pre-packaged food, that doesn't mean that they should automatically hate Trader Joe's, for instance.  If anything, I don't feel that most chain establishments on Steinway are part of the problem, and that's even though I'm not a big fan of most of them personally.  I feel that what cheapens the look of Steinway is the overall *bad* quality of stores, which is a combination of some really bad independent stores (so, no, there is no blind love for "mom-and-pop" stores here, quality still matters), along with the realization that there are many *voids* in Astoria, while subsequently, there are many vacancies on Steinway Street that offer opportunities for those voids to be filled.  Though, I will say, that on the topic of chain stores, some of Astoria's existing offerings are still mediocre compared to those same stores elsewhere.  The Gap on Steinway, even after its recent renovation, is tiny compared to the Gap on Austin Street.  Express on Steinway only has women's clothing, but no Express Men, whereas I've always seen the two together at every other Express I've seen in Queens or elsewhere.  So yes, the quality, overall, is lower, but it's a simplification to just blame it all on too many chains, or not enough chains, or so forth.

Title: Re: Changes all over
Post by: Squirrel on January 27, 2008, 10:10:15 PM
Can you photograph the graffiti? We found that a very effective way to pressure property owners into cleaning up is to send them a "photo letter." Here's what we do:

1.  We take a digital picture.

2.  We write a formal letter, and print the picture directly onto the document. We don't attach a photo, which could get lost, and can't be faxed or scanned so easily.

3.  In the case of a business, we look up an upper-level manager's name, and address it to them.  Not to the CEO, but some regional VP or whomever.

4.  We print the letter on a good quality color printer and send it.

This worked very well on several occasions, including with a GM dealership. It's especially effective with big business chains.  Often senior officials in a company have no idea that their local branches are covered with graffiti, and when they find out that local management has allowed it, boy do they get mad!

With smaller properties, just look up the owner and send it to them. Always try and get a name. That shows that you are resourceful, and you know with whom you are dealing.

Hope this helps.
