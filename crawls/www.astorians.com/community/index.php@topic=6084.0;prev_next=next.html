Rant on NYC developments
« on: April 18, 2007, 11:16:46 AM »
First off, let me state that I am a free markets kind of guy, however, we New Yorkers need to begin to make a stink about these heinous developments going up throughout the city, specifically the LES and village area. These neighborhoods have a feel, a certain appearance to them, yet these monstrosities that I see on sites like curbed belong on Mars. I work in tribeca, and there is a hotel going up across the street, at least at part owned by Robert DeNiro. The building is classy and fits the general appearance of the neighborhood, beautiful brick exterior with big windows. I have no problem with old building being replaced, but developers, and politicians especially, have a responsibility to maintain the traits of our hoods.

Thanks for starting this thread. I am in need of a rant space. First, let me say that aesthetics alone won't preserve a community. Just look what's being done to Harlem. The psuedo-brownstones and row houses that are being newly constructed on raised blocks sure do blend in with the surroundings. But, if you see what they're going for and who's moving in to them they no longer resemble the neighborhood.

On another note...

I was standing on the platform at Queensboro Plaza looking at an existing condo and another one going up on the next block when my eye stopped on the word "luxury" in the beautifully designed banner wrapping the scaffolding. It struck me so hard that I was cringing the entire way home. Perhaps it was the just timing as I've been thinking about the concept a lot lately, but I really felt it more than ever before. Not that I was feeling sentimental for QBP. Please, that place is a sh!thole of noise and traffic and abandoned office/warehouse space (no offense to any artists living and working there). But it was the mere concept of luxury and those who persue it that practically knocked the wind out of me. The concept of "luxury" pretty much covers the majority of the seven deadly sins with sloth and wrath being the possible exceptions. Real estate developers market this concept from offices in a high towers far from future condo sites to individuals who desire such a thing in their [otherwise meaningless?] lives. Those individuals are moving to NYC--one of the few places the real estate slump hasn't really affected--in droves to snatch up their McCondos before they're all gone. Manhattan is gone. Move on. Forget about it. Brooklyn is going, going, gone to the highest bidder! And Queens (at least the Western swath of it) is up for grabs. Is anybody doing anything about this? Am I?

The concept of capitalism and consumerism? And you live in NYC? You must be sick very, very often.

Yes, I am sick quite often.  :-P  And I don't agree that "luxury" and consumerism are synonymous. The pursuit of luxury transcends the need and acquisition of essential goods and services. It's the desire to elevate of oneself above others for what I perceive as purely vain reasons that sickens me. Yes, I know I live in the epicenter of this activity. Yes, I know that our whole economic and social system is based on the reality that in order for there to be "haves" there simply need to be "have-nots." I know, I should just give up and move away to Ecotopia and stick my head in the sand, right? WRONG.

Anyway, if it sounded like I was COMPLETELY against development, let me clear that up. I am not. I AM completely against unregulated development. I also don't agree that NYC needs more housing--luxury or otherwise. There are already too many people here. NYC's not like other cities that can sprawl and grow horizontally. NYC must grow vertically in order to create more housing. Vertical expansion leads to greater pedestrian and vehicle congestion and that's not what I want to see happen here [especially not in Astoria  ]. New York's crowded enough. And now I hear there's talk about the fact that there are around 2,200 empty lots in NYC that can be used for housing development. Why housing? Why not build green spaces and community gardens and recreation facilities for kids, and sports fields, etc, that support the folks who already live here? Why not make NYC a better place to live instead of a more crowded, congested, poluted, fill-in-the-blank place?

Oh, I love smell of a rant in the morning.
