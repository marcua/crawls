I think the recent success of Republican candidates for mayor in NYC has as much to do with very weak competition and certain circumstances as it does with actual party affiliation.  It's a known fact that the large majority of voters in the five boroughs are registered Democrats, and that turnout for the Democratic primaries is typically far larger than that for the Republican primaries as a result.

That said, Giuliani came on the scene in 1993, defeating his opponent, David Dinkins, at a time when crime had spiraled out of control in NYC.  Four years later, and with all due respect, Ruth Messinger wasn't a very strong opponent and Giuliani coasted to re-election.  Bloomberg then came on the scene and as many will remember here, he was originally far behind in the polls.  However, one can argue that he benefited from Giuliani's "star power" after 9/11...Rudy's endorsement was BIG for Bloomberg and definitely helped him get over the top.  For Bloomberg's re-election, I think his opponent, Fernando Ferrer, was widely seen as "more of the same" from the Democratic party and as a retread.  He also didn't come close to having the money and resources that the Bloomberg empire had at its disposal.

That said, New York City has consistently gone towards the Democrats in most other races, including the Presidential race.  You cited Reagan in 1984, but the Democratic candidate, Walter Mondale, was probably the weakest major Presidential candidate in recent history.  Didn't Mondale carry only three states?  Therefore it's no surprise that Reagan did, indeed, carry New York that year.  However, Republicans have not been able to accomplish that since then.
