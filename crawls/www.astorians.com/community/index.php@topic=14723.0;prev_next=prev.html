When I learned to drive I was a student in a private high school in Manhattan and it was one of the courses they taught.  We had regular classroom time to study a text book on driving and then we had group practice in the car (taking turns at the wheel driving, making Y turns, parking).  You got to hear the critique of the other students as well as your own.  I've also seen that community colleges used to have this kind of class in their continuing ed programs, so they might still.  Also, try asking at the private high schools because they also have opened up driving classes to the general public.

As for Ferarri - a few years ago a tv station did an investigation into schools that teach truck-driving, and the lack of quality truck drivers on the road.  An employee of Ferarri, in shadow, said "You know how you see these truck drivers and you wonder how they got their licenses?  They're from us."  That sealed my impression of the school.

I didn't learn to drive until last year (I was 27-grew up in Queens, what did I need a car for?) I recommend Ferrari. I took the 20 lesson package, and even though I passed my test (though I should have failed since the examiner hit the brake) I should have taken more. I am still very scared to drive, especially my monster of a car, and I think if I had more lessons I would be more confident. It also may have had to do with the instructors. 2 out of 3 taught you to pass the test, the other (who i found all too late) taught you how to drive. His philosophy was if you can't get on the Grand Central and drive, or if you can't do things like drive in reverse, you shouldn't be taking the test. I wasn't a fan of Sam, he made me very nervous b/c he was so quiet. Susan was very calming and zen, but like I said, she only taught me what I needed to pass. Ed on the other hand, was the best. He took my mind off of being nervous and threw me on the GCP my first time out with him (past the 10 lesson mark). He's great.
I really think at least a few lessons should be done with a driving school-their cars have the secondary brake. Which is helpful if you accelerate when you should be braking. Also, when you have to take the test, they supply you with one of their cars, and your last lesson is a refresher right before the exam. If you don't have an arrangement with a driving school it means you have to borrow someone's car and their time since they will have to take you to the test site.
BTW the test is ridiculously easy (case in point-I passed it). I was talking to some folks from Ireland about their driving test and you actually have to drive backwards around a corner and crazy stuff like that. This is why there are so many bad drivers on the road-our standards to become one are LOW! (and let's not forget the permit test is given in like 15 languages, but all the road signs are in English!!! That's just wrong)
So yeah, I would say take a lesson or two, especially with Ed at Ferrari if you can, and then decide if you want the whole package. Otherwise you'll have to find someone willing to take the time to teach you AND trust you behind the wheel of their car AND have the patience to deal with it.
I actually have to have John take me out over the next few weeks in the evenings to get me reacclimated with driving. You should have seen how long it took me to park yesterday, and it wasn't parallel! I still can't wrap my head around which way to turn the wheel while in reverse to make it go in certain directions (i had to call him to verify which way to turn the wheel while reversing out of the driveway to straighten it out). John even tried showing me with a remote control car and I still don't get it. I told you I needed more lessons!
