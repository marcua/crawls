i'd say that if your management company warned you about bug problems then someone probably has them and they are trying to fix it. However they should do the whole building and not just which apartments have the infestation as they will just move to a chemical free apartment.
do everything the instructions say. make sure they send the exterminator more than once. and be vigilent about vacuuming and keep checking your matress. wrap both matress and box spring in plastic. industrial grade from home depot if you can. line your walls by the rug/carpet with painters tape and lay double sided tape on it. do the same with your bed frame legs. this will trap em if they are coming from down below. keep nothing on your walls for a while and make sure to plug up all holes and cracks with expanding foam and caulk.
and read all our old tips from the bug threads.

there's no harm in talking to your neighbors. if you don't want to start out telling them that you have them. just bring the notice you got and ask them if they've had any problems.

GOOD LUCK!!

After reading this forum's other bedbug thread last week, my stomach hurt from thinking that moving to western Queens might mean moving to bedbug-central.  It immediately triggered my catch-all anxiety management response - i.e. I ran to dr. google.  I spent a large part of the weekend poring over the archives of http://bedbugger.com.  It's this super-comprehensive blog about all things "bedbug."  Lots of resources and FAQs. 

Warning - the deluge of info was unsettling at first.  But ultimately, all the good info proved comforting.  Since the problem is worldwide, and people are suffering in far away places like Stockholm and Sydney, I figure I'm vulnerable to bedbugs almost anywhere on the planet - not only in NYC.  And most importantly, it seems the bedbugs can be defeated if you follow proper protocols.  Just knowing that fact made me feel better.

As for me - I used to spend days roaming second-hand furniture stores & antique flea markets hunting for "treasures."  Yeah, those days are OVER.

hardkore -- sorry to hear about your recent discovery.  As for moving, it might not even be a 100%-guaranteed solution.  There were anecdotes at the bedbugger blog from a couple of people who chose limited or no treatment and, instead, just moved away from their bedbug-infested apartment.  Unfortunately, the bedbugs followed them to their new place.  Or maybe it was a new batch at the new place.  One woman abandoned all her possessions: walked away with the clothes on her back and not much else.  And still, the suckers turned up at her new apt.  That's soul-crushing stuff.  Something to consider when deciding whether to fight or flee.  In any case, best of luck to you.  Hope your victory over these bugs is swift and total. 

aquaecny -- wow.  What an ineffective response from the school.  The bedbugs found on these kids might not have even originated from their homes.  There might be colonies hanging out in the school itself.  Bitten the kid after crawling from its hiding space in the school radiator (?)  Doesn't the principal or whoever the decision-maker is care that one of these bugs might hitch a ride to her house?

As an update:

My management company sent me an email of points to follow prior to the exterminator visit. The exterminator also called prompty and we've set up an appointment. He also gave me points to follow. Yesterday, I vaccuumed my bedroom for about 3 hours and sprayed rubbing alcohol on the mattress, along baseboards, used it to wipe out shoes, etc. Bagged all clothes which are going to be run through the dryer a couple times at high heat and then vaccuumed sealed in space bags and put in the bathtub. It can be hard to tell if you have bedbugs, especially since they are so good at hiding, you can't feel them biting you, and it can often take more than a day for bites to appear.

I "inherited" them when I moved in to my Astoria apartment last fall. My friend who had the room before me left her bedroom furniture. She had brought them back with her from Florida (she slept with her suitcase on the bed when she got back) . She had no idea they were there - thought they were mosquito bites.

It took me a few weeks to figure out what they were (at which point I was COVERED in itchy red blotches), then about a month to get rid of them. I came very close to throwing everything out, but I did find this stuff, which works:

http://www.bed-bug.net/bedbugproducts.html

If you do have them, I would HIGHLY recommend this combo: the Bed Bug Patrol Spray to kill them immediately AND the Fossil Shell Dust to prevent them from coming back for up to a year. Both are all-natural (I think the first is made from plant oils, the second from crushed fossil shells) and non-toxic, which was important for me since I have two cats. I also didn't want to sleep in chemicals.

If your bed has a wood frame, check there too - mine were on the underside of the frame. You will need to strip all bedding and either discard it or boil it. Then spray all other components of the bed with the Patrol Spray. Once that dries, apply the Fossil Dust to the bed and on the floor all around it. You may need to do this more than once.

The Fossil Dust is a little messy - it looked like a coke fiend lived in my room for a few weeks when the ceiling fan kicked up the dust. And sadly this stuff isn't cheap - I think with shipping I paid around $75, but the site now says that they have free shipping, which must be new. But it's cheaper than an exterminator OR getting new furniture, which can get its own infestation if they are in other places as well.

I sincerely hope you don't have them, but I know Astoria has a high rate of bedbugs. If you do some googling, you'll find tons of pages on identifying them and saving your stuff.

Good Luck!
