Title: Re: Wedding cakes
Post by: NYCGoalie on July 11, 2007, 10:48:11 AM
As the groom I didn't really want to do anything, so my wife made me in charge of getting the cake.  Hey, I liked to eat cake, so I thought it was going to be easy.  It was actually a pretty long process and I'm glad she didn't have to deal with it (all this extra work would have made her more of a Bridezilla than she was during the planning stages). 

Ultimately, I had my wedding cake in 2005 done by Luguli's Bakery on Ditmars.  I decided to go local since I had my wedding at Astoria Manor and didn't want the cake to get potentially damaged by a long commute.  Also, the wedding hall cake (I thought) wasn't very good.  Plus, I even found out that most halls only give your guest a sheet cake, while they wheel out a plastic prop cake for photo's.  An idea I thought was kinda cheesy.

The woman who owned Luguli at the time made an incredible cake for us...the bottom layer was made of fresh peaches, then two layers of white vanilla cake over that, with the middle of the two layers made of french vanilla cream and chocolate chips.  Everyone loved it (granted, I'm a sugarholic, but who isn't)!!!

But in 2006, the woman sold Luguli back to the original family that owned the bakery.  However, for our one year anniversary, I asked them to make a much smaller version of our wedding cake (we weren't going to freeze a piece as tradition dictates.  I found that idea kinda disgusting).  Since ours was a custom cake, we showed them a picture of what the woman had produced for the wedding.  They replicated it pretty well, though it did taste a little different this time around.  Then again, different bakers always produce different results.

Matter of fact, the three layer model they have in the window is based on the cake made for my wedding.  They had a different cake in the window prior to my "anniversary edition" which they made for me.
_____________

I also second the endorsement for Artopolis.  I've had several cakes done for smaller family events and they all came out great.  More expensive though, but hey, it's a wedding.

There are a couple of good places in the City that specialize in wedding cakes as well.  Yes, they are REALLY expensive but you can't deny they have a long track record and even more flexibility in designs/offerings than bakeries who produce wedding cakes as a side project. 

My suggestion is to get a solid number of how many guest you're going to have (don't use a range of guest as a guide...get a solid number).  This lets you know how big a cake you'll need and how it will ultimately be designed.  Then try out different bakeries; forget about bakeries that say "we can make it however you want"...that means they have no experience with wedding cakes.  Wedding shows held at banquet halls throughout the City also let you try out many different bakers (though truth be told, after my wife dragged me to my third one of those, I was done with them...they get repetative and stupid).  After you get tired from all the tastings, make your comparisons and when you have two or three bakeries in mind, start the negotiating process.  When discussing the price, have them figure out how much the cake will cost per slice and THEN make your decision.  This is all going to take some time but then again, the presentation of the cake is probably one of the most memorable parts of the event (how often do you ever hear someone say two years later "hey, those hor devours were fantastic!!"). 

Skimp on other things...don't skimp on the cake.

Post by: NYCGoalie on July 11, 2007, 03:11:20 PM
Quote from: haner on July 11, 2007, 10:52:51 AM
Does it make sense to get a cake smaller than the expected amount of people showing up?  Not everyone will eat it and they can always cut the servings a little smaller.  Soutine on the UWS quoted me $7/serving but I'm sure those are decent sized servings.  If I'm expecting 150 people, it may make sense to say 100 are showing, then cut the servings smaller.  Since you have experience in this, what do you think?
You definately do not want a cake which is designed to serve fewer guest.  First, the staff at the banquest hall cut slices for everyone; they never ask people if they want cake or not until they arrive with the plate at the table.  Second, if they cut the slices smaller, you can't really taste the cake.  And if you can't taste the cake, what the the point of having a good cake?!?!

This is why I specified that a cake needs to be based on the actual number of people attending.  Makes the whole process easier.

$7-8 buck a slice is a little above average, but not too much.  Although one "top-of-the-line" place I tasted was an astromical $12 a slice.  Yes, the cake tasted great.  And yes, the designs they had were cool.  But $12 per slice was just way too much for me.  If I had a smaller wedding, maybe. 

Again, if you know you have 150 comming, then maybe shoot for the $4-5 range if you can.  8 times 150 is $1,200...which is a lot of dough (pun intended).  Now you're starting to see how the price per slice (instead of price per pound) is a better method of seeing how much and how big a cake you'll need.
