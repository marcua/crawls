My family (Irish/German) escaped Manhattan in the late 1880's and moved to the "county" - Astoria. They started in the Lower east side and made their way up to Hells Kitchen.  Their living quarters were to small and dirty - due to local industry.
My family has always told me that every morning the kitchen had to be washed down and the dishes rewashed due to the soot that accumulated during the night from the local factories - hence, a hell to clean the kitchen every morning = Hells Kitchen. That was their version :)
The open spaces began to disappear once the bridges & subways were developed, even some graveyards.I have ancestors that are/were buried in Astoria Village cemetery which is now no more.  Everytime I walk down 18th street & 25th rd (by Astoria Park), I say a prayer to my buried relatives - family lore was that they were buried in that area :)

Astoria was first settled by Europeans ca. 1640s by Willian Hallet (Hallet's Cove), an Englishman who obtained a patent for the land in the vicinity of the cove.  It was rural farmland for several generations.  The Village of Astoria was established by Stephen Halsey in the early 19th Century.  It was named after John Jacob Astor, the weathy fur trader and New York land owner who had a house across the river near where Gracie Mansion now stands.  This was an attempt to get Astor to invest in the area; it was unsuccessful.  After the Civil War, it became a fashionable area, with many mansions in the area of "the hill", 27th Avenue, from the river up to 14th Street.  Most of these were (disgracefully) torn down in the 1960s before any attempts were made to landmark the area of Old Astoria.  The village itself was obliterated after World War II by the building of the Astoria Houses project.  Steinway was a seperate village for the workers at the piano factory.  Some of the houses till sdtand in the vicinity of 20th Avenue and Steinway Street.  By far your best resource for research is the Greater Astoria Historical Society.  Check out their website.
