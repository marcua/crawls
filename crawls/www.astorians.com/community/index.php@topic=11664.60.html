Roasted chicken is one of my BF's favorite dishes!  Last Sunday-since it wasn't too hot, I decided to roast a whole chicken ( a "broiler"-don't like the "roasters"). I always kosher mine by letting it soak in cold water for a bit, drain, then sprinkle lots of kosher salt all over it, and then let it sit for 45 minutes or so. This process alone makes for a real tasty bird! I always mince lots of garlic, mix it with some cold butter in a bowl and stick the mixture under the chicken skin- the breast, back, thighs, drumstick-wherever you can loosen the skin, throw some whole peeled cloves of garlic into the cavity and squeeze lots of lemon juice over it, sprinkle with salt and pepper. Then with the drippings I make beer gravy. Yum! We had leftover breast meat and the next day I just heated the slices in a pan with the leftover beer gravy. Don't forget the mashed potatoes and peas - great comfort food!

theres nothing like a pot of homemade chicken noodle soup in the winter.
Take a whole chicken. Put in a pot. Cover with water.
Cut up chunks of carrots, celery onions, a few cloves of garlic, a small handful of peppercorns, fresh herbs (or dried).
Bring to boil, lower to simmer.
When chicken falls off the bone remove chicken and put in a bowl. Let cool.
Let broth cool. Strain fat off. Take out veg.
Pull meat from chicken, put into broth.
Cut up whatever veg you want and add to soup. Add noodles at the last minute (not too many though)!!

mmmmmmm
screw canned soup! Homemade is so much cheaper and better for you!

Quiche is also a great way to save on breakfast/lunch

mix together (beat well)
6 eggs
1/2 cup heavy cream or half and half
1/2 cup of whatever cheese you like, shredded
then add whatever meat or veg you want; pour into pie crust, bake

All right - it's not very specific, but here are the things you'd need for vegetarian chili a la essen:

vegetable oil
2 cloves garlic, minced
1 medium yellow onion, chopped
1 green pepper, chopped
1 package white mushrooms, cut into wedges, 4 or 6 wedges per mushroom depending on size
1 large can whole peeled tomatoes (whatever the biggest standard size can is)
1 can black beans
1 can red beans
approx 1/4 cup of bulgar wheat
approx 2 TBSP Badia chili powder (I use Badia brand, but you may want less depending on what brand you use)
approx 2 TSP cumin
some ground black pepper
dash of cinnamon

Get out a very large pan or pot. Cut the green pepper first and sauté it in the vegetable oil, and as it's cooking cut up the garlic and onions. The pepper should sauté on low-medium heat for at least 10 minutes before adding the garlic and onions, and those should cook for a few minutes. Before you add anything else, the peppers should be starting to look cooked and the onions should be softening.

While that's cooking, cut up the mushrooms and then add those at the appropriate time and mix it all around. Open up the tomatoes and break them into the mix, including the juice from the can, and then add the two cans of beans. (You may want to drain the bean juice, but I like leaving in some of it. I like the organic beans from Key Food; the juice in the other stuff skeeves me.) Then add the spices. Then add the bulgar wheat. Add as much of the bulgar as you think you want - for me it serves to provide a meaty texture without being "fake meat," but the chili shouldn't be overwhelmed by it, so if I were to measure it I think it's around 1/4 cup.

The wheat will absorb the juice from the tomatoes and bean cans. This should all simmer for at least 20 minutes. Stir it occasionally and taste it to make sure you like the spice level. Add whatever other spices you want; keep in mind that the chili that results from this is not spicy at all, but it is tasty. If you like spicy chili you will need to add your own spicy things. Simmer until it's not so liquidy and the mushrooms have shrunk. I like serving it with rice, and I cook the rice in a method inspired by Chipotle, which is to cook it with fresh cilantro and lime. But that's optional.

Served with rice, I would say this makes 8-10 servings.

Tasty recipe I just learned after eating some amazing Cuban food in Miami a few weekends ago:

Beef Picadillo

1 lb ground beef (or any kind of ground meat - turkey, tofu, etc.)
1 yellow onion, chopped
1 green bell pepper, chopped
1 clove garlic, minced
1 8 oz. can tomato sauce
2 bay leaves
1/4 cup chopped pimento olives (and a few extras thrown in if you like them)
salt & garlic to taste

saute onion, garlic and green pepper until soft, add beef
once beef is cooked, add tomato sauce, olives and bay leaves
simmer for 30 minutes, serve over white rice or with tortillas

AMAZING! it is so flavorful and not to mention you feel like you are getting the real culture experience and it's cheap  :-P
