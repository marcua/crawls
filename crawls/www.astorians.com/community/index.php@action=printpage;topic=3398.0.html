Title: Re: How do I become an expatriate?
Post by: schmennifer on September 14, 2006, 11:54:04 AM
I lived in Japan for six years. Like you, I just wanted to live in another country for a while. I didn't have any specific interest in Japan before. I picked it because I knew it was one of the easiest places to get a job and a work visa. I also wanted to learn more about Asia firsthand, but knew that most of the other countries would present too much of a culture shock for me. So I just needed a place to live and work that gave me easy access to the other areas.

A friend from a previous job had worked at an English school in the countryside near Hiroshima, and he highly recommended it. They hired me and I moved without knowing a word of Japanese (other than the obvious--sayonara, arigatou, etc.). I picked up a lot more while living there, of course. I don't remember how much my expenses were, but I had a lot of money from the Internet bubble, so it wasn't a worry. However, many of the other teachers at the school were able to borrow against their salary to move there.

I was there for a year, and then I moved to Tokyo. I knew I wanted to experience a Japanese city too, so it was either Tokyo or Osaka. I interviewed for four jobs and accepted the first offer I got. That employer renewed my visa.

I didn't get too homesick because, like I said, Japan wasn't as big a culture shock as, say, Thailand would be. I do notice that among the foreigners in Japan, they tend to love it for the first 3 or 4 years, then they resent and even hate it for a few years. Eventually, they go back to loving it. (Except for my friend Ann, who loves and hates it at the same time.) The most frustrated people there are the ones who can't accept that they'll never be fully embraced by Japanese society. You'd always be an outsider in Japan, no matter how long you lived there or how good your Japanese was.

I have to admit that one of the best things about living in Tokyo was getting to know the foreign community. Tokyo's foreign community is no longer the wealthy clique it once was. A lot of companies have been pulling out of Japan or re-establishing their Asian bases in Hong Kong or Singapore. So I mean the large variety of people, from ambassadors to businessmen to artists to strippers. You get to know all these people from different walks of life simply because you have something in common. You're all outsiders. I don't know if foreign communities in other parts of the world are like that.

Just wanted to add my two cents, even though you already have plenty to go on! 

It seems that with your degree, you should certainly be able to find employment in the country of your choosing.  I know that Australia has a specific list of who qualifies as someone they allow to work in their country (nurses, engineers, construction workers), and that their policies are in line with most of the British Commonwealth.  (nota bene: an Art History degree will NOT qualify you, no matter what country!!  i spent 9 months in Australia trying to get a visa to no avail......but I was able to get under the table catering jobs).

However, when I was looking into this whole thing last year, I discovered that Scotland just implemented a new immigration policy that allows anyone who gets a professional degree at a Scottish high ed intstitution to work while they're going to school, and also allows people to stay and work in Scotland for up to two years once their degree is completed.  And one would think that after two years of working somewhere, one might develop a rapport that would get your employer to sponsor your visa beyond the two years  :-)  I was super excited when I found out about this option, as it's a fantastic place to be for those of us who would prefer to be in an english speaking country, and yet so close to the European continent.

So thats what I think  :-D

Regarding your initial question regarding expenses and home sickness.....I was able to live relatively cheaply while abroad, as I was living with family.  I imagine that after living in New York, any other city would either be similiarly expensive, or more affordable.  Melbourne is obviously cheaper than New York City (rent-wise, food-wise, entertainment-wise), but more expensive than Syracuse (where I grew up).

And the home sickness thing is weird......Melbourne has consistently been voted one of the most livable cities in the world, so it was very easy to get comfortable and be happy.  However, after a while, the little idiosyncracies of living in a foreign land got to get to me (i.e. trying to get the health care system to work for me as a foreigner).  I didn't realize it until I returned to NY, but I missed the hell out of America (despite the Bush re-election that occured in my absence).  But it was an awesome way for me to reconnect with a country I had become completely disillusioned with......and I think it's probably the same for many who travel abroad.  You realize the things that work, and the things that don't work.
