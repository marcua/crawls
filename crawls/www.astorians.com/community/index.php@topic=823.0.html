Rather than hijack WindowsRefund's other thread, I'll just post this smoking portion on this new thread.

--
Speaking of smoking, it stinks. I’d not only ban smoking in public, but I’d label tobacco a controlled substance and outlaw it in this country. It pleasures only the smoker who gets the drug right into the blood through the lungs and leaves the nasty cloud of smoke for everyone else to deal with. How many people, smokers included, like the smell of secondhand smoke?

To use a hyperbolic example of how an avid non smoker (me) feels, suppose the following. I take a joke shop stink fluid that smells like rotten eggs to a public place and splash it around on people’s clothes, hair, the furniture, etc. Would I be subjected to censure and possible jail? Absolutely. I’m a public nuisance when doing that. I’m making other people uncomfortable, etc. Pick your argument and I’d probably agree with it. One thing I am not doing, however, is speeding people along to bronchitis or lung cancer or other health risk. Extreme, yes. Good for society, yes. Smoking tobacco has just been so ingrained in people’s culture as acceptable for so long that we refuse to give it up and look for ways to rationalize how it isn’t all that bad. Used to be not so long ago that we used to marginalize the effect all this toxic stuff had on people. Now, 9 out of 10 people would probably say that smoking is bad for you, but people are still reluctant to regulate it. All hail the tobacco lobby.

Physically & Emotionally addicted...
« Reply #2 on: September 27, 2005, 07:14:50 AM »
I wish I could quit. I want to quit. Or do I...

I like the idea of people respecting no-smoking policies and are courteous to put out a cigarette when asked, but I have some reservations with the government taking this matter into their own hands. I don't believe that prohibition is the answer. Nor censorship.

This is a very difficult issue for me to look at objectively being within the smoking pardigm. I agree with Telesphoros in the equation of cigarettes with controlled substances, and I know that they are one (if not the) leading causes of death and disease in the U.S. It makes me sick to imagine what my lungs look like, and to see in the mirror what my teeth look like, the smell of my clothes... it's a sickening habit. To know that I am some tobacco companies bitch, a slave to a chemical, an object, does wonders for my self-esteem. :-/

Second-hand smoke is indeed harmful and that's why I go outside when I smoke. I'll stop smoking outside when people stop driving cars or using goods that are produced in factories.

I think that filing suits against companies that have dangerous products is a good thing, even if the cost is passed onto the addicted consumer. Perhaps the high monetary cost will serve as an incentive for younger generations not to begin this debilitating habit and encourage older ones (by hurting their pockets) to finally quit. I don't mind a "luxury tax". At the same time I can see the problematic nature of getting a junkie to stop her/his habit... the more serious cases will require that he or she get a fix no matter how high the cost. Are these people in the drug consuming majority?

I think that cigarette smokers should be more welcome at hospitals if they wish to discontinue their addiction. There should be more secular/scientific programs available to them. More education. There is a curious absence of concern for victims of this dependency, even though it is proven to be fatal.

Being looked at with disdain by non-smokers does not bother me personally... I mean it bothers me, but it should bother me. If I saw someone doing something irrationally self-destructive I would look on with pity and disdain as well. The righteousness of the smoker or problem drinker is the unquestioning belief in his or her own self-destruction. How do you defend that?

I can say however that putting such things down is not always easy, if it were, then liquor & beer companies and tobacco companies and casinos would all be bankrupt. People like me are victims, whether self-victimized or victimized by outside influences is IMHO academic.
Hope to put these damn things down soon if it's not already too late... :-(

-zeno.

If the government was really out to better the overall health of its citizens, its only acceptable response to the issue of cigarettes would be to put a stop to them being marketed and sold legally.

Since the government is not currently trying to put a stop to the legal sale and marketing of cigarettes, we must admit to ourselves that the underlying agenda has nothing to do with health. So what are the motives? Honestly, I don't know...... although I can surely speculate.

Aside from the personal violation I felt at the time of the ban, I really felt for the business owners who's livelihoods were now subject to terms created and supported by self centered ego maniacs (IMHO). Imagine what it must have been like to run a pool room, a bar, or a restaurant, and not allow your guests to enjoy a cigarette just because someone *might* be offended. Businesses should be free to create an environment of their choosing. If the smell of cigarettes was so offensive all those years, how did we ever find ourselves eating out so much?

Even now, just a few years after the fact, I can barely remember a time when it was considered rude to not have an ash tray available in the home in order to compensate a visitor who might decide to light up a cigarette. Personally, I believe that most of the people who are so "sensitive" to second hand smoke have simply allowed themselves to be conditioned by all the hype. I don't buy it for a second. After all, I realize that just like reality TV, being "sensitive" to second hand smoke is very trendy at the moment.

I guess I'm not sensitive to it because I was born in the 70s :)
