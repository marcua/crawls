Quote from: merm on September 24, 2006, 10:01:07 PM
. . . and try and lose 25 lbs.
This is *not* an insignificant amount of weight to lose. Even if you already know how you added those pounds on, I would have a total physical exam before starting any diet where you intend to drop that much weight. I would want to know my lipid levels, and be sure that I could fly through a stress test. Dieting is not just a matter of will power—contrary to popular belief. Many times our body adds the extra weight as a symptom to show us that something is wrong.

If you have health insurance, I would get an appointment with a doctor to do the testing, and then with a dietitian to advise me on the proper way to reach my goal.

Dieting is not a healthy approach to losing weight. Behavior modification is a far better approach. In the end you *never* have to worry about doing this weight loss thing all over again, and becoming one of the statistics of ping-pong dieters. Dieting can make you feel deprived. Lifestyle changes can make you healthy.

Just my $0.02 here.

I respectfully disagree with NYCMacUser. But just slightly. There are healthy ways to diet. You can simply limit your portions to something more reasonable than the average American-sized portions. I noticed that when I first moved back from Japan, it was very difficult for me to get through the entrees at restaurants. A few months later, I was clearing my plate with room for dessert. I gained so much weight since I got back, that I have no choice but to change my eating habits.

I signed up for Weight Watchers online and use it as a loose guide. Since I have never had to go on a diet before, I really didn't know where to start and what to look out for. They emphasize that you shouldn't starve yourself. If you need to eat something, then eat. But they also remind you to watch portions and keep up your physical activities. So far, this has been working for me. It's not a lose-weight-quick scheme since they warn that losing more than 2 pounds a week can be dangerous. (So it should take you at least 13 weeks to lose 25 lbs.)

I don't lose weight every week, but I haven't gained. I feel healthier now than before I started five weeks ago, and my clothes are feeling looser. Also to add a bit of geekiness, the whole points system works for me because it almost feels like a game. I'll be damned if I use more than 5 weekly points in a day, so if I think I'm going to go over, I'll just exercise harder that day and do cardio ten or twenty minutes longer.
(Man, I bet this doesn't make sense to anyone. If you want a better explanation, merm, PM me.)

I don't go to the meetings because I don't actually feel the need to talk about it. But their online forums are actually very helpful (if sometimes a bit bitchy) and you don't need a membership to read them.

Wow, I went to bed and then got back and there's so many responses.

The Daily Plate website does allow you to enter in foods you eat (there's a lot of them) and it pulls the results out of their database and you just enter how much you ate and it calculates it all for you. It's pretty cool. You can also enter in foods that aren't in the database pretty easily. And yes, it's free.

I'm just trying to lose 1-2lbs each week, so it will take me a very long time to lose the weight I think. I don't want to do anything unhealthy, but then again, being overweight as much as I am is not healthy either.

I've been doing that website for about a month or so and have already taken off a few pounds in the last month, so it does seem to work for me pretty well. I just need to keep up with entering stuff, that where I fall behind. I find though that if I enter in what I ate BEFORE I start my evening meal it helps me to remember not to overeat for some reason. Without tracking my calories I can easily put down 3000 calories in a day. By tracking I can keep it at a recommended level for my age/sex/weight according to their calculator.

You can also make a public profile. As you can see I haven't entered anything for a very long time :(
