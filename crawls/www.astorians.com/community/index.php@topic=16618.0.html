I do not know much about how a woman protects herself in New York City, but now that my daughter lives there (having just moved there this past July), I would like to know what others recommend. Our daughter was walking to the laundromat and a man greeted her and said that she was attractive. He followed her into the laundromat and kept asking questions, trying to find out more about her, such as whether she lived nearby and whether she always did her laundry there. I do not know exactly what my daughter said to him but he eventually apologized for harassing her and went away. Of course, one has to wonder whether he just waited out of sight somewhere and followed her home.

She told me about this incident and asked me where she could buy pepper spray.

Can anyone tell me where she can buy some in Astoria? Is it legal to carry and use pepper spray?

(On a related note, I advised her to take self-defense lessons.)

When I also advised her to report the incident to the police so that they would know there is a suspicious person in the neighborhood, she said it did not seem worth it because a friend of hers had had a similar encounter (in another part of NYC) and when she reported it to the police, they did not do anything except say that she should tell them if the same person accosted her again.

My thinking is that my daughter should describe the man to the police in case he is perhaps already a known offender, or, at the very least, in case he should approach her again. If she reports him now, and later reports him a second time, the police will see that there is a pattern and may be more helpful or interested in at least advising her.

Can anyone offer insights or suggestions that I can pass along to my daughter? Thanks.

I would not advise your daughter to carry mace or any other "self defense weapon" that can be taken away from her and used against her. I'm a small woman myself and once I was shown how quickly I can be disarmed, I stopped carrying anything like that.

Instead, I recommend self-defense classes. The New York Jiu Jitsu Center offers free rape self-defense seminars once a month (call for dates). The best thing about these classes was the understanding of how to handle situations BEFORE they escalate to the point that a woman needs to physically defend herself. The sensei (a woman) focuses on language to dissuade a potential attacker or simple harrasser from bothering her. I also recommend the book The Gift of Fear by Gavin de Becker.

In this particular case, since we don't know what your daughter finally said to get the guy to leave her alone, it's hard to tell whether the guy was actively threatening or simply a creep who wasn't getting the picture (yes, I agree that an harrassment is implicitly a threat, but there's also a difference between Ignorant Creep and Guy Testing Her Boundaries To See If He Can Rape Her). In the future, she should try saying whatever it was louder and sooner. I think going to the police is a bit of overkill at this point but if the same guy talks to her a second time, she should go talk to them.

I do think this type of behavior is unusual for Astoria, and actually for New York City in general. I've been catcalled or had guys attempt to strike up a conversation when it was clear I wasn't interested, but I've very rarely been followed and never in broad daylight.
