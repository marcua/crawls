Wish we could have joined y'all today, sounds like a great time! I love cemeteries but haven't had the chance to check out any here yet. The headstones, especially really old ones, are so fascinating with the beautiful decorations, poems or bits of history and sometimes gossip.

One of the more unusual graves I've seen is in a cemetary in a tiny little Texas town named Shamrock (near where I'm from). The crude and time-ravaged marker is for a young girl who died in the 1880's and instead of being covered by grass or dirt has hundreds of seashells carefully placed over the entire plot and affixed with adobe. The shells are sun-bleached a brilliant white and make quite a striking and thought-provoking scene. The Texas Panhandle is high and dry so just the thought of what it took to get the shells there is interesting in itself! Was a family travelling west in a covered wagon when their child died, as so often happened? There aren't any other graves nearby with the same name. Was a mother afraid she wouldn't be able to find her child again and marked it with an unmistakable sign? I just imagine someone so lovingly placing each shell while the dusty and contant Plains wind whipped their face and the hot sun beat down on their back. 

Old cemeteries, or the oldest sections of the cemetery, always look from a distance forlorn. But when you walk amid the graves it seems like you stir up an energy that has been waiting to be released. When I come across a grave that probably hasn't had a visitor in decades (or more) I just feel, I don't know, that by stopping and reading the marker its an acknowledgment...this was a person like me who lived, loved, cried, laughed...maybe they have been dead a hundred years but because I stopped and thought about them, or ran my fingers across their chisled name, for that small moment in time they aren't forgotten. Isn't that what each of us wants ultimately, to be remembered?

So Mugshots, I think the graveyard spirits probably enjoyed the positive energy this nice group of people brought to a place all too often empty and silent except for the sound of sobbing.
