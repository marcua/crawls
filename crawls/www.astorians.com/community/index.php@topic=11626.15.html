I was glad that they posted the picture, actually. What makes trolls so alarming - to me at least - is that they are faceless. When I read about the Kathy Sierra death threats/harrassment, or how women bloggers receive comments such as "I hope you get raped", I wonder who says those things. Who believes that it's excuseable?

Part of what gives them their power is that they are anonymous. It could be anyone, so you suspect everyone. I wonder who these men are. The nice I.T. guy at work, my neighbor who always says hello, my patient, cute doctor, that guy I went on a date with, my smart and snarky cousin - it could be anyone. When I strike up a conversation with a stranger over beers at my favorite pub - does he go home and log in to tell a random woman "I hope you get raped to death, you stupid whore?"

Having faces and names and descriptions - even though it doesn't provide evidence that none of the men I care about and respect are secretly harrassing women online - turns the "enemy" from an massive anonymous representation of most men, to a smaller, describeable group. People who are a little crazy and living on the fringe.

Knowledge is power.

There's actually a political board that I will occasionally "troll". I do this when someone who is new posts a topic that we've beaten to death in the past. I then like to post some pretty ridiculous position and see if I can get them into a debate.

The other regulars know what my real position is and it's almost like it's a simple hazing of goading people into weird arguments. It can be fun or at least funny and it's something that happened to me when I was there.

It's like a form of protection to keep the board from rehashing the same discussions over and over. It's not meant to be malicious; it's more like "c'mon man, search through the old stuff before you post the exact same thing that we just talked about last week". It gets people up to speed with the attitude and tonality of a board.
