Foreclosures and the blame game.
« on: February 18, 2009, 02:05:02 PM »
I had read on this forum several comments made that the people responsible for this catastrophic debacle are those who purchased 'too much house'.  When reading this statement, I think of mansions, estates and large fancy houses.  Well, recently my husband and I were looking for a house in Florida.  The foreclosed homes down there are small, simple, family homes that certainly do not seem to qualify as 'too much house.'  Now, before you respond that the statement 'too much house' means that it is too expensive for someone who makes a certain wage, please read on:

In 1972, my parents purchased a house in Astoria for about $50k.  Only Dad was working.  He was an immigrant who did not know much about purchasing a house.  He relied on his banker, real estate agent and his mortgage broker who told him he could afford it.  Had this occurred in today's environment, with all the complicated mortgage dealings and interest rates, we would have been on the street.

When I purchased a house in 2000, I was not sure as to whether I would be able to afford it. Once again, I relied on my banker, mortgage broker and real estate agent who walked me through every detail and were honest about my monthly payments.  If it had been in today's environment, I am not sure I would have survived.  I am a professional but these things are complicated and I can only imagine how some of these people with less experience were conned.  After all, they were fulfilling their 'American Dream' which these days seems to be a malicious hoax.

Recently, a Librarian who purchased a home stated that she was convinced by her banker, real estate agent etc. that she could afford the home.  She is now in foreclosure.  Should she have studied this more carefully?  Of course.  Should she have read the fine print? Naturally.

The point is that these people were lied to by people they trusted.  The 'too much house' that they purchased for $500,000 was overpriced to begin with, as we in Astoria all know.  The house that I purchased for $265k was worth twice that five years later.  The bubble we all lived in has burst.

Finally, I read about a group that is standing up to foreclosures in New York in today's New York Times.  People are gathering around foreclosed homes and not allowing others to get thrown on the street.  I am going to join this group.  I have had enough and I am angry.  I do not understand why people are being thrown out of their homes while others get millions in bonuses and I do not give a sh-t about a 'free market economy'.  This has become an extreme situation and requires extreme action.  I can see why the masses beheaded Marie Antoinette, although I do not condone violence.  Perhaps a bit of civil disobedience on the other hand...

Does anyone out there feel the same way?  I would venture to say that there are a lot of angry people out there, especially in other parts of the country.  Perhaps even the Conservative who has lost his job and is living on unemployment on the brink of foreclosure?  Is that person angry at Wall Street and the million dollar bonuses?  I bet, and I bet he is ready to protect his home with his rifle!  Will there be civil unrest?  What do you think?

Let's stir things up a bit and have a civil discussion.  Perhaps we can all learn something!

There is a great article called The Ponzi State in the Feb 9th&16th issue of The New Yorker about what happened in Florida. The abstract is here: http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2009/02/09/090209fa_fact_packer

Quote
Florida is one of the places where the financial crisis began. Gary Mormino, a professor at the University of South Florida in St. Petersburg, tells the writer that, “Florida, in some ways, resembles a modern Ponzi scheme. Everything is fine for me if a thousand newcomers come tomorrow.” The state depends for revenue on real-estate deals and sales taxes. By 2005, the housing market in Florida was hotter than it had ever been. Flipping houses and condominiums turned into an amateur middle-class pursuit. Writer tells about Floridians with modest incomes who made money buying and selling real estate. Mentions one case in which a house appreciated in value by almost fifty per cent overnight. According to an investigation by the Miami Herald, government oversight of the real-estate market was so negligent that more than ten thousand convicted criminals got jobs in the mortgage industry. Flipping and fraud burst the bubble. But in places like Pasco County, it was the ordinary desire of ordinary people to buy their own home that turned things toxic.

Definitely worth the read for anyone still trying to figure out what happened down there. Honestly, getting kicked out of your house is sad, and there are some true victims here. Quote from: JoeyC on February 19, 2009, 01:38:16 AM
People were buying homes with no money down, and had rates that would balloon after 5 years.   They were planning on such things as hitting the lottery or a relative leaving them money to pay when rates went up, Naturally, they got stuck, and want bailout.  They can rent, why do they deserve home on taxpayer money?

They don't, of course, but unfortunately you can't treat individual cases in isolation. The economy is bad enough right now. If I owned a home (I don't) in a neighborhood where 30% of my neighbors were in foreclosure, that's going to drop my property values. And when my property devalues, then I'm underwater, and I can't take the great new job in San Francisco, because I can't sell my house. So because my neighbors made a bad bet, all the sudden my financial prospects have become dire, my real estate agent is flipping burgers to make ends meet, and the economy as a whole suffers. Everything is related. Having large numbers of homes in foreclosure hurts everyone, worse than the moral hazard issue of bailing out some people who don't deserve it.

These new plans to reduce foreclosures won't do much to deal with falling property values, but it'll reduce the contagion and allow things to stabilize. Unfortunately, real estate in much of the country is still way too high, and it needs to fall back to sane levels before the economy can turn around. Policies should be to allow that process to happen as quickly as possible (without artificially re-inflating the bubble), while minimizing the symptoms to the economy as a whole.
