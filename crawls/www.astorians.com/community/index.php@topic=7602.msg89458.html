Juveniles are treated different from adults. When arrested, they're brought to the precinct and are to be kept separate from the general population in the "juvenile room." The parents are to be contacted immediately. Any interrogation is to be done after Miranda warnings are administered to both the child and their parent (a rule rarely followed). Kids are supposed to be released to a parent or brought to court as soon as possible (if the arrest takes place during court hours). Again, this is rarely done. I once represented a 10-year old boy held in Spofford (the secure juvenile jail in the Bronx) for an entire weekend for stealing a Yu-Gi-Oh card from another kid. The poor kid was traumatized after a weekend of being housed with violent kids who were significantly older than him.

No counseling is offered, no services provided until after the case comes to court. Once the case does come to court, then the probation department can "adjust" a case -- where the case is handled directly by probation and the kid never sees the judge -- or they can refer the case up for filing. Once the case is filed, it goes before the judge and the case follows a path similar to that of any adult criminal case, albeit with slightly different timelines.

And I understand that Billz not advocating the use of excessive force, but I do think to some extent he's justifying it and frankly, I can't see the justification for any degree of force to be used on a kid. Even if the kid wasn't beaten by police, I often see kids who come in with red welts where handcuffs were put on too tight, clothing is torn, or the cops spoke to the kids using obscene racist and sexist language. If the parents of these children treated them that way, the kids would be removed by ACS and an abuse/neglect petition filed against the parent, yet the police treat kids like that on a regular basis. Problem is, who is going to believe a kid?

No, I'm not justifying the use of excessive force.  Although, I will admit that perhaps what I define as excessive force might be harder than what your definition might be.  I think that the justification for a kid to be arrested is to maintain the rule of law that ties us together as a society.  Children are not exempt from right and wrong, and it is the duty of their parents to teach them as such.  Neither poor impulse control, nor poverty exempts them from this.  And it is best that they learn that now before they commit offenses at an age when the book could be thrown at them.  In the case of a 10 year old being locked up for taking a gaming card, it becomes a little bit of a gray area.  Normally, I would say that locking such a kid up is a misuse of my tax money, and another example of nanny-statism in a misguided attempt at zero tolerance.  But on the other hand, I remember when I was younger, and a nerd, and I used to play Magic: The Gathering.  (Awkward, awkward times!)  Some of those cards back then in the mid 90s were worth 20 to 100 bucks.  I imagine that some of the rare gaming cards now might have similar value.  So if a kid took an expensive card, perhaps that should be an arrestable offense. 

Back to our varying definitions of excessive force.  If you are arrested, it is not going to be a picnic.  The cops will yell at you.  I am not saying they should use racist or sexist language, but I also don't think they should blunt the experience.  Imagine a criminal perpetrator hearing "Pardon us, good sir.  But since you have shoplifted, we are forced to arrest you.  I am sorry if this is an inconvenience."

And yes, handcuffs are not made with ergonomics in mind.  I guess that being arrested and confined for committing a crime is forceful in certain regards.  In that case, I can justify the use of force for the good of society.  But I don't equate that with beating a kid or helping them to fall down the stairs.
