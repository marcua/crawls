Owning a place provides psychological advantages that can't always be boiled down to dollars and cents.

A landlord can decide he doesn't want you anymore and kick you out.  Sure there are laws to protect people against this, but it all depends on how good of a person you are dealing with. 

If something breaks, you are at the mercy of someone else.  My last landlord wasn't interested in spending money on anything important (although he was always spending thousands of dollars to renovate his daughter's apartment downstairs).  It took 3 times to get the leaky roof fixed correctly.  The shower assembly inside the wall was leaking, and I told him about water accumulating outside of the tub.  He ignored it until finally the leak caused some kind of damage to the ceiling downstairs until his daughter's apartment. 

Who wants to deal with that garbage?  Sometimes you are lucky and get someone really good that cares about their property.  Sometimes you don't.  Just like there are good and band tenants there are also good and bad landlords.

Someone posted above about looking at ownership as a forced savings plan rather than an investment.  Sometimes that's a good way to go about it.  When my wife and I had triplets we really needed a place.  Whether or not I can sell it later on for more money than we bought it for is irrelevant - the primary concern was having someplace big enough and safe to raise our kids in.  Finding a big enough place to rent in Astoria or LIC would have been hard enough, so it was pretty much trial by fire and buying a place.

New single-family home sales down
February 24, 2010 01:00PM
 
Sales of new single-family homes were down 11.2 percent in January from a month earlier, according to a report from the U.S. Census Bureau and the Department of Housing and Urban Development released today (see report below). The Northeast saw a 35 percent decrease in new-single family home sales, the largest for any region from December 2009 to January 2010. The South decreased 9 percent, the West 11 percent and the Midwest had a slight increase of 2 percent. The national seasonally-adjusted annual rate was 309,000, down from December's 348,000 and 6.1 percent below the previous January estimate. The median sales price of new houses sold last month was $203,500, and the average sales price was $245,500. TRD
