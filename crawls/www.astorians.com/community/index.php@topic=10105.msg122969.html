Quote from: newastorian35 on April 12, 2008, 10:22:46 AM
When you're in a store and the person who is supposed to help you doesn't speak English (only Spanish, usually).  It's great that there are so many different cultures in this country, but we are in America, where the primary language is English.. and you should be able to go into a business and be able to communicate.  This is also why I rarely order food from a place you have to call and prefer to use delivery.com.  If you call, the food order is usually ok, but you can't communicate the delivery instructions. 

This is actually something that grinds my gears, so I guess it's on topic.

I agree that if you work in a customer service capacity, you should probably speak English.  However, there are certain realities that should be taken into consideration before making a general statement about everyone who doesn't speak English.

(And by the way, I'm not trying to make excuses for people who make no attempt learn and have the time and resources to learn English)

1.  At the restaurant I managed in Manhattan, my bosses hired non-English speaking people from Latin America and Bangladesh to do deliveries because no US citizen, black or white, would have worked for their wages or would have done the work that they did.  I know because we tried.

2.  Many of these businesses that you are referring to are owned and operated by people who don't speak English as a first language.  I go to a bar that is owned and operated by a group of guys from Southern Europe.  Sometimes we don't understand each other, but they treat me with respect every time I go and despite our language differences, we got to know each other.  Besides, coming from Minnesota, the Irish accent sounded foreign to me until I started associating with folks from Ireland.

3. Many of these store owners and employees, while speaking some English, aren't proficient and do not feel comfortable with their English.  Having been brought up in the Midwest by English speaking schoolteachers, I found it frustrating to go into some stores and trying to communicate in English with the owner, but then I realized that many of them were honestly trying, but they are not fluent.

For example, when I used to help my Dominican friend in his bodega on 36th Avenue, I saw that he was uncomfortable speaking English around people who were fluent in English and he started stuttering.  However, we used to have the greatest conversations, mixing what little Spanish and English we both knew.  It somehow worked.  When English speaking customers would ask him for help, though, he would be embarrassed and sometimes just give them an odd stare.

4. This ties in the first three points, in a way.  Every person that I have befriended who doesn't speak English as their first language, yet serves in a capacity where they have to interact with English speaking customers, have told me that they want to learn (Yes, I'm sure there are folks who don't, but I have never met them)  However, as was the case of my Dominican friend and most of the people who worked in the restaurant, there was not enough time in the day to learn.  They would constantly ask me how to say things in English and I would try my best, but my grammar is far from perfect (have you read my posts lately  :-D ) so I would tell them they should find some sort of ESL program.  That's easier said than done, especially if you're working six days a week, ten hours a day, which many of them were.

4. Also, the fact that we live in NYC makes our situation unique.  If you were in Minnesota, this would be a moot issue.  You have to know English to run a business, bottom line, because virtually no one from your vendors to your customers, speaks anything but English. 

However, in NYC, if you own a bodega, say, many of your customers will be Spanish speakers and even your vendors might all speak Spanish, which was the case at the bodega I helped in.  In any case, there's so many languages being spoken by the customers as well, that just knowing English is not enough.  It might not be perfect, but without the hard work and entrepreneur drive of many of the immigrants, who would own all of the business that they occupy or do all of the work that they do?  Many (not all, of course) of the English speaking business owners left NYC and bought a house in the 'burbs.  Something that many of these same immigrants in NYC hope for as well!  My friend's brother-in-law who used to own three bodegas in the Bronx, now owns a successful small business in Rockland county 9he was actually one of the financial backers of my friend's bodega on 36th Ave).  He's living the American dream, Dominican style!
