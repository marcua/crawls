Quote from: paratactical on August 07, 2008, 01:20:42 PM
NYCGoalie implied that all people who become parents end up the same way
Uh, no, that's not was I was implying.

What I was stating (not implying) is that as a parent, your perspective changes.  What was once seen as unacceptable response becomes an acceptable response when you are in the opposite part of the situation. 

"Why is it that those parents do that with those kids?"  "Why don't they do this, or that?".
That's what I'm hearing from many of the people here who don't have children of thier own.  Again, when you have a child, your perspective changes and so do your reactions.  We all change our perspectives as we get older and go through life experiences.  And for most people, having a child does change your perspective on the world around you.

Another example:  I never liked the idea of the V-chip on TV's, or rating systems in general for that matter.  When I was childless, I said "shouldn't parents be aware of what thier kids are watching/doing anyways?  Isn't this some pre-cursor to full blown censorship?  Censorship is BAD!!".

Now that I'm a parent, I still don't use the V-chip and I don't really know what the ratings actually mean, but I get the point now.  It's a tool to help parents make better decisions.  If I didn't watch South Park, I would probably think "oh, well, it's a cartoon; how bad could it be.  I grew up watching Wyle-E-Coyote being blown up by every ACME product in every which way possible.  How bad could a cartoon be?".  Well, the ratings system gives a parent a little clue that maybe Cartman makes Mr.Coyote look like a smurf. 

The point is, I now get why there are ratings.  Not that it's going to replace my responsibility of actually knowing what my kid will be watching; but it gives me a little heads up beforehand.  What was once a symbol of evil is now a useful icon...perception changed.

Again, until you become a parent, this really won't become as clear.  While the world stays the same, you change.  It's only natural.

Quote from: lanseaux on August 07, 2008, 01:13:36 PM
I recall in another thread you said you'd never choose to have a family in NYC (or don't think it's appropriate to have a family in NYC, or something like that).  It's your prerogative if you don't want to, but NYC is a place like any other place, and there are a lot of us who have deep roots here.  I've got roots here going back five generations, and my husband's family has been in Astoria since 1923.  This is where we live and where our families live, and it would be odd if we didn't think it was appropriate to have a family here.  It's not a place where we've come for some city living for a few years.  It's home. 

So yeah, we're going to "try" to raise a family here, and the social scene around us doesn't just exist for singles and 20-somethings.  I'm not going to stay in my apartment behind closed doors, never going out, to raise a child.  That's bullshit.  Our children, just like us, are part of this community, and will avail themselves of the great things this community has to offer.  You'll see them.  You can curse at them.  You might even hear them cry.  But that's life.

When I was growing up, there was no Internet, there was no Fresh Direct.  (And btw we didn't have a TV until I was 13, either.  I never owned an Atari or any other videogames.)  My mother took me food shopping because my parents couldn't afford a nanny or babysitter to watch me while she did.  Taking a child places teaches them vital life skills, even from an early age, and yeah, I think that includes restaurants and shopping and many other places.  If they never went anywhere, how would they learn to act in public and in other social situations?  By watching videos online?  The Internet is great, but it's not a substitute for life skills.

I grew up in Dutchess County, which is way more country than Astoria will ever be.  We were taken to stores to go shopping with one of our parents all the time.  Not everyone has the money or the time to get a babysitter on simple everyday errands, and yeah, while I'm sure we weren't well behaved all the time, it did teach me essential life skills which I still use to this day.  Having to take your kids shopping isn't unique to city life.  I think raising children in the city is a wonderful chance to expose your children to diversity and tolerance.  To other cultures.  To other foods.  To all types of people.  To all types of art, music, literature, architecture.  To the whole world.  There is nothing wrong with raising kids in the city.  And nothing wrong with raising them in the burbs.  To each their own.

While I hesitate to judge the use of the DVD in a restaurant, yeah, I wouldn't want it next to me.  It's not a practice I'd want to encourage.  But I do wholeheartedly encourage children in restaurants as they should be exposed to the situation and be taught how one must properly behave in one.  Not all kids are well behaved though.   I'm around lots of kids as my extended family is huge, and I don't envy parents, as it is a real tough job.  While I love kids, I love when they go home too.
