Title: Re: Gotham Writers' Workshop
Post by: sarc on January 31, 2009, 02:13:03 PM
I haven't taken Gotham, but I've taken writing classes through the University I work at.  They were all non-credit and I think fairly comparable in size, structure and price to Gotham's.  The general consensus is they are helpful, but not THAT helpful.  So the question becomes, does what you get from the class justify the cost?

Online classes versus in person classes don't vary too much.  You ultimately are paying for feedback from the teacher.  Maybe there's a few exercises you can do in class, but you don't really get anything out of writing in a class setting.  Live classes do give you feedback from other students and perhaps networking opportunities.  So this depends on the type of writing.  Screenwriting might be a better candidate for online since you're not going to get usable feedback from students by reading 3 pages of a screenplay.  While, comedy writing is better suited for live classes so you can hear if something's funny. 

Both classes I've taken were a diverse mix of students.  There was always a couple of "serious" writers, an 18 yr old fresh out of HS, a bored housewife, a couple young professionals, and a few people just there on a whim.

The best advice I can give is figure out what class you want to do, find out who the instructor is and research them.  What are the credentials?  How long have they been teaching?  And keep in mind this information will probably not be easy to come by.  Also, shop around.  We're in one of the greatest cities in the world for this.  There's a decent amount of competition.  For honest testimonials and review, do some extensive google or blog searches.  People (especially aspiring writers) always blog about this stuff.  "I'm taking this workshop at so-and-so and the teacher sucks cause they say everything is 'great'..." 

Ultimately, if it's something you really want to do, you might just have to roll the dice on it.  Worse comes to worse, what a class does do well is it forces you to write.  You become a better writer from instruction, but more so by practice.  Being required to produce new material once a week is sometimes enough of a kick in the butt to get people motivated, and start taking it more seriously.
