Re: New Japanese Fusion Restaurant on Ditmars
« Reply #22 on: July 23, 2012, 11:06:32 AM »
I have to say I agreed with the thinking that this place sounded disappointingly like all the others on Ditmars.  Well, we tried it last night and it turned out to be the second hugely pleasant Japanese restaurant suprise following my recent meal at Tobiko on Steinway.  First about the place. It is definitely a bit loungy but it's not quite as dark as people may have thought from the street.  It's actually pretty cool and relaxing.  The black chairs are comfortable.  The bathroom is a little odd.  Glittery and very Studio 54.  But who cares?  Overall I think the atmosphere is pretty nice.  As for the service, it's very good and very attentive.  Maybe too attentive.  I think people who are uncomfortable with a certain degree of friendliness from strangers might be turned off.  I'm sure they'll learn to tone it down a notch.  To me, it was harmless enough.  As others stated, the menu doesn't sound very fusion-like or exciting.  I think it's a huge failure in the naming and descriptions of the dishes.  The food is actually very impressive.  We sampled many dishes and they all were hits with us.  Sesame Crusted Calamari sounds so pedestrian but this a winner.  Very tender rings (no tentacles) lightly coated in a slightly salty tempura batter and black sesame seeds.  Served with a sweet and hot Thai chili sauce.  Excellent and not heavy or greasy.  The Kobe Meatballs were delicious.  It's an uncommonly meaty item for a Japanese place.  Very hearty but flavorful with an Asian style sauce.  Lean meat but not much filler (a good thing).  The Crispy Duck hot appetizer is another poorly named item.  They are actually these small rolls of non-crispy lean duck meat wrapped with sushi cucumber in moo shoo pancakes served with plum sauce.  She said it was a Beijing specialty.  They were warm and delicious.  The most amazing appetizer yet was the Red Snapper Carpaccio.  This is a must have item.  Thinly (but not paper thin) slices of fresh snapper placed atop a chunk of mango on top of a wedge of avocado. It's absolutely delicious.  It's perfect for the sushi lover who can do with the rice.  We had two entrees and both were surprisingly good.  The Hibachi Steak at $19 turns out to be this huge dinner plate with a perfectly cook thick stead cut to chopstick sized bites.  I asked for medium rare and it was juicy and perfectly pink.  The marinate was slightly soy but heavily garlic.  I loved it.  Then there's a huge portion of broccoli, carrots, and either rice or noodles.  I got the soba noodles which were tossed with more garlic and sesame oil.  Rich but not greasy.  It was a plate that was a cross between a Japanese place and a American/Euro continental restaurant.  The other entree was the Spicy Garlic Chilean Seabass.  Again, a huge meal for $22 and very fresh seabass (2 large, thick fillets) with a delicious slightly sweet and very garlicky flavor.  As you can tell, we really enjoyed this.  It's not super creative fusion and I appreciated that.  It ventures toward Chinese, Thai and even basic American.  And I think it's smart and well done.  This is a Japanese place that would satisfy someone with a big American appetite while offering authentic Asian flavors along with very fresh and healthy ingredients.  By the time we left, it was crowded.  And we heard talk like there were already repeat customers.  And it's 15% off for the grand opening.  When we walked out and looked across the road at one of the old-school sushi places, suddenly it looked totally irrelevant.  Then we walked past a slickly renovated and expanded Watawa that was jam packed.  I think there is an exciting remake of our local Japanese restaurants underway and I like it.
