On PCness
« on: June 29, 2006, 11:31:13 AM »
I'm curious about this. I only ever hear PC used as a derogatory term.

Either "That's not PC" (judgemental) or "The stupid PC crowd."

Other people are always identified as PC, never one's self.

When I think of Political Correctness, I think of a general sense that you really shouldn't make racist, homophobic, sexist, etc., comments. Clearly this is not what everyone else thinks.

I had a near-explosive but quickly squelched (to spare our poor brunch buddies) discussion with my roommate about whether or not it is a good thing to discourage people from saying "non PC" things. She wants to know what people really think so she can judge them accordingly. I would rather see what they do and not have language get in the way. Language can - possibly - be the result of ignorance, background, or some other factor. Plus, we all have "bad" thoughts - but I think there's a difference between having them, expressing them, and acting on them.

I think that changing what people say means changing what they think. If it's socially unacceptable to say racist, sexist, homophobic things, then hopefully it will become socially unacceptable, eventually, to DO those things.

My third argument for the social mores of language is that words are more than a reflection of the speaker - they also have an affect on the hearer, whether that person is an impressionable youngster or a person who belongs to the group being slurred. While we all deal with hearing unpleasant things now and then, for a group to be consistently subject to verbal slurs and prejudice means that they will, over time, accept what is said of them and grow into the slurs, rather than defying them by showing that they are not true. So by speaking cruely, you create a cruel truth. (I read this in a psych book somewhere - I'll try to look it up). It also teaches others to think the way that you do. And while we might all like to think that we are total individuals, there's a reason we all act and behave in remarkably similar ways.

I think it's good that it's socially unacceptable to say the N word, for my boss to make comments about my chest, or for gay people to be referred to with slurs. If that's what PC is, I'm for it. If PC is something else, I'd like to to know what.

I think over the years saying something or someone is "PC" has become a way of stiffling people's ability to challenge derogatory remarks or belittle progressive politics.  It shuts down the discussion before one even has a chance to raise issues of privilege or discrimination.  
I remember back in the 80s before people started to redefine the term that saying someone was Politically Correct was a good thing, that they had good politics and were committed to the ideals of feminism, opposing racism, sexism, homophobia, classism and bigotry in all its forms.  I think the shift is very similar to the redefinition of the term "liberal" to be a bad thing.
I think it's important to challenge people, politely, when they say something that is inappropriate.  It may make for short term awkwardness, but it often gets them thinking and they may not even realize the implications of what they had said, or how it might hurt or demean another person.  I think judging people by their actions, moreso than their words, is also important.  A person may use crass language but could still live their lives in ways that respect other people.

I agree with you!  I'm for Political Correctness.  However, I think it's to a point where we're starting to feel a little backlash.  

I have certainly heard more people use the term "PC" as a negative thing - "Oh, aren't YOU being terribly PC!"  

PC seems to be at a point where it can be seen as going too far.  Is it PC to state the obvious?  "Wow, that person is HUGE!  Time for a diet!!"  "Wow, that person is so skinny that if you held a light behind them you'd see their organs!  Eat some food!!"  "Man, they're ugly and smell bad."  Is is un-PC to say those things or just rude?  Some feel that it's not PC to say things like that but then I think you're starting to get too close to Free Speech infringement.  

Some would even argue that being PC IS Free Speech infringement.  I don't subscribe to that school of thought.

Though the samples you gave regarding racism, homophobia, sexism, and whatnot are things where people should certainly exercise Political Correctness it's scary to me to know that there's a possibility that the person on the other side of the table may just be biting their tongue.  If I'm having a conversation that's condemning homophobia or racism for example, then they are judging me that entire time and just holding their breath.  Now this person knows me and I don't know them.

I don't think there is a right answer to this question.  It depends on how far it goes.  And how far it goes is totally arbitrary and is not something that a standard can be developed for.  If that were to happen then we'd all be living in some bizzarro Ayn-Randian civilization.

In the end, I like PC.  I'd like to be a mind reader too, though.

Hmm...

Granted: we do not want to hear racial slurs, etc. because it is true that an environment where people can just say whatever comes into their heads-no matter how ignorant/mean/unkind/ etc. is not one that will further conversation among all participants.

But, at the same time, I think that some part of the power of language is the fact that it can be extremely manipulative and manipulatable (if this is not a word, it is now). That is, people can hide all kinds of feelings, innuendo, etc. behind language. For example, when Bush was running for President (the first time) he kept repeating the phrase, "I will bring honor and dignity back to the White House". Now, no one could attack him for saying this. Who doesn't want dignity in the White House? But what he was implying was, "Clinton degraded the office of the Presidency and I will restore it". 

Of course we all know what kind of BS this is-but no one took him up on this statement-"What exactly do you mean Mr. Bush, what are you implying?" Because he didn't just come out and say it, he hid behind language and hit the opponents (Gore) right where Gore was most vulnerable (being associated with Clinton).

Whatever you think of the politics behind this-it's not important. My point is that language is most manipulative and most destructive-in my opinion, when it works by implication and innuendo-rather than when things are said outright. Not only that, but it is a cowardly way of attacking someone and leaves the other person unable to defend themselves-or even bring things up for discussion.

If I'm in a group setting and we are discussing a problem-for example the recent hate crime-I'd rather know if the person across from me is homophobic or racist, or whatever. That way, I can talk with him or her, we can have a discussion as to why she feels this way-and maybe, just maybe I can bring up some points to make that person think about things differently.

But if this person hides behind PC statements or holds back what she's really thinking-then there can really be no discussion, no debate, no honest exchange of ideas.

And that's my 2 cents.

PCism is vile because it is an attempt to manipulate and control another person's thinking.  It devalues a person's beliefs and opinions and sense of worth by explicitly stating that even before there is any dialogue you must conform to a standard method of communication before you are even allowed to expres yourself.

People seem to forget that there is a connotative aspect to language that cannot be separated from the denotative meaning.  Children and immature adults are masters of playing the game of language by exploiting the denotative meaning and couching it as the basis for their communication when they know full well that there is a connotative meaning that is far different than the original meaning of the word.  Sample dialoge:

11 year old Tommy:  "You're gay, Bobby."
12 year old Bobby:  "Shut up, fag."
Tommy:  "Why?  I just said you're happy.  Don't be so mean."
Bobby:  "I wasn't mean, I just called you a cigarette."

As we get older we are capable of much more sophisticated language manipulation, but you get the idea.

The PC set predetermine that a certain way of thinking is the right way to think.  The correct way to think.  The only way a good, upstanding, intelligent, modern person would think.  They couch it in the belief that they are being sensitive and enlightened and that only through discourse on these terms can anything be accomplished.  The problem with this is that is just not true.  The problem is we don't all share the same belief set or the same communication tools.  They will never get a 30 year old man who dropped out of high school who works long hours in a job that doesn't afford itself to any free time to improve his skills to have that PC conversation they are looking for.

Another problem is the issues themselves start to lose relevance and take a back seat to the language.  Let's not have an open honest exchange of ideas about politics, religion, or anything else because we are going to stop the debate before it starts because we didn't use gender neutral terminology or [fill in the transgression].  They start nit picking the language and ignoring the content of what is being discussed.

The goal seems to be to change the world by changing the language.  While it is great to have lofty aspirations on making the world a better place as you see it, the truth is, most people don't want to be changed.  We resent when others tell us what to do or how to do it.  Nobody likes to be wrong and we like it even less when it is pointed out to us.  So the PC person enters the argument with guns blazing and shooting down what people say just because of how they are saying it.  I certainly don't like this and am sure plenty of others will agree.  Surely there are those who disagree.  Just don't mock what I say or ridicule it without giving some thoughtful reasoning to back it up.  Otherwise we're back to the Jerry Springer mindset of he who shouts loudest and longest wins.

I could go on.  This is a thesis worthy topic, but my voice has been added to the thread and I am content (as well as pressed for time).  If anyone needs elaboration or clarification, just let me know and I will happily oblige.
