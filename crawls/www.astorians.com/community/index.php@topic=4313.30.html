I've never really been into gems. I barely wear jewelry.

I think the vintage solution is a good one. Or flooz's unique approach :)

I remember working with a girl who informed me that a diamond engagement ring should be at least so many carets (10?). At first I thought it was really stupid, but eventually I figured that - especially in the early 20th century and before, when most women didn't have their own incomes - a diamond ring would be a thing of value that a woman would always have at her disposal - if the hubby walked in front of a bus or ran off with his secretary, she would have *something* that was hers and hers alone that she could sell so she could pay the rent and buy diapers, if only for a couple of months. Something to fall back on. Insurance.

But having read the Atlantic Monthly article, it seems that diamonds are a crappy investment and impossible for consumers to sell at anything near the price originally paid. Their whole cachet is that they are rare - but since diamonds were discovered in huge numbers in Africa (and later in Australia), it's clear that they are NOT rare - and the whole diamond industry stays alive by masking this fact. Essentially american men are being programmed to give up 2 months salary for a glittery but valueless rock, and american women are programmed to see these as powerful symbols of love. Which they should not sell. The whole thing relies on a diamond being "forever" - you don't sell it. You don't find out what the real value is, most diamonds go off the selling market after the initial purchase, and the diamond market stays aloft.
