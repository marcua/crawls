I have lived here for 30 years, and when I discovered Astoria, it was a wonderful place. It was the best of both worlds, a few minutes from Manhattan, but unlike parts of the south Bronx or Jersey, or downtown Brooklyn that were next door to 'the rock', not trashed. Cheap rent, good food, clean steets, quiet in the airshaft. Which the exception of good food, that is no longer the picture.
 
In Astoria, as the rest of Queens, a small clique controls everything. What you read in the media, how funding is given to programs, how the community goes forward was (and is) decided in smoke filled rooms. There is the fig leaf of public participation, but most of it is staged. They don't particular like, or understand (and therefore fear) hipsters or some of the young people that are moving into the neighborhood. They dread change.

Unfortunately, no group of people can forever be at the cusp of good influence. This same old guard who protected Astoria from some of the unpleasant changes that hit other areas (in the 60s, 70s, 80s) are now is a straight jacket that stifles forward progress in the community. This is why the same old tired formular is applied to shopping, entertainment, the very image of this community.

Everything changes. You cannot live in 1968 forever. And the future of Astoria is one of two directions: Hoboken, or Elmhurst.
 
Manhattan people always thought they were in a class by themselves, and Astoria was regarded a place you grow up and move away from (or sometimes stay). The problem is that rather than competing with Brooklyn as the second borough, we are now facing the bottom against a resurgent Bronx. Yes, if you compare us with the worst, we still look great. But the direction of Queens in general, and Astoria in particular is clear to us long termers.

All you need to do is go out to central Queens to see our communty's (potential) future: poor planning, ugly out-of-scale buildings, a community of transients where you get the sense that people are yearning for someplace else.
 
Enough for now, will elaborate in further posts. These comments are meant to further discussion not to assign blame or insult.

Most Starbucks I've been to use beans that have been roasted to death.  So the resulting coffee has a bit of a burned taste, at least with their drip coffee.  They haven't gained the snarky nickname "Charbucks" for nothing.  However, I have been to some Starbucks that make their coffee in a way where it doesn't taste burnt.  Not sure why it is so different among stores, since "consistency" is the brass ring.  And I've had coffee from Starbucks on both coasts and in airports. 

There are independent places that make terrible coffee, too.  I don't go there.  I happen to like getting my coffee where I like to get it, which isn't Starbucks.  And I have my reasons, a lot of it having to do with what I like coffee to taste like.  I totally understand why people go to Starbucks, and that's their thing.  And I have mine.  I try not to give my friends who drink SB coffee a hard time anymore.  When I lived on LI, I finally understood that little thing called "lack of choice" that my friends in less urban areas used to talk about.  When I was younger and less tolerant, I thought that people who went to SB were crazy - why didn't they just support a local coffeeshop?  Well, there wasn't one.  Just Starbucks.  During 4 years in Stony Brook, I drank my fair amount of Starbucks.  I found that I enjoyed the soy lattes there.  The soy milk diluted the burnt taste.  ;)

I like living Astoria because I have options that match my tastes and my worldview.  Others have theirs.  It is such a refreshing change. 

I also don't think all the architecture in Astoria/LIC is ugly.  Some of it is unappealing, but not all of it.  I'll post about that at some point.
