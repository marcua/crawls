Homeless problem in Astoria.
« on: July 04, 2008, 08:01:23 PM »
Now, before  we get  into who is more  compassionate, or there goes this right-wingnut again railing against homesless  folks. Let me say  that I have contributed much time, to homeless  vets, worked in soup  kitchens  and helped stock pantry's  over the years, and on holidays, while others were having a great weekend. I'm not saying this to blow my own horn, or to point out how great  a guy I am, although, 'we' all know that to be true.  :lol: 

Seriously, over the years, I have noticed it gets  worse in the summer. In my neighborhood, a few blocks from the supermarket, the 'bottle' guys who congregate, sit on milk  cartoons and drink themselves  to uncosciousness  is  bad enough, but, when they sneak into your  yards and defecate  and  urinate, steal bikes, and in general create a nuisance or try to get into basements, it's out  of  control, again. During Mayor  Guilianis  tenure, it  had gotten better, but, it's getting bad again, not 70s-80's  bad , yet,  thankfully, but, bad. There is  these  half dozen guys, most likely either  illegal aliens, since I've tried to speak to at least two and they have no clue about english, none, zero, and they are not faking  it.

The truth is  to get a handle  on this problem, people  have to be taken off streets and evaluated, mentally ill, drug and alcohol problems  have to be separated from those who are just die hard criminals or people who are here illegally and should be deported. This is also a federal problem, but, locally, the city should do alot more, and could do it. Unfortunately, we  have judges  like  this 'Sweet', from a NY Times  article.. “In 1992, a federal judge in Manhattan, Robert W. Sweet, ruled that a state law prohibiting begging on the streets was unconstitutional….Judge Sweet, citing the First Amendment’s guarantee of the right to free speech, ordered the New York Police Department to stop issuing summonses for begging on the street. He even directed the police to stop telling beggars on the sidewalk to “move along.”

This sort  of  nonsense, coupled  with de-institutionalisation of the  late 70's and early 80's, has made it into a  chronic problem. Granted, there  are  socio-economic reasons  for many folks to be out there, including, sadly, families, but, we  are not talking about those folks. These people are chronic, and in some cases, violent people. One time, I nearly ran over  a guys legs, lying under a black  plastic bag, in a driveway, in the rain at night, where I was going on a job.  :-(  This person, for whatever  reason, should not be 'outside.' Obviously he has lost all sense  of reality. When I got out, and removed the bag to see what was under, he pulled at it and mumbled something. I called  an ambulance and he was taken away. No one should be allowed  to block sidewalks or loiter all day outside  homes and business's. It's not a 'right' to be in the street, it's a disgrace, it devalues  the neighborhood and is potentially  dangerous  on a whole host of levels.

As for  the local problem, all I got from 114 Community Affairs is, call us if you see  someone lying on the sidewalk. Well, folks, someone  drinking  on the sidewalk, with his shopping cart buddies, all day, when I go to work, will end up in someone's back yard, or  my  sidewalk, as sure  as I'm sitting  here.  Children and families  live here, and seeing these  guys urinating against  the building, sometimes  making lewd  remarks, to women passing by, should  be  stopped, now. Let's  separate  the truly bad off folks, from the 'bad'  people, who are lumped  together  as  'homeless.' Otherwise, the problem will get worse for the 'working class', and nothing will be done for those in genuine  need of help.

Here is how you get rid of this "homeless problem". First, you order the development of affordable housing (not the BS affordable housing you hear Bloomberg and all our other electeds bragging about... but real affordable housing). Then, you create jobs by getting rid of the incentive for corporations to send jobs overseas (IE: repeal NAFTA and all other "free trade" agreements). Then, you build more homeless shelters and help homeless people find jobs and eventually get affordable housing. This should help get many of the homeless people off the streets.
And then.... you increase the graduation rates at schools to over 99 percent, because since half of our kids aren't currently graduating high school, they have to go somewhere, and while I'm sure not all homeless people didn't graduate, many of them probably didn't. Allowing our kids to go into society without being educated is grounds for disaster. We have to make sure that tomorrow's generation does not end up with a lot of people being homeless.

Of course, this is just a very general and nonspecific plan to help address the "homeless problem". I'm sure a bunch of people will say this involves too much government spending and such.... but hey..... if anyone else has any better ideas I'd like to hear them.
