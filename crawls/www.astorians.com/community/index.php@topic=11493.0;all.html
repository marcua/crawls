I want some freaking delicious coffee to make at home
« on: July 24, 2008, 01:00:06 AM »
All right, people. I currently have no job, so my usual coffee shop trips are out. However, I still want coffee. I have some beans and ground coffee at the moment that I find underwhelming. I have a grinder and a french press, plus one of those melitta one-cup things that's good in a pinch.

What I want is coffee that tastes as delicious as it smells. The coffee I have now smells great, but when I make it the taste is uninspiring. I've tried changing the amount of time it's brewed and the amount of coffee I use, and I've tried it both in the french press and the melitta, but it's still not very good. I do not like a bitter or burnt edge to my coffee, but I don't want it to be so smooth that as soon as I add half & half I can barely taste the coffee anymore. I do not want decaffeinated. I just want coffee that tastes like coffee, with a full-bodied, bold flavor. For reference, since coffee taste is so subjective, a place that I think has good coffee is Veselka, at 2nd Ave and 9th Street. (They also have good tuna sandwiches and cold borscht for summer, btw.)

Flavored coffees are acceptable as an alternative to traditional coffee, but I want them to really taste like the flavor they're supposed to be - none of this "chocolate & peanut butter" stuff I bought that tastes like neither chocolate nor peanut butter - and I'd prefer them to not be artificially flavored. Now, I'm not sure a flavored coffee that fits that description actually exists. Recommendations for tasty, all-natural flavor syrups that people have tried would not be out of place in this thread, as then I can make my own flavored coffee.

Thanks everybody.   :mrgreen:

I bought mine from the Mediterranean Foods II shop next to Artopolis. Anyway the way I make it, and the way i made it when I was living in Israel is like this:fill up the ibrik about 2/3 with water, bring to a boil on a medium to low flame add as much coffee as you feel necessary. Usually I add 2 teaspoons since I usually drink 2 cups. As soon as it gets to the boiling point and is about to foam over take it off the flame and stirring it. Put it back on the flame and let it come to a boil again and repeating the removing it from the flame. At this point add the sugar, again this is all taste based and I like mine very sweet, like 3 spoons for each cup. Put it back on the flame and let it bubble for about 1 minute. Take it off the flame, pour into cups, drink. Enjoy. And this is the usual recommended way of doing it. Boiling it 3 times. Some do it only once, some do it twice.

Can you strain the grounds out before drinking or is that considered incorrect?  Asking this because I really like the last sweet drops at the bottom of the cup and the grounds really mess with that notion.

Well it's probably considered incorrect, but you're certainly free to make it however you want. ;) I occasionally add half & half to the final product. :oops: I've strained the grounds out in the past when I've used turkish coffee to make iced coffee. I liked the Natasha's Blend so much that I wanted to try it that way. Took a long time to make, but it was good. I also know a Turkish girl who drinks the grounds at the bottom of the cup, but I think you're probably not supposed to do that either.

Edava, Bentley's Dad's description sums up the ibrik method. Similar instructions came with my ibrik. I usually use equal parts coffee and sugar, or slightly less sugar. This is from their website:

Quote
The ibrik was originally designed to brew coffee in hot sand in the desert, but a stove top will do fine. First fill the ibrik 2/3 full with water, add sugar to taste (if you like your Turkish coffee sweet) and top it with a heaping teaspoon of finely ground coffee. The coffee seals the narrow top creating an oven effect. As the water begins to boil it will foam up through the coffee. Let it foam up three times. Stir. Pour slowly into two small demitasse cups and it's ready for savoring. Pay attention to the foaming. It is the skill part of the process. If you don't your ibrik will become volcanic and deposit your Turkish coffee on your stove....what a loss and what a mess.

They also recommend letting the grounds settle in the pot before pouring.
