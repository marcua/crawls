Anytime you are looking for a rent stabilized apartment you need to know that the super is your best friend. The 'key fee' has been around since the 1940s. Supers, and not brokers or management office personnel, have always had the power to rent to appropriate tenants. They stock the building with people who are friendly and cooperative. You just need to cough up around one month's rent. They will be readily available for all your needs.

I live in a building with 140 apartments. Ten years ago my super gave me his home telephone number. Anytime I need him, I can call, and he is here within an hour or less. If it is an emergency . . . like when a pipe burst in my kitchen, he was here in 60 seconds.

He can be your best friend . . . or your worst enemy. Tip well.

Zytos and Litza- I can't agree with you guys there. The harm is that it keeps perpetuating itself. I would not like to pay increased rent, but I would like to pay just the rent. The super does nothing to justify getting paid basically.

NY Apartments basically rent themselves. The demand is so high that brokers are really not needed. If they didn't exist, landlords would end up making more money because they would not have to pay out the broker, and tenants would pay less as well. It would be a win-win. Basically these people ultimately increase costs from both ends and profit off it. It's the biggest scam ever: none of their clients benefit or save money.

To answer your question, Litza, about why one is legal and not the other- Key Money is just a preference bribe basically, and Licensed Real Estate brokers are (Allegedly) charging for the service of showing the apartment, running credit checks, etc and (are supposed to)hold some level of accountability. Unfortunately, in my opinion, they seem to blend together as basically the same thing.
