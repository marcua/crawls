I'll bring it back on track.

My wife, our little Nugget, a couple of friends and I went at 5pm today to celebrate the opening of Pachanga. 

A quick review:

The decor and set up is very Vesta-like, with neat artwork on the walls and cozy seating arrangements - with a Mexican twist, of course.  There is space in the back that will be used as a "garden" for the warmer times.

It was great seeing Joe and Leo, the owners, and Michelle Vido popping out of the kitchen.  It's like visiting old friends in a new apartment. 

They've got a guy, I think his name was Don, set up behind the bar exclusively who made excellent drinks and kept the 90s rap going to keep the "pachanga" atmosphere flowing.  I had a "Boludo" and my friends had Margaritas, all very, very tasty drinks.  The man makes a good drink.

And, finally, the food.  The food!  Excellent. 

I'm a fan of Vesta so I'm probably biased, but the food was damn good.

We started with a Tomatillo and Avocado salsa and some Guacamole, both good.  They came with chips that were outstanding. 

The four adults each ordered a different set of tacos.  Between the four of us we had the short rib tacos, the fish tacos, the mooshoo duck tacos, and the pork shoulder tacos.  All four of us walked away raving about the food.  The ladies seemed to really love the mooshoo duck tacos.  My wife called them "out of this world."  Our friend Bob would not share his short rib tacos; he grunted his approval with a mouthful of meat. 

I fell in love with the pork shoulder taco.  With my wife sitting in front of me, and my six month old baby in his seat to my left, all my attention was on the tasty, crunchy pork in front of me.  My weakness is crunchy, fried proteins.  They put toasted pork skin bits in the taco!  Love.  Pure love.

We topped it off with a panacotta with peanut brittle on top and a Little Devil cake (like the Baby Jesus cake, just a little darker).  The Panacotta was nice and the Little Devil cake had a delicious essence of chocolate.  Nothing will take the place of the Baby Jesus cake for me, but for chocolate lovers this might have more appeal.

Overall, I think they're off to a really good start.  These folks know their food, know about atmosphere, and know how to treat their customers well.  I'm looking forward to visiting again.  MMmmm, pork shoulder tacos...

Post by: El Squid on April 21, 2011, 02:52:48 PM
So artgirl and I just tried this place and really liked it.

We had the good luck to snag the corner of the big banquette in the back, so space wasn't a problem. The place was basically full at 7 on a weeknight, but there was no line and we didn't have to wait for a table at all.

We started with cocktails: Death in the Gulfstream (kind of a souped-up gin and tonic with bitters) and the Tanteo Mole Manhattan (sort of a chocolate Manhattan). Both were very good. My Manhattan was quite intense, a more serious cocktail than it sounds. But as I'd hoped, it worked well as a sharp counterpoint to our mellow shared starter:

The black bean soup, which was one of the best I've had. Smoky and rich, with nice grace notes from the lime sour cream. Pretty big, too, for six bucks. I could make a light meal of that and some chips and salsa.

Then we shared three taco orders: the short rib, pollack and duck. The duck was our least-favorite; it was good, well prepared, but didn't scream "duck" and (we agreed) needed something, maybe another sauce (maybe orange, says artgirl). The other two were awesome. The short rib had some heat, with what I called vinegary and artgirl called peppercorn heat. The pollack had some great onion flavors going on, and I'm not usually a huge onion guy. I will definitely be eating those last two tacos again.

We chased that with a liter of sangria, also great. We could really taste the fruit and wine and rum in balance.

Finally we took home an order of the diablito cake. Definitely a worthy evil twin to Vesta's Baby Jesus. The icing-slash-syrup on top was almost a "cake gravy," well integrated and complementary to the cake. I had it with Frangelico (our own stock); I'm thinking it would go very well with maaaaany other such things.

Our server was friendly and attentive. The busboys didn't hustle stuff away too soon (as some have complained above), though I can't say how that would go on a really busy night, since it wasn't overflowing when we were there.

Bill was a hundred before tip.

Already looking forward to returning.

"Grace note," which is indeed a musical term, is often (maybe too often) used metaphorically in criticism of food, architecture, arts other than music, etc., to mean "a small element that adds to the overall composition very subtly, perhaps below the level of full consciousness," or something along those lines. I've heard "grace note" used in reference to food before, too. But "overtones" would mean something else, b/c overtones and grace notes are very different creatures. "Overtones" refers - in music - to the "overtone series", which are pitches that are discernable but not overt. Grace notes are always notated, too, whereas overtones aren't (for the most part).

Artgirl and I went back Monday night for our second visit, and overall had another fine and fun meal, repeating some favorites from last time and trying some new things.
  • The Pachanga Sangria (apples, oranges, mango juice, rum, full liter $24): Again, flavorful but not too sweet. I ate all the boozefruit lustily.
  • Black Bean Soup (BB Soup) (lime-sour cream, crispy tortillas, $6): A favorite last time, the soup itself was equally rich, smoky and flavorful this time, though there seemed to be less of the lime sour cream, or maybe it had just broken up more into the soup. We missed attacking the big dollops that had floated on the previous edition, which really set it apart, and we later told our server, who said shed tell the chef. Id definitely go for it again, but I might ask for extra lime cream (or possibly more grace notes). I reiterate that this big bowl and a drink and maybe some chips and salsa could be my lunch or a light supper most days.
  • The Stuffed Chili (roasted poblano, quinoa, cous cous, cranberry beans, walnut cream sauce, $6): I feared this might be rather dry and cardboard-y with all the grainy stuff listed, but it was a very good starter. The single large pepper was spread open and flat, with the stuffing on top and the sauce mostly underneath and alongside. The overall effect of the grains was kinda like a toasty, savory breakfast cereal with beans, which (like much else here) is slightly offbeat but in a good way. The pepper itself, sometimes an afterthought in stuffed peppers, stood up well, tender and tasty. The sauce was great, it was like sweet cream for my Mexi-Wheatena. Today I realized I might have enjoyed a kick to this dish; maybe Id dash on a little of the hot sauce from the table bottle next time.
  • Steamed Mussels (chorizo, corn & jalepeno, $8): My favorite dish of the night. The delicate mussels in the broth with charred bread for dipping would be a fine dish in itself. But then you find, and scoop up, some of the many corn kernels and chorizo bits under the broth mmmmm. Among the many ways people do mussels in a broth, this is instantly one of my favorites. This dish is listed as a small plate, but as artgirl notes, you get a fair number of mussels, and the corn makes it more substantial, so its suitable as a smaller entre. And its awesome.
  • Roasted Pork Belly Enchilada (chocolate, fig & peanut sauce, cumin crema, $14): When I first tasted this dish, the bright, sweet, peanut and fig flavors of the sauce jumped out at me and seemed a little strange. Artgirl liked the pork a lot but said she found the overall dish perplexing and probably wouldnt want a whole plate of it. I agree that its a little off the wall, but I think thats part of the point at this place, these unexpected, slightly thrown-together juxtapositions. I enjoyed the madness. Id order it again and embrace my perplexity. You make the call!
  • Panna Cotta (peanut brittle crust) (desserts not on written menu, but recited by server): Im a big fan of custardy desserts, and desserts with savory elements, and this is a good example of both. The brittle was basically just perched on top, and was quite salty as well as sweet. A fine complement to the dishes wed eaten.
  • Other stuff: We arrived early, like maybe 6:45, and the place was only about a quarter full, not surprising for early on a Monday evening. Artgirl had a banquette seat, and I had a chair, which wasnt plush or anything, but didnt cause me any discomfort. The backyard was open and seemed thoroughly set up, and a large party trooped out there shortly after our arrival and seemed to enjoy themselves.
