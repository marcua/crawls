I like to think of myself as being made up of a little bit of everywhere I've lived -- California, Oregon, New Jersey, New York.

I think the biggest adjustment for me when I moved here almost 4 years ago was getting used to the incredible convenience of New York. In most cases, ANYTHING you could possibly want is within walking distance and/or a short cab/subway ride away. Or better yet -- it can be delivered. My non-native friends and I refer to New York as "the bubble," because it really is its own little world. The concept of getting into a car and driving long distances to reach a destination is lost on a lot of native New Yorkers, and yet, this is how (most of) the rest of the country lives.

I think my first taste of New Yorker-ness came after I had been here for a year or so and went home to Cali to visit my parents. I was shocked and appalled that the lone Chinese restaurant in the neighborhood 1) closed at 9 p.m. 2) didn't deliver.

One more note on this topic- I just ate a quick lunch at Joe's Pizza in the West Village.  Not anywhere close to the best, but one has to admit that it serves the quintessential New York street slice, which begs to be folded.  I looked around, and saw that a good half of the people eating there were not folding the pizza (it's near NYU, so the chance for non-New Yorker patronage is pretty high).  Among the non-folders, I observed two dominant approaches.  The first: the slice is held with two hands (very non-New York- who has a second hand available?  You're shlepping all your stuff!), the tips of the thumb, index, and middle fingers gingerly grasping each end of the crust, ring finger and pinky dangling in thin air.  The person who eats the pizza this way obviously is concerned about getting his or her hands dirty, but doesn't realize that if he or she doesn't eat quickly, the molten-cheese-laden bulk of the slice is going to come crashing down onto the counter or into his or her lap- you just can't support a floppy New York street slice by holding onto two square milimeters of crust.  Or maybe the person does realize that this is going to happen, but they are praying to the god of skinny punks that it doesn't happen.  The second approach (and this is quite ridiculous) is keeping the slice flat on the paper plate, lifting the plate up to the mouth, and inching the pointy end of the slice bit by bit off the plate while taking smallish bites (and the person so engaged usually gets a mouthfull of paper plate at some point).  Pardon me, but this is wayyyy un-New York.

And one more sign of a non-New Yorker (or perhaps a New Yorker who is being a BAD New Yorker, or even worse, an UPPER EAST SIDER): somebody who orders a bagel "scooped out."  If you don't know what this is, I'm not going to tell you (not into spreading bad ideas).  But it makes me wince every time I witness it.
