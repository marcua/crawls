I'm OK with anything that improves our transportation infrastructure (although I consider bikes as more of recreational vehicle). 

CitiBike would be great for Astoria and at least the bike lanes would be put to good use. 

As a driver and public transportation rider myself, there are pros and cons to both:
Cons: Cars are an expense, they need a place to park, they need maintenance and insurance
Pros: Transportation you can use anytime you want, leaving town, loading stuff etc (I don't want to rely on car rental/sharing services)
Cons: Public transportation is unreliable especially in Queens, constant repair work on weekends.
Pros: useful for getting into Manhattan (or as some of you call it, "the city") during work hours.  Cheap (compared to other transportation).

BTW I was born/raised in Astoria and adapted to my environment.  I don't get angry over bicycles as I do ride them (recreationally) myself.

I literally just purchased my first (used) bicycle as an adult.  (The last time I had a bike was as a young teen!) I'm super excited with my cute little bike, and after literally just 4 rides of just about 20 minutes each, I've already learned and figured out a LOT, just by being aware and using common sense.

1) I completely avoid streets such as Steinway, Bway, 30th Ave, 23rd Ave. (at least, definitely the more narrow 'park end' of 23rd Ave.), 21st St., 31st St, Ditmars.

2) The first time I was going to ride from Point A to Point B - to bring my used bike to a bike shop for them to check it and give it a clean bill of health, I first looked at Google Maps to figure out what streets I should take, and the direction of the traffic.  I figured it's best to know the route and memorize it, before I hop on a bike.

3) I only ride WITH the traffic and I behave just like a car, stopping at all red lights.

4) When riding down a one-way street, I stay on the RIGHT side of the road.  I figured out (all on my own, and thought I was pretty 'smart' to do so ;-) ...that ..... if I stay on the left side of the street I'll be riding alongside Passenger side doors.  If I stay on the right side of the street, I am riding alongside Driver side doors.   So then I thought 'hmmm...I'm thinking that Drivers, who always look out into the street before pulling out...I'm thinking that even after they've parked the car, that they are more in the habit of still looking out into the road, BEFORE opening their door.  Whereas a passenger is somewhat LESS likely to be in that habit, and especially if they themselves don't drive.  So I decided it's much safer for me to ride closer to the driver's side door of parked cars.

5) As I ride up streets, I ring my little bell every few seconds.  I figure better safe than sorry, and that I should always make it clear to people that I'm there.  You never know...there could be a pedestrian I can't see that's about to step out between two parked cars.  There could be a driver inside a car, and I can't see him, and he's just parked and is now about to open his door.  There could be a car that's parked, and they've started their engine, and they think it's ok to start inching out just as I'm  approaching their car.  So yeah, I'll be the irritating lady in the nabe constantly ringing her little bicycle bell.  ;-)

6) During heavier traffic hours, if I find myself up near the front of a line of cars waiting for the light to change, I will often just pull over to the side a bit, turn around and watch for all the cars to pass, and then I will pull back out and continue on my way.  I am being extremely cautious riding on the streets, because I know that even something as simple as a little pebble under my tire the wrong way, could send me flying.  So I don't want to add to that mix, any additional stress with cars and traffic.  I'd rather wait a few seconds, let all the cars pass, and then feel safer proceeding, and without a whole line of cars behind or alongside me.  And I have to say...thus far I've noticed that in such instances, the cars have seemed to defer to me...almost like they are holding back waiting for me to go ahead and pull back out into the lane of traffic.  But I just motion to them that it's ok for them to just go ahead and pass...and that I plan to wait until the road is more clear.  So thank you to all you considerate drivers!

And now for a question for the 'bike people', esp those who keep their bikes locked up on the street.  My plan is to keep my bike on the street overnight since I live in a very small 3rd floor walkup.  I currently keep it locked up (using 3 different locks overnight!) onto one of those circular bike racks that has a horizontal bar.  It's near a bodega which also means constant foot traffic and eyes. If it gets stolen it gets stolen.  I realize I am taking a chance, but it's a calculated risk.  I also plan to 'rotate' the corner where I park my bike, switching it between bodega corners (as someone had told me that if the same one bike is seen locked up in the same spot night after night, potential thieves may eventually assume no one 'cares' about the bike, or that it doesn't have a vigilant owner, that it's 'abandoned' etc.  So they said it's best to move the bike often...make it look like it has an 'active' owner)

Anyway..... I want to buy a bike cover for inclement weather (rain).  During the dead of winter, that's the only time I will hoist my bike up onto my 3rd floor balcony and keep it there.  What I'm trying to figure out is....if I get a bike cover, and my bike has two chains plus the U-lock, all connect to the horizontal bar of the bike rack....once I try to put the cover over my bike, I guess the cover can only go down as far as the horizontal bar...cuz then the chain locks will be in the way.  So the cover won't be able to go down to the very bottom of the bike.  Will that cause a problem...is rain very problematic say for the wheel spokes? 

Thanks!
