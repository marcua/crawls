First, a thanx to aquaecny for hipping me to the fact that the N was running express Manhattan bound Sunday from 7 to 7 because I wouldn't have known.    One of the better MTA innovations in recent memory had been the weekly e-mail list they send out every Friday listing all the weekend service changes.   For some inexplicable reason, I was dropped from their e-mail list.

In any event travel on the N was a horror show literally all day yesterday.   I didn't have much trouble getting in to Manhattan shortly after noon -- thanx to aquaecny -- but when I got to the Astoria Blvd. station it was packed to the gils with people waiting for the train and a collective howl went out when a recording came on for explaining that there will be service delays due to "necessary trackwork."   Lucky for me I didn't have to wait for long.

Then shortly after 3 I was Astoria bound again trying to pick up the N at 57th Street and I didn't keep track of time, but there seemed to be an interminable wait for the Astoria-bound N as two R trains came and went before an N finally arrived.    Then they held us at the Lexington Ave. stop for a while and then again just outside of the Queensboro Plaza station on the curve.    Finally home.

I wanted to go back into Brooklyn last night to catch a movie at BAM and gave myself 70 minutes -- I left my apartment at 8:06 so I must have gotten to the 30th Avenue station at 8:12 give or take a minute or two and two trains ran right by us before a third train finally arrived at 8:45.   Was I pissed!   I got to Queensboro Plaza about 8:50 and said "I can't make it to BAM in time" so I turned around and went home.

Bad day to take the subway I guess.

Not a horror story, but a story of an incident yesterday evening...

I took the 6 train up to Lex and was transferring onto a W yesterday evening, around 6:30, when there was a commotion on the train car I was boarding. People were yelling for a doctor and to stop the train. Turns out a young guy (maybe 18 years old) was having a seizure. The conductor came back, and got a supervisor, but they weren't very helpful. Much more helpful was a doctor (dressed in blue scrubs, even!), who figured out what was happening, and helped as the kid's seizure ended. (Much less helpful was the women who said "pull the emergency brake!" even though the conductor was right there, and "give him water!" even though a doctor was standing next to the kid). Anyway, after it became clear that the kid was OK, everybody got on the train and we went on to Queens. The doctor was great, very calm, asked nearby people what they saw and the kid how he was feeling. He tried to call the doctor on the kid's medalert necklace, too, once we were out of the tunnel. By the time we got to Astoria Blvd, the doctor had left and the kid was calling his friend saying he'd be a bit late...

Anyway, that's what happened if anyone wondered what the 10-minute delay at Lex was all about last night.

Hooray for responsible bystanders.
