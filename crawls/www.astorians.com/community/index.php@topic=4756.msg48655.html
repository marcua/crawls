At the risk of being politically incorrect.....

I like American Idol.

Some of you may miss the point and that it is 3 different shows, each distinct in its own way.

First, are the Audition Shows. These are there for comedy. To take these shows seriously is like doing an indepth social analysis of the 3 Stooges. The Stooges are funny slapstick comedy. Woody Allen once said something about what a waste it is to analyze and dissect comedy and how its a waste of time. Either you dig the stooges or you don't. Some people like South Park    

Second are the elimination round shows. These are drama shows where one can look at potential talent and see backstage workshops and molding of talent and personalities. If you like to see talented people persue their dreams and what it takes to conquer their fears,
this show can be compelling

Lastly there is the talent competition, where truly talented unknown can go from diamonds in the rough to shining stars. Kelly Clarkson, Jennifer Hudson, Paris Bennet, Carrie Underwood, and so many others grew into popular singers.

Is pop music for everyone? No some are classical purists, some are jazz aficianados and never listen to anything else.


American Idol at its best also does contribute 2 things to our culture today IMHO.

In the Finals they have theme shows, one of which is Broadway.
Last year I watched and said to myself this is like a live Broadcast of a Broadway Show. Kathy Mcphee performing was so reminiscent of see Kristen Cheniworth on Broadway. There are a lot of people in Kansas or the like who have no access or exposure to live Broadway, and many people, families and children got some exposure to a cultural experience they would not have otherwise.


Second, AI is the one family type of show that all the family can and does watch together. Mom, Dad the kids and even Grandma actively watch and share their opinions and ideas and interact together.  If there's something out there that help Granny and the kids get together on,  it can;t be all bad.

I like how Paula balances out the other two.  She has a really nice approach most of the time.  And the other two, when they put their mind's to it, can be pretty nice, too.  Simon has his schtick that he developed from Season 1.  I think the viewers expect him to be a snarky bastard most of the time.

I like what Bryan calls the Second Show.  It's so interesting to see those backstage workshops.  Most of the participants sound pretty good, too.  Right now in the early stage of the show, I find it fascinating to hear all the voices, and it's really crystal clear who is singing well and who is not.  Those who move on sing with an effortlessness that is really appealing to my ear.  I like also seeing the nervous people conquer their performance anxiety.  I have accompanied perfectly fine singers in the past who suffer from acute performance anxiety that let it get to them, and that is harder for me to experience than watching any of this American Idol.  I can't tell you how helpless I feel sometimes to sit there playing while the singer nosedives into their anxiety, knowing I can only help them in a limited fashion; but I will admit I've become a better accompanist by being in these situations!  Those who let it get to them all exhibit classic symptoms:  shaky voice, bad intonation, unfocused tone, and bashful body language.  So, it is so great to see people work through their anxiety!

Anyway, I'm watching American Idol with no shame.

I've watched American Idol every season since it came out, and I find no shame in that. I'm a singer and was a voice major in college, along with being well educated in various facets of music, but I find the show entertaining and enjoy watching the competition as it progresses. I like hearing the same singers sing different genres every week.. that's actually my favorite part of the show, the variety as it goes along. Some very talented singers have gotten their names out there because of the show, and I think that's a good thing in a country where you can only hope to be heard by the masses if you're signed to a major label. I don't like the crap music the winners have to sing either, but Kelly Clarkson's come a long way since she won and has actually come out with some very listenable stuff, albeit strictly pop/rock.

Most people seem to like these early auditions more than the rest of the show, but I don't like the early stuff. Mainly I don't like that they focus on so many awful singers who are clearly just trying to get their 15 minutes of fame by being the worst singer/personality imaginable. But I also don't like this new thing that they seem to be doing more and more: they play a sob story about the person who's auditioning, like that it's a someone from a really poor family who's saved up just to go to this audition and it would mean so much for them to make it through, or someone who was teased throughout their childhood and is trying to prove everyone wrong.. etc.. and so you're rooting for this person and expect them to sound good, and then they sing and they're just horribly, horribly bad.

Why does the show choose to shame people like this on national television? They're not arrogant, or nasty, or otherwise annoying and therefore deserving of national mockery. They're nice people who've had some serious problems and are genuinely trying to do something to improve their lives a little. I'm not saying that auditioning for American Idol is a great way to improve your life, but it would be nice if the producers of the show would grow a conscience and pick their losers a little more thoughtfully. I mean, at the very least, they could just show the auditions without playing the life story. I have no problem believing that someone who's tone deaf doesn't know how bad a singer they really are. I'm just confused as to why none of their friends or family would warn them not to audition if they didn't want to make fools of themselves. It's one thing if they're auditioning for the show secretly, but for the ones with parental support I just don't get it.

That kind of brings me to my next point. A lot of people have been talking about a sense of entitlement. The convenience of our society has lead a lot of people to think that things should just be handed to them without their having to work for them, and the seemingly ever increasing focus on children's self-esteem creates people that think they're great and special regardless of how good at anything they actually are, realistically. Society doesn't preach humility and respect; it preaches "you" and "me" and how we can do things to benefit ourselves. And being a rock star seems, to the general public, to be a great way to benefit one's self.

But people need to learn and accept when they're just not good at things. Parents are afraid to discourage their children by telling them they're bad at something. Some will even lash out at teachers when their child receives a bad grade or doesn't make it into an honors course. I have a friend who's a middle school band director, and she's actually gotten phone calls from angry parents demanding to know things like why their daughter isn't first flute. So of course they'll be supportive when their little one wants to audition for a nationally televised show to fulfill their dream of being a pop singer. You'd think the bad auditions would make anyone really cautious about auditioning, but it seems to have the opposite effect as horrible people who truly think they can sing just keep coming through.

Then again, I can be kind of extreme in that I basically think society only develops to destroy itself. We become smarter only to become dumber as a whole, all our great inventions only serving to make us lazy and complacent. I'm also aware that this particular sentiment has been around for longer than I have. Oh well. I'll watch American Idol again tomorrow night or whenever the next one airs.

I don't think that we as a society are afraid. I think it's that the people who are there for moral support/teaching aren't being frank. I've been around enough truly talented singers to just smile and say thank you and laugh internally when I hear that stuff, but someone who was really invested in being the next rock star might surround themselves with people who truly don't know any better.
