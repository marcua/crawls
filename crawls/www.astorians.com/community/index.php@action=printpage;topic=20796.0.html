Post by: replan on April 05, 2011, 01:23:19 PM
The terms "legal rent" and "preferential rent" usually only apply to rent stabilized leases.  Look at your current lease or the renewal that your LL sent, they both should say right on the top of the first page if it's rent stabilized (RS).

If your building is only 2-3 years old and you have a RS lease, I'm betting your building was built with the aid of a property tax abatement called 421a. In the 421a program, the developer gets reduced property taxes for a defined period after completion. In return they have to put the building into RS for the life of the tax abatement.

So there are 3 possibilities, and the third is the most likely:
1) You're not in an RS building and somehow the terms 'legal' and 'preferential' are being used inappropriately (it happens, frequently people in property management offices become so mechanized in their processes that they make this kind of mistake, but as above looking at your lease should answer this question).
2) If you are RS, the 421a tax abatement could be expiring, at which point the LL can exit RS and charge whatever they want as long as the predicate notices have been appropriately given (I doubt this because 421a in most circumstances runs for 10-25 years depending how it was filed, and the fact that your LL is still using the 'legal' and 'preferential' terms, but hey, see #1).
3) If you are RS, you were given a preferential rent on your last lease(s). Under this circumstance the LL is allowed to bump your rent up to the legal rent starting with the next renewal. But there are conditions to this – you have to be notified in the lease or in a lease rider that this situation exists, what the legal and preferential rents are, and there has to be a statement to the effect that the LL has the right to increase the rent to the legal rent at the end of the current term.  If you had multiple renewals from the time you moved in, then you have to be informed of the above with each and every lease – one break in the chain can negate the LL’s ability to charge the legal rent.

So you really need to pull out all of your leases and examine them closely to make sure you were given the correct information at that time.  If they didn’t do everything correctly, you can file a complaint with the NYS agency that oversees RS, DHCR http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent (http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent). But if they dotted and crossed their I’s and T’s, this is probably legal.  For more information go here:  http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/FactSheets/orafac40.pdf (http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/FactSheets/orafac40.pdf).

Hope this helps…

Post by: replan on April 06, 2011, 12:49:29 PM
Hey Griff.  So it does seem that you have a RS lease.  If the Dept of Finance or HPD are unhelpful post or PM me your address and I can look up pretty quickly what they’d tell you.  But all that’s going to do is confirm why your building is RS, and won’t have an effect on your immediate circumstance.

In re: rent increases, I understand your confusion about the RGB guidelines; the RS system can be really byzantine, and 421a abatements can make it even more complicated.  The RGB’s mandate is to set renewal rates that are intended to be applied to legal rents, not preferential rents.  And where 421a is involved there are certain increases that are allowed beyond that.  But conceptually, think of the legal rent as being a rent cap.  The LL is allowed to charge anything they want up to and including that cap.  If they can’t get the maximum they’re allowed to charge less for the term of a lease and then increase it to that cap (plus any RGB/421a allowed increase) after that lease is over, BUT the tenant must be notified of the situation in writing in the preceding lease or lease rider.  If you didn’t get such a notice when you signed your lease, then they can’t increase your rent to the cap.  But if you did get such a notice, then they can. 

So you need to look very carefully at all of your lease documents – you’re looking for something that effectively says that you acknowledge the legal rent is $XXXX, but for the term of the lease you’re being charged $YYYY, and you further acknowledge that the LL can increase the rent to the legal rent plus any lawful increases for any renewal.  For 421a there should also be notification of the increases under that program.  If there’s no such statement or rider, then they can’t increase your rent beyond the RGB percentages, and you may want to file a complaint with DHCR.

Presuming that all of your lease documents are in order, what appears to be happening here is that your LL is allowed to increase your legal rent to a point that is well in excess of what the market will bear, and is increasing your rent to what they believe market rates are.  But at the same time they don’t necessarily want you to leave because they’ll lose income while it’s not rented and have to paint/prep the apartment for the next tenant.  So if all your lease docs are indeed in order, what I’d do is try to negotiate the renewal rent down some.  They may not budge but at least you’ll have taken a shot.

There’s a bit more generic info about lease renewals here:
http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/FactSheets/orafac4.pdf (http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/FactSheets/orafac4.pdf)

And info about 421a rent increases here:
http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/PolicyStatements/orap922.pdf (http://nysdhcr.gov/Rent/PolicyStatements/orap922.pdf)

If ultimately you think you didn’t get the appropriate notice about the legal/preferential rents and you want to file a complaint with DHCR, the appropriate form is here:
http://nysdhcr.gov/Forms/Rent/ra90.pdf (http://nysdhcr.gov/Forms/Rent/ra90.pdf)

But If you don’t have a rider or statement in your lease re: the above, before filing a complaint, I’d advise asking your LL for a copy of any notice that they gave you at that time.  If they can’t produce it, tell them that they can’t increase your rent beyond the RGB increases.  If they persist, then file the complaint.

Post by: TrueBlue on May 11, 2011, 04:44:57 PM
Two things:


  • Search the lease and confirm that when the preferential rent is listed it DOES NOT state that it's for 1 year only.  If the preferential rent does not specifically limit itself to a period of time it is deemed to be in effect for the entire term of the lease (including all renewals).  That makes the preferential rent the amount that all increases are based on.
  • The limits for increases for leases commencing on or after October 1, 2011 are 3% to 5.75% for 1 year leases and 6% to 9% for 2 year leases.  Given the timing of your post you're probably still in last year's window, in which case the increases are limited to 2.25% for 1 year leases and 4.5% for 2 year leases.

Post by: ManWithNoName on June 19, 2011, 12:41:43 AM
Griff,
 
Is this issue still ongoing, or did you reach a resolution with the LL?  If this renewal issue is still ongoing, I have a couple of observations/suggestions, based on your information:
 
1) 421-a is a program administered by HPD.  The owner gets a tax abatement on his/her new construction by participating in this program.  In return, the owner agrees to be subject to the RSL for a period of 10, 15, 20, 25 years.  HPD also establishes a maximum rent the LL can charge  (for the building as a whole, and per room), and still receive the tax benefits from the City.  The Dept. of Finance is the agency which provides the actual tax break.
 
2) You can verify the 421-a status of the building in a number of ways - check the lease provisions carefully, there should be some language that the building is under the 421-a program.  You can also contact HPD to confirm that the building is/was a 421-a construction (they may even provide you with documentation, such as the Certificate of Eligibility).  Finally, you can go to DHCR to get a rent history for your apartment, which should indicate the program(s) which it is operating under, as well as whether it's rent stabilized.
 
3) I assume you are the first tenant in your apartment, since you mentioned new construction built 2-3 years ago.  If so, you may want to determine whether being a first tenant may have granted you special rights or privileges under the RSL or RSC.  The question is obviously going to boil down to what the initial legal regulated rent for your apartment is.  The LL is claiming 2,800, and asserting your rent is preferential.  Do not simply assume your LL is in the right because he says so.  Note that the LL is required to register rents with DHCR (which is mandatory initially, and annually thereafter), but this registration does not establish this rent as the "legal rent."  DHCR HAS THE FINAL WORD ON THIS.
 
4) As I understand the RSL, it's supposed to afford great(er) protection for tenants, and supposed to create stability in tenancies through the city/community.  Assuming your apartment is RS, the owner's effort to raise the rent on you by 12% seems inconsistent with RSL.  As you mentioned, the RGB establishes percentage increases, which are set for 1 and 2 year leases (421-a also allows for 2.2% increases per year, which must be calculated off the base rent).  Allowing the 421-a owner to increase the rent by 12%, or 36% max increase in your situation, seems to either be a big loophole in the law or the LL is making a mockery of the RSL.  If you think about it, the prospective 2nd tenant in your apartment will be subject to the same issues you're currently facing, assuming the tenant pays 2,300.  What stops the LL from asking for 2,500 when the 2nd tenant's lease is up.  This is an 8.6% increase, and once again makes the RSL look like a joke.   
 
5) You may want to consult an attorney if you want to explore all your rights as a RS tenant in a 421-a building.  I think the focus should be on the 2,800 figure that the LL claims is the legal rent....
 
Hope this is helpful.
