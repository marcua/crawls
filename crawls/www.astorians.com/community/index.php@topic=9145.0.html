I've heard the same--that she can charge you the increase, even if you didn't break it (because conceivably, she would raise rent on a new tenant to cover the costs).

An article in the NY Times says that's the case:

"Ms. Herlihy said that under the Rent Stabilization Code, a landlord would be entitled to a rent increase for the installation of a new refrigerator only if the landlord supplies the new unit and only if the tenant consents to the increase."

So your option is to keep the old fridge, or pay the higher rent, looks like.

Nosill, the only circumstances where what your landlord is telling you is true is if you wanted a new one and the old one worked fine, or if you had a hand in breaking the fridge.  If you broke it, they can make you pay for it outright.  If you just wanted a new one, and they agreed to replace it, then the landlord can add 1/40th of the cost to your rent.

If you are rent stabilized and the fridge was in the apartment when you moved in, then a refrigerator is a required service for that apartment that the landlord must provide.  Period.  If the fridge breaks through no fault of yours, then your landlord is required to repair or replace it at their cost, and if they chose to replace it, that's up to them.  The only way they would be allowed to add 1/40th of the cost to your rent is if you sign an agreement atating that you agree to this.  So don't sign anything.

My landlord/tenant attorney girlfriend says...

There's a chart somewhere (offhand I don't know where it is) that lists the "lifespan" of certain household appliances. If the appliance is over the age of the lifespan, the landlord is not entitled to the 1/40th improvement increase. If the appliance is under the age of the lifespan, then they are entitled to the increase.

Also, do you have proof that the refrigerator was broken, like a call to 311 or a letter to the landlord?

And finally, if the appliance was under the refrigerator lifespan (which I believe is around 20 to 25 years), and you don't have proof that it was broken, and you plan on staying in that apartment longterm, you may want to just make the landlord an offer... that 1/40th is on your rent forever, not just until the appliance is paid off. If you decide to go that route, then make sure you get that agreement in writing.

As per the NYS Division of Housing and Community Renewal's (DHCR) Fact Sheet #3:

"Required services or essential services for apartments may be building-wide, such as heat, hot water, elevator service, and maintenance of public areas of the building. The service may also be something furnished within an individual apartment, such as a refrigerator, stove, air conditioning equipment, or painting.

When an owner provides equipment or services, such as a refrigerator or an air conditioner, the owner must maintain it in good working order. Defective equipment must be repaired or replaced. The owner does not have to replace defective equipment with brand new equipment. The defective equipment may be replaced with reconditioned or used equipment, provided it is in good working order. The owner is not entitled to any increase in rent based on the cost of reconditioned or used equipment."

And DHCR's Fact Sheet #12:

"An owner who pays for a substantial increase of dwelling space or an increase in services, or new furniture or furnishings, or the installation of new equipment or improvements provided in or to an occupied rent stabilized or rent controlled apartment, and has obtained the written consent of the tenant to the rent adjustment, may adjust the rent by 1/40th of the cost of the new item(s) including installation costs, but not finance charges. An order from DHCR is not required before the owner may so adjust the rent. However, for rent controlled housing accommodations, before an owner may so adjust the rent, such owner must file an "Owner's Notice of a Rent Increase Based on Increased Services/New Furnishings/Equipment/Painting; and Tenant's Statement of Consent" (DHCR Form RN-79b) with DHCR."

ANd DHCR's Fact Sheet #26:

"Where an owner installs a new appliance in, or makes an improvement to, an apartment, the owner may be entitled to adjust the rent of that apartment for the new appliance or improvement without approval of the DHCR.

If there is a tenant in occupancy of the apartment, the owner can only apply a rent adjustment for the IAI if the tenant consents in writing to pay an increase in rent for the improvement(s). However, if the apartment is vacant, tenant consent is not required. The adjustment to the tenant's rent , which is permanent, is 1/40th of the total cost of the improvement including installation. For example, if a new refrigerator is installed in an apartment, and the owner's expense is $320.00, the tenant's rent would be adjusted by: 1/40 of $320.00 = $8.00 per month. The increase, if taking place on a vacancy, is not compounded by the vacancy adjustment described in Item 1 above, but rather should be added to the lawful rent after application of that adjustment. (See Fact Sheet #12, " Rent Adjustments for New Services, New Equipment or Improvements to An Apartment", for additional information.)"

The link to the Fact Sheets are here:

http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orafac3.htm
http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orafac12.htm
http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orafac26.htm

It's important to note that this is only in reference to Rent Stabilized apartments, and similar provisions may or may not apply to non-rent regulated apartments, depending on the circumstances.

In re: the useful life of appliances, I belive the L&T GF is referring to building wide Major Capital Improvement (MCI) increases, as noted here:

http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orafac11.htm
http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orafac33.htm
http://www.dhcr.state.ny.us/ora/pubs/html/orao902.htm

but this only applies when the landlord has replaced appliances building wide, in all apartments, not in an individual apartment, and the landlord has applied to and recived from DHCR for permission to implement such a rent increase.

There's significant case law to back this up, but I don't have the resources personally to post those links.
