Hi nyinspring,

Here's what you should know first...the N train to Astoria has six stops once you're past Queensboro Plaza and most people refer to one of these stops when they talk about where they live - 39th Ave, 36 Ave, Broadway, 30th Ave, Astoria Blvd, and Ditmars Blvd (the last stop on the train).

Ditmars is not the main commercial strip, but you will find all your basics (groceries, drugstores, restaurants, etc.) on Ditmars between 31st St. and Steinway.  The rest of Ditmars area is more residential and Astoria park lies at the west end of Ditmars (a great place to hang out in the summer).  I lived off Ditmars when I first moved to Astoria and I thought it was great!

Steinway Street is probably the most commercial area, they have a lot of big chain stores (Gap, Victoria's Secret, Payless).  I would not reccommend living on Steinway, but that's just my personal opinion.

Broadway is pretty busy too but it's a good street to find bars and restaurants and some shopping.  If you live off the Broadway stop on the N train you're pretty centrally located.

30th Ave is where I live now and I really love living here.  Once you cross Steinway it becomes quieter and more residential (this is true for most streets) but you can still get everything you need in a few short blocks.

36th Ave and 39th Ave areas I don't know too much about but I know they can be a little more industrial.  I'll let someone who lives in one of those neighborhoods fill you in more.

The area west of 21st St. and south of 30th Ave can be a little sketchy in some parts but as far as Astoria goes you really can't go wrong.  I'm sure other people will fill in any blanks that I left.  Good luck and (hopefully) welcome to Astoria!

I live just north of the Ditmars stop and recommend it highly because there are a ton of great restaurants and useful shops. Lots of the restaurants are open until 11am or later, so you can walk around at night and there's usually lots of people around, especially near this stop, and it feels really safe.

One of the best things about the Ditmars stop is that you are the last stop on the train. That means when you are leaving to go out to other parts of Astoria (or more likely, Manhattan) that you can always get a seat, since the trains sit there for maybe 3-5 minutes on weekdays and 10+ minutes on weekends, and you are less likely to see the doors close in your face as you run up the stairs. In the winter, there is almost always at least one train waiting, so you don't have to be out in the cold (the stations in Astoria are above ground). Lastly, sometimes the train unexpectedly turns into an express if they are off schedule. In that case, it only stops at Ditmars, Astoria Blvd, and Queensboro Plaza. This is another small little benefit to know about that pays off in bad weather.

Another great thing about Astoria is if you walk about 20 minutes (from this part) westward, there is a huge park called Astoria Park and the view of Manhattan is phenomenal. You can't get that view living in Manhattan! In fact, there's also a a pretty great view just from the subway platforms, since most of Astoria is 3 stories or less.

To add a little bit aobut the other stops:

Astoria Blvd is surrounded by on/off ramps to a highway and the Triborough bridge, so it seems a little hectic there and I wouldn't necessarily recommend that immediate area to live in.

30th Avenue and Broadway are very commercial, mostly between 31st Street and going out to Steinway, but these shops also extend to Crescent Street on the other side of 31st St. You might not want to live ON these streets (as you'd be living above a shop) but being within a block or two will make shopping and getting to the subway easier.

Astoria has tons of: 99c stores, beauty parlors, small furniture stores, Greek restaurants, banks (there are about 7 on one block near Ditmars), butchers, and delis. Everything is "mom and pop commercial", save Steinway Street, which has name brand stores (as someone mentioned, Gap, etc) mixed in w/ Mom and Pop stores.

My last tip about Astoria is if you can buy something, consider it. This is the new hot and upcoming area. Even though I think some of the co-ops are overpriced (esp the new ones), but buying in an older building could be a great investment.

All this said, we might be moving to Brooklyn soon (for my husband's job), but Astoria was great to us.

Teresa

Hey nyinspring!

A few more things to add....

I have always gone thru a broker for my apartments but I do know that Astoria is one of the few places that you can still find an apartment without one.  Just don't let them push you around (and some of them can be pushy!), tell them what you're looking for, set a maximum and stick to it!  They always try to edge you upwards in price.  The norm is roughly $1100-$1300 for one bedroom, $1300-$1600 for two bedrooms.  If you have anymore questions, keep asking, everyone here has an opinion!

I love Astoria and have lived here for around 2 years, and everyone's pretty much summed up the great things about the area. But I'll mention some of the negative stuff too, because it's good to keep in mind and so you're not surprised.

The area smells like sewage fairly often, usually at night. It's not as bad as it used to be, but it reeked for a couple nights last week. I'm pretty sure everyone who posts on this board has smelled this, and we've had threads about it. It's not enough to make me want to move, but it is annoying and also annoying that the city doesn't seem bothered to do anything about it, which brings me to my main point: Astoria, and Queens in general, doesn't get the care and attention from the city that Manhattan does. Did you hear about the week-long blackout that happened last summer? That involved much of northwestern Queens. We lost power during a normal summer heatwave. The city didn't even address this until four days into it, when Bloomberg made a snarky speech about how we're whining about the power being out. Over 100,000 people and all the local businesses were without power aside from the few places that could afford large generators. ConEd, the electric company, lied to the city about the severity of the problem. The city didn't start showing signs of giving a crap until after 4 days. Then we finally had cops directing traffic at the intersections that had no lights, and we got a couple vans handing out food and ice to people. My power was restored after 6 days. The blackout put some places out of business. Both ice cream shops in the Ditmars area, which is where I live, never re-opened after that. This was just last year. There's a power plant down the street from me, a 10-minute walk away, ironically.

Even though ConEd lied about it and prevented us from getting city aid for days, Bloomberg insists that they did a fine job dealing with the problem. Well the problem is obviously that the electrical system hasn't been maintained well enough to support the burgeoning population of the area. Some people blame this on illegal conversions, but ConEd isn't stupid. They know this is a pretty chill part of the city, made up for the most part of working class people who aren't rich, and we aren't going to cause much of a stink about anything. The city craps all over us because they can. That experience left a very bitter taste in my mouth about Mr. Bloomberg.

But what impressed me about that week was how well we dealt with it. There was no increase of crime, no looting, no riots. Just people complaining peacefully, albeit angrily. That, I believe, is the best illustration of how great this area is. Now, there is still crime as this is part of New York City after all, but as a small female who walks around at night a lot I feel much safer here than in most other parts of the city. We might not have the best infrastructure, we might have a bedbug problem, we might not have the stylish boutiques and gourmet markets that Manhattan has, and we might have high pollution, but this is still a very comfortable, convenient, and good place to live in.
