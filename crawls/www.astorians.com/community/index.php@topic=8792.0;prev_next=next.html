First of all, the NYPD has other motives rather than effectiveness in evaluating the effectiveness (or lack thereof) of its bag check program, or its random checks of cars and trucks entering bridges and tunnels.  For one thing, the program creates the impression that they are "fighting terrorism."  Creating an illusion of public safety by doing something very visible but which is, in reality, ineffective, can placate a lot of people and reassure them that they are "safe".  I think, to me at least, that it's just common sense that such a program can't be an effective deterrent to crime, for the reasons I've already written about in my prior posts.  Now, we can argue about whether the fact that some people might be "reassured" by such measures by the police is enough to justify those measures.  In my opinion they are not justified, because the police can have just as visible a presence and create a better deterrent to crime of all kinds, not just a possible terrorist attack, by reinvesting those resources which are now being used for the random bag checks, into more active patrols in every station...more "eyes" and "ears" to thwart crime on the subways. 

Regarding everything else you've said, regarding civil liberties and data/info mining: you're just deflecting the issue.  My argument thus far has been that the bag checks are poor and ineffective law enforcement.  Civil liberties are an afterthought, not because I don't value them (I do, quite strongly in fact), but because I don't feel that the argument even needs to get that far.  I feel that from a law enforcement standpoint, the random checks are ineffective and wasteful, which for me at least, means that the argument against them doesn't even need to proceed to the more highly-charged arguments regarding civil liberties and info gathering.  Making references to data mining and the info gathering (or "cookies" and IP logging) that you're referring to is also deflecting the discussion far from its original purpose.  Oh, and if I'm going to be that paranoid about info gathering, I might as well stop posting here too, since my IP address is logged by the site.

As for the reactions of people surrounding the baggage check, again, this all has to do with visibility versus effectiveness.  A couple of cops performing random bag checks are highly visible, but for those same reasons, it becomes an ineffective tool of policing, in this specific case.  If I had a backpack full of razor blades, or knives, or explosives, or a bomb, and I walked down the stairs and saw random checks being performed, I'd leave, and go to another entrance or another nearby station without checks, and enter from there.  And to seem as inconspicuous as possible, I'd probably go to a Metrocard vending machine or something of the sort, buy a Metrocard and take off, just to make it seem like my trip into the station was justified.

Personally, I think having those cops as extra eyes and ears on patrol would be more effective in picking up on suspicious behavior, than ridiculous bag checks.
