Quote from: Frappe on February 08, 2008, 10:59:37 AM
Adam, I cannot help but think that someone who has looked at history even with a marginal amount of objectivity would make such statements.

But you're not looking at history objectively, either. Based on the few historical precedents you mentioned earlier, you are ascribing to the quite narrow view of economic determinism:

Quote
I can tell you that throughout history, such thinking has not been oboslete.  Severe inequities of wealth distributions are almost always one of the chief components of the downfall of most civilizations.  The Greeks in the 3rd century BC, Roman Republic in 2nd century BC, Roman Empire in the 5th AD, Bryzantines (nearly!) in the 7th century, Franks in the 10th Century, Croats in the 11th century, Spanish in 17th, French in 18th, Russians in 20th.

And even in that statement I emphasized, you're being equivocal. But let's look briefly at the facts:

- In Greece and Rome, wealth inequality existed long before other conditions arose (namely warfare, population shifts and political upheaval) that precipitated their respective downfalls. Especially in Rome, massive influx of non-Romans, whether refugees or invaders, simply overwhelmed the state's ability to maintain civil order.

- Byzantium found itself in a religious war with Muslim Arabs, and among themselves over doctrinal Christianity.

- The Frankish kingdom did not die out in the 10th century. What they had was the equivalent of a regime change, replacing one leading clan (the Carolingians) with another (the Capetians), leading to the creation of France. Even so, Hugh Capet was elected ruler of France -- he didn't take the throne by force.

- Croatia essentially got its assed kicked by a stronger, more aggressive and expansionist Hungarian kingdom.

- Spain was also embroiled in conflict: Protestant reformation, nascent nationalism in the Low Countries, ongoing wars with England and Ottoman Turkey in the Mediterranean. Even so, you can't say that Spain "fell" at this time -- its Empire lasted for another 200 years.

- In Russia, wealth inequality was actually mitigated by the end of the 19th century. War exhaustion, made worse by increased repression and a homegrown militant insurgency killed the Tsar and his ministers.

Little spurts on each one and we can go into greater detail of any one of them.

The democracy and power of the greek cities rested on its citizens.  These citizens had enough wealth to purchase their own arms and their want to defend their state.  Greek cities become impotent as they lost their relative wealth distributions, and less and less citizens were able to be in the army.  Sparta was never really conquered, it just faded away as it found itself unable to defend itself anymore, as there were fewer citizens able to fight. 

The roman republic also fielded armies made up of citizens who purchased their own arms.  As the wealth inequities grew, less Roman citizens were able to (or wanted to) defend the state.  This necessitated the Marian reforms.  Armies were now professional armies loyal to generals and the republic fell soon thereafter (The whole of Roman history upto the reforms were based on this).  The Roman Empire continued on of course, but the republic was done. 

The Roman Empire and the Byzantine Empire both had huge populations but could not fill those armies with citizen recruits.  They increasingly had to rely on barbarian, mercendaries and conscripts, none of whom particularly cared about the state as a whole.  This is especially true with the arabs being able to conquer the severely demoralized Egyptian garrisons.  In the 10th century, the Byzantines recognized this and specifically redistributed wealth to recreate the citizen army.  And it worked for a couple of centuries until Manizkert.

The armies of Charlemagne shrunk in size as the frankish nobility kept taking advantage of the warring between the states created after Verdun to increase their own power.  The disparity in wealth distribution caused the clannish army which originally gave strength to the franks (and goths, vandals, et cetera).  They were replaced by a smaller army of nobles, who could not keep the empire together nor fight off the Vikings.

The Croats also lost its citizen armies as the nobility pushed feudalism on the nation.  The citizen became a serf and no longer had the means or inclination to fight and they got their butts kicked by the hungarians.

Spain didn't fall, but it was well in decline by the 1600's.  The gold they brought in from the Americas was a retardant on their industry and towns, which are two things which bring about a middle class.  These did develop in their competitors, which brought strength and prosperity to eclipse the Spanish.  A poor peasantry existed here (and in Portugal) longer than anywhere else in Western Europe.

There was income mitigation in Russia, but it was far too late and moved way too slowly for Russia to keep up.  It had appalling wealth inequities in 1917 (this has to be looked at in a relative manner).  Industry did not develop nor did a middle class, especially in comparison to the rest of Europe.  The homegrown military insurgency was in part because of the wealth inequities and the lack of a stake people had in the government.

I am a big fan of economic determinism.  I'm not sure how that is unobjective view of history.
