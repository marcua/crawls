Okay, so I finally found Heliopolis (actually thanks to the brunch thread, I ended up walking down Broadway) and when I saw the beer there, I rejoiced. The Trade Fair on 30th ave by the subway has a pretty boring selection of beer. I mean, they have St. Pauli Girl and they have all the Brooklyn beers--but there really isn't anything there that's Belgian style beyond Blue Moon (which is a good standby, but not my favorite beer).

The Key food on 30th ave seems to be hit or miss. I went in there once and they didn't have much at all, but yesterday, they did have Fin du Monde, which was nice to see.

I am really happy with Heliopolis. They don't have Shipyard or Berkshire, but they do have a lot of other similar mircobrews and also stuff I haven't tried. The guy there knew Berkshire, but apparently they don't send beer out of Massachusettes, so I won't find it anywhere anyway.

Also, just so everyone knows, right near Hellsgate Social, there is a more beer distributor type place on 21st around 28th Ave, south of the 99 cent store. They have a really respectable selection and sell almost everything in twelves or cases for really great prices. All their stuff is really well-priced and probably the most bang for your buck that I've found as far as beer in New York. They have lots of imports, but not so much in the microbreweries, though they do have a couple of staples (like Magic Hat mixers, but I'm not that crazy about Magic Hat).

Post by: DJArturo on April 01, 2007, 05:36:08 PM
Quote from: eblack on February 24, 2007, 09:58:25 AM
Has anyone brewed their own beer?  How did it go?  I've heard the actual brewing phase is smelly (hence my wife doesn't want me to do it).  Is this true?

This is sort of true--to an extent. The smell will vary depending on the type of beer you are making since the ingredients are different with each style: one variety of hops vs. another; one variety of yeast etc... Also, the length of fermentation (one week or two, for example) will determine how long you will be living with a beer-making odor. I would say that a traditional pale ale using an American hops, such as Cascade, will fill your house with a floral, somewhat malty, bouquet on your cooking day. Then, for about a week during fermentation you'll get a slightly yeasty odor similar to rising dough, but still with hints of the hops and malt. This scent is stronger for the first few days because that's when the yeast is going nuts. It calms down after that until the end of your fermentation cycle. Once you bottle the beer it's a moot point. Mind you, it will not smell like your local pub. That's rancid beer that's spilled and gone bad (not to mention the other smells that might be mixed in). Describe it more like cooking or even baking and it might be easier to sell to your wife. I used to brew all the time in a tiny, one-bedroom apartment in Manhattan. It was fine. Cheers!
