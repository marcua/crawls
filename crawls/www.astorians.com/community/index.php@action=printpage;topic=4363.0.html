Post by: merm on December 10, 2006, 02:33:26 PM
I have a couple suggestions for dealing with stuff like this. MCI did this to me a couple times and it's becoming more and more popular as corporations realize that many consumers don't even know their rights.

First - Don't ever call. It doesn't really work and just gets you mad. If you want to call just do it for fun, but don't expect to get anything done that way. You are talking to a collector - they are not interested in "correcting" the error, they are interested in "collecting" the money whether you owe it or not. They get paid based on how much they collect, so they are not interested in solving the problem.

Second - Write them a letter (everything should be in writing) explaining that you dispute the full amount and that they are attempting to bill you for services not rendered. Request that all documentation related to the account be sent to you for your review. They are required by law (see below) to do so before they report you to any CRA (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credit_Rating_Agency).

Third - Include in the letter that you are giving them notice to cease telephonic communication (though they haven't started, this makes sure that they communicate to you via letter and don't start calling you). If they call you after you've sent this to them make sure to log the date and time they called. Also, if they call make sure you get the name and telephone number of the person you are speaking to.

Lastly - Sign and date the letter and send it via Certified Mail with return receipt. That way when they write you again claiming you are ignoring them you can give them the Certified Mail number and include a copy of their signed return receipt (which the post office sends back to you.)

When they follow up with you with another threatening letter let them know that you have already disputed the amount in full and that they haven't rendered any services to you. Also let them know that violations of the FCBA (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fair_Credit_Billing_Act) and FCRA (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fair_Credit_Billing_Act) will be dealt with accordingly.

This stuff happens all the time, it's really no big deal. It's not like they have anything personal against you - it's frustrating, but just the reality of corporate America sadly. I hope this helps.

AOL settles over consumer complaints
BY MONICA HATCHER
mhatcher@MiamiHerald.com

Florida subscribers to AOL who have had problems with billing and membership charges could be entitled to refunds, credits or other restitution following a settlement between AOL and the state attorney general's office.

Problems at AOL made national headlines after one customer, Vincent Ferrari, recorded an infuriating exchange and posted it online. All Ferrari wanted to do was cancel his AOL account. But it took him 20 minutes and a heated row with a customer service representative to accomplish his mission.

The attorney general's office began investigating the Internet provider last year after getting more than 1,000 consumer complaints about cancellation requests being ignored, erroneous charges and unauthorized reactivations of accounts.

AOL helped in the investigation, the attorney general said, by identifying consumers who were harmed by the practices.

All Florida consumers who filed a complaint with the company are eligible to take part in the settlement. The attorney general's office also encourages customers who have had problems with the company to file a claim with their office by visiting www.myfloridalegal.com (http://myfloridalegal.com/newsrel.nsf/newsreleases/7784A3DA8241EDEB8525724200511B75).
