Undercover Police Charge 13 With Lewdness on Subways

By JENNIFER 8. LEE
Thirteen people were arrested on charges of exposing themselves or groping during a two-day undercover crackdown on sexual misconduct in the subway last month, police officials announced yesterday.

The campaign, called Operation Exposure, dispersed undercover officers throughout the subway system, the police said yesterday at a Metropolitan Transportation Authority hearing.

In total, the officers made 88 arrests, including 48 for fare-beating, from 6 a.m. on May 23 to 8 p.m. on May 24, police officials confirmed afterward.

In some cases, men were accused of touching or rubbing their bodies against undercover police officers. The men charged with lewdness ranged in age from 21 to 58, police officials said.

"It was part of ongoing efforts by the N.Y.P.D. to make the subways as safe and hospitable as possible," said Paul J. Browne, the department's spokesman. "We hope there will also be a deterrent value for potential perpetrators who recognize their victim may be a police officer."

While public lewdness on the subway has been a problem for years, the police campaign came in the wake of increasing publicity in recent months over sexual misconduct, especially in Manhattan. In a number of cases, women have used cellphone cameras to take pictures of people they say have harassed them and posted them on the Internet. The site www.hollabacknyc.blogspot.com takes submissions via e-mail.

During yesterday's evening rush, female subway riders said cases of indecent exposure on the trains were commonplace, describing their own experiences when men either touched themselves or women.

"It happens a lot but women don't tell anyone," said Sylvia Cortes, 20, a nursing student at Mercy College.

She said one man who flashed her on the No. 4 train recently also flashed her 8-year-old neighbor.

"It's about time they did something about it because there's a lot of perverts around," Ms. Cortes said.

The crowded upper platforms of the West Fourth Street stations were particularly common for harassment, said Carline Virot, 39, a manager at J. P. Morgan.

"You'd be surprised how many of these guys there are out there," she said. "It makes you sick to your stomach."

Ms. Virot, who regularly rides the No. 6 train, said she once sat across from a man who was touching himself.

Jeanine Cruz, 27, a medical assistant who rides the subway two hours on most days, was confused recently when she saw a man on the opposite platform "making a weird motion."

"I was really disgusted when I realized what he was doing," she said. "This is a big problem."

Al Baker, Kareem Fahim and Kate Hammer contributed reporting for this article.
