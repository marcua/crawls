You're right, and I think that as we get closer to the deadline and the many millions of people who do receive television the old-fashioned way and are still unaware of the change find out what's going on, it's going to become a huge issue. 

Personally, I think the real issue behind this is not "improved picture" or any of the other supposed benefits of DTV (which, by the way, is not HDTV in most cases, with a few exceptions, like CBS2...because most stations split up their frequency to broadcast several sub-channels which reduces the amount of bandwidth available to each one)...but the fact that once the switchover is complete, the lower VHF frequencies as well as the UHF frequencies above 51 will be decommissioned entirely from television usage, and will be auctioned off, which is sure to bring in billions of dollars to government coffers.  Telecommunications providers, companies like Google, etc. are already clamoring for a piece of that action, to expand cell phone networks, set up nationwide wi-fi, etc.

Wilmington, North Carolina, actually became the first television market in the country to go "all-digital" as of a few days ago.  The analog signals are all broadcasting text informing viewers that programming will only be available via DTV, and will continue doing so for another month before going off the air completely.  This is supposed to be a "test" case leading up to the full switchover in February.

It's also a sign of the times in many other parts of the world:  the EU countries have a directive of their own to fully switch to digital television broadcasting by 2012, and some countries are already well on their way towards having phased out analog completely.

Interestingly enough, our neighbors to the north and south will probably be sticking with analog for a little while longer.  Even here in the states, analog TV itself won't die completely for a few years.  Low-powered TV stations are not covered by the February 2009 switchover, and there is no switchover date set for them yet, though it is more or less inevitable that they will make the switch within a few years.  Locally, there's a few LPTV's that will continue broadcasting as a result, such as Channel 6, which actually operates as a radio station, Pulse 87, broadcasting from "the top of that big blue building in Queens" (the Citicorp Building in LIC).  If you're wondering how this is possible: in the NTSC television system used in North and South America, Channel 6's audio always comes out on 87.7 on the FM dial.

Of course there's no NY1 or TBS or Food Network, as those are not over the air stations.  The converter boxes are only for over the air stations that will be shutting off their analog signal in February (if not earlier) and broadcasting only in digital after that.

One bonus, if you will, of DTV is that some stations add subchannels, which is how you get 3 channel 4's, for instance.  Some of them are kind of neat, others are a total waste of space.  For instance, 4-2 is NBC Weather Plus (something like the Weather Channel) and 4-4 is Universal Sports (shows track meets, swimming and other sports that don't usually get aired on commercial TV).  5-2 is just a rebroadcast of my9 (and 9-2 is just a rebroadcast of Fox 5), as News Corp. owns both of them.  7-2 is WABC+, which seems to just show repeats of Eyewitness News and infomercials, and 7-3 is WABC Now, with news and weather throughout the day.  11-2 is LATV, a Latino music video channel.  13-2 is Kids Thirteen and 13-3 is a Spanish-language version of Thirteen.  25-2 shows terrible quality feeds from cameras at major roads and intersections throughout the city.  31 has some subchannels, including qubo (all children's programming), and you may be able to get NJN, which has three subchannels with various PBS programming.

Quote
Best Buy's return policy is a full refund until March.  I'm putting the box away for safe keeping until I lose all signals, then I'll hook it up again.   To be honest, it's a big pain in the ass to have all these wires all over the place, and yet another something to plug in, and yet another damn remote.

Definitely a pain in the ass, but, one positive with the Insignia tuner is that you can "program" the remote control to also turn your TV on and off, which would then make your TV remote redundant.  However, this feature seems to have spotty success...it doesn't seem to work on some TV's or stops working after a while.

Quote
And it doesn't work well with VCRs at all.  I read that you actually have to turn the box to the channel you want to record, even if it's pre-programmed in your VCR.  So that means if I want to tape Lost every week, and have my VCR set to tape it automatically, I will have to remember to keep the channel on ABC, negating the whole purpose of pre-programming!   :-P

Of course.  That's because the converter box, much like a cable or satellite box, uses Channel 3 or the "line in" channel on your TV.  So the VCR has to be tuned to either the line in or channel 3, and the converter box has to be tuned in to the channel you want to record.  A big pain in the ass if you, say, want to record on two different channels and won't be around to change from one to the other.
