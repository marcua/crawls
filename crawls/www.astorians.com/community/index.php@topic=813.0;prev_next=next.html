You'll have to define overdraft protection.  Most banks have reinterpreted overdraft protection as your bills getting paid regardless of what money is in your account (up to a prespecified limit; $1,000 for example).

What you may want instead is where the bank pulls funds from another account like a savings account to use the money to cover checks.  These are much harder to find because they provide a real service that is of value.  This also cuts into the banks fees.

Banks typically charge in the neighborhood of $30 to cover a bounced check with overdraft protection.

I digress as I usually do when it comes to finance....

Long story short, check out savings banks and credit unions.  You usually get the best deal.

I've used Washington Mutual since I've been in NYC mainly for the free ATM and as a place to handle checks people might give me that I need to cash quickly.  My main banking is done through ING direct which has the best savings rate I can find and takes my work check through direct deposit.  All my checking is done through my trusty credit union in Texas which still has the best perks I've ever been able to find.

Well... if you are spending money you don't have in your account, you'll still have fees.

I have an overdraft protection that pulls from savings.  It's something like $10 per $100 where it pulls $100 at a time to cover any mess ups.  It's still not ideal but it's MY money I'm overdrafting with so it's not catastrophic.

If you have bank over draft protection and you overdraw, it's effectively a high interest rate loan you've just tapped into.  There are fees associated with it (the overdraft) but your checks won't bounch (no $35 bank fee + $25 -- or more -- returned check fee based on the institution you've written the bad check to).  In the end, if you overdraft you owe back what you've overdrawn, plus interest, plus fees.  Better than bouncing but still not great.

Also you need to have your account with your current bank in good standing before you try to open a new account somewhere else.  There's a check system in place, not quite like a credit application but similar so you want to have everything all in the clear.

I justed opened a washington mutual account -- which I like -- for the locale and lack of ATM fees.

This is turning into banking 101.  I kinda like it.  Maybe I'll learn something new too!

Credit unions are bands of people who pool their finances under a nonprofit charter.  Big banks don't like them because they steal customers away and offer better fees and services at cheaper prices.

You generally get cheaper loans and while it isn't necessarily easier to get the loan they are willing to work with you and if your credit is less than stellar, they won't jack up the rates as badly as commercial institutions.

I get free checks with a minimum balance which is easy enough to dodge by ordering more checks than you need and then let the balance slip.  I'm still using checks I ordered 6 years ago.  Who writes checks anymore?

My overdraft takes money from my savings account and pays the shortfall.  I think it's moved in $100 increments.  There are no fees associated with this and no interest paid by me.  It's simply an in house transfer of money from my savings to my checking.  I keep the minimum amount in the savings account just in case I overdraw.

My first home purchase was through the credit union and they were great and the rates were nice and there were no fees for early payment which lots of lenders like to tack on.

New York banking laws are a bit archaic and that likely has a lot to do with them not being able to connect up with the CA account mentioned before.  Won't go into it here.  Research it for yourselves.
