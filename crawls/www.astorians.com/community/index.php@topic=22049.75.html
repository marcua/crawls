I have been reading this thread with fascination for the past month or so.
I want to say that clearly people with cars and people without cars all have valid reasons for their choice. It is ok to own a car and ok not to own a car. However I don't think that just because you own a car you should be expecting to find easy and cheap parking anywhere you want and just because you don't own a car you should not be expecting that your car owning friends will always want to help you drive around your huge purchases.


For the record, we own 1 car. Prior to moving here from the Midwest we owned 2 cars. Had we not, it would have been impossible for 2 people to work in 2 different places! We sold 1 before we moved because we knew that it would be impossible to deal with 2 cars in Manhattan, which is where we first landed. Honestly dealing with one was hard enough even though we lived in an easy street parking area at the top of Riverside Park near 125th Street. After 6 months of moving the car around but not really using it much, we rented parking in a scuzzy lot hidden among a bunch of abandoned buildings in Harlem. It was cheap and we knew we were moving within 6 months anyway, so it was ok.
When we moved to Astoria we lucked out big time. Not only did we get a freshly remodeled 2 bedroom plus den for $600 less per month than our cave in Manhattan, the landlord threw in a parking spot behind the building for free. If it were not for the parking spot, I am not sure if we would have kept the car or if we would have tried to rent a space somewhere. In any case it makes life very convenient for us. I use it to do a big grocery shopping trip to the Stop and Shop on a weekly basis and I use it to drive my child to piano class twice per week. The Stop and Shop trip would be impossible without the car. The piano trip is doable on the subway but takes 2-3 times as long as driving. We also use the car for infrequent Costco trips and jaunts out to Long Island.
Is it an absolute necessity in this neighborhood? Probably not but since we are 2 full-time working adults with a child it surely makes life less stressful because we can get where we are going much quicker in most cases.
We still use the Subway during the week for work and occasionally on the weekends if we are going to Manhattan and certain parts of  Brooklyn because I don't mind spending more time getting somewhere if it ends up being less stressful...I hate driving anywhere in Manhattan lower than 59th street and I hate trying to park anywhere in Manhattan lower than 125th street...it just isn't worth the time spent finding an open spot. Certain parts of Brooklyn are hard for parking as well, such as near Prospect Park while others like Greenpoint seem easier.


FWIW I have lived in Chicago and Oakland and had access to a car in both places. For me it was a matter of what was more convenient for getting around. I biked to work in Chicago except in the  dead of winter and took the train in bad weather. The car was mainly there for day trips and shopping. In Oakland it was the same. *shrug*

« Reply #87 on: January 06, 2012, 11:07:42 PM »
I grew up most of my life in Astoria, without a car. I'm away at college now and my parents still don't have a car. I love walking around and on some occasions I enjoy taking the subway. Plus, being in DC, public transportation in New York doesn't seem quite as terrible by comparison, because even though the service is atrocious, you can actually get anywhere you want to go comfortably. I don't have a desire to go to places like Home Depot or Costco, and nowhere in the tri-state area outside NYC interests me enough that I would pay the tolls to get there.

That said, a lot of this debate is not making much sense. People are basically debating only using a car versus only using public transportation, and that's a pretty silly way to view the situation. I don't have a car right now, but if I could afford it, would I get one? HELL YES. That doesn't mean I wouldn't walk, or stop taking the subway full-stop. It's fun to walk around, and the subway is a great place for people-watching and reading. But it really sucks, especially being female, to constantly have to take taxis at night, for instance. The movie theatre in Astoria is far away and unless I want to go in the middle of the day, I have to take a cab because there is no way I'm walking anywhere in that sketchy area at night. There are a lot of things I would like to do when I'm back in NYC but I don't do because I don't have a car. I don't shop at Trader Joe's even though I really like it. I don't go to the beach. When it rains or it's freezing out, all my plans for the day are cancelled.

Even if you live right next to the subway, you have to admit that taking the subway is oftentimes a really frustrating experience. Every time it rains or snows, it's like a natural disaster has occurred and the entire system shuts down. During the summer it is TORTURE to be waiting in that sauna for a train. And it is faster to walk most of the time than to take a bus which stops every 2 blocks and at every light. Even taxis are frustrating - anyone who goes out in Manhattan knows that for cabbies, anything outside Manhattan does not exist. Half the time they will get lost in Astoria and circle around forever. Sometimes they'll refuse to take you.

Bottom line for me, no, having a car is not necessary, I've survived without one so far. That said, it is still way preferable to have the option available, and I will buy one when I can. A car does not need to be a huge expense if you get a second-hand one and use it only for occasions like I've mentioned. And public transportation, at over $100 a month per person, plus cabs and the cost of getting out of the city - that's not exactly cheap either.
