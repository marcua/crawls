Talk about clearing the air.

Anyway, a lot of folks on this board have asked for more specifics from the candidates. So here are two policy statements Jeremiah has made which weren't picked up by the press, so you probably didn't know about.

Sensible Zoning:

"The Zoning Proposal coming before City Council today is the result of eight years of hard work carried out by the people of our communities. I've been proud to stand with them and stand up to over-development that distorts the character of our neighborhoods and puts unsustainable stress on our infrastructure. Over a year ago, as a member of the Long Island City Alliance, I called for rezoning to protect and preserve the unique character of our neighborhoods and the quality of life in our communities. I am very proud to see this process finally come to a vote in our City Council.

But this is only the beginning. More needs to be done to ensure that our subways, our electrical grid, our schools, and our hospitals can handle our growing population. Many times, I've joined my neighbors to rally and speak out against over-development. Recently, I have helped gather signatures to prevent tenement-style construction near Athens Square Park that would over-stress our resources, contribute to over-crowding, and reduce the quality of life in our area. Time and time again, we've shown that when people come together as a community, we can enact real change. And that is what we need to bring to Albany now. In the Assembly, I will continue to stand with the people of our neighborhoods to bring change to Albany and fight to keep over-development from disfiguring and overwhelming our communities."

Living Wages:

"I strongly support the Fair Wages for New Yorkers Act. It is unconscionable that any working people live in poverty in a city that has more billionaires than any other place on earth. The working families of New York deserve better. This bill is an important first step to securing the foundation of our economy: the hard-working men and women of our communities.

When I'm elected to our Assembly, I pledge to introduce a similar bill to protect the middle and working class people of our State. By guaranteeing them the fair wage they deserve, we will provide much-needed security to the people who drive our economy and we will invest in sustainable, bottom-up growth that will strengthen and rebuild our State."

After reviewing the positions and qualifications of both candidates, I am pro-Simotas. I am not a troll, don't work for her campaign, and have engaged in discussions on this board that have nothing to do with politics.

Simotas and Frei-Pearson have very similar positions on the issues that are important to me. Where they differ is their background. Aravella is a lifelong Astoria resident with strong ties to the community. As a lifelong Astoria resident myself, I respect that and believe that she can appreciate both the new, younger generation moving into Astoria as a less expensive alternative to Manhattan and Brooklyn, as well as the old Astoria residents who laid down roots here decades ago.

I firmly believe it's important for both Astorias to be represented -- the old and the new -- and think that Aravella can do that. While I'm sure JFP would do just as good of a job if elected, I think Aravella would be more responsive to the needs of the community based upon her strong ties and roots here.

While I do believe that JFP has laid down roots in this neighborhood and I greatly appreciate what he has done in the time he's been here, I just don't believe he has the same insight into the community that she does.

In reality, endorsements don't typically influence my vote, but in this situation, people who I respect have thrown their support her way and I can appreciate that. Had they endorsed JFP, I would have considered the reasons why as well.

The "change" and "more of the same" argument may have worked for Barack Obama's campaign in 2008, but it's really not applicable here. Mike Gianaris did a great job as Assemblyman and quite frankly, if we can expect "more of the same" of that from Aravella, I'd be perfectly happy. The status quo in Albany can be blamed on senators like Ruben Diaz and Pedro Espada and assembly members like Sheldon Silver. Onorato represented this neighborhood for so long, he became completely out of touch with it. Fortunately Onorato will be replaced by Gianaris, so at least one creaky cog in the wheel will be gone. I suspect that Aravella has been "hand picked" because she's capable, intelligent, respected, and will do an excellent job representing this community.

And fwiw, check out her Facebook page and you'll see where she's been. I haven't seen JFP anywhere, does that mean he hasn't been out there? No. I don't profess to be everywhere at all times, I can't imagine you are either.
