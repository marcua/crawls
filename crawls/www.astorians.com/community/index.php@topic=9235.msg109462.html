Astoria and LIC is going through major changes and being here since 1991 I have liked many of the changes.
The only problem i see is that Steinway st. has not been part of it. I know that in the last 6 years Steinway has gotton more then its share of national chain stores but that is not enough. If you look at other neighborhoods in Brooklyn or forest hills the main shopping street is intergrated into the growth of the neighborhood.
Here in Astoria Steinway doesn't have any shops that appeal to the new people who moved here. If i have to guess I would say 24 to 30 single making over 50K a year. That customer spends alot of money on restaurants here because they have taken the next step and opened new and exciting places to eat that rival manhattan.
Now that there are more stores then I ever saw for rent on steinway and 4 are closing down by next month I think the change will come on the retail side of the new Astoria. The rents are still very high but they will start going down in the next 12 months when there are more vacancies and then you will have a new kind of retail in the street.

I think the OP brings up many good points.  Astoria has gone through many changes in the past 10-15 years, many of them for the better (in my opinion anyway).  However, it seems to me that Steinway has regressed during that same period.  Sure, some large chain stores have come in, but the overall quality of what you'll find on Steinway has gone downhill.  I remember a time when Steinway still had true mom-and-pop stores which sold quality clothing, shoes, etc., nice little boutiques and so forth with products that didn't look like they fell off the back of a truck.  Now, most stores really just seem to be really tacky, whether they are name-brand chains, or independent. 

It's also noteworthy to see how many vacant stores there are on Steinway Street, and also, after a decade-plus of extraordinary growth and change for Astoria, how it's suddenly far easier to find, say, a parking space on Steinway Street.  Honestly, the whole street also seems much less busy and crowded than it used to be even 10 years ago, and the quality of its establishments surely has something to do with it.

I grew up in Astoria and still live here, and frankly, with the addition of a few chain stores here and there (i.e., the Gap, Gamestop, Bath & Body Works, Express, Zales), there hasn't been much in the way of improvement -- if anything, even with the addition of these "type" of people, it's gotten crappier and crappier. Growing up we had two toy stores (Valco and Playworld [I think that's what it was called]), there was a Lerner's shop on the corner of Steinway and 31st Avenue that had adults and kids clothing (I believe that company ultimately turned into NY & Co.), there was the Wiz for electronics (and they had a great music selection downstairs), there were two independent record stores, LTR for jeans, and Yogi Lala was the head shop it was meant to be (I remember buying a black Jim Morrison jersey with the white sleeves and thinking how freakin' cool I was). The crappy stores that people think are a blight on the street have only cropped up in the last 10 years or so -- these stores aren't really the "stuff that was here before."

If the stores want to succeed in this neighborhood, they will have to adapt to the changes in the neighborhood's population. A bookstore would be a great start and the PC Richard location would be a great place for a Trader Joe's or Whole Foods. You mention the "the 24-30 50k+ people" that have moved in, but not all of those people have just moved into the neighborhood -- some of us have been here for a long time and would love a decent place to shop again and Steinway, unfortunately, ain't it.
