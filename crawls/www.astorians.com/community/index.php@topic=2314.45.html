Quote from: Jonathan on June 13, 2006, 02:30:54 PM
JD, why is pastry a 2 or 3 day affair?

Are you sure you want to know?  Ok, here goes . . . (Strap in folks; it's gonna be bumpy ride!)

Mostly just the laminated doughs are time-consuming (like puff pastry, croissant dough, or Danish dough).  Laminated doughs are tricky because you're not just mixing butter into the dough, you're actually placing it on top of your dough and folding it like a package, then rolling it out and folding 4 more times.  The butter in between the microscopically thin layers of dough then melts during baking, and the steam rises to create small pockets of air, hence the flakiness.  It can definitely be done in one day, but temperature is such an important part of it.  If it's too hot (as our kitchen tends to get), the butter that you've so carefully rolled in starts to melt and leak out the sides of and into the dough and then you don't get the proper flakiness (it ends up having a more brioche-like texture). 

Since Danish and croissant dough include yeast, you need to let the dough retard in the refrigerator at least overnight to get good fermentation (which means flavor).  Plus, Danish dough is particularly sticky (lots of eggs and some sugar), so it should stay in the fridge at least overnight to get a good chill on it which makes it infinitely easier to work with. 

Then, once you start rolling in the butter, the dough has to rest 15 minutes to half an hour (longer if it's hotter, or if you've taken a long time to roll the dough) in between turns, and properly lamintated Danish dough will require 4 separate turns.  Then, once you've got all your turns into the dough, it should rest in the fridge another hour or so to make it easy to cut and shape, and so that it won't shrink so much as you work with it. 

After that, you have to fill the Danish shapes, egg wash them, proof them (which can take 20 to 40 minutes depending on heat and humidity of your kitchen - for those of us without a proof box).  Then they get baked in batches since I don't have a commercial sized oven with commercial sized sheet pans.  Once they come out, each piece gets brushed with clear glaze.  Whew!!! 

People take Danish for granted, but it's very time consuming and must be made with love and care.     :-D

Sorry, this is all very long-winded and Alton Brown-ian, but once I got started, there was no stopping till I fully extolled the art that is Danish making.   8-)
