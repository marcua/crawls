I don't think it's owned by the Czech government.  The beer garden is owned by a benevolent society, and I think that is the ground for their being able to discriminate in hiring practices.  In New York City, private clubs are covered for discriminatory purposes under New York City Local Law 63, which was enacted in 1984.  It's part of the NYC Administrative Code - Human Rights Law.  Generally speaking, private clubs with fewer than 400 members, benevolent societies and religious organisations are exempt from the anti-discriminatory provisions of the NYC Human Rights Law.

According to their website, it is owned by the Bohemian Citizens' Benevolent Society.  To belong to the Society, you must be of Czech or Slovakian descent.  As it is a benevolent society, it would fall within the exception to the NYC Human Rights Law as mentioned above.

The New York Secretary of State corporations website lists the BOHEMIAN CITIZENS BENEVOLENT SOCIETY OF ASTORIA L.I.N.Y. as a current domestic not-for-profit corporation, with an original filing date of March 10, 1902.

The Czech Republic owns a building in Manhattan called the Bohemian National Hall, at 321-325 E. 73rd St.  They purchased it in 2001 from another Bohemian organisation called the Bohemian Benevolent and Literary Organisation.  Information here and here.
