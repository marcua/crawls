bstewart, you may enjoy this New York Times article about this sort of thing: http://www.nytimes.com/2009/08/09/business/09privacy.html?pagewanted=1&_r=2

Particularly this part from the second page:
Quote
SOME people think that the stimulus law doesn’t go far enough to protect patients’ privacy. While it bans paying a pharmacist for marketing to patients, it does not bar the sale of personal drug information by one pharmacy to another, as happened to Randee Lonergan, 35, a school administrator who now lives in Florida.

She says that when a pharmacy closed in a Stop & Shop supermarket on Long Island, it sold her information to a nearby Target store. She was upset when her new pharmacist asked if she was still taking injections for a skin problem. “They knew all about me and my family,” she said. Adding to her chagrin, she saw a person she happened to know working at the pharmacy. A Target spokeswoman says the company complied with all privacy laws.

True, in my cases there is a fine line between selling people's personal information. I think however, in this case it was a smarter step. Like essen said, most people see it as being pretty convenient.

In an ideal world everyone would be able to choose where they would want their prescriptions transferred to, but the transfer of one medication alone could take 10minutes, and its also by New York state law, that once you do a transfer you lose the remaining refills, if you had any. Of course with places like Rite Aid and CVS there is a central search so refills aren't lost.

As for the lady complaining about the Target pharmacy knowing her history, I believe she's got it all wrong. First of all; a pharmacist's knowing your medical history is very assuring. He/she knows what you've been through, what is and is not good for you,how to give recommendations, and how to make sure your health and life is kept in capable hands. Secondly there are privacy laws so what is said between the patient and pharmacy staff, is purely confidential. Trust me, we rather keep our mouths shut than be sued.

Back to Rite Aid, dear lord was it a mess. I'm surprised it lasted like that as long as it did. And if ONLY they had decided that having 23123 stores in Astoria was much, lol. They had actually lost their lease. Just could not pay for it. Bye bye.
