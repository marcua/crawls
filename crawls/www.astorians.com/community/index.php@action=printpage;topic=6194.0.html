Title: Re: Chicken Feed Tainted
Post by: Harlan on May 03, 2007, 03:26:51 PM
Quote from: Frappe on May 03, 2007, 02:59:08 PM
This might seem is naive, but what exactly qualifies a veggie or meat to be organic?

There are USDA standards (http://www.ams.usda.gov/NOP/indexNet.htm) now. For veggies, briefly, you can't use chemical fertilizers, pesticides, or herbicides. "Natural" fertilizers (manure) and pesticides (Bt) are fine. Meat is complicated, but I think it involves feeding mostly organic feed, and no prophylactic antibiotics or hormones. Note that certification is expensive, so most labeled organic is from medium-sized producers and larger. Small producers, such as those at farmers markets, often grow organically but are not "Certified Organic."

Quote
Does organic farming generally involve an increase or decrease in costs as well as production per acre?

Usually, but not always, organic farming costs more and has lower yields. Nitrogen fertilizer made from oil is really, really cheap. (Thank you Green Revolution.)

Quote
And what makes organic products healthier than non-organic.

Usually nothing, except that some of the pesticides and herbicides have residues that may be harmful if ingested regularly. Oh, and organic produce has more bugs, which are high in protein. Personally, I think most of the benefits of organic are environmental more than medical, especially if you're careful to wash produce before eating it.

And that's not even always the case. For example, with row crops like corn, you have to till the soil regularly to keep weeds down, resulting in more runoff of topsoil into rivers. That's not sustainable. With non-organic production, you can dump broad-spectrum herbicides that degrade harmlessly in 24 hours onto the soil, grow GMO corn that's resistant to the herbicide, and use no-till methods that don't waste topsoil. I think it's a toss-up which one is worse.

Oh, and note that you literally can't grow organic apples in the Northeast. The bugs here are so nasty that you have to use insecticides if you want more than two apples per tree.
