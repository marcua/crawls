Sure, (and you are going to love this)

I was in the building about 15 years ago when the furniture company owned it and hand a chance to inspect it closely.

The building is built in three parts. The southernmost section was built over the former entrance to Sunswick Creek and has settled about six inches. Massive wooden beams and posts (perhaps locust) are seriously cracked and held together by metal braces. The pressure to split them must have been enormous.

In several sections the building had settled so much the floor actually sloped. The owners at that time informed me the building is still settling.

I will not even speculate the impact that breathing in decades of lacquer and varnish and all the other nasty things from industry that soaked into the walls and floor would have on tenants, particularly children.

Hope they have good insulation. You are right on the waterfront.

Hey, good investment. Go ahead sink a ton of money into it.

Not.

The only sad thing for our community (investing in that building is your problem not ours) is they unfortunately named it "The Piano Factory" ensuring that this money making scheme will be confused with one of the remaining islands of quality in our community: Steinway Pianos.

At least it did not end up looking like the visual nightmares of Sterns or Eagle that Pistilli mutilated.

I am waiting to hear details on the pier he wants to building in Hallet 's Cove. As usual, everyone seems to know about it except the community.

But hey, he is a developer, so recieved wisdom makes him a community hero, but those of us with longer memories recall the time he tried to seize Socrates Park.

Finally, have fun walking to the subway (I think the Lexington Line might be closer but that is in Manhattan - maybe you can take a kayak over there) and driving your car along Vernon. Once the development of a several tens of thousands of apartments goes on that street it will become the longest parking lot in Queens.
