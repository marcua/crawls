Good or bad idea, addressing it from this level?

Article.

Excerpt:

Quote
Europeans OK anti-obesity charter

European health ministers from 53 countries approved the world's first charter to fight obesity on Thursday, vowing greater action against the epidemic of expanding waistlines across the continent.

The charter, approved in Istanbul, Turkey, was drafted by the World Health Organization in consultation with its European member states. It is the first real attempt to compel national authorities to take concrete action to combat obesity.

"Lots of governments have good recommendations and nice guidelines, but in terms of nutritional goals, most countries haven't achieved them," said Dr. Francesco Branca, WHO's European adviser for nutrition and food security. The charter commits governments to things like improving the availability of healthy foods and adopting regulations for safer roads to promote cycling and walking.

The prevalence of obesity in Europe has tripled in the past two decades; half of all adults and 20 percent of all children are overweight. Being overweight or obese increases the likelihood of diseases such as diabetes and cancer, and shortens life expectancy. Obesity is also responsible for up to 6 percent of all health care costs across Europe.

The WHO charter aims to curb the epidemic in the next five years, and hopes to reverse the trend by 2015.

Good idea, I think. I don't think governments should be in the business of (say) banning trans fats from (yum!) biscuits and apple pie, but I do think health departments/ministries can do a lot to encourage access to healthier lifestyles. The NYC health and zoning boards should be helping poorer areas of the city, where there are no decent groceries within walking distance, by zoning specifically for greengrocers and large grocery stores and giving appropriate incentives to business owners to locate in underserved areas. And the efforts to make walking and cycling easier are definitely a great idea. Streetsblog has been covering this second issue very well, I think...

This said, genes are genes, and I'll be astonished if the obesity trend in Europe can be reversed by 2015. Beating obesity is fighting an uphill battle against human nature.

I support the ban on trans fats. There's no reason anyone should be able to sell this stuff as a food product. These fats are poisin to the human body and have no known benefit (other than they are the cheapest "fat" you can use in food where people won't notice the taste).

I'd also like to point out that the government restricting the food industry from putting trans fats in your food is not an attack on your individual freedom. You can still put those things in your food and eat them if you wish. They aren't restricting your right to consume, they are restricting a corporation's right to produce and sell something as "food" that really isn't food at all. Just like you can't start a company that boxes used motor, label it as "cooking oil" and expect to get away with it; but, you could as an individual cook with it and eat it if you want.

Just my opinion :)

I think in theory a ban would be nice -- but our society doesn't really have a good track record on that type of thing.  And as far as not restricting the right to consume, only the right to use/produce/sell, well, isn't that what they did during prohibition?  It wasn't illegal to possess or consume alcohol privately, it was only illegal to produce and sell it.  We all know how successful that was.  It's certainly not a perfect analogy - after all, I don't see people making bathtub partially hydrogenated soybean oil - but I think it does have some validity.  There are also easier and more socially acceptable alternatives to trans fats than there was/is to alcohol.

Jtrane -- as far as the oils for street fairs and similar things, well, trans fats haven't been around for that long (assuming that the oils they use are in fact partially hydrogenated).  Assuming there is some sort of ban or restriction, I'd imagine they'll go back to using saturated fats, or whatever chemically altered oil the big conglomerates come up with next, until it's discovered that is also bad for us.  Personally, I'd rather consume naturally occurring saturated fats in my once a year zeppole than have something that again has been artificially produced. 

I'd also like to see as much outcry against high fructose corn syrup as we've seen against trans fats.  I think hfcs is an even bigger health problem than trans fats.
