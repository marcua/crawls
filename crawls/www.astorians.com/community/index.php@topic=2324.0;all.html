I lived in Murray-- nee, CURRY HILL for two years when I first moved to NYC and immediately undertook a methodic sampling of the neighborhood's varied Indian, Pakistani and Bangladeshi eateries.

Maybe its because I was a destitute grad student, but the best of all was Curry and Curry, a silly name for a hole-in-the-wall dive with amazing samosas, chaat, and COMBO MEALS!!. Who doesn't love theory of the Combination Platter?

They have a tv that blares Bollywood DVDs day and night. Its awesome.

Its not very nice for sit-down dining (did I mention that I was living on a graduate stipend of $24,000/yr?) but the food is authentic, plentiful, heartfelt, fresh, hot, spicy, and CHEAP!

My favorite was the Vegetarian Lunch Special, which was a platter with a mountain of fluffy basmati with a sampling of the 4 veggies of the day. These tended to be the comforting standbys: bindhi masala (chickpea heaven!), saag paneer (creamed spinach with homemade cheese), aloo gobi, and eggplant+okra stuff that I forget the name of.  Also, a spoon of daal and raita if you ask real nice.

Try the paneer paratha! The best in the city!

YUM!! Oh wait, did I mention that it was $3.75?!?!

Curry and Curry (they deliver too): 33rd St between 2nd and 3rd.

Quote from: goldfish boy on June 10, 2006, 01:50:37 PM
It's my (possibly completely incorrect) impression that what we know as "Indian" food is actually either food that was tailored to British tastes  by Indian cooks during colonization, or food actually cooked by British cooks using indigenous ingredients, and that it has little or nothing in common with actual Indian food.

I'm not an expert, but I think this is partially true. A lot of it was, I believe, a class distinction. Many of the dishes we think of as "Indian food" were the very expensive foods eaten by the rulers of India, be they the Mughals or the British. This is the same way that "French food", with demi-glace and other fancy sauces, is not hardly the food of a plumber from Lyon. Certainly, the typical poor Indian farmer did not eat a lot of expensive lamb stews, but instead ate a lot of daals and simple vegetable dishes. This said, I don't think much of the Indian food that we know and love was cooked for British tastes by British cooks, but instead was cooked for British tastes by Indian servant cooks.

There are a couple of books on my Amazon wish list about the history of Indian food, but, well, I haven't read them yet. :)
