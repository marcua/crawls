Quoting secondary and tertiary sources like these that editorialize is not a good way to obtain accurate information about any scientific research or data.  For instance, the first link is not a "scientific review about the risks associated with fluoride"; it's an anti-fluoride blog entry, and it completely misrepresents the purpose of the NRC report.  It also provides numbers in a vacuum.  For instance, do you have any idea what the concentration of fluoride actually is in NYC tap water? 

According to the link I posted upthread (the NYC DEP's annual drinking water quality report), fluoride is added to NYC water at a concentration of 1 mg/L, and has been since 1966.  Actual detectable levels in 2007 averaged .56 mg/L from the Catskill/Delaware system (1405 samples averaged); <.30 mg/L from the Croton system (94 samples); and .7 mg/L from the groundwater system (18 samples).  All of Queens (except for a very few areas in Southeastern Queens which are groundwater-based) obtains its water from the Catskill/Delaware system.  Thus, the conclusions of the national report, even interpreted in the most alarmist and inaccurate way as in the link, are nearly meaningless for NYC tap water.

The full report is available only for a fee, but here is the press release, and here [link is to a PDF] is a free synopsis from the NRC.  It notes that "The committee did not evaluate the risks or benefits of the lower fluoride concentrations (0.7 to 1.2 mg/L) used in water fluoridation. Therefore, the committee’s conclusions regarding the potential for adverse effects from fluoride at 2 to 4 mg/L in drinking water do not apply at the lower water fluoride levels commonly experienced by most U.S. citizens."

(Emphases mine)

Here, the CDC discusses the actual purpose of the NRC's report, and its response.  The CDC also states:

Quote
The NRC released its report, Fluoride in Drinking Water: A Scientific Review of EPA’s Standard, on March 22, 2006. The purpose of this report is to provide recommendations to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) on whether the current maximum contaminant level goal (MCLG) of 4 mg/L protects children and others from adverse health effects. The NRC committee did not examine the health risks or benefits of water at the levels recommended for fluoridation of water to prevent tooth decay. The current range for water fluoridation of community water systems is 0.7 to 1.2 ppm (equivalent to 0.7-1.2 mg/L). Water that has fluoride at these levels is safe and effective for preventing tooth decay.

(Emphasis mine.)

Here is Quackwatch's take on the health risks and/or benefits of fluoride.

In short, no one can stop you from trying to filter out fluoride from your water, but I certainly won't filter it out of mine.
