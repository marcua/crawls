I have been thinking, over the last two days, of an important element to this debate that seems to come up over and over again. And that is the comparison of the LGBT rights struggle to the African-American rights struggle.

First off, I want to say this. I have come to the realization that there are two key elements in my fight for marriage equality. They are the personal fight and the societal fight. Everyone who has been reading this thread is aware of the personal stake I have in this. Tonight, I want to address the societal stake in this.

A few weeks ago, when the Maine Senate held their hearing regarding marriage equality, I heard a comment from a gay teenager that struck home to me and put into words what I instinctively knew.

He said that (and I am paraphrasing) as long as Maine writes discrimination into their laws, they are giving permission for the citizens to discriminate against me.

This is how I see it and why I think it as parallel to the Black civil rights movement. Civil unions and domestic partner registries are second class systems. They seek to address something that I desire. But, in a way that is discriminatory. The laws create a parallel system that is not equal to what heterosexuals have. And by doing so they are allowing people to think that we are less than them. I remember about three or four years ago when 3 gay men were attacked in Astoria. These attacks occurred during the same month that Congress was considering a Constitutional amendment to make marriage only between a man and a woman. I recall that year as the year that Karl Rove et al decided that same sex marriage was the wedge issue that would win Bush a second term. I am not saying that it is an exact correlation, just that the trends in gay bashing rose during the period the Congress was debating.

Now, Why do I think this relates to the Black civil rights movement? Because ultimately, they were trying to erase the same type of laws that created a second class system that dehumanized them. The government back then gave permission to whites to abuse and even kill blacks. Every separate drinking fountain and separate bathroom and separate dining areas perpetuated the separate but equal myth that we now know is false. And perpetuated the dehumanizing treatment of Blacks.

As has been discussed previously, I am unaware of any claim of the LGBT rights movement being the same as the Black civil rights movement. It is my belief that there are differences. For example, I know that not a single gay person was denied the right to vote, as long as they were white. However, I am stunned that there are African-Americans that cannot see the relation between my struggle and what they struggled for.

As I said in a previous post, a member of the New York State Assembly said what I believe to be true; "I believe that Matthew Shepard was Lynched, they just didn't use a rope".

Ultimately, as long as society thinks that I am less than equal, they have permission to hate me, discriminate against me, and yes, even kill me.

We went to the rally this afternoon -- great turnout, the usual political cast of characters, celebs, performances, etc. The message -- the single most important message that they tried to get out -- was to contact our state senators. My family has been in Astoria since the 50's. My mom and uncle graduated from Bryant H.S. After my mom got married, she returned to Astoria and raised her family here. I was born here at the old Boulevard Hospital on 31st Avenue. I graduated from PS 17 and IS 126. I spent years as a girl scout in the basement of the Methodist Church on Crescent Street. I still live here, now with my partner. We've been engaged since 2006. My 93 year old grandmother still lives 4 blocks away. She adores my partner and is completely accepting of me and my life. So when people talk about what the native Astorians want vs. the newcomers, well, I *am* a native Astorian. My grandmother is a longtime Astorian. And we want marriage equality and we want it now.

My mom was an Onorato supporter and I vividly recall when he first ran for state senate. But that was 26 years ago. Things have changed around here, Onorato has not. What was once a constituency primarily of immigrants and blue collar workers is now a neighborhood of young professionals -- educated, and progressive. That he's so unaware of who he represents is bad enough, but that he's so uninterested in finding out is downright appalling. I'm sure he's not concerned about reelection -- the man is 80 years old. He's not voting against this because of what he believes his constituency wants, he's voting against this because it's what HE wants. That's not why we elected him. I'm sorry to say I voted for him. Like many Astorians, I voted on name recognition, not on the issues.

It would be a shame if Onorato's legacy of a committment to serving the people of this community is marred by a single vote on the wrong side of history.
