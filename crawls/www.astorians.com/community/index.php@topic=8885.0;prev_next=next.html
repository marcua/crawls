I'll agree with you.  If a fare hike came with a clear-cut promise of everything that you mentioned...more frequent service, cleaner trains and stations, less incidents and maintenance issues, newer trains, environmentally safe buses, and so on, then yes, I think more people would not mind a hike.  The problem is that this most likely won't be the case, and people know it.  People are expecting more of the same even after the hike, and as a result, are not too happy about that possibility. 

It really depresses me how shabby a state the NYC subway system is, especially after having been on some metro systems in Europe, some of which are just as old or older than ours, yet, the stations and trains are usually sparkling clean, the trains often run every 90 seconds during rush hour (and actually stick to the schedule), there's electronic signs in every station listing the upcoming trains and their expected arrival time (always seemingly accurate), and so on.  I'd be willing to support higher fares for such a level of service as well, but it's not happening.

As for subsidizing public transportation by tolls and other fees on private vehicles, I agree as well, if it comes as a package with specific expansions to the region's public transportation network.  Proposals such as the Mayor's congestion pricing plan were, in my opinion, totally misguided, as the plan had many flaws.  A large percentage of the cars traveling through Manhattan, for example, are only going from LI/Brooklyn/Queens to NJ.  Those cars would either pay the additional tolls but still clutter the city's streets, or they would take one of the many alternate routes, all of which are already insanely congested as it is and some of which pass through low-income areas with high asthma rates already exacerbated by the traffic and pollution...and just make things worse.  If the plan outlined some real, major expansions to rail and bus service, especially in the suburbs and towards areas where public transport currently is spotty at best, and was combined with a plan that would truly reduce congestion everywhere and not just Midtown and Lower Manhattan, because they are NOT the only parts of NYC suffering from traffic congestion and pollution, then I'll support that too.  The Mayor's plan really only served as a selective tax without many real plans to implement true public transport solutions that the city and region desperately needs, and does not offer enough of an incentive for many drivers to avoid high-congestion areas.
