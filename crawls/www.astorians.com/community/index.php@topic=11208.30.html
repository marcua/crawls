Thanks for the rousing discussion.
A few points. While a 40% drop seems enormous to some. You must realize that home prices this decade have risen 300%. There is no good financial footing to explain this. That's why it was a bubble. So a 40% drop is not uncalled for.
Thinking Manhattan prices are immune shows a bit of amnesia. In the crash of the early 90s prices in Manhattan dropped 30% -40%. So given the current nose bleed prices, and Wall Street in the worse mess it has been in since maybe the Great Depression. A big decline is highly likely. As for no space for new homes in Manhattan, there is plenty of space, it's just vertical instead of horizontal.
As for Astoria. Most of the posters acknowledged that prices here are so high that people who live here cannot afford them. Yet they seem to think there are always enough people to buy, no matter the price. (This is sometimes called the Greater Fool Theory).  When homes can stay on the market for over a year and inventory keeps rising, these are indicators of a housing market in collapse. Prices are slow to react, but in the end, they do.
Remember, "It's never different this time." and "It's never different here."

Ed – if you’re arguing that the prices will most likely fall then you get no argument from me but what’s your point really -- that a market collapse is inevitable? That  wouldn’t surprise anyone I think, or are you trying to say it’s a good thing or a bad thing that prices drop? Or that property is a crap investment in the first place? I’m not sure.

This is just my feeling I have no evidence to back up this theory is that what’s happening in Astoria is that more and more houses are being rented out instead of being owner-occupied because the rental market is so strong. And the apartments being built seem to be mostly condos, which usually have no restriction on renting for owners (that’s what disturbs some people, including me -- that the ratio of renters to owners is shifting -- and I’m a proud renter by the way).

You frame your argument around people being able to afford property to live in themselves but in Astoria this isn’t the case much of the time. Many people / companies are buying houses as an investment and as long as the rent-roll covers expenses, most investors will be satisfied even if prices collapse in the short-term because in the long-run prices will rise.

And that’s what frustrates me and others here: even if prices fall by 40%, the average Astorians still won’t be able to buy the apartment or house they are currently renting and not just because they’ve been frivolous and don’t save enough. Most simply don’t have the income to get a mortgage. In my case I work in a low-paying industry and I had to decide whether to save for a house/apartment or to save for retirement. I chose to save for retirement because I couldn’t do both and to me, it didn’t make sense to save for a house that I would never be able to afford.

I also like this discussion but there's no way I'm getting into an emotional debate of who feels secure or not in this economy or any other economy.  You may feel like your job is in jeopardy or that you may lose money or take a salary cut.  Understood.  But you need to understand that isn't everyone's reality.  There are a lot of people, whether you like it or not, who do very well in a tight economy.  So no - everyone is NOT losing money right now.  And the sky isn't falling for everyone.  So maybe you need to take some time to see why your situation is so volatile and try to take steps to protect yourself.  I hate this medium for discussion like this so I have to add that I mean that sincerely and not in a condescending way.  

And I do know Greek property owners in Astoria - many in fact - and as Greek nationals, they use the Euro.  And yes - condo buyers may be investors of all types.  Makes no difference.  The point is that whoever they are, they're buying up the inventory.  So prices will stay at certain levels as long as there are willing buyers.  Homes that stay on the market for long periods are usually resales/older homes that are priced crazy high by their owners.  New construction is a whole different market.  And a resale that is priced right - yes lower than a year ago - will sell.  And it's simply wrong make blanket statements about who can't possibly make a profit or find a buyer.  People are selling homes today and making profits all over Astoria and the public records are there for inspection.

And I totally agree with all the remarks about what the banks, realtors, lobbyist, the Fed Reserve, etc etc did to create the housing bubble.  But I'm sorry.  I know this sounds mean.  But for those of us who signed mortgage loans and read every single line carefully with our attorney, I must say - if you signed a loan and didn't plan ahead on what your costs would be, and what your projected financial picture would be - then you are to blame.  You alone.  Grow up and read things before you sign them.  And I don't believe the government or the taxpayers should bail these people out.  Then what next?  Buy back cars from people who foolishly bought a lemon?  Life isn't easy.  And anyone who's bought real estate in NYC knows it can be hellish from beginning to end.  But sometimes you do what you have to do.  Just do it carefully.

I'm not that old but I'm old enough to have lived through some of these ups and downs.  All I can say is that things do get better.  Maybe they get worse first - but they do get better.  And I think personal satisfaction is key to riding out the storms.  If buying something isn't in the cards right now, then why waste energy getting frustrated over it?  Especially dealing with things that are completely out of our control.  Why not do some goal setting and financial planning?  Then find a rental apartment that you love and enjoy it to the max.  Then, when the time comes, you will reach your goals, whatever they may be.  And if that takes 5 years or 15 years, so what?  You can have an awful lot of fun during that amount of time!  And maybe someone chose a profession that make certain things like home ownership very difficult in NYC.  So what?  If that life makes that person happy, then that's more important than having a certain house or apartment.
