Does your employer have a credit union? Did your college have a credit union open to alumni? It's almost always cheaper and more pleasant to go with a credit union if you can! I'm still happily a member (not victim!) of the University of Illinois Employees Credit Union. The only drawbacks about not having a local bank are that on those rare times when I need to deposit a check, I have to send it via mail which takes a few days (or use an ATM that accepts deposits, which is also very slow, and more expensive than a check), and I have to use out-of-system ATMs. Fortunately, McDonalds' ATMs are on the Co-op Network, meaning the machines don't charge me a fee, but I still have to pay $1 per withdrawal to the credit union. (This is the only reason I go to a McDonalds!) I've also had a good rate on an auto loan through my credit union, and I get a Visa card through them (although the APR's not the best).

Aside from that, I've heard relatively good things about Commerce.

Quote from: mcdirk on July 05, 2006, 06:01:13 PM
Anyone really like their bank?  Any deals better than others?  Any thoughts on Commerce, Washington Mutual or any of the small Astoria banks?

I'd start by making a list of my criteria. If you need someone to smile at you when you enter your bank, then go local. If you travel, and need to use ATMs a lot, go big.

I bank with Citibank. No, I am not happy, but I am loyal. They meet *all* my criteria. I opened my checking account with them when they were First National City Bank in 1960, when I was 16 years old and leaving for college. They have branches all over the world. Wherever I traveled to, they had a presence. That was very important to me. Converting currency or withdrawing cash whenever I needed to was a great influence in my staying with an impersonal financial institution. It has also had a few fringe benefits. I got terrific rates on car loans, consolidation loans and educational loans. I also have a 6.5% interest rate on a MasterCard which I hardly ever use, and their Preferred Money Market accounts now have a 5.50% APR.

My sister always changed banks to get better deals. So did my parents. Remember all those freebies? They supplied the whole neighborhood with toasters and glassware! Just don't pick a bank based on its proximity to your workplace—find one nearer home.

I'm very interested in finding out about this 5.5% Money Market fund at Citi that NYCMac is talking about.
Sorry, mrm, I misspoke. It's actually a 6 month CD at 5.50% APY. Still worth looking into, especially if you are in a position to let a chunk of money sit for a while without any risk in original principal. I generally do this with liquid assets as a forced savings. Should the need to withdraw early arise, I still have not lost anything and I may have earned more than if left sitting in a regular savings account at a measly 0.2%–.0.5%.

Most banks will offer special rates for large deposits. These are generally called jumbo accounts. They will also offer long-term customers special high(er) rate savings and/or Money Market accounts. I have banked online for at least 10 years. Citibank used to have proprietary dialup software before they moved to the internet. I transfer funds, pay bills, open accounts . . . heck I do it all. The *only* time I ever walk into a bank is to use the ATM. I hate lines. BTW, I am also a great fan of ING. I have them linked to my Citibank accounts so that I get a high(er) rate of day-to-day savings interest. This combination has worked very well for me.

Dirk, are you finding this thread as amazing as I am? You asked a pertinent little question and people are so willing to fess up to the reasons they use a bank. I always thought that money was such a personal force in our lives. Perhaps it was in mine. Now I am noticing that how I/we are treated takes on a larger aspect!
