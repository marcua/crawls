Greetings,

I'm kindof a techie I suppsoe.

1. It's too much hassle to try and recycle computers. If you can't find someone to give the computer to, I would throw it away. This is the nature of technology. There is no completely safe way that is accessible or financially reasonalbe to dispose of some computer componants.

2. I wouldn't pay $30 for a program to erase all my data. I'd just throw away the computer. If you really are paranoid about someone reading your loveletters then remove the hard drive from the computer case and smash it with a hammer a couple times. It would cost thousdands of dollars for someone to recover a hard drive in a smashed condition like that. Highly doubtful that anyone would be interested in such a drive.

3. The best bet is to find someone who can use the computer or its parts and give it to them. If you can keep a computer out of the garbage that's a good thing, but if you can't - then just remember that it's going to end up there anyway ultimately, even if you pay for disposal.

The best way to make sure your data can not be recovered is to "zero out" the disk. you can do this using standard Unix utilites such as dd and wipe. Here's an example of how you'd accomplish this task using dd:

#> dd if=/dev/zero of=/dev/hda bs=1024

Now that you know that, just grab a GNU/Linux floppy distro and have fun. Tomsrtboot (toms.net) would be a good start.

By the way, I'd strongly advise that you do NOT use Drive Scrubber since it is a binary-only, proprietary program. In other words, this program could be doing anything to your computer without your knowledge. For example, it could be uploading your files to some 3rd party while you think it's
"protecting" your computer. If you're really interested in security, you should be using software that is transparent and freely available for others to audit, redistribute, and enhance.
