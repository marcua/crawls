If the United Food and Commercial Workers Union has placed their confidence and support for Aravella Simotas, I guess that's good enough for me.  The good people representing the United Food and Commercial Workers Union has never let me down.  The old Local 1500 wouldn't steer us wrong!

As a unionist (UFT), I agree that the union endorsements mean a lot.  If they have endorsed her, they obviously see the same good candidate that some have spoken about here.  However, it does not necessarily mean she's the best candidate.

As an active union member, I can attest to the politics involved in endorsements.  I have been a part of the endorsement process (not necessarily for the UFT) where the membership votes one way, but the Executive Board decides to go another way.  It happens in almost every union and advocacy group.  It has less to do with who will do the better job and more to do with who did the better politicking.  In this case, I can imagine how strong the lobbying is on Simotas' side on the part of Gianaris and his colleagues.  It probably makes the endorsement process a virtually one-sided affair.

So, like I said, a union's endorsement does go a long way and has been able to help inform my decisions in the past.  But, I encourage everyone to look beyond the endorsements and learn more about the candidates themselves. 

I agree with zippycar85 that I'd love to hear both candidates in a forum or debate.  That way you can decide for yourselves and not based on the politics of endorsement. 

Perhaps we can get an Astorians.com Candidates Forum going?  :?  A forum by the people and for the people.

Everything that transpired in the ConEd situation is a matter of public record.

Jeremiah and Western Queens Power for the People took ConEdison to court through a "prudence" proceeding via the public service commission. The settlement is publicly available at http://www.powerforthepeople.info/63millionproposal.html

It was signed by a variety of parties, including the Staff of the Department of Public Service, the New York State Consumer Protection Board, Western Queens Power for the People, New York Assemblyman Richard L. Brodsky, The City of New York, and the Public Utility Law Project.

The settlement did not prevent people from pursuing additional damages against ConEdison. What it did do was ensure that:

(1) ConEd apologized in writing (something they had refused to do at all, initially)
(2) Require ConEd to make restitution to everyone who lost money
(3) Ensure that the extent of the damages were documented
(4) Prevent ConEd from passing the cost on to rate payers
(5) Bring home money for greening projects to address the health concerns created by ConEd power plants in the area.

As for the many folks who suffered more than $100 in losses, State Law limits the maximum that ConEdison can pay. They actually brought home significantly more than most thought we were entitled to under state law (see this NY Times article saying ConEd was only liable for 9.3 million http://www.nytimes.com/2007/01/19/nyregion/19coned.html?_r=1).

The law needs to be updated to allow for greater reimbursement.  This hasn't happened because our broken government in Albany hasn't updated the reimbursement rates in over 30 years. In the Assembly, Jeremiah will fight to increase the reimbursement rate allowable.

Jeremiah Frei-Pearson stood with his neighbors and together, they fought to get what they could out of ConEd. Aravella Simotas did nothing and now she has the nerve to criticize Jeremiah for not doing enough.
