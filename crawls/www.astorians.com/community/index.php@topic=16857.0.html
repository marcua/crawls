MTA Facing More Budget Woes; Will Propose Cuts

 
NEW YORK (1010 WINS)  -- Stop us if you've heard this one before: the Metropolitan Transit Authority is considering service cuts.

Facing another budget crisis, the MTA will propose a shutdown Monday on dozens of bus routes, the elimination of the W and Z subway lines and the overnight closure of four subway stations.

It's the same service reduction plan threatened earlier this year when the MTA was lobbying for a bailout from Albany. The MTA got its bailout and the cuts never happened.

Among the problems the MTA has faced over recent weeks were a $143 million slash in state transit funding and a lower than expected return from state payroll taxes.

Even with the service cuts, the MTA will have to find other ways to make up the lost cash, sources told the Daily News. "We're not going to rely on anyone else to do anything for us. We're going to rely on ourselves," MTA board member Mitchell Pally told the newspaper.

If it's any consolation, the proposed cuts were drawn up with affecting the fewest possible number of commuters in mind, transit officials said.

A few thoughts... actually quite a bunch of them

- I always thought that living an equal distance from the N and R trains was a boon, but I never thought I would be walking to Steinway more than I would Broadway.  If this goes into effect, that may well be the case now.

- They better do something to compensate for the loss of the W.  N trains alone at their current frequency will make rush hours a living hell, and I'm afraid, start to undo all the progress that this neighborhood has made.

- Where are our local officials on this?  The elimination of the W, without any substantial effort to make up the lost service has significant implications for Astoria in a lot of ways.

- I'm waiting for the Bloomberg speech on the theme of 'shared sacrifice'.  It won't be of too much consolation to any of us Astorians, but something from a high government official would be nice.

- The MTA's finances are highly dependent on taxes and Wall Street (well the public finances of the entire metro region are highly dependent on these two things).  So it's a double edged sword - as long as the economy stays murky, and unemployment remains relatively high, the MTA's ability to continue to provide service will be tested. 

- Anyone who's taken the NQRW lines through Manhattan knows that they're significantly less crowded than the 1-2-3 or 4-5-6, and that made the W, a relatively young and probably under-utilized line overall, an easy target.
