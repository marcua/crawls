AWG: Astoria Writer's Group @ Freeze Peach
« on: October 13, 2004, 05:06:18 PM »
8-9:30pm @ the Freeze Peach Cafe (http://freezepeach.org -- 29th Street & Ditmars) area writers will be having their weekly get together.

Come out and join us for tea and company.  

The meeting format is changing some so if you're read this announcement before and think you can just skim over it now, watch out, I'm making chaaaaanges.

We always knew November would end.  It's time to start thinking about life after Nanowrimo.  Here's how the Astoria Writer's group is adapting to change.

- Next meeting we're going to include a writing activity. A group game/prompt/kick-off at the start of the meeting.  If you have a suggestion for a writing activity or prompt, I'd love to hear it.

- To close the meeting, we're going to do some reading out loud. It's important, as a writer (and a reader) to consider how words sound spoken and to give the power of the spoken word a place. While the format of how these readings will fit into our meetings isn't totally filled out yet, we're adding more. Bring a book, a poem for next time if you'd like.

- The middle of the meeting we're keeping open topic.  Got something on your mind?  Bring it up.  Have something to share?  Bring it in.  

Most importantly, we remain committed to writing, to fostering a community of writers that will encourage, enrich, and inspire our writing lives.  Be it a page, a poem, or a novel, make the time write it - daily, weekly, as often as you can.  

The Writer's Group meets every Monday.  

Subscribe to the announcement list at: http://astorians.net/phplist/index.php?p=subscribe&id=10

Questions?  Ideas?  Email me at jayme@nurbn.com

Points to Ponder -- Monday Oct 18th.
« Reply #2 on: October 21, 2004, 03:52:34 PM »
Monday Oct. 18th @ the much beloved Freeze Peach, Astoria writers gathered around for the first Writer's Get Together.  The group was small (Meesh, Samantha, and myself), friendly, and full of good ideas.  

The good ideas:

- Informal group meetings once a week.  We've moved the time back to 8pm.
- A writer's box for inspiration, prompts, new ideas.
- A blank box/forum to share written pieces encouraging feedback in a non-threatening, non-judgmental, informal setting.  This might include an open box for writers to place written works into (on the Freeze Peach coffee table even) requesting written feedback.  Also perhaps some venue for sharing online and/or making completed works available online.
- The fun of viewing a shared experience from different perspectives.  We want to take group field trips!  Maybe sit in the park, meditate, take a train ride, share a pot of tea, and write the narrative of our perspective.
- Create in a group setting, an environment that fosters creativity and encourages productivity.  
- The practice (and joy) of writing itself is the focus of our gatherings.  Come have fun!


As it concerns National Novel Writing Month:

http://www.nanowrimo.org/

- If you'd like to officially participate, the last day to register is October 31st.  Do it today before you forget!
- We will be deciding upon a //daily// meeting schedule for the month of November.  Suggested time is 9-11 or so weeknight or potentially a morning session and then an evening session.  Let's get that novel written!

Meeting Minutes Oct. 28th
« Reply #3 on: October 29, 2004, 10:42:43 AM »
From the meeting Thursday Oct. 28th:

Excitement was in the air for the second Astoria Area Writer's Get Togther (the exact name of the group fluctuating from post to post).  Meesh and I held it together mid-day, nearly calling the meeting to order a good 6 hours ahead of scheduled get together time.  Luckily we managed to keep it together until 8 when....

2 new soon to be novelists joined us -- Andrew & Eric.

The good ideas:
- Write what you know.  Fictionalize a narrative from your colorful past.  Make it funny.
- Write where the energy is.  Write in cafes, bars, diners.  For bar writing it is suggested you meter your drinking by word count -- and keep track of those beers.  We want to know how many beers it takes to get to 50,000.
- Which lead the conversation to... the writing drinking game!  When our looming word count overwhelms us and the holiday stupor has decended, we're getting back together to write it, chug it, keep it all down.  
- Two technical notes:
  1) Back up, back up, back up (maybe even buy yourself a new toy -- you need a USB flash drive!)
  2) Turn off those obnoxious features in word.  Kill your spellcheck, your grammar fixer upper, and anything that might inhibit your free form progress.  

Next meeting:  Monday Nov 1st, Kick-Off and write-in, 8pm Freeze Peach.

Meeting Nov 1
« Reply #4 on: November 03, 2004, 09:15:28 PM »
From the meeting Monday Nov 1st --

National Novel Writing Month has kicked off and we've hit the ground running.  A huge (gigantic!) number of writers gather at the freeze this ground breaking day to celebrate the start of the big write.  New faces, new voices, one new kind of tea, and seven brand spanking new novels were started today.  

Here's what we know:
- We might want to share, we might not want to share.  It's all okay either way.
- We want to get together!  Weekly, socially, and in high spirits, we're keeping the Monday date and plan to get together to foster the writing community we've just begun to grow.
- We're committed.  Personal goals are more important than a word count in the end.  It's the journey, not the destination.
- 1,666 words a day for 30 days (we checked the math).
- How about the National Writer's Guild?  Take yourself seriously!  
- Invite people.  Share the news.  Tell them what you're up to.  All our welcome to join in at writing practice.

Friendly reminder.  The next Astoria Writer's Group Meeting is Monday Nov 8th at 8pm.  

AND(!) daily writing continues to draw a crowd every night 8-10pm at the Freeze Peach.

From the meeting Nov. 7th
« Reply #5 on: November 16, 2004, 06:38:41 AM »
Monday Nov 7th, the Astoria Writer's group veered off topic.  Yes, it happens.  We were wrapped up in our word counts for November (week 1 was enthusiastic) and we were all about the plot.  Or at least that's what it seems like in hindsight.  

Mentioned, at least briefly, were the following things:
- The second Matrix really sucked.  Thank god the third was better.
- We like it when writers write about writing.  Case in point: Tom Robbins, still life with woodpecker - there is an entire side story about the typewriter and how it keeps messing things up.  Eric also brought up a novel with leaves in the title (forgetfulness is what happens when group minute writer procrastinates for a week leaving a backlog of minutes and a bad memory to mix).  Novel with leaves in the title sounded genuinely disturbing in it's many layers -- a novel written about a documentary, footnotes written about the novel by a crazy guy (?????).
- How much does the method of writing effect the end result?  Who was the first to write on a typewriter?  How did that tool change writing?

From the meeting Nov. 15th
« Reply #6 on: November 16, 2004, 07:02:27 AM »
Monday Nov 15th, we were productive.

Another gaggle of local writers took up space and talked a lot this week.  We had a nice mix of the old bunch, some returning second timers, and some newbies.  We tried, still enthusiastic about this novel thing, to balance chatter about word count with something more.  We have new ideas now and a renewed vision about where our next meeting will ge going.  Details in the bullet points.

- Nanowrimo is halfway over with roughly 25,000 words done.  The page count is smaller than we thought.  The experience is bigger than we'd imagined.
- The sharing, the way in which our novel writing experience has been so far is impressively similar.  Week two was a little painful but we found plot.  For week three we wonder what is left to write about but we have fewer doubts about pressing on.
- Where do characters come from?  Do you take them from real life?  And when you borrow a character, basing them on someone you know, how do you name them?  Is there pressure to find the perfect name?  Nicole brought up Dickens and his picture perfect names.  Maybe the Victorians in general just had phenomenal names.  (Mark Darcy, Jane Eyre, Nicholas Nickolby, Tess of the D'Ubervilles)  Is the name more or less important in modern literature/fiction?
- How about some love for Queens!  Seaching for the Poet Laureate of Queens.  What famous writers have come from queens?  (Playwright mentioned)  And how do you include Queens in your writing?  Mention here of the sound of the broken glass in the river on the park.

Homework:
In the pre-meeting reminder email, I'd asked for a little show and tell time.  We passed around some inspiration --
- Print outs from a goth/incense website with descriptions that are great writing fodder.  "Undertow is the dark shadow of a wave."
- Nicole brought an article on the search for the poet laureate of queens and a snippet of Madelaine L'Engle reflection on how much bigger a novel can be after it's done.  ("Bigger" here being the weight/meaning of something where small choices that seem random/unexpected add gravity to the work that the author might not realize while in the middle of writing.)
 

The future:
- Next meeting we're going to include a writing activity.  A group game/prompt/kick-off at the start of the meeting.
- To close the meeting, we're going to do some reading out loud.  It's important, as a writer (and a reader) to consider how words sound spoken and to give the power of the spoken word a place.  While the format of how these readings will fit into our meetings isn't totally filled out yet, we're adding more.  Bring a book, a poem for next time if you'd like.

The next meeting is Monday Nov. 24th, 8PM at the Freeze Peach Cafe.

Hi guys, this is Tam

Apologies, but I will not be able to make it tonight - I ate something argumentative.

Since it was my turn to bring the prompts, I submit the following:

1. In the vein of the crazy personal ads last week, try this:
Xmas/Holiday Tabloid Headlines.

Come up with as many crazy tabloid headlines as you can in five minutes. But make them legible.

After you have written these, hand the list over to your neighbor, so they can pick one and run with it.

2. In the time honored tradition of holiday stories, take a moment to imagine the most unreasonably radical perfect holiday. I don't mean the perfect cliche holiday, I mean the perfect holiday to YOU. Whether that is being lost in the Himalayas away from all possible human contact, to the Ye Olde Merry England one. Please feel free to make it as unrealistic as possible, and as vivid as possible.

Get a really clear picture and take a lot of notes.

Now, draw a line from the holiday you are most realistically going to have to this perfect holiday. Also feel free to be as unrealistic as possible. Throw everything from Xmas miracles to deranged reindeer and kidnapping by aliens. Everything is permissable.

If you are already going to have your perfect holiday, then you are obliged to add any one of the following:

Santa. (Robotic and or mutant santas are ok)
Disguised Elves
Lost Reindeer
Three Xmas Ghosts
A repeating day (a 'la Groundhog Day)
Any other holiday cliches anyone can think of.

Have fun tonight everyone, and see you next week!

Tam
