The only way I can more succinctly put it besides what I posted on the petition is that Seaburn left me feeling icky, like I was in some sort of cult bookstore. At first it was the lack of organization which, while not a huge problem, gave me no compelling reason to return to the store, whether to browse or look for something specific.

What did it for me was the basement where there were stacks and stacks of Black Power/Black Nationalism/Black Racism/who knows what books. These were the only thing organized about the place (besides the Greek-language shelf next to the front door on the main floor). And I got the feeling that there was some sort of political agenda at work here behind the business, and I like my bookstores neutral and non-partisan, thank you, not agit-prop centers.

My girlfriend as about as liberal as they come and even she was turned off by it. Me, I'm your average bourgeois libertarian, and proud of it, so you can see why I wouldn't want to do business with Seaburn.

I know the previous store was left-leaning, and that didn't really bother me. However with Seaburn, after having seen the stuff in the basement, I (being white) had the distinct feeling that I was on some sort of hostile territory...

Title: Seaburn Books
Post by: zeno on December 01, 2005, 08:16:45 AM
Last week I walked into Seaburn Books just to see how disorganized they were. I talked to the owner who was very nice. I walked downstairs (after I was pleasantly surprised there was a downstairs) and saw the solitary employee shelving books.

I remembered when I was younger bicycling into town to go to all three bookstores in the area. One sold "new" books (some of which had been on the shelves since the sixties) & remembered how one of the proprieters was an old time Beatnick and political activist now grown weary and bitter with age; the other two sold used books... and these were arriving by the day. This was before Border's moved in. I remember just looking at every title every time I went. I remember asking the chain-smoking woman there to hold on to a particular title if it happened to come in. They usually did.. or she went out of her way to inquire. She was a retired English professor grown tired and cynical with age.

Then I think about how the proprieter of Seaburn books assured me that she could order anything that I could possibly want. She smiled a genuine smile. When I asked her what her stock was quoted as on the NAS she gave me a puzzled look. I thought about how much more expedient Amazon.com would be. Or, if there was a Border's or a Barnes & Nobles how they would probably already have whatever I wanted already in stock; no need to wait. I thought about how Starbuck's coffee is perhaps more convenient and expedient than the Peach. The staff there usually have better things to do than talk, although they are also friendly and perhaps genuinely so. I thought about how the manager assured me how he wanted to hang a sign in the window for AHR but never did. Probably afraid of corporate policy, I guess. Who can blame him?

I wonder what it's like to be a food critic. Going from place to place, determing whether a business or reputation should live or die. I'd never actually have to open my own restaurant, cook dinner myself for my 100 or so guests (if the tempermental chef suddenly decided to take off or quit) what some old school establishments still consider what are now labeled customers or 'targeted markets') or put up with the day-to-day drudgery of trying to please everybody, feeling bad about the few times that I was actually irritable and lashed out with a sarcastic or rude remark to people who have been patronizing me and who I served consistently with a smile for years.
Just as long as other people trusted me and my tastes, my livlihood would be secured. Just as long as they served turkey on white bread, I would be lenient. Such power is amazing.
