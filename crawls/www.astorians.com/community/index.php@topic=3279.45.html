Hi guys,

Yeah, if you click on that "check astorians" link through dnsstuff.com it will say the same thing for everyone. Doesn't actually matter which service provider you are reaching us through, I just asked that early on to try and pinpoint the problem in case it was with people's local ISPs - it's not.

What's happening now (the reason some of you can access and some can't) is that despite the fact that the address has been removed from the root DNS servers, some of your computers and ISPs are "remembering" the address from when before it was deleted. Every computer "caches" the address for a while, and most ISP's do as well. Commercial ones cache DNS for longer, which is why some of you can access from work. At this point though DNSStuff is going to report the same for all of us because it's not reporting on your local internet connection, but that is the report for the ROOT domain servers for the entire internet. If they can't see us (they say "astorians.com doesn't exist") then nobody can.

No word yet on a cause, so I don't want to start any rumors.

I don't mean to butt in here (though it seems appropriate to do so after getting this e-mail announcement from a board I haven't looked at in 12 years :-D ) but I don't understand how you can you lose control of a domain without anything happening to its WHOIS record? WHOIS shows astorians.com at ns1/2.windowtree.com, which appears to be a virtual DNS hosted at defenderhosting? (Maybe you get to them through a reseller)

If this happened to me I would not wait around for or even bother with the registrar, I would check my own virtual DNS settings, reboot the DNS and/or Apache (or the whole box if you have that option) and then rattle the cloister at the data center/hosting facility to see if astorians.com is not sitting on their 4th or 5th (or 50th) strength DNS server. And I would keep calling back or working their other support channels until you get a qualified tech person who can look at the data center's routers and tell if the one(s) propagating your domain are working well or at all.

I don't know how much this means, but a full traceroute to ns1.windowtree.com sends one to a strange looking defenderhosting hostname, at which it appears the local router might have failed:
unknown83.126.65.69.defenderhosting.com.

The IP address works: http://69.65.104.197/ which further suggests a local name server or router glitch.

I am not questioning what you've said, just asking out of curiosity should something like this happen to me. I have never seen a domain name get hijacked without some evidence of tampering in the WHOIS record. But domains vanish from the Internet for a lot of other reasons, like when some cPanel weirdness blows up the local configuration for a particular domain.

Not saying that's what happened here, just that dozens of factors could contribute to something like this, and the least nefarious is usually the culprit.
