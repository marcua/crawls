Waive in... Where can I research this? Would there be a database of who waives for who or is it that certain state bars allow it regardless of where you came from?

AJ - the Multistate score waiver is only valid for a couple of jurisdictions for two years after you take it.  General reciprocity, however, is much broader, and usually kicks in after you've been a practicing attorney in good standing for a period of time, usually five years.  For instance, I could have waived in to DC within two years of the bar exam based on my Multistate score, but since I didn't, I then had to wait three more years, to have been admitted for a total of five years, to waive in based on experience.  (I've never bothered, though.)

Here (pdf) is a chart from 2005 listing reciprocity among the states, and here is some general background information on reciprocity.

In practice, many attorneys are admitted temporarily - for instance, for one case - to the bar of another state under a procedure called pro hac vice.

Yes, they help.  I am sure that private prep courses would help, too, if you want to go that route.  But I didn't have the money for one when I went through the LSAT process, and I had just left a job so I had lots of time to devote to studying on my own every day.  I relied on the following three books:

1.) 10 Actual LSAT PrepTests (A book containing the questions and answers, but little more than that, from actual LSATs.  I recommend a lot of repetition to become as afmailiar as possible with the questions you'll face on the real test.  Additionally, and this probably goes without saying, it's a great idea to conduct these under timed conditions.  The time constraints are a major hurdle on this test)
2.) LSAT 180, by Kaplan (This book is a comprehensive review of strategies for all sections, but it focuses on the most difficult questions in each of the sections.  As the title implies, the book is designed for people who are trying to get an extremely high score.  Depending on your comfort with the material, it may be more adviseable to start with their more general book.)
3.) Logic Games Workbook, by Kaplan  (As I mentioned earlier in this thread, I found this book to be the single biggest difference maker for me personally.  After taking the actual test the first time, I identified that this was an area of struggle for me, and I used every inch of this book to turn that around.  They teach you essential techniques about how to map out or sketch out your solutions to the logic games.  After using this book and faithfully applying its principles, you should not have a problem with the much feared logic games.)
