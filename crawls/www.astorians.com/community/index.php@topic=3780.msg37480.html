I wouldn't recommend it for everyone. If you are over 40 - severely myopic, have health conditions such as diabetes or high blood pressure it might be contraindicated.
If I researched it more I might not have done it. I'm OK now, but still need reading glasses on ocassion and it's not perfect.
Had it done about 2 years ago.
The procedure is not that long - but then again roller coasters don't take long either.
After being given half a valium and eye drops to desensitize the pain:
Eye clamps exactly like those used on Malcolm McDowall in the movie A Clockwork Orange are inserted in your eyelids to keep them open during the procedure. THe pain begins.
What seems to be shotglass - but is a vacuum is placed on the operated eye. The other eye is covered. As the vacuum begins the one eye loses sight and fades to black.
This is explained before hand but still the temporary blindness is unsettling.
As it is removed blurry vision returns and you get to see the scalpel cut your cornea and you see the Doc holding it. You hope neither he or you sneezes as it's there like a contact lens, except it's your real cornea. You then are swung under the laser which looks like HAL in 2001 - a circle of white light with a red laser in the middle and you are told to keep staring at the red dot. This is hard. The laser grinds down the lower layer of the cornea at a programmed time according to your degree of visual acuity.
Then the surgeon makes flaps at the edge to tuck the upper cornea in place. Not a comfortable experience.
THe procedure is then repeated for the other eye.
At  home for the next few days I could not read - or even watch TV. I cried that I would not see again.
The Dr and staff acted like everything was OK. I had my doughts. One eye is undercorrected for closer vision but mine in my estimation was way under corrected.
THe other eye could not see well  either.

After  a couple of weeks the Doctor noticed that the one eye didn't settle in pace right and had a wrinkle on it. I was rushed into an operating suite and had the non laser part of the surgery re-done. Without a valium and the drops tossed in quickly it felt like I was punched in the eye 1000 times. He then placed a temporary contact lens over it to make sure it stayed right.

After a few months the other eye was redone but I think he over compensated for the under correction.

Sensitivity to light  still lasted for many month later and good that this period was winter.
By next summer I was OK - not perfect but OK.

This wonderful adventure cost over 3,000 bucks - just to let you know I didn't go to cheapie laser Dr. Nick. The surgeon is Chairman of Opthamology at my Hospital and has impeccable credentials.

Later I did find though that many surgeons would not list me as a good candidate and do my surgery for what I mentioned above. I don't know if I would have listened and might have just searched for one who would. I felt very desperate that even with contacts I was using reading glasses and still having problems.

At least I removed that 1 level of the lenses and it's easier for water sports which I love.

Quote from: daisy on October 13, 2006, 10:15:57 AM
Well BigBryan said they used a scalpel to cut his eye.  Is what he describes as what happens, clamps and all, what exactly happened to you?  I'm not talking about the side effects, just what exactly happens during the entire procedure. 

It would be nice if insurance covered at least part of it.  Has anyone gotten insurance to cover any of it?

Yes, they make a small incision in the cornea.  But it's a device placed on the eye.  It's NOT done free hand.

Basically, they give you a mild sedative.  Cover the eye not being operated on.  They pry the eye open that is to be operated on with a speculum.  They continuously place drops in your eye to moisturize.  They place a small wring on your eye that has the blade.  Make a small cut to create the corneal flap.  The entire time, you're staring at a red light.  When they make the cut, yes, you lose vision briefly.  but it comes back very fast.  Then, while continuing to stare at the red light, they zap your eye with the laser.  They place the flap back, and smooth it out back on your eye.  They close that eye, and do the same with the other eye.  The entire procedure takes about 20 minutes.  But they explain everything they are doing as they are doing it, so nothing is a surprise.  And I was sedated and quite comfortable.
