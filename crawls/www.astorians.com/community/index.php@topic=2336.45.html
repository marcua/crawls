Community Boards are created by the city charter and are appointed by the Borough President of each borough.  They have 50 members, half appointed directly by the borough president and half in consultation with the local council member.  Usually people stay on for sometime after being appointed, though terms are for two years.  There is some turnover each year, but generally the elected officials recruit from local community based groups and institutions, as well as local political organizations.  Some boards are very divided with lots of fights, others work as a body and share a dominant viewpoint.  They are advisory and make recommendations, they have no absolute ability to stop a project, but can influence local elected officials and city and state agencies.  They are a very grassroots level of government.  The members give their time for free - they're all volunteers.
The Precinct Council is an advisory body of citizens who work with the local NYPD Police Precinct.  These meet monthly and are a good way to make sure the officers, community affairs unit and Chief of a precinct know what's going on in the community.  They often deal with noise issues, graffiti, attacks and similar issues - anything in which the cops or detectives get involved.
LICA is a community organization, like a block association from what I can see.  It's local members organizing on issues.  I've been to their website before, which is well done, but never to a meeting.   As I mentioned earlier, they seem more interested in development issues - new construction, etc. than other issues, but their statement of purpose seems open ended, and I have no idea who their leaders are, so it could be a venue to raise awareness and issues, though I don't know if the people who can directly address the problem attend or not.
Going to the press is a great idea - it's important to get the issue out in the media.  Does anyone know folks at the local Queens papers?  If you do, now would be the time to call them!
