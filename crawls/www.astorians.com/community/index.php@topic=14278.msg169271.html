Quote
But my question is real. That government initiative that I was citing exists -- It's called the Food Stamps program.
No it's not. It's now called " Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program" (SNAP). I wonder why the federal government decided to change the name and add a snappy acronym.

Quote
The money that funds the program not only allows people to buy food, which in turns alleviates hunger and promotes nutritional health and well being, the money spent on the food bought with food stamps is spread far and wide and funneled back into the economy.
The money, collected through taxes, is sent to Washington and distributed back through the states. A processing fee is charged. It is not the government's money. It is collected from taxpayer #1 and given to citizen #2. Money removed and then replaced is not stimulative. Funding program through deficit spending is, but that has other consequences down the road.

Quote
Supermarkets, distribution centers, transport/trucking, agriculture are just of industries that benefit, industries that provide a variety of jobs at a variety of income levels and pay taxes back into the system.
The benefits of those industries come at a greater cost to other industries that would have benefited had the tax dollars stayed in the hand of the earner. I say greater cost as a reflection of the bureaucracy fees.

Quote
In the end though, if every penny the government spends is confiscated as you say, how can any government spending be justified? You may believe that the government needs to build infrastructure, run courts and defend borders but what do you say to people that don't believe that their money should be spent on those things?

The same way it is justified today. I do believe that property, in this case money, needs to be confiscated in order for a well functioning society. But it is still confiscated. And confiscating money from citizens to give to other citizens is to much power for those who have the legal means to do so.

But why not use "food stamps" in your initial question? I suspect it was to lay a groundwork of "who could be against such a program?" But in failing to include all the negatives of food stamps, and welfare in general, you were being somewhat disingenuous. First off, it does not do all those wonderful things without a net negative economic effect. Second, the removal of responsibility from the parents to the government has had a catastrophic effect on the American family.
