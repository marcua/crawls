Quote from: wasabisam on October 16, 2006, 10:46:34 AM
But I will say that feminism has often been misrepresented if NYCMacUser's dictionary definition is accurate.

What you have just proven is that it makes little sense to quote dictionary definitions as authoritative descriptions of phenomena.  Words have meaning based on popular usages, not based on the consensus of a committee of dictionary editors.  I'll tell you a funny story.  Last year my undergraduates were writing a paper on early Christianity, and one of the topics was to write about St. Augustine's influence on the development of the religion.  So one of my students begins the paper by quoting a dictionary definition of "Christianity," and then goes on to attempt to explain how Augustine adapted that conception.  The problem is that the dictionary's definition was actually a reflection of the orthodox Christianity that Augustine himself helped to create!  In other words, using the dictionary, the student got caught in a circular trap, because he didn't bother to ask himself what the dictionary was trying to do with its definition- that is, provide a base level of information, which is by no means nuanced or exhaustive.  The same thing is going on with this feminism question.  I think you will be very hard-pressed to identify some single unified entity called "feminism."  If you want to say it's about equality of the sexes, fine, but that's definitely not the end of the story.
