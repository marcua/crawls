There's an intrinsic problem with their ratings, which is that the people who rate Kyklades are NOT the same people who rate Michael Psilakis' modern Greek restaurants in Manhattan. The assumption underlying Zagat is that there is a population of people with opinions and that people go to restaurants randomly. That is empirically false. There is a correlation between the types of restaurants people like and the types of restaurants they go to (and rate). So you end up with noncomparable ratings.

They have enough information in their database of ratings to do a much better job. You should be able to log into Zagat.com, rate a bunch of restaurants, and have personalized, predicted ratings for a restaurant *for you*, along the lines of what Netflix does. And they should be able to convert the numerical ratings that they get from everyone to rankings, and then use those to recreate more accurate ratings. The Zagats may be clever entrepreneurs and foodies, but they're not statisticians...

Quote from: Harlan on October 31, 2008, 06:21:54 AM
The assumption underlying Zagat is that there is a population of people with opinions and that people go to restaurants randomly.

I don't think that's an underlying assumption of Zagat's.  I think most people who use Zagat's understand what it is and where the reviews are coming from -- that it's a given that they are not necessarily comparable.  Certainly, before the Internet became ubiquitous it was the only reference most of us had for a large swathe of restaurants across New York (yes, it's always been Manhattan-centric, but that's another issue).  When using it, we always took into account what type (local, big draw, hole in the wall, etc.) of restaurant we were looking at -- I've always considered the review of a popular neighbourhood restaurant like Kyclades to have a different starting point than reviews of popular city-wide restaurants like Union Square Cafe, and I don't think I'm the only one. 

They also used to (maybe still do) have a square next to the head of the review - a solid black square meant that reviews submitted were consistent, and a half black/half white square meant that reviews submitted were inconsistent.  For instance, when Kyclades first appeared in Zagat, it had a half black/half white square next to its name.  In the 2001 and 2002 editions, it had a solid black square, but by 2004 it had gone back to a divided square.  I think I joined the online version in 2005, so I don't get it in hard copy anymore (don't ask why I still have the 2001, 2002 and 2004 editions on my bookshelves), and it seems they don't do this or something equivalent online.

Now, it's one of many reference points, and I always cross-reference (if I have the time) when I'm choosing a new place.  I have a basic distrust of the reviews on sites like Yelp, Menupages and Citysearch, because the users are generally unverifiable (even though it's often easy to spot a shill post).  I do, however, use them in conjunction with Zagat, as well as sites like Chowhound.

Zagat is far from perfect, but it has its place.  Personally, I'm glad they're not statisticians.
