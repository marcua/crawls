Although I agree with much of Rose's sentiments, I have to say that it's what I love about this neighborhood.
For the many of us who grew up here, recent times have brought about such a homogenization of the city. New high rises, luxury apartment buildings and generic (but fancy) chain stores are cropping up everywhere. I think Astoria has done a fairly decent job of staving that off so far.
As far as cost, I think it's one of the best deals in the city. I live in a very safe neighborhood, where I have no problems walking around by myself at 4 in the morning if I wanted to. Just like any neighborhood, there are pockets here and there where I would avoid late at night, but for the most part I'm ok wherever. What I am paying in my place would elsewhere get me (If I was lucky) a place that's at least 20 minutes farther from my office by subway on an even more unreliable line, and probably shady neighborhood.
I definitely would advise most people that if they can afford $1500 per person room shares to go move to park slope or the UES. But for what I'm paying, there is no where else I'd rather live.

As for the racism issue- I had a friend visiting me about two weeks ago, and he asked me why there were no black people in my neighborhood. This is something that never really occurred to me, but it made me think. I can't help but think there is some underlying racism going on either from old-school landlords or elsewhere. It seems that relative to the rest of the city, Astoria has a proportionally smaller percentage of Black residents.

I either do not disagree or else I have no opinion on much of you say, though I think bringing racism into the mix is a stretch. We're in an area inhabited by virtually every race, creed, and religion on this earth, making racial tensions a near certainty. But it's a certainty pretty much anywhere. I encounter it in Astoria, but I also encountered in when I lived Upper West Side, Upper Manhattan, Upper East Side... To me these summertime Astoria street fairs are simply astonishing, to walk among people from everywhere without fear of firebombings and attacks. There can't be too many places like that.

But where I take issue (and I mean no offense) is that you seem to blame your decision to move here in large part on the content of a message board, and to me that is borderline ridiculous. Message boards are useful for some things and entertaining for others, but (speaking for myself) this space does not look, feel, or sound too much like the Astoria I see when I look out the window or walk around outside -- in fact I would move far, far away from Astoria if my spoken curse words were magically replaced with euphemisms like POOPIE.

But I don't mean to single out this board, nor do I mean anything specifically disparaging. I think the same could be said of almost any online space: the number of people who inhabit the online space and fill its pages represent a tiny, tiny fraction of the general population, making it ludicrous to characterize something like a city based on a bleepin' message board.

I used to manage message boards for some high profile web sites and it was no secret: the tiniest fraction of 1% of the sites' millions of visitors ever even *considered* posting to a message board. I don't have any facts to support it now but I'd bet that a similar ratio holds true in Astoria and elsewhere.

In a sense I actually think your "warning to lurkers" is valid, but I think it's valid in a general sense that message boards are an unlikely resource for serious decision-making.
