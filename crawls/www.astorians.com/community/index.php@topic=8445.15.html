Debbie, I agree that our impact has been substantial and that we've done damage to this planet.  Sure, humans don't make meteors and such, but it doesn't mean  However, you (and not just rebutting you, but the general thought as well) are not taking into account a few key facts. 

First, we didn't really have adequate temperature measurements before the Industrial Revolution.  So, there is no way to know if such serious temperature swings occurred before then, because we couldn't measure them.  Secondly, we have not had a SERIOUS volcanic eruption http://www.volcanolive.com/large.html or http://www.volcano.si.edu/world/largeeruptions.cfm since the Industrial Revolution.  While these pump out MASSIVE amounts of greenhouse gases, they also can cool the planet for years at a time.  When large eruptions occurred previously, they caused ice ages. 

Thirdly, climate change takes hundreds, thousands, or millions of years.  The previous 150 or so years is just a speck of time in that total span.  We could have already been in the middle of a climate change and, as humans with a very short time on this planet and an even shorter recorded history, could have no idea.  This cycle could have started in the 1600s and just beginning to come to fruition; we'd have no idea.

Lastly, (well, for my rebuttal, anyway) I saw a show on NatGeo last night that talked about the major extinctions that have happened on this planet.  The scientist presenting the program stated that we have a massive extinction on this planet about every +/- 62 million years.  This is due to the fact that our solar system moves up and down in the Milky Way, which puts it in and out of the main protection of the Milky Way's magnetic field; which protects us from radiation.  They use the example of a diver observing different levels of light by being closer or further from the surface.  Anyway, we're about +/- 10 million years away from another extinction.  So, who is to say we aren't beginning to see another one approaching?  Here's the article if you'd like to read it: http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2007/04/070420-extinctions.html

I'm not trying to argue with you.  I believe we have sped up the process and in no way think that we shouldn't do everything in our power to curb our greenhouse/toxic gas emissions, live as green as possible, and save the bees.  We aren't helping a thing at this point.  But, I think that, honestly, a lot of it is out of our hands.  We can adapt, or we can die, as we have since we stood up on two legs and were able to give the thumbs-up.

Just a couple quick corrections, for the record.

A significant source of methane, a greenhouse gas (NOT related to the Ozone layer, which is an entirely different entity in a different part of the atmosphere with a different role completely) IS 'cow farts' mostly due to the huge numbers of cattle kept in industrial feedlots and fed on corn (grain fed cattle), which is not the diet to which their digestive system is adapted, being grazers. Ergo, lots of methane (and antibiotics, etc. etc.).

A very large number of very good scientists for very many years have been working on ice core data, which does give us a climate record that extends far, far back before the Industrial Revolution - 100s of thousands of years. Furthermore, geologists and geochemists have been able to use similar sources of data (isotopic ratios) in the rock record to push it back into the millions. Temperature data. We have it. Its long term.

Mass extinctions are particularly tricky things. Whether or not the apparent periodicity of mass extinctions is a 'real' phenomenon has been debated amongst us paleontologists for years now, ever since Raup and Sepkoski showed some nice comet stuff that fit with the ever-popular Alvarez hypothesis for the demise of everyone's favourite fossils, the dinosaurs. However, the evidence is very far from clear on this stuff, National Geographic specials aside. We are in the midst of the sixth mass extinction, the rate far exceeds the 'background rate' of extinction and appears to be higher than any previous mass extinction, including the Permian/Triassic event which was the largest in Earth's history (~90% of species lost).

I have absolutely no idea what the volcano example is supposed to show, but the sophisticated models being built by the nobel-prize winning scientists who work on this have *gasp* actually taken this into account. Surprise! we took geology 101! See Hansen et al. latest publication in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, USA for an example of this modelling and an assessment of its accuracy.

The ice record etc. shows pretty uncontrovertibly that climate is changing. Extinction rates are very high. Correlative to the temperature change is an increase in greenhouse gases caused by human activity. The greenhouse effect is responsible for Earth not being cold like Mars but also not being run-away hot like Venus (where all C is in the atmosphere, rather than locked up in rocks/fossil fuels/organisms as it is here on Earth). Sometimes, 2 + 2 = 4!

Now, you may not agree with some of the way activists on this issue have behaved. But for please get your science right and stop listening to the spinners.

I fail to see what the point of the argument is. If we're not the sole cause of climate change that proves.. what, precisely? Furthermore, who was arguing that we are? If you've read the scientific literature, you'll see the term 'human-mediated climate change' used, because that's what we're talking about. If you need to reduce every argument to black and white in order to understand it, well, I'm sorry, but this isn't the issue for you.

Of COURSE climate change is massively complicated and essentially always occurring. From a geologist's standpoint, the question 'is climate changing' is to some degree nonsensical. Like extinction always occurs and we can speak of a background rate of extinction, climate always always changes, tracing climate change and the effects its had on biota is what some of us dedicate our lives to. And ice core records won't show anything for temperature millions of years ago - the ice caps haven't been around that long.

So is climate change a problem for humans? Yes, because its going to affect our lifestyle quite significantly. Large area of the planet are in the grips of massive drought. Wildfire frequency and intensity is increasing. Tropical Storm intensity may also be increasing. Distribution of species is changing and biodiversity is declining. Biodiversity maintains various ecosystem services which are essential for our survival. There's a very long list of things that go with the type of change we're seeing. Is it different to what happened to humans in the past? Yes, partly because its happening so rapidly, and that doesn't seem to have happened globally previously (nor, incidentally has the rate of excinction). Partly also because we're different - there's many, many more of us and we rely on climate patterns to do things like grow our crops, keep our cities and homes safe, provide water, etc.
