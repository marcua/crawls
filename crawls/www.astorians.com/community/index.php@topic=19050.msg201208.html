I would agree if what they are offering is actually better, but it's not. As I understand it, they are simply cutting a deal that would allow content providers to pay internet service providers to have their content prioritized for transmission over other data. Say Google pays Comcast, Time Warner, & Verizon to have YouTube prioritized. Under this circumstance data from YouTube would be given a higher priority on the internet and presumably allocated a larger bandwidth for data transmission than say Vimeo or Flickr. It's anti-competitive and in a capitalistic system, such as the US economy, it's bad news because lack of competition leads to poorer quality products. It could basically mean that the company with the most money to burn would control the internet. It's also resembles the internet version of Payola.

To clarify/update this story, it seems as if content-specific throttling being discussed is limited to wireless networks, not to wired home/business internet connections. Here's a story about it:

Quote
Google and Verizon aren't saying much more, for now, and The New York Times appears to be tacitly sticking to its story. But if you read between the lines, and read CEO Eric Schmidt's comments on Net neutrality from this week's Techonomy conference, it's clear that the real traffic battleground is wireless networks.

My guess: Google and Verizon are almost certainly negotiating in a way to keep wired networks neutral and open, but to preserve Verizon and other telcos' ability to tier and shape their wireless networks as they see fit. It's bad news for mobile broadband in the United States, but it's clear that Google's not standing in the way.

FWIW, I'm opposed to this too, although I'm not opposed to modest preferential treatment of certain protocols. Companies should be able to register certain data streams as particular types of content (streaming video, etc, based on protocol only), and ISP should be allowed to make data quality guarantees for specific types of content, but ISPs should not be allowed to engage in financial relationships that would yield faster load times for one company's web page versus another company's web page, period, regardless of wired/wireless issues.

I don't agree with these arguments about "level playing field". Big businesses are big because they are money machines. On a principle level, I have no real objection to the concept. In any business, having the deliverable reach the customer at a timely rate is key. If you are looking in terms of a website, then the deliverable of the business is the web page content. If a company will pay to have this content reach their consumer at a faster rate, then I have no problem with that.

To bring it back to your Amazon vs. BN analogy: what if either BN or Amazon decided to pick up the tab on free overnight shipping to their customers? Their deliverable gets to the consumer faster because the company paid a premium. Doing this has not actually slowed down or hindered other companies which choose to ship their items via regular mail. In this hypothetical, the company chose to pay a premium, and their content got to the consumer faster. I would consider that a competitive advantage, certainly- but not an unfair one.
