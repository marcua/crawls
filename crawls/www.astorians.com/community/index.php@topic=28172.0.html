Hey all,

What would be a reasonable rent increase for a significant other moving into your 1 bedroom apartment?

My landlord and I have a fairly good relationship, he has met my boyfriend a number of times, and was cool with it when I broached the subject of him relocating to NY and moving in with me.

My rent for a 1 bedroom in upper Ditmars is $1,075. Low I know, but the kitchen and bathroom ought to be gutted and I have a 15 minute walk to the subway. He had just raised my rent from $1,000 to $1,075 in January - fair.

He just surprised me by saying he will raise the rent from $1,075 to $1,500 once my boyfriend moves in. I was shocked. That's a 30% increase because another person is using your water? I pay electricity and gas too.

Any insight would be appreciated, but I was thinking more along the lines of a $100 increase after asking around.

Thanks for your time.

Emily

I was once in the same position as EmilyB. I lived on the top floor of a private house that was divided into two apartments. Another tenant lived downstairs. The landlord lived a few houses down the street and was a lovely person and very reasonable.


The apartment was in decent shape, not the best but not the worst I lived in, but the rent was very, very cheap and part of the 'unwritten' deal was in exchange for a low rate, the understanding would be that I would live there alone, I wouldn't be a nuisance to the neighbors and I wouldn't be a nuisance to the landlord. He would take care of the big things that could go wrong and I wouldn't bother him every five minutes to fix the little things. It was an arrangement we both lived by and were happy with.


A couple of years later, when my partner moved in, my landlord was still a lovely person, but he was less happy about the thought of renting to two people and what that may entail. He made the deal with me, not me and my partner.


So he felt an increase was in order and it was less about the money and more about he thought his aggravation level would increase and that he didn't want to seem to be taken advantage of.


At first, I felt the same as EmilyB, but then I realised, I changed the deal with him, he didn't change the deal with me, so we met somewhere in the middle and that was that. When I moved out at the end of my lease he wanted me to stay and hoped that the increase didn't make me leave.


Just offering another perspective. The only people who know the reality are EmilyB and her landlord and we only have Emily's side of the story. The rest is conjecture on my part. To me the rationale for the increase is that two people will be living in the property instead of one but it could very well be something altogether different.

Is this a Rent Stabilized apartment?

If it is, the addition of your boyfriend to the lease is a change of tenancy as far as the statute is concerned.  Accordingly, your landlord is entitled to increase the rent by the maximum amount permitted by Rent Guidelines Board.

If this is NOT a Rent Stabilized apartment, your landlord can raise the price however much he wants.  That is the downside of asking him to put your boyfriend ON your lease. 

Alternatively (and regardless of whether the apartment is or is not Rent Stabilized), you can have your boyfriend move in and not put him on the lease.  Under those circumstances, there is nothing the landlord can do.

Google: "New York City Roommate Law" for more information.  You can also read this: http://www.nycrgb.org/html/guide/basics.html#Roommates

Finally, if you go with the Roommate Law option, at least you know what to expect when he renews your lease the next time.  That should give you ample time to look for a new place.
