Here's an article on MSN Encarta that suggests a few possibilities: Celebrity Obsession: Is it Wrong?.

Quote
Joel Stein, who often interviews celebrities for Time magazine, and who wrote a star-studded column for Entertainment Weekly before he was replaced by a bigger celebrity known as Stephen King, thinks star gazing could have something to do with the demise of small-town America. When people started living in big cities, they lost having that common group of people to gossip about. Celebrities filled that niche.

Kat Giantis, who writes about celebrity gossip for MSN, has another theory. By tracking celebrity successes and glamorous romances, we get to experience their lives vicariously. And the flip side of that coin is schadenfreude, she says. When the rich and famous screw up, our normal-sized lives seem all that much better.

My own theory is a little wackier, and based on my lifelong interest in mythology. I think the modern celebrity fills a bit of the same role played by the Greek gods. Unlike more modern deities, the Greek gods made mistakes, had feuds, and often got into trouble--sometimes of the most personal romantic nature. The Greeks shared these stories, in part, as entertainment--a little bit like a prototype People magazine. As those gods were replaced by an all-knowing, all-seeing, and always-perfect creator, stories about people behaving both bravely and badly had to fill the gap. And so, in my theory, were invented the first human celebrities.

Francisco Gil-White, a psychology professor at the University of Pennsylvania, has yet another theory--that celebrity obsession has to do with how human beings form relationships. Gil-White says that human beings are unlike other animals in that most of what they know about the world, they learn from other humans. As part of this, the most successful human beings are ranked the most highly socially. We look up to them and even suck up to them so that they will want to interact with us. This is an adapative behavior. We benefit from using information and from being in the good graces of the successful.

It's not much of an article, but it has this interesting tidbit:

Quote
Researchers believe that in a media-orientated society, celebrities have taken the place of neighbours, relatives, friends and family for many people. They feel that the respect for family members has been replaced by worship of the famous, as this is the new method of associating with success. In modern cultures, there is less emphasis on the value of experience, but instead knowing the 'secret' of success, and celebrities are seen to hold this, since they often come from mediocrity.
