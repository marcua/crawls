Does anyone else get the impression that people's expectations for Sarah Palin are so low that as long as she manages to form sentences she'll still have wide support? In her Charles Gibson interview, her answers swayed between being purposely vague and overly rehearsed, or simply non-answers, like they told her to just focus on a few key ideas and keep your composure and people won't notice you're not actually saying anything. I feel like if anyone else even trying to run for President or VP answered questions the way she did, they would've been laughed off the scene.

And no, I don't fault her for not knowing what the Bush Doctrine is, but it was clear in her answers to that question that she is very skilled at debating and public speaking, but doesn't have the knowledge on a national level to appear as more than an empty shell. But the consensus seems to be that she did well, since she didn't "crash and burn." She's also a very good speech giver, but people don't seem to think the of the fact that she didn't write that convention speech. She delivered it well, but it's more important to me what these people say outside of speeches and what their plans actually are than how well they can read a rehearsed speech. And in regards to her "finally being against the bridge," if Obama had done that and acted like he was never in favor of it, of course it would be deemed a flip-flop and he would be run into the ground like John Kerry was. Apparently, lack of experience is bad unless it's Sarah Palin, being a celebrity-like political figure is bad unless it's Sarah Palin, and flip-flopping is bad unless it's Sarah Palin. And right, Sarah, just keep telling everyone that being able to see Russian land from one of Alaska's islands makes you experienced in foreign policy. Maybe enough people are stupid enough to believe it. And regarding the "Obama is a Muslim" story, it's not being spread by any credible new sources, but people are working behind the scenes (whether on an official level or not, who knows) to make sure the story doesn't go away and to try to scare people who would be scared of that sort of thing.

Palin does remind me a lot of Bush - overconfident and uninformed. Not a good combination.

Quote from: enigmacat on September 12, 2008, 01:25:27 PM
I'd like to hear more about your thoughts on that....
Political parties combined with the plague that is the electoral college system undermine the voting process, IMO. With the system being dominated by two parties it has become extremely divisive. The mentality is "us" against "them". Why should we be at odds with each other? The nominees from their respective parties I don't feel represent their party particularly well either. It becomes more about "electability" or the best candidate to beat the opposing party. I don't know of many Republicans that really thought/think McCain was their ideal candidate but he became the "best" or "most reasonable" choice. Shouldn't we be voting for the candidate that aligns closest to our beliefs and ideals? The right seems to be extremely fragmented since the rise of the religious right. You have social conservatives and financial conservatives competing on the same ticket, but you generally end up with something in the middle. This isn't just on the right either, as we saw with Hillary v Obama. Of course once their have the nomination from their party (or become the presumptive nominee) they usually end up pushing themselves further to the middle, once again that whole "electability" thing and the "us vs. them".

Then of course there is the issue of people voting blindly along party lines. Even if the ballot had candidates such as A. Hitler and B. Mussolini I am sure they would garner a fair share of the votes. Can't let the other side win, you know.

I guess the real question is what good has come out of our party system? I can't think of much. I suppose it wouldn't be as bad if there were say 5 major parties, instead of two, but the division would still be there.
