Here I am, I do sleep sometimes.

The link to the pdf for the plan is right here. (http://www.streetsblog.org/wp-content/pdf/vernonblvd.pdf)

Parking will be removed along the river side of Vernon Blvd, parking will remain on the eastern side of Vernon.  The majority of bldgs currently along the river side are parks or large industrial buildings that often have offsite parking (ConEd, Costco, Keyspan).  In exchange for this there will be a bike lane going in both directions with a 5' painted buffer between the bike lane and traffic.  It is our hope that once that is done (which should start summer of this year), the DoT can begin to explore raising the money to fund a capital project to make the painted buffer a physical buffer and make the bike lane a proper green way.  The parking removal aspect of Vernon will begin at 45th St and end at Main St.  The commercial district of Hunters Point will not get a bike lane, rather it will get on street markings and signage alerting cyclists and drivers to be aware of each other as they share the road.  At Queensbridge Houses parking will not be removed and a pedestrian relief green street median will be installed to make it easier for residents crossing into the park there.

I disagree with the statement that no one bikes along Vernon.  Many people do, myself included, to access the parks and to commute to Brooklyn via the Pulaski Bridge.  Vernon is also one of the few roads that actually goes continuously past the QBB.  A recent survey by the group Green Shores NYC found that 80% of respondents biked or walked to the riverside parks.  If you stand along Vernon during commute times during the week or any time during the weekend you will see cyclists using Vernon.

Vernon is dangerous to cycle and cross as is b/c it is a local truck route and b/c of some of the blind curves.  Other than signal changes a bike lane has been found to slow down traffic by about 10% on a busy road.  Additionally, zoning changes are most likely due to come to the waterfront area, to put a bike lane like this in place now, before the changes take place all over, "reprograms the street" to encourage new residents to leave their car behind, or for builders to have more parking offsite.

The Transportation Alternatives Queens Committee (http://groups.google.com/group/TAWQ) (you knew I was going to get that link in there somewhere) has been working towards this for over a year.  We sent a letter, signed on by 15 community groups, to DoT Commissioner Sadik-Khan, we've done outreach at pretty much every event we go to, talking up the river side greenway.  We've met stay at home mom's who don't bike but like the idea of calming traffic and giving cyclists somewhere safe on Vernon that isn't the sidewalk.  We've met commuters from Brooklyn who come over the Pulaski to work at locations like the power plants down past Astoria Park.  We've met families that live in Ravenswood, Queensbridge and Astoria Houses and would like a better connector for their kids to use to get to the parks for all the great events that hold there.

Yes parking will be lost, around 250 spots.  But DoT is open to create more parking along side streets by changing the regulations.  It probably won't replace all the spots, but it will replace some.  And it is being lost for a good cause, by building a useful and safe bike network the City encourages a healthy, cheap and emission free form of travel.  Some us may think it's insane and strange, but our children and grandchildren who grow up with the bike infrastructure won't think it's strange, they'll just think it's there to be used.  This is something that will benefit our City's population into the future.

Finally, I'd like to address to taxes comment.  This is often the one that bugs me the most, because I also pay taxes, the same taxes everyone pays.  I pay taxes for the QBB and I pay taxes for Vernon and I pay taxes for free parking all throughout the city.  And I barely get to use any of these facilities, b/c I don't own a car.  I'd like part of my taxes to go to a bike lane, how about that?  While legislators are pushing for a tax rebate for hybrid drivers, I get no rebate for riding my bike and taking mass transit, while the gas tax is being bounced around as something to eliminate for this summer I will get no discount for riding my bike around town.  While people cried that $2k a year for congestion pricing is too much to drive, often alone, into the busiest part of the city, I pay $972 a year for a metrocard to cram onto often crowded trains.  What do I get back for my taxes, hmmm?  More parking and wider roads is not what I want and does not serve me.
