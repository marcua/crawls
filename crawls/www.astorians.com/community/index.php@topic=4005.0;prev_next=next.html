In defense of outsourcing, it's incredible how much India has changed, just in the three years since I first went there. The biggest change is probably the way in which people approach their future. There is now a lot more reason to stay in India than to leave. And the boom in education in unprecedented- colleges, especially technical colleges, are bursting at the seams with new students.

Needless to say the unfettered development is leading to all sorts of other problems- pollution, inadequate transportation, corruption, overbuilding... but even all this provides jobs to thousands of people who were previously living on the streets.

But the most encouraging thing is probably the large number of Indian owned companies that are popping up. The boom is not just foreign companies hiring Indians, there is also a growth in local business, which do work both locally and on the international market.

All that said, yeah, it would be great if companies would hire within the country but there are so many mitigating factors. Wages need to be higher here because the cost of living is so much higher, but this will probably even out over time. The real problem we need to be worrying about is education. The fact is, many of the Indian 20 somethings that work in call centers probably speak better English than the 20 somethings that would get hired for the job here... and they are a lot less likely to curse you out when you tell them you used your CD drive as a cup-holder.

But putting aside low-level jobs like answering phones in a call center, the truly disturbing thing is that this country is not turning out enough engineers, scientists or mathematicians to fulfill the needs of industry, and needs to import them from, or hire them in other countries. At the end of the day, it is industry, not the ability to answer phones, that made this country what it is, and that is where we are really losing out.

For me, the most troubling aspect of offshoring is the lack of worker protection, both for domestic and overseas workers.  While offshoring might benefit consumers, stockholders, and especially CEOs, the economic gains are disproportionately higher at the distribution end than at the manufacturing end.  Loss of jobs for unskilled workers to offshore manufacturing leaves a gap in the domestic labor market - how will that be filled?  And even though offshore workers might be earning more income than they would otherwise, they don't have any protection either - witness the shift of mass market textile production from India to China, where this type of labor is more cost-efficient.  If the bottom line is squeezed, it's always the workers who pay the price.

I kinda agree with both sides on this one. I don't think that all outsourcing is bad. I don't think that Americans somehow have more of a "right" to work than an equally skilled person in another country. But my concerns for outsourcing as a whole center around the conditions the workers work in, and the benefits they get. I don't think it's fair for companies to exploit (abuse) workers in any country, of any nationality - however, I don't think that outsourcing done right is exploitation. This isn't an issue of "Americanism" for me.

As for Jonathan's frustrations, I too have had similar experiences and frankly it is upsetting. Here is a case (Jonathan's case) where an english speaker (and someone who was well trained) could indeed do a better job than the person at the other end of the phone. I'd do the EXACT same complaining if I was calling an american customer service rep who provided the same low-quality customer service, so the issue for me really isn't about whether or not I'm speaking to India or not, but whether or not the person on the other end of the phone is qualified and capable of doing the job. I've spoken to several customer service reps in other countries (such as Canada or the UK) and not had these problems. And frankly I've spoken to american customer service reps who provided extremely bad service. The issue for me here is companies that just don't give a rip about their customers, and don't train their employees well, and don't hire competent CS reps.

Generally I do think that if you can find equally skilled labor in another country, and you can provide a good working environment for them, that outsourcing is okay. The problem I have with outsourcing stereotypically though is that (as has been pointed out) many of the people who are doing these jobs are working in very very poor conditions (I believe Walmart and some other big companies are the worst at this) and are essentially slaves, this makes me feel bad to support companies that do it. However, I don't think that makes all outsourcing bad, to follow this argument to its logical extreme we'd never buy products made outside the U.S., yet the very computers that we participate in this board on -- every single component more than likely -- was assembled in another country at a low wage. The bottom line is that we all outsource every single day with the clothes we wear, the subways we ride, and even the energy we use, cars we drive, oil we consume, food etc.

Ultimately the question for me is not whether or not we outsource, but how we outsource.
