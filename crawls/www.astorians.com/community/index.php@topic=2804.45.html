Personally, I would vow to stop using Amazon.com for my books (I purchase about 5  per month) and will come to the bookstore.  I also purchase many Coffee Table Books every month for work. 

Just some info - Years ago, there was simply no market for a bookstore in Astoria.  Most of the locals were either first generation immigrants or people who lived here for generations but whose reading capacity never went beyond the New York Post.  Now, there is a market.

When Susan Terry's on the corner of 31st and Ditmars went out of business, Barnes and Noble received an amazing number of phone calls to open there.  Naturally, an independent bookstore is preferable, but it goes to show you that there is a market.

To those of you who mock all the Greek Cafes, I prefer them to all the banks and places like Kay's Jewelry (sp?), Starbucks, fast food stores, Payless Shoe Stores etc. that seem to be going up all over Manhattan.  In the neighborhood where I work in Murray Hill, all of the interesting little shops have closed and all of the aforementioned have opened.  Furthermore, there are four Starbucks within a five block radius.  Would you prefer to have Starbucks replace all of the Greek Cafes?  At least the offerings and design of the cafes vary somewhat!

To all who are part of this post...
    Thanks for keeping this topic alive...
Last month I stood at the subway at Astoria Blvd and at Ditmars, handing out cards asking for people's input about what they would want in a bookstore...and the responses I got were spectacular.  Whether it was event-related, cafe related, or book subject related, you all had gold to offer.
   As a result, I've decided to move ahead with making this happen.  The plan in to create a new and used bookstore (the final decision as to whether it is profit or nonprofit is pending) with a cafe that specializes in board games, toys, and puzzles - and changes in purpose from morning (getting your coffee and a magazine to go) to evening (imagine the possibility of coming home to Astoria and having a cafe to sit down, drink a glass of wine, and curl up with a good book) with plenty of events and programs to go around.
Currently, I have a business partner who is in charge of the cafe side of the project, and we have begun to put together a business plan...in addition, there are a few of us who will be meeting and brainstorming regarding details and events.  To be honest, though - i could definitely use the community's help... I asked a while back and got a few responses, but was not really ready to move forward with the project, so I did not follow up very promptly....so now I ask again - who would like to contribute to strengthening our neighborhood?  Who would like to have a place to read, a place to work outside of your apartment, a place to go after work to relax?  And what are you willing to do to make it reality?

I recently started a blog at http://goodreadingbooks.blogspot.com/ , which will, as long as I keep in integrity about it, show the progress made on the store.  Feel free to stop by, comment, or send an e-mail to goodreadingbooks@nyc.rr.com. and thanks for all to support so far.

i would LOVE it if there was a bookstore opening in astoria. i too remember when susan terry closed and there were rumours flying EVERYWHERE that B&N was going to come in.

at one point there WAS a bookstore at ditmars, right under the train where the italian food store is now (drat i forgot the name, i have been gone too long!) - it was there when i was in middle school (mid 90's) because i remember getting some Nancy Drew Goes to College books. I was really dissappointed when it closed...but it seemed really sparse now that I think about it. there was just alot of space and not enough books.

thats one thing...try to make the most out of the space to give the most offerings. this may seem obvious - don't clog up the shelves with like 5 copies of the same book on the shelf (sometimes i see that alot in bookstores in nyc)...one, maybe two will suffice especially if its a thick book.

i see this alot in the shops in japan - they don't have much of one thing out on the floor, so that they can display the most amount of merchandise as possible, same thing goes in japanese bookstores, there is only maybe one, two copies of a book and if its not there is ask - most of the time its in the storage section. if you can say find a way to keep one or two copies out to maximize the shelf space, then say if a customer buys the book make a note of it, and refill the slot when necessary; to encourage people to ask if you have a particular book, u can place a sign or a notification to encourage people to ask the booksellers.

pity harry potter is over - that would be a great way to bring people to the bookstore with a party like that - i remember mentally bitching on how i ended up slogging voluntarily to manhattan to wait all night for number 6.
