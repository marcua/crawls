This is a tough call.  About five years ago my cocker spaniel started to lose her vision from cataracts.  She had been diabetic for several years at this point and was requiring two shots of insulin per day.  I took my dog to a doggie Opthamologist surgeon in NJ.  He quoted me similar prices and said there were no guarantees and that the cataracts could grow back.  He also said the dog might get glaucoma.  I decided not to do the surgery.  About a year later the dog who had been seeing silhouettes went totally blind while she was losing her hearing.  Soon she was totally blind and totally deaf and her spirit was broken.   She no longer wagged her tail and paced in circles at times.  It was very sad.  I finally put her to sleep.  I often wished I had tried to save her sight despite the cost and their being no assurances of success.  You face a very tough emotional and financial decision.

Thank G-d cataracts don't happen overnight and require immediate intervention.

Researching canine diseases can be quite challenging.

Frankie's blood test results came back today. He is not diabetic. (Thank You G-d.)

He is low in potassium and since I prefer not to use supplements unless absolutely necessary, and since he won't eat banana, I will have to give him a small baked potato twice a week with a little yoghurt.

This is what I've learned about canine cataracts:

The most common cause of cataracts in dogs is an inherited defect in the lens. These cataracts usually occur at a young age and are called juvenile cataracts. Cataracts may occur due to an injury to the eye, inflammation in the eye, or a systemic disease such as diabetes. In an older dog, the lens may develop into a cataract as a degenerative aging process. It is normal for the lens to become slightly gray to blue in color when a dog reaches approximately 8 years of age. This is called nuclear sclerosis, which means hardening of the lens. Nuclear sclerosis occurs in every animal and does not affect vision. Many times nuclear sclerosis is mistaken for cataracts by the non-medical professional.

The only treatment for cataracts is surgical removal. Many claims for nonsurgical “cures” have been made by unscrupulous individuals.

It is not necessary to replace the lens after surgery as most dogs do not need depth perception in order to drive an automobile.
