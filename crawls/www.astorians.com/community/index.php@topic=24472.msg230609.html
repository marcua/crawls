 Nigel McKenna · Executive Estate Manager at Stephen A. Schwarzman
Success Academy gets a 15% management fee on school expenses. If an average school has 400 enrollments and the operating cost is $13,500 per pupil (400X13,500 = $5,400,000) X 15% management fee = $810,000 to Eva Moskowitz’s Corporate Office.


NYC DOE notified PS 122Mamie Fay in Astoria, Queens last week that they plan to remove the 30 year old Gifted & Talented program by 2019 to a different school in Long Island City, making that school an all chartered school. It is obvious that someone in the DOE is getting paid off to make this happen. Why would anyone destroy a district to gentrify one neighborhood? PS 122Mamie Fay School is not only one of the best schools in NYC & NYS; it is also a flagship school.


For about 30 years now The Academy for the Intellectually Gifted, which is located inside P.S. 122 (21-21 Ditmars Blvd), has produced some of New York's brightest minds. The numbers speak for themselves. Over 90% of these kids attend schools such as Stuyvesant H.S., Bronx H.S. of Science, Hunter College H.S., Townsend Harris, and Bard H.S Early College. Almost all get into screened program. It is important to note that the vast majority of these students come from immigrant families that are either low or lower middle income households.


Much can be said about The Academy's track record. However, in our times, the city loves to talk about "data", so we'll share a couple of hard data points so you can get an even better picture. If you look at the top ten middle schools in NYC, The Academy at 122 is the only one where 100% of the students meet or exceed the standards in English Language Arts and Mathematics. Also, it is the only school where 100% of the kids get to take high school courses and 100% of them actually receive credit for those courses. In other words, when it comes to high school readiness, The Academy is doing an amazing job.


As a result of the aforementioned successes, the Department of Education has decided on phasing out this outstanding and time tested school. Starting next year, any incoming kindergarten student will be allowed to stay through grade 8 despite the fact that the building at 21-21 Ditmars Blvd has never been a K-8 school. At the same time, the number of Academy classes will have to shrink from 11 to 3! Additionally, the building's population will increase by at least 50% which will have a severe impact on programs such as Art, Health, Dance, etc. As one member of the Community Education Council 30 pointed out, this will also mean that kids at 122 will have to start eating lunch at around 9:30 A.M. due to the scheduling nightmare that will be created. In other words, this will be a disaster for all children, whether they are in the gifted program or not.


We need everyone's help with trying to stop this monstrosity. This is bad for our community in so many ways. However, another Community Education Council member made a great point. By forcibly converting the 122 building into a K-8, and ostensibly killing the gifted program there, the local Junior H.S., 141 on 23rd avenue, will lose a great number of students. The extra space that will be created there is actually just the right amount for a charter school opening. What a surprise.


This point is re-iterated by another gentleman, John Collins, who is a Queens College professor. This is yet another example of NYC politicians closing off educational opportunities for middle class NYers by privatizing public education...they're trying to clear a space for a charter school at IS141...it's pretty obvious. As an educator and former 122 parent I can say clearly that right now 122 does perhaps the best job of preparing non-elite students for NYC's specialized high schools. By destroying our Academy they're simply reserving seats for Manhattan's elite...and they're doing it by hitting the kids early in life so they have no chance to catch up or learn what the masters of the universe have the $$ to buy. Sad. But that's our city today. No wonder middle class people leave Astoria/Jackson Heights/LIC for the suburbs...


School Background:
Based on the 2012, NY State Math & ELA test results, PS 122 Mamie Fay is Astoria is has a grade A student performance rate. The P.S. 122 Mamie Fay has a very Popular Gifted & Talented program and an extremely successful middle school with creative instruction and projects for all students. P.S. 122 is a neighborhood school drawn from the district and to a highly selective middle school called The Academy. With almost 1,400 students, the school is large, but teachers and staff pay keen attention to student needs which include classes for English language learners and special education services.


PS 122Mamie Fay School is located in a working class section of Astoria amid well-kept row houses and trendy eateries and shops. The school's early 20th century red brick building is clean and well-maintained. The school is affiliated with Columbia University's Teachers College (TC), follows its writing curriculum, and gets support from TC staff developers. Beginning in kindergarten, students write and revise multiple drafts of nonfiction, realistic fiction, and memoirs. The school uses the same curriculum for its G&T and general education classes.
Beyond core academics and gym, elementary grade students enjoy art and music classes. Fifth graders take ballroom dancing. Middle school students take art, dance, music, and health classes and enjoy weekly clubs such as theater, art, music and gardening.

Hi folks,

I attended the meeting on Wednesday night and am behind you 100%.  It isn't about Gifted and Talented vs. General Education - it's about keeping an incredibly effective K-5 school that provides students with a rock solid foundation for their futures.   

PS 122Q reminds me very much of my childhood K-5 elementary school in New Jersey - a warm, safe space where my classmates and I learned how to be creative, caring, curious, compassionate and collaborative.  Those dispositions were nurtured by the caring teachers of my youth and are central to who I am as an adult. (Don't get me wrong - my love of reading, writing, programming, math, social studies, trumpet playing and science were also nurtured in elementary school!)

Cutting the science labs, computer lab, library and art room are unacceptable, especially in this day and age where STEM (science, technology, engineering and math) education and the development of critical thinking, reading and writing skills are vital to future success no matter what field a student goes into.   

My overall idea, which I'm putting out there for anyone to use and expand upon, is to fight data with data. The PowerPoint slide with the 102% capacity vs. 129% capacity needs to be expanded upon and supported by detailed data collection, hopefully before the CEC 30 meeting on March 21st.     

Questions I have after the meeting:

-Why does Chancellor Walcott believe students should have the right to go K-8 at the same school?
-What peer-reviewed academic and quantitative research informed the NYC DOE decision?
-Where else has this happened in NYC?
-Does the DOE realize one size doesn't fit all communities?
-Isn't there some cognitive and developmental growth that takes place when students move to a new school?

I was shocked to hear that the DOE has not visited the school to see how space (including the reading room in the closet mentioned during the public comment period) at PS 122 is used. This is where I see the biggest opportunity to make a strong case to the DOE: their data about PS 122Q is old and doesn't reflect the 102% capacity of the building. (I'm also thinking about the teacher who mentioned she has 10 students in a classroom designed/slotted for 8.) 
   
-What is the NYC DOE Blue Book?
-What types of data does it contain?
-How old is that data?
-What was the methodology used to collect and analyze the data? 
-Can the parents of PS 122 in particular and other schools come up with a data collection tool and method to
ascertain when and how classrooms are used?

Here's what I'm thinking: a floorplan of each room (including labs, library, cafeteria and gymnasium) with the placement of furniture and other classroom components.

The floorplan would be paired with a logbook that records four pieces of data:
-time of day(in ten minute increments) This recording would begin the moment the school is opened and end when the afterschool program ends for the day.
-number of adults in the room
-number of kids in the room
-type of activity (example: 4th grade science lesson, 2nd grade science lab/microscopes)
-photos (of the empty classroom/spaces - no kids) would accompany the data for each room/space.

This data would then be compared to the Blue Book data/benchmarks, perhaps through some sort of data visualization or graphic design.

-What triggers an Educational Impact Study (EIS)?
-What are the components of an EIS?
-How can PS 122 parents and others conduct a fair EIS equivalent?

-We need to find and have access to the data the NYC DOE uses. What are the enrollment trends here in District 30?
-What about with PS 122 and the other schools here in Astoria?
-Why was 2019 chosen as the implementation date? Six years is around the corner.
-We need to find out the DOE methodology and, if applicable, the algorithms used to predict enrollment.

-We also need to find out as many details as possible about their decision making process. What is their timeline? Who is involved and how? What is the process by which decisions are made?  What are the checks and balances in place? Where is the transparency?

-We need to connect with schools in Brooklyn that have had this happen to them. I'm thinking about this article in the Village Voice: 
http://www.villagevoice.com/2013-01-30/news/Eva-Moskowitz-Bloomberg-Charter-Schools/

-We can also look here for background/ideas:
http://www.villagevoice.com/related/to/Success+Academy/

Finally, we need to explore the possible decision to make PS 122 a K-8 school in relation to two things: the Success Academy letter of intent
for District 30 and the plans for new housing along the waterfront in Hallets Cove (26th Ave and 1st Street).  Is the DOE's decision influenced by those two issues?

I hope this helps!
