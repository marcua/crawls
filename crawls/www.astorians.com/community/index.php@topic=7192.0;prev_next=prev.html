Biking basics for the 'hood
« on: July 21, 2007, 06:37:45 PM »
Well, there seem to be quite a few people around here who *want* to ride more but are just too nervous.

I enjoy riding in the city, even worked briefly as a messenger for a few weeks just to give it a try, and they gave an orientation before setting employees loose on the streets. Here's some of what they told us (and no messengers were ever killed while working for their company, BTW) mixed with some of my own advice:

- Ride sober. Being drunk or high might give you confidence, but you're not riding as well as you *think* you are, and your reaction time is hindered. Apparently this doesn't go without saying! A couple of drinks is one thing, just don't go nuts.

- You are not "IN" traffic; you "ARE" traffic! Dammit, you've got the legal right to take up a car's worth of space on the road. Don't abuse this just to be obnoxious, though; pull to the side and let drivers pass when you can. But if for whatever reason you can't, don't let their honking change your mind.

- Be considerate. It's illegal to ride the sidewalks, but there are times when the sidewalks are soooo wide and empty, and the traffic sooo dense and angry.....Crescent Street comes to mind! I say when you're freaked out enough, bail onto the sidewalk and be careful of pedestrians....

...*especially* toddlers and kids- they are completely unpredictable and will toddle into your path without a care in the world. Make sure you slow down well in advance so the parents don't think bikers are baby-killing jerks. Oh yeah, and always be polite- I'm always saying "sorry" and "thanks" even if the other person wasn't so nice. When in doubt, walk. No shame in walking.

- Obey the rules of the traffic.   {{{sigh}}} okay, what fun would biking be if we did that 100% of the time? As a beginner, ride like the :police: are watching; as you gain confidence, look both ways before you blow lights and stop signs. Don't cut people off or flaut it- startling and aggrivating drivers doesn't help our cause, but if the coast is clear, just be careful. I can't guarantee you won't get ticketed or yelled at. It's up to you. The more predictable you are, the better.

- Watch for doors. Nobody looks behind them when they exit their car, so you have to be defensive. As you ride by all those hundreds of cars, keep a wide berth (about three feet) and glance through the cars' back windows to see if anybody's inside. When it comes to cabs, listen for the ticking of the receipt printer- a sure sign the door's gonna open at any moment. Be especially careful approaching vans and trucks, whose doors are at head-level!  :ugly: Try to spot any potentially-exiting drivers by glancing in their side-view mirrors, or simply by keeping your distance.

- Predict the future. Check the front wheels to see if a vehicle is turning toward the street and about to leave the parking spot. When you see pedestrians crossing, make your intentions clear by steering wide of them, preferably behind them. If they see/hear you approaching, they will stop like a deer in the headlights!

I find it's best if they can't hear me at all, and I can steer around them; however, they will sometimes do an about-face in the middle of the street or something else unexpected and throw me off- doesn't happen often but just be aware that it can.

If you see a pedestrian hailing a cab, pay attention! A couple of cabs could be behind you, ready to barrel in his/her direction. You're in between them and their $$$.  :-o

Driveways are another hazard, sometimes drivers barrel out of them w/o looking (esp. if you're riding the wrong way up a one-way street- they're not looking your way at all.) So be especially careful when coming up on driveways, gas stations, parking lots, etc.

It's nice if you hit a "wave" of lights that carries you for many blocks. Keep an eye on the pedestrian signals to gauge how much time you have to cross: Eight blinks of the hand before the light turns yellow; you have about five seconds after the blinking stops to make it across. If it's a one-way cross-street and you have the room, cross to the opposite side of the street to get in that *extra* second- it'll take that long for the cars to get moving again (again, be wary if a moving car in another lane is coasting through, always look!)

- Watch for potholes etc. This is why I prefer a front suspension mtb over a rigid road bike- I feel ready for the obstacles. Some of those potholes are at least a foot deep.

If you're headed for something and it's too late to avoid it, put your pedals in the 3 o'clock and 9 o'clock position (or if you can't, just stand a little in your pedals and a bit off your seat so you absorb the shock with your knees.) Give the handlebars a sharp tug upward to help the front tire clear the obstacle more easily. For a big obstacle, like a curb, pull up on the bars and push down your highest foot for a little leverage. As long as you can keep your front tire rolling and prevent it from turning sharply, you should be fine. Practice these helpful techniques in the park.

Also watch for water drains that have the long, lengthwise holes in the grates. Most of them are small enough to accomodate a tire; a few have holes big enough to grab one! Those big metal plates are normally okay, but very slippery when wet. When going over one, just don't hit the brakes or change speed/turn suddenly. Same goes for sand and oil slicks.

Security: If you plan on locking up the bike outside for any length of time, have the bike shop chain the seat to the frame, and remove the front tire and lock it with the frame and rear tire to a reliable object (not a tree!) For Queens, the U-locks seem okay; in the city, don't bother with anything but a Kryptonite Quad or New York Chain (thick oval-ish links) with a U-lock as a backup.

By "reliable object," I mean bike racks or street signs. Make sure the sign is well-cemented into the ground, otherwise a thief could shimmy the sign out of the dirt and slip the lock off the bottom. A small tree could be cut down (and damaged by your lock); a parking meter is too short; chain-link fences can be cut; scaffolding is fine BUT ask yourself if part of it could be unbolted quickly. It also helps to have a generic, cheap, and/or crappy bike with the brand obscured. Only my old bike gets locked up anywhere; my other bikes are either under me, in the car or in the same building as I am.

It helps to remember that the average speed of city traffic is around 10mph, so keeping up isn't that hard! Around here in Queens, it's another story, but the same rules apply.

Mandatory equipment:

  • Helmet
    Lock
    Noisemaker (bell, buzzer, airhorn- choice is yours.)
    Basic toolkit (spare tube, minipump, patches, tire levers, quarters/money, emergency contact info, MetroCard*  )

For more ambitious or frequent riding/commuting:

  • Water bottle or CamelBak
    Sunglasses
    Lights (white for front; red for back- the red lights can be found really cheap at most street fairs, like $2 or $3.)
    Panniers/rack or something like a messenger bag to haul stuff (my co-worker recently broke a wrist and chipped some teeth when his grocery bag swung into his front tire.)
    Comfy clothes (that wick sweat and pad your fanny and cushion your hands and make your pedaling more efficient.)
    Snack (just in case you "bonk," aka run out of energy.)
    Street or bike map

*When you bring your bike on the subway, make sure it's not rush hour. The technique is to alert the person in the booth that you'll need to go in via the service gate, swipe the card, turn the turnstile manually, then enter through the gate.

No permit needed for the subway; $5 lifetime permit needed for the LIRR (obtain at the Penn Station ticket booth) + check the schedule for bike-friendly hours. MetroNorth has a different permit, probably works about the same. The average railroad commuter hates bikes, so keep out of their way by standing near the doors and being humble.

Okay, so this is probably more info than most of you need/want, BUT there it is for reference. I'll be happy to do a beginner ride, just post if you're interested. Most weeknights are good 5:30-9; weekends it depends.

Riding around here can be hairy, but it's a blast and a great way to get stimulating exercise. The more you do it, the more routine it becomes, and the faster you can go. Beats the indoor bike that's for sure!
