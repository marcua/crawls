HOW TO FORM AN ANTI-NOISE GROUP IN YOUR COMMUNITY

Taking the Necessary Steps

 Get Organized: The noise in your neighborhood must also be disturbing your neighbors. Knock on some doors, speak to neighbors in the stores, and set up a small meeting in someone's home. Get yourselves a name, e.g. Citizens Fighting Community Noises; elect some officers, add some well-known names to your advisory board, secure a mailing address, and print formal stationery.

 Be Prepared to Do A Lot of the Work Yourself: You may find that it's only a few of you that will do the bulk of the work - expect that. However, you can always get lots of signatures on petitions to give the impression that your group is large. Remember there must be a We to battle noise.

 Take Advantage of Existing Community Groups: Some communities have block associations, local community boards or other existing groups that may welcome a sub-group on noise. You may be able to attach your group to this existing organization. In that case, the existing group should allow you to use their already formalized stationery.

 Become Knowledgeable About the Noise Problem, the Effects of Noise, and Potential Solutions. You will have to do a great deal of homework to be able to discuss the noise problem intelligently. Document the noise problem: keep logs of noise occurrences including time, date, and duration. You can also purchase an inexpensive decibel meter to measure how loud the noise is. Know whether there are laws that can be used to correct the noise problem. Seek out possible solutions. Remember you may end up knowing more about the noise problem than the public officials or enforcement agencies.

 Go to the Media: Publicize the noise problem. Create events to highlight the problem. Send out press releases to all the media, e.g. radio, television, daily newspapers. Don't forget the small local community newspapers that may give you more coverage. Work hard at getting publicity for the problem.

 Enlist Your Public Officials: Your local public officials, e.g. council members or aldermen, will be more accessible. Enlist public officials in your efforts to quiet the noise and educate them about the problem. Ask them to join the noise group and add their names to your stationery.

 Discover Which Agencies May Be Able to Tackle the Problem: Is it the Police Department, the Transit Authority, the Department of Environmental Protection, or the Buildings Department? Write a letter on your impressive stationery to the appropriate agency asking for a meeting on the problem. Have representatives from your group and local representatives attend this meeting.

 Hold Community Meetings on the Noise Problem: Ask agency representatives, local public officials and knowledgeable group members to speak to the community about the problem and possible solutions. Publicize your meetings - you want large turnouts.

[quote="merm" Whether the most effective remedy be through legal means, or another approach has yet to be put forward in a concise way on this forum.[/quote]

There is no way to pose an effective remedy.  The "problem" is simply one of one's annoyance level.  There are lots of annoying things in life.  We all have sets of them.  In many cases, our sets overlap in many areas with others thereby forming a societal norm.  In other cases we just have individual pet peeves that bother only a small segment of society or perhaps even an individual.

Mobile phone technology is really in its infancy and people are still exploring how to deal with it.  There is no standard norm for any society yet.  It will take years for those conventions to mature and evolve.

You can't honestly legislate poor choices people make any more than you could legislate the obnoxious loud guy at the party away or two people sitting next to each other on the subway talking loudly and obnoxiously to each other.

I'd propose that instead of trying to alter other people's behavior so much that working on strategies which help us control our frustration would be more successful.  Being rude generally only begets rudeness from others.  The only people shaming and rudeness affect are those who are inherently not rude but are making an honest mistake by forgetting one's surroundings.  Those who weren't raised with the same sense of propriety as ourselves will not get the message and will probably become offended.
