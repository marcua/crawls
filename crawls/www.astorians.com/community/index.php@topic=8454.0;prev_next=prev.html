Quote from: essen on October 30, 2007, 01:54:32 AM
Not sure if it's true, but the argument for Trap/Neuter/Release is that it prevents the feral cat population from increasing and it will slowly dwindle away. If you kill off the present cats, they will merely be replaced by more cats for whatever reason the cats were there in the first place. But if sterile cats are around, they won't multiply, and they'll also keep other cats away as cats are very territorial.

This doesn't really make sense to me. According to this scenario, you have a neutered/spayed feral cat population that scares away other cats - where does it scare them off to? Somewhere where people will be happy to have them and won't think they are a problem? And what happens when the neutered/spayed feral cat population dies off --- how are the new cats kept away then?

And what happens in the meantime? Cats can live for upwards of 20 years. Feral cats probably have a shorter life span, but for whatever reason they are considered a problem now, they won't stop being problems because they are sterilized.

And - once the feral cat population starts to dwindle - won't there be a decrease in territorial activity, as there will be fewer cats to defend the territory? And won't the cat population just grow again?

here are a few sites/news items I pulled up:

National Geographic: US. Faces Growing Feral Cat Problem
The ABC's Winter, who is vocal in the battle between wildlife groups and feral advocates, says she opposes the TNR method because the released cats continue to kill wildlife. Another problem, she said, is that feeding stations maintained by caregivers attract animals like raccoons and skunks that carry rabies and other diseases, creating a public health threat.

The Race To Outpace Feral Cat Over-population
Many people think pets can regain their so-called "natural" instincts and hunt to survive if they are abandoned or lost. The truth is - they can't. Their lives are a grim struggle to survive in back alleys or in rural areas on whatever scraps of food they can beg or steal. Unless they are cared for by a feeder, most die young from disease, starvation, abuse, and accidents - or die violently as food for a predator. The ones that are lucky enough to end up in shelters are usually put to death. Shelters are forced to kill millions of homeless cats each year at a hefty tax bill for citizens, and at an untold emotional burden to shelter workers.

Wikipedia: Feral cat colony
A feral cat colony is a population (or "clowder") of feral domestic cats (not to be confused with wild cats). Members of a feral cat colony can include cats that have strayed after living with human caretakers as well as their offspring, which have had little human contact or none at all. Established feral colonies are located worldwide.

Alley Cat Allies
