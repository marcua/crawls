 :-)

I've been a Manhattan Mini customer since 2004. I've got stuff stashed at their 44th St. facility on the West side.  Come back from a shoot at 3 AM? No problem, it's open. Need to get anywhere in NYC or out of NYC? You're at midtown and bridge and tunnel options are super accessible. Clean and safe facility. Very cost-effective and reliable. Can't recommend'em highly enough.

The ad? :mrgreen:

Of course, on principle, one should be in favor of any person or company using his/her/its money to express itself on any issue in any way whatsoever that isn't fraudulent or coercive. On a political/legal level, I'm an advocate of the free flow of ideas, all of them. Of course, on a moral level, it's important to take stands against objectionable content, to boycott, to shame, to expose, to challenge, to debate. As long as we never let our zeal push us to call for government to shut people up or ban ideas.

A brief aside:

In college, my school's newspaper decided to run an ad submitted by a Holocaust revisionist group. The paper claimed they were justified in doing this by the First Amendment. I debated that notion, holding instead that, as a journalistic enterprise, they had a responsibility not to run ads that are, in fact, maliciously misleading. I argued that they should have used the ad as a starting point for an investigation into Holocaust revisionism, assigning the story to writers, perhaps commenting on it editorially. But I considered it negligent to run the ad as if it were as valid as any of the other ads on the same page, one from Dunkin Donuts and another from a study-abroad program focusing on Israel. Caused quite a stir at Brandeis, you can imagine.

I was accused on being anti free speech by very confused people. Nobody has a right to express themselves at the involuntary expense of anyone else. Newspapers have a moral responsibility to ensure that their advertising meets certain standards. I'd say the naked advocacy of racial hatred is something no newspaper should ever let itself be in the position of knowingly advertising. Newspapers are definitely NOT required by some kind of First Amendment imperative to publish every ad they are offered. 

Cheers to Manhattan Mini! Even if the ad is primarily there to create buzz, at the end of the day, they staked out a position that will be unpopular enough to lead to boycotts, and apparently the owners are betting that the positive benefits will outweigh the negative consequences. Risky? Yes. Courageous? Yes.
