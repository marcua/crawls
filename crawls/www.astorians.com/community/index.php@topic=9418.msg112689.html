I will quote Electric Light Orchestra by saying "It's a livin' thing."  I have an aversion to the possibility of being killed.  I'm not comfortable with government regulation, but I believe that there is a looming threat of terrorism and that we have to take reasonable steps to prevent it.  If we don't take such steps, we'll be watching another blue ribbon panel on C-Span about what went wrong.

Plus, I think it is a matter of when I am willing to have faith in government and when I am not.  I believe that the primary role of government is to keep you and me safe in the immediate physical sense so that we can live our lives and own our property.  If some guy gets stopped and asked why he is taking pictures of the Indian Point power plant, and the cop asks a few follow up questions, that is not a problem.  If the guy hasn't done anything demonstrably wrong and gets thrown in a secret prison, that is a HUGE problem for me.  I believe in transparency, and I don't think it's right that there are apparently secret lists and so forth.

I feel most threatened by government when they try to curtail individual liberties.  For example, I have a real problem with the increasing number of restrictions on firearm ownership, which apply to lawful citizens, but not the criminal element.  I have a problem with the philosophy of redistribution of wealth as opposed to creation of wealth.  But I am not against security programs that are executed in a transparent and common sense way.  We all need to keep the government in check to make sure that is what goes on, and conversations like this are part of that process.

In the example you cite, Officer Jones might approach the guy, ask him why he was taking those pictures and have a chat with him.  He might in the course of the conversation ask if he could step inside.  He might notice that the guy has nothing but canned foods and a mattress on the floor, the hallmarks of a safe house.  He might run the guys name and find out that he has aliases in other places or is wanted for skipping his expired visa.  There are lots of things you can find out just by taking the initiative to ask the right questions.  Another example.  Some of the 9/11 hijackers got pulled over in NJ for a routine traffic stop.  If the cop had known to look for fake or multiple drivers licenses, or had access to the right intelligence sharing, he could have nabbed the guy, who were already being checked out by the FBI.

And I respect your views as well, though we often fail to agree.  I would much rather discuss ideas and respectfully disagree than blindly thumb people down, or in an offline context, shout over them.  I think there's value in these kinds of discussions because we are each forced to strengthen our ideas.
