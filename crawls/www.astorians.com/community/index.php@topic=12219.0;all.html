Just as a side note, from my understanding of the subject, the state did not limit the franchaise to property owners.  Rather, the state was a collection of property owners and having property gave one the right to participate (a publically traded corporation is a fair modern analogy).  The owning of land historically meant the ability to serve in the military, which was the basis for the establishment and maintence of the political units.  As political units moved away from defining its wealth strictly though land, the franchaise opened up to varying degrees of wealth, not because of any presumption of business acumen or intelligence, but rather that the wealth makes one a part of the state. As anything else, the political realities did not always keep pace with the socio-economic realities, especially as class divisions became entrenched and ossified.

I'm reading a pretty good book now which is at home, but it talks specifically about Madison's worry about the "tyranny of the masses" and that the constitution was specifically a measure to stop what was becoming runaway democracy.  Its such an interesting topic.
