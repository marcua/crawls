There is one small musical instrument store on 30th Avenue between 35th and 36th Streets, Astoria Music.

FWIW, The guy that runs Astoria Music is a whiz at guitar repair!  A couple of years ago, I found a beautiful 1959 Kaye arch top acoustic guitar in the trash a few blocks from here.  I thought I would take it home with little expectations of it being playable--it wasn't.  I took it down to Astoria Music and invested a couple of Benjies to have the neck reset and buy a new bridge.  It was money well spent, the guitar plays like a dream, has a great tone, and is now worth about US$900.  It's amazing what people here in Astoria throw away!
  When I first moved here I found an antique bazouki with an ebony fingerboard and mother of pearl inlays in similar circumstances.  It was obviously "well played" over the years, but it was still in pretty good shape.  All I had to do to make it playable was to buy a new set of strings and a 10 cent nail to keep the tail piece in place.   
  My only regret :-)--and a F--in' great NYC moment--was the Greek hausfrau yelling at me as I walked past her apartment on a later day chastising me for picking through her garbage.  "You like our trash!  You should be ashamed!"  I just laughed and told her to keep it coming...

Quote from: essen on June 03, 2008, 12:44:03 AM
Well, it supports the authors, right? Do authors get any kind of compensation for their books being taken out of the library?

Libraries buy a lot of their books, and if a book is really popular they may buy several copies of one book. They also replace stolen books. So an author could potentially sell 15 or so copies to one library, maybe more.

Also, authors benefit indirectly from libraries. There are several authors I would never have stumbled over at Barnes and Nobel, who I did find at the library purely by mistake, and have since bought their books and raved over them and got my friends to read them. So yes, even though it's not measureable, there is a benefit.
