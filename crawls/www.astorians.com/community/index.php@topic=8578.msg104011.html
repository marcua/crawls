Basically the dollar bills in your wallet are a promise from the Federal Government that they will honor the value printed on them. They back that promise with hard currency reserves like the gold locked away in Fort Knox. However -- and I might be mistaken -- I believe that even though your bill says $10, the government is not completely beholden to honor that promise to give you 'ten dollars' should you demand it.

In other words (though this is highly unlikely here, but it has happened in other countries), the government can say that they don't have enough gold to back up that 10-spot, along with the gajillion others in people's pockets, and make it of less real value. Like $8.50, or $3.12 or what have you. That's when people start dumping (er, exchanging) US currency for either another one (Yen, Deutschmarks, Pounds Sterling, etc.) or for literally cold hard cash -- ie gold and silver.

But still, your average American keeps the faith with the government that the dollar is worth something. Otherwise, folks would be ditching it for fear of it collapsing entirely. YOu'd see rampant devaluation and the dollar would become something like the Russian Ruble in the 1990s or the Brazilian Real in the 1980s. IMO, faith in the dollar not near breaking point, but we're approaching it and I fear most of us will be shocked when it does happen.  :|

The value of what that guy was minting wasn't based on a promise of value, but an actual, measurable market value at a given time for the metals contained. I've been trying to learn more about the history, beliefs, and doings of our fore fathers and, seriously, Sam Adams is one bad mo fo.  I'm reading a book about him, Radical of the Revolution, and I have to say that I'm very impressed.

Also, many of the things he rallied people about and the obstacles he faced are the same things that we have problems with now.  There are a few quotes I've found already that make me think of today's issues, and I'm only about 1/3 of the way through the book.  He strikes me as being very similar in views as Ron Paul.

"I'm tired of hearing that we cannot change the status quo! I'll never believe that! Not when there are so many intelligent people in this country. Not when they could be made to realize that they are losing more and more of their freedom as time goes by. If they could just be awakened to how dangerous Parliament is; if they could just come to know how easy it would be for the aristocrats to take all their liberties away! Then surely people would rise up and force Parliament to guarantee their freedom. Someone must make the people understand how great their power is! Someone must teach them that they alone have the power to govern!"

This quote makes me think so much of now.  I know so many that are unhappy with the current situation and not too impressed with the "front-runners," but it just seems so futile to go another direction.  Just think about us, even.  Many support Paul but don't think he's got a chance.  WHY?

When Adams was talking about why he and his "workingman's" group has been unsuccessful at making any headway against the Court party, "The governor has every avenue of communication open to him: his public proclamations, the courts, the legislature, the newspapers. We have nothing but our tongues, and I know now that persuasive talk alone is not enough."

Again, this makes me think of so many things.  Paul's inability to receive as much "face-time" as the other candidates.  On top of that, it makes me think of the stranglehold the government and the front-runners seem to have on the media.

Another one that hits home to our economic conditions now. He talks about suggesting that the government investigate the great fortunes made in the past and currently. "Would such an investigation not reveal that many such fortunes have been made at the expense of the public?"

This makes me think of the oil companies, the banks, Haliburton, etc. that have all prospered while the average American citizen has been trampled on.

It's amazing to me how history repeats itself and how, even 250 years ago, things really haven't changed when it comes to power struggles and government.
