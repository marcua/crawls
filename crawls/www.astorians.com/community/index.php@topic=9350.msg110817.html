Lately I've been noticing a large number of out-of-state license plates parking on my street -- Tennessee, Alabama, North Carolina, Texas, and Massachusetts, in addition to the usual Connecticut and New Jersey. These are cars that park here every day and every night -- it's not like they're just visiting. Isn't there a law that requires you get NY plates within a certain amount of time after moving to NY?

I know there are many more important things to grouse about, but when I spend 45 minutes driving around in circles looking for a parking spot when no less than 10 spots on my block are typically taken up by out-of-state cars, it gets to be incredibly frustrating. There have always been a few cars with Connecticut and NJ plates, but lately (within the last few months) it just seems like there's this huge influx of out-of-state cars. I wonder, are they just becoming more lax about enforcing the "must get NY plates" rule?

Oooh, I had out-of-state plate drama.

When I moved here from Connecticut about 2.5 years ago, I was a full time student and interning, so before I moved, I asked my insurance agent, and he told me that I didn't need to register my car in NY because I wasn't working. I would have if I thought I needed to. Of course, when I got rear-ended and had to file a claim, all hell broke loose about the whole out-of state thing, even though I had the car towed back to CT because that's where I "officially" (according to paperwork) lived. They found out (through credit reports, or the fact that my name is on the cable bill) that I had an apartment in Astoria, and tried to charge me more money for it, and my agent even called me a liar and threatened legal action (?!) when I mentioned that he told me I didn't need to switch my registration. (I know I couldn't have misunderstood him - I have an email exchange saying "I'm moving to NY to go to school, and I'm taking my car with me. Do I need to switch my registration to NY?" He replied "no, as long as you aren't employed in NY, you don't have to register your car. It's like someone living in a dorm from out of state." Now I realize he only told me that because he wanted to keep his commission.) Then when I did switch my registration, I called that company's office here in Astoria, the guy who answered flat out refused to help me unless I could come in the next day at 2. (I couldn't, I had class. When I said, "2 tomorrow isn't good for me," he replied, "Well, I don't work over the phone, so if you can't come in tomorrow at 2, I can't help you.") Needless to say, I'm no longer with that company. (And they're a big national company! "On your side" my butt.)

ANYWAY.

It actually turns out that it's cheaper for me to have my car registered in NY than it was in CT - CT has a yearly car tax, and my insuance here is actually a little bit less than it was there (although I am obviously with a different insurance company, and my deductible is a bit higher. I have made a couple small claims though, and I'm still happy with them). I will say that that I'm from (and my car was registered in) Bridgeport, where insurance and tax rates are pretty high.
