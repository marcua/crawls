Quote from: adamwc on September 26, 2007, 02:35:07 PM
At some point, the people he is hiring gain the moral right to dictate the terms of their employment to him? No, I don't think so.
As long as the persuasion and compromises involved are voluntary, the consequences should be legal.
The government should just stay the hell out of our way.

The hold that capital represents the only legitimate moral power has been outdated since the days when only property owners were the ones who went off to war, but we can still be confuse it with the workplace because of our system of private property.  Labor always has the moral right to equally participate in determining terms of employment, be it one worker or a hundred workers.  Choosing to bargain collectively only represents a point wherein labor decides to negotiate with their employer as a unit.

Any negotiations are voluntary, it is the union membership which is not.  Its pretty easy to see that if labor is not unified, they cannot bargain on equal terms with capital.  This is why labor dispise scabs and this is why capital is always trying to foster scabs by pushing for open shop union laws.  Does one think they do so because they are genuinely concerned about the workers themselves?  Binding arbitration is only required when the contract which capital and labor negotiated requires such, or if the workers are in a field which are banned from striking.

The government does stay out of the way.  Unions are not hand outs imposed on companies by the government.  They are fought for by the workers themselves.  All the government does is provide laws to stabilize the process, akin to the regulations on driving, construction and zoning (and presently these laws are in favor of the capital, if historical comparisons are made between the Wagner Act and the Taft-Hartley Act)
