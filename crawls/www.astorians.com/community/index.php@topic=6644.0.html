The Effectiveness of the Community Board
« on: June 12, 2007, 05:51:03 PM »
I have read a number of threads related to the overcrowded conditions in Astoria and the lack of services.  This inspired me to attend a Community Board Meeting.  This is what occured:

I got up and read a three minute statement about how the infrastructure of Astoria has not kept up with the development and population explosion which has occurred over the last decade.

One of the leaders (who shall remain nameless because it was so embarrassing) pointed out that there are so many more people living here now.  That is all he had to say.

Someone finally spoke up and questioned why nothing has been done to focus on this issue.  This seemed to be a revelation to the group and a discussion began to ensue.

Someone pointed out that our zoning dates back to the sixties and that we are classified as a 'low density zone'!!!  This has NOT changed since the sixties, and no one has done anything about it.

To me, this is a key issue and we must encourage the Board to get this changed.  We are high density now.  We need additional resources.

The next meeting is being held on Tuesday, June 19th at 7:00 p.m. at the Astoria World Manor and there will not be another meeting until September. 

Would anyone be willing to speak out about this issue?  If they hear it from numerous individuals perhaps it will sink in!  Keep in mind that all of our services (electricity - hence the black-out last year, transportation, schools etc.) are based on our low density classification.

Who is willing to address this at their next meeting???

Actually the community boards are very powerful and even though they are an advisory role, everyone hates to cross them.

If you have a chance to go to a community board meeting in another neighborhood or borough, you will immediately see the contrast between how they conduct their affairs, and our local board's behavior, the sort of [pressing?!] things the local board discusses, and how they treat the public.

The experience is striking. Try it and share your experiences on this board.

As to zoning, the city is divided into different classifications depending on density. For example, residential areas are R-1, R-2, etc. Why doesn't someone look up how Astoria is zoned, read the captions, speculate on how much each block is allowed in development, and of course, contrast that with what is here already?

Now think how Astoria will be different once it is developed up to full zoning. Think of how resources will have to be ramped up to service this level. 

The other useful exercise is to take a look at population within the community board, say 1970, 1990, and projected out to 2030. Now take a look at hospitals, police, etc and contrast the growth of those numbers with the population.

Has services kept pace with population growth to date? Does it make sense to add to the population with that track record?

Should we not catch up before we add to the density?
