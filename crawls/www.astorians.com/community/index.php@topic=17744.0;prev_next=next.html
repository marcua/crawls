Sarah, I can tell you from my own personal experience that while you are planning your future, life will get in the way.

Your own gut instinct is probably the best way to go—as long as your plans are in pencil!

I paid a Certified Financial Planner who laid out my retirement for me. It really was a wonderful plan. First Enron took a dive into the cesspool and then the market crashed. I was devastated; emotionally and financially. When I hit rock bottom I started reading Suze Orman. She makes so much sense.

Your financial future is not at all like finding a doctor or a good restaurant. If you've made a huge mistake it can cost you what you can't afford.

Put it all on paper. Get rid of any liability that includes interest that is not tax deductible, like that of a mortgage; use credit cards that require full payment each month; do not relinquish any credit card as the clearing houses do not differentiate the difference between being refused a card and your relinquishment; hopefully, you will only be getting married once, so make yourself the wedding you always dreamed of or you might regret it later in life; try to live for today so you will be well equipped to deal with tomorrow if you are flexible; never give up the little things in life that make you happy like the monthly mani/pedi or the weekly takeout; use grocery coupons auspiciously; comparison shop until you are comfortable that you are getting the best value and don't forget to look into Roth IRA savings.

I wish you all the luck you wish for yourself.

Congratulations Dr. Sarah.

Congratulations!! That is truly an accomplishment.  :mrgreen:

We resisted the temptation to elope and had a small-ish wedding (that is, by our families' standards- they did contribute. I hate the fuss and attention, but we did want to throw a tasteful party).

Looked at around 25 places (not counting the ones we read about online) and finally settled on a place near where the groom grew up, in Nassau County. Parking was important, as most of the guests were older and/or driving in, so that ruled out Queens and NYC (which were overpriced for what they offered anyway.)

We booked within the same year, which saved about 50%.

Ditto the photographer- no video, basic package (even that was a bit much for me- who the heck needs 500+ photos?!  :roll: )

Also went with a Sunday morning/afternoon- more savings. Basic menu. Open bar, though.

Ordered personalized favors online, pretty cheap.

Managed to meet the exact minimum of guests- 125.

Cousin was the officiant.

No bridal party- most of my close friends are out-of-state, and they have kids. Just ring bearer/flower girl/MOH/Best Man.

Dress was less than $1,000, including the alterations and accessories. Veil was ordered online. Wore old (but decent) silver shoes.

No limo- just a car service. Would've driven myself if my car weren't all crunched up and grungy!

I splurged a little on the flowers, as I'm not a white-rose kinda gal. Had to be custom. But I did have low vs high centerpieces.

Honeymoon was super-cheap: Regular hotels, hiked the Grand Canyon. Gives us an excuse to take a grander trip some other time, when we have time to plan!

Made our own invites and had them printed at Copy Com, although I'm not sure it was THAT much cheaper than the pre-made. Except there was no velum, ribbons and whatnot- just reply cards, directions and return envelopes.

You can have a nice park wedding then rent out a (small or local) restaurant for the evening. Or, elope to an exotic island, then have a nice dinner at a fancy place when you get back. Vegas has some really nice packages; even City Hall has re-done their on-site wedding area.

When it comes to finances, the bottom line is to not worry about peer pressure. There is no "right" way to have a wedding; it's all about what YOU both want. Ditto everything else in life.

NYCMac knows, there's no such thing as a "five-year plan," really just save where/whenever possible. I've been unemployed for much longer than I ever cared to be (that sure wasn't part of the plan!), but still managed to save a bit after all these years. Having to tap into my mutual funds was heartbreaking, though.

Keep us posted, I'd love to hear how the wedding turns out!  :-)

Get Rich Slowly is a good resource for reducing debt and saving money. I highly recommend it. Here's a post on getting out of debt.

I use ING direct for my savings and try to only carry a credit card on planned occasions. In ING direct you can set up different accounts for different savings goals - I personally find it very helpful to keep an emergency fund separate from my home-buying fund and my travel fund.

I'm not good at number crunching either. In the past I've cooked dinner for friends who are better at personal finances than me in exchange for their advice. But if I'm patient I can break things down - how much debt do I currently have, how much do I currently make, how much can I save, how much can I spend - and I do ok. Excel is your friend!

As far as the wedding goes- one of the most charming weddings I went to was incredibly simple. The only money spent was on the groom's suit and the bride's dress. No bridesmaids, no DJ, no flowers. But it was clear they loved each other and we were all happy for them. If you want to, there's nothing wrong with getting married at City Hall and then having a reception in your home. Give some thought to the amount of money that gets spent on the average wedding and how long it would take to pay that off and how much of that money could go towards a house payment. Or actually having a couch to sit on and eating something besides rice once you are actually married. Not that I'm biased :)

Like enigmacat said, the smaller weddings are often the most memorable and charming. I wanted so badly to make it smaller, but it was 75% groom's side anyway. It really is nuts that weddings cost so much these days.

I used to have CDs and mutual funds for investment purposes, but cashed out of both since the CDs are especially lousy these days. My mutual funds weren't doing much, figure those could be applied toward a house.

Be sure to start an IRA or some other retirement fund, and if you're lucky enough to have an employer that matches in a 401k, contribute the maximum allowed- that's free money after all! Did this, and even nearly a decade after losing the job, it's doing very nicely.

And don't be shy about asking for discounts. Many places will be helpful. Especially hotels- AAA? Student? Senior? Online? Coupon? Weekday? Gov't employee? Ask at the desk if you qualify for SOME kind of discount.

Learn to cook- loads of savings there. Use fresh produce vs premade meals. Even coffee made at home adds up.

And online coupons- always check before shopping anywhere.
