Perhaps your family were secret vegetarians, or maybe a dairy product was always served on the same occasion the tzimmes was made?  I'd feel deprived if there weren't any meat in it.  But it an absolutely brilliant use of prunes.

Now that I stop to think about it, tzimmes is a Russian-Jewish version of sambar, the difference being that sambar used tamarind instead of prunes, and as with your family's version of tsimmes, doesn't have meat.



No, tzimmes was always served on special occasions like Rosh Hashana or Sukkot where there wasn't usually dairy served. And I can guarantee that my family are far from vegetarians being that practically every meal had either a brisket, a london broil or some such "Jewish inflected" food. But with any food stuff, every family has its own way of preparing it. But, it's funny about the tzimmes, as a kid I loathed it, the prunes/raisin part of it, the warm mushy carrots. I used to think that that was what served in nursing homes after having seen one of my fathers much older cousin in one, eating tzimmes- and it was on Erev Sukkot! As an 8 yr old it grossed me out.

Huh?  The very first microwaves were marketed on the East Coast and in the Midwest in the late '40s, only a few years after the microwave was developed.  Commercial microwaves were first widely marketed in the US in the mid-50's, and the first home microwave was introduced in the US in 1967.  Maybe they became common in Canada earlier, but they were never banned here.
