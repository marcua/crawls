I like being a devil's advocate here...  Maybe I shoulda been a lawyer...

McDirk quoted in the other thread a letter from the NGLTF:

Quote
“The majority opinion offers utterly absurd reasons why the legislature could choose to limit marriage to opposite sex couples. For example, it says ‘the legislature could find that unstable relationships between people of the opposite sex present a greater danger that children will be born into or grow up in unstable homes than is the case with same-sex couples, and thus that promoting stability in opposite-sex relationships will help children more.’  If the results of this decision were not so profoundly hurtful, this kind of reaching – which permeates the entire opinion - would render it laughable.”

Again, I don't think the proper thing to do is to state that this rather convoluted arguement was the view of the court at all. Instead, they were trying to figure out whether there were legitimate arguments that the legislature could conceivably make that would pass constitutional muster. If there were any, they had to uphold the existing law. It appears that they identified two. Given that appellate judges are supposed to decide the law, not the facts, it would be inappropriate for the court to evaluate the validity of these arguments, I think. Based on how convoluted the justices were, I suspect that the majority would personally prefer for the law to be changed by the legislature. In some sense they were saying: "Look, here are the type of arguments you have to make to find that same-sex marriage should not be legal. By virtue of how convoluted the arguments we present are, and how empirically unlikely they are, we're kinda hinting where we think the legislature should go on this. But changing the law is not our job."
