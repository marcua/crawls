Quote from: kempsternyc on July 04, 2009, 12:27:52 AM
Hey Maurice, were these pics done in Raw? We're trying to figure out when to use that format as opposed to jpeg.

It's pretty simple actually. The advantage that RAW has over JPEG is that it basically allows you to adjust the white balance (overall color balance of the image) and the exposure (lightness or darkness of the overall image) by about +/- 1 ev stop during post processing. In contrast, JPEG records a hard setting for the colors and the exposure according to data compiled by fancy sensors and algorithms designed by the camera manufactures. In other words, the color and exposure in JPEG files can not easily be changed in post processing. To be quite honest, the cameras these days are pretty good at choosing the correct white balance and setting a correct exposure. But if the scene has a bunch of different light sources (florescent, incandescent, halogen, etc) and/or it has a lot of sharp contrasts between brightly lit areas and dim shadowy areas you would be wise to shoot in RAW and touch it up during post.

If a scene seems particularly tricky and I have a feeling that it will trip up the camera's sensors and algorithms then I usually shoot in RAW + JPEG mode. That way I get the best of both worlds, I have the camera's rendition as a starting point and I have the RAW file for flexibility in case the camera mungs it up. Just be sure you have at least a 4gb or 8gb card when using a 10mp or greater camera, but large memory cards are pretty cheap these day, so it usually isn't a problem.

I could get much more complicated than this, but that's the basics. There are bunched of other reasons to shoot in RAW versus JPEG, but this pretty much covers the basic reasons -- flexibility where color and exposure are concerned.

If you want more info we probably should start a new thread to share the tips with everyone. I don't want to hijack this one. :mrgreen:
