I read somewhere that they cannot live and breed in foam mattresses, because they are not porous enough for the bugs to get into. There are no hollow parts for them to live and breed. The article went on to say that they could still get into your bedroom, and crawl up the legs of your bed and bite you, but they would have to live elsewhere and breed elsewhere, like in your walls or something. So having a foam mattress doesn't mean you can't get bed bugs, but it means its a lot harder for you to get a total infestation as far as your actual bed is concerned.

As soon as hubby and I got married last year, we used gift money to buy us a foam mattress. We haven't had any problems, but to be fair, neither has anybody else in our building. So the whole foam mattress thing might not even be the ideal solution, but it does give me some peace of mind. And boy oh boy is it COMFY!  :mrgreen:

As a survivor of bedbugs, NYC will never take the bedbug problem seriously until there is a financial incentive to do so, and until bedbug infestations are categorized as public health issue rather than a "nuisance" or "problem." Unfortunately they don't spread disease, (no one wants them too... but if they did they would be taken more seriously) and their reproductive and feeding habits are severely understudied and misunderstood. The resource you mentioned is an excellent one, and it needs to get out particularly in neighborhoods where people don't speak English or who are more fluent in another language. (I recall hearing two ladies at the laundromat discuss how washing a load with bleach gets rid of "los chinces"... and I shuddered, because this is just soooo wrong!)


May I suggest looking up Lou Sorkin, a well-known forensic entomologist who has done some major work on bedbugs.
