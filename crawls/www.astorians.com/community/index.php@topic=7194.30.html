I think there are definitely some legitimate complaints to be had about these establishments -- they do increase the noise, increase the traffic, and negatively impact the quality of life in the area for people who have lived here for decades, long before the cafes started sprouting up in such numbers and size.

For example:

-- Athens Cafe was a pizzeria/shoe repair (and some other business) before it took over the "triangle"

-- Grand Cafe was a bank

-- Flo was a health and beauty aid-type store

-- The place on the corner of 29th Street & 30th Ave (don't remember the name, sorry) was a pharmacy

None of those businesses generated the traffic or noise that exists now. 30th Avenue is a residential neighborhood -- not a main thoroughfare designed for excessive traffic. Clogging up a residential area with business that attract large groups of people that hang out until all hours of the night is not a way to make nice with the neighbors -- it's just inconsiderate. The more cafes that open, the more congested the neighborhood gets.

Again, I have nothing against the cafes, but I am unhappy with the effect they've had on the quality of life in this residential neighborhood. It has nothing to do with the ethnic makeup of the clientele or owners, it has to do with the impact on the area. The middle eastern cafes on Steinway also attract crowds of people, but Steinway has always been known as a more commercial area (and therefore a more appropriate location).

Just my two cents.

Quote from: Alexa on July 23, 2007, 07:42:03 PM
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groupthink

Really?  Groupthink?  According to that link,

Quote
Social psychologist Clark McCauley's three conditions under which groupthink occurs:

    * Directive leadership.
    * Homogeneity of members' social background and ideology.
    * Isolation of the group from outside sources of information and analysis.

Point two may be arguable, although I submit that we've got sufficient diversity here to prevent conformity as you're alleging.  But are points one and three really close to applicable here?

To go a bit further, Irving Janis, the social psychologist who coined the term "groupthink", documented eight symptoms of groupthink:

   1.  Illusion of invulnerability –Creates excessive optimism that encourages taking extreme risks.
   2. Collective rationalization – Members discount warnings and do not reconsider their assumptions.
   3. Belief in inherent morality – Members believe in the rightness of their cause and therefore ignore the ethical or moral consequences of their decisions.
   4. Stereotyped views of out-groups – Negative views of “enemy” make effective responses to conflict seem unnecessary.
   5. Direct pressure on dissenters – Members are under pressure not to express arguments against any of the group’s views.
   6. Self-censorship – Doubts and deviations from the perceived group consensus are not expressed.
   7. Illusion of unanimity – The majority view and judgments are assumed to be unanimous.
   8. Self-appointed ‘mindguards’ – Members protect the group and the leader from information that is problematic or contradictory to the group’s cohesiveness, view, and/or decisions.

Source Watch on groupthink.

Calling this groupthink is a bit like aiming a cannon at a mouse.
