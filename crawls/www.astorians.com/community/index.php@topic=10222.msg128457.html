For those interested, Transportation Alternatives a pro-cycling, pedestrian and open space advocacy group has had a Queens Committee for a year and a half now.  We've out together a Tour de Queens for this June and several smaller rides, many of which are coming up in Bike Month, and sponsored Teach Your Child to Ride events.  We also lobby for safer conditions for cyclists, in fact we are in the middle of a fight to have painted buffered bike lanes run along Vernon, a proposal that DoT has put forward but was tabled at last night's CB2 meeting, despite getting an earlier unanimous vote in favor by their Land Use Committee. 

You can check out our google group and read the message board and info we have on our pages to learn more about us.  We meet the second Monday of each month at the Greater Astoria Historical Society on 35-20 Broadway 4th floor from 6:30p-8p and all are invited.

As for a bike helmet, I would think that all bike shops sell them, but the DoT gives out free helmets that are actually pretty spiffy, you can find the next event they will handing them out at by checking the DoT April Biking Newsletter or by 311.

You can probably just clip the lock, salvage the chain, and then buy a keyed lock, so that you don't have to go through the trouble of the combination.
As for bike trainers, there are a lot of different versions out there.  The most common is the magnetic type offered by CyclOps:
http://www.rei.com/product/707996?cm_mmc=cse_froogle-_-datafeed-_-product-_-na&mr:trackingCode=CCD8B126-AB22-DD11-98CA-001422107090&mr:referralID=NA
you can get a less expensive version off the E-bays from a company called Minoura.  I have the low end Minoura:
http://www.bikesomewhere.com/bikesomewhere.cfm/product/349/2866/10457
I don't know about all of these trainers, but at least with the Minoura one that I have, I had to replace the back axel with one that hooks into the trainer. 
Honestly, the only time that I use the trainer is in the winter to try and keep in shape for spring / summer riding.  I think cycling is so much fun because of the stuff you can see outside.  For me it's the same way with jogging / running.  I can't jog or run on a treadmill, because it's so boring, but I don't mind jogging outside.
If you really want to get used to your bike, my recommendation would be to ride outside, take your rides at your own pace, and give yourself a day to recover in between rides.  It will allow your body to acclimate to doing real rides better than the trainer, as you'll have slight terrain changes. One of my personal favorite rides is going down Vernon into LIC and then back into Astoria.  The traffic is usually fairly light, it's not confusing, and there aren't a whole lot of major hills or anything.

If all you can do is short distances just start with that.  Bike to the grocery store or a nearby friend's.  Take a trip to the closest park for a picnic lunch on a nice day.  Bike to the farthest subway station you can and lock you bike up there, then take the subway the rest of the way to work.  I work in SoHo and there is no way I'm riding 10 miles each way to work on any kind of regular basis.  So I ride to Queens Plaza, lock up there and take the train the rest of the way.  When I come home, I hope off there and ride back.  It's about 20 minutes of riding, not too much, but enough that I feel good for being a little active.  If you start out killing yourself you won't have fun and you won't do it again and you won't get better.  It's all about baby steps.

You can also ride the stationary bike at the gym, which is easier than riding on the actual road I find but good for getting your wind up.
