Para, as far as Tulane goes, my understanding is that in the second year of law school you could opt to take either the common law track or civil law track.  Most people that don't plan on staying in Louisiana, or plan to do something internationally, opt for the common law track.  So I suppose if you were really sure you wanted to be in New Orleans long term then Tulane would be a great place to be and the civil law track will help you learn the law of the land.

I went to law school in NY so I don't have anything else to compare it to but I would say that it is probably easier and more desirable to work during your law school years in the state you want to live.  So if you live in NY but go to law school out of state, the best thing to do is to find work during the summer in NY if that's where you want to be.  That has also been the trend that I have seen with the attorneys that have worked in my firm.

Quote from: paratactical on October 03, 2008, 10:34:02 AM
1) Do you think that the need to be in a super elite school is necessary primarily in NYC and the north east because of the abundance of T14 schools? Can a smaller school, like Tulane, be a good choice provided that you want to remain in the place with the school?

I don't think it is necessary to go to a super elite school to get into a good firm even in NYC.  I'm at a top firm, and every single New York law school is at the very least decently represented among associates, counsel and partners in the New York office.  Not only that -- Tulane itself is also well represented.  That said, I have very limited knowledge of the legal culture outside of NYC.  My experiences with lawyers in our other offices lead me to believe that it's more relaxed in other places, but I don't know how that affects hiring.

If you go to a top national school like Harvard, it will almost certainly make your job hunting easier, but there is a lot to be said about going to a top regional school.  The top national schools tend to take a more theoretical approach in their teaching.  Top regional schools, while still not teaching you how to be a lawyer, do tend to offer a more practical education, and you'd be surprised at how many employers appreciate that.

Quote
2) Did you all go to school in NYC? If so, how hard would it be for you to move to another area and work there? What kind of things would you have to do to be able to move if you wanted to still work as a lawyer?

I did.  I've never actively considered moving to another area, so I'm really not sure.  If I wanted to work as a lawyer somewhere else, I would have to make sure it was in a jurisdiction where I could waive in (i.e. they would accept my NY Bar and my years of experience, plus a large amount of cash), since at this point in my career I'm not willing to take another bar exam.
