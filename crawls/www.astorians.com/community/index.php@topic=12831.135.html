Broccoli-Potato-Cheddar Soup

3 medium roundish potatoes, diced
1 big bunch o'broccoli, chopped
vegetable broth
cheddar cheese, shredded
half & half
olive oil
1 red onion, chopped
spices

Sautee most of the onion in a couple of tablespoons of olive oil in a big pot (about 5 min).

Add vegetable broth and some water, filling the pot up about halfway. Bring to a boil.

Add potatoes, spices to taste (salt/pepper at minimum), and some half & half. Simmer for 20 min.

Turn off heat and let cool (add a bit more half & half to cool it a little faster.) Reserve about 1/4 of the soup and blend the rest.

Pour blended soup back into pot, add broccoli and shredded cheddar, and heat up again. Simmer another 5 min or so while stirring.

Serve with lightly toasted slices of Italian bread, wasabi peas, or regular crackers.

Of course, this is the "hard" way; you can buy a box of potato soup and make this a LOT easier. But having the potato chunks is nice.

The potato-broccoli, cheddar soup sounds great.  It definitely sounds like a hearty soup.  I suppose if you add bacon bits on top it'd be the perfect baked potato blended and ladled into a bowl =).

Your soup reminds me of two soups I love to make.

My broccoli soup is a little bit different.

Broccoli
Chicken stock
Lemon

I like to blanch broccoli in homemade, strained chicken stock although boxed chicken stock works fine as well.  Once slightly tender and bright green, put broccoli into blender along with some of the blanching stock and blend.  Add salt and pepper to taste.  Pour into pot.  If too thick, add more stock to thin out.  If you like, add cream or milk to smooth out taste (I prefer no cream).  Before serving, squeeze a wedge of lemon to brighten the flavor.  If you want to be fancy, drizzle a bit of lemon oil (made from warming canola and lemon zest over low heat in a pan).

The other soup I'm reminded of is my kabocha pumpkin soup.  This is actually a joint recipe by my wife and me.  This soup definitely impresses guests because of its rich color and unique taste.  It looks and tastes like the "exotic" version of butternut squash soup.

Onion
Dashi Powder (Japanese kelp and fish based flavoring)
Kabocha (Japanese/Asian) pumpkin
Garlic (1 or 2 cloves max)

After prepping the pumpkin by removing the skin and cutting into large chunks, saute onion with olive oil in a pot.  When onions are tender, add pumpkin.  Sautee for a minute or two with garlic.  Add water to cover pumpkin, add a healthy amount of dashi to taste.  After cooking till pumpkin is fork tender (an hour), blend.  If you want to be fancy, serve with toasted pumpkin seeds on top and a drizzle of truffle oil or dollop of creme fraiche.

Also, Harlan, how did you make Pho stock?  Everytime I've attempted it using fresh, natural ingredients, it never turns out exactly like the soup in restaurants.  I've tried quite a few times and have even asked my Vietnamese friends to share their recipes.  My friend's mom has even given me a homemade spice packet for Pho before, and it still wasn't quite right.  This leads me to the conclusion that Pho soup at restaurants must use MSG or some sort of pre-made, processed bullion.  When I've used these bullion cubes, the taste is usually pretty spot on.  That being said, I try hard not to use MSG when cooking, so I'm definitely looking for a natural alternative.

Gotta resurrect this thread, since I nailed dinner tonight. (For a change... I don't think I've been fully happy with dinner since the last time I posted on this thread!) I wanted to use up stuff in the house without shopping, so I thawed some pork loin, used up some sprouting potatoes (CSA), made a dent on a container of sun-dried tomatoes (Rosario's) and finished off half a bunch of mustard greens.

Pork loin, salt and peppered, seared on a cast-iron pan in a bit of oil, then roasted at 400 until 140 degrees, then sliced. Served over sliced onions that were cooked very slowly in butter and chopped sun-dried tomatoes until soft and golden.

Potatoes were coarsely chopped, then boiled. Served with a mustard green sauce. Sauce was made by boiling the greens (not the stems) for 10 minutes, then removing the greens. 1 cup of boiling liquid was seasoned with salt and a bit of sugar, then reduced to 1/4 c and put in a blender. Cooked the greens some more in plenty of butter, until very soft, then threw that into the blender too. Pureed, seasoned with salt, pepper, and white wine vinegar. A really brilliant color and flavor.

It was good. The sauces were the key. (Inspired by some somewhat similar recipes in Peterson...)

So Rick Bayless has been impacting my cooking lately with his Frontera recipes and travels to Mexico in his show "One Plate..."

I feel like I've got a pretty solid grip on authentic Mexican now, including being able to differentiate most of the peppers along with their flavor profiles.  Tonight, I'm making a Yucatecan specialty: cochinita pibil with pickled onions...except I'm making it my way, not Bayless's.

First off, I used dried pasilla peppers reconstituted with stems and seeds removed.  I toasted cumin seeds, peppercorn, Mexican oregano, and nutmeg.  I ground the spices in my spice grinder and popped all of it into a food processor along with fresh garlic and ginger.  This created a dark, mole-colored paste.  I then marinated a pork shoulder roast for two days in this paste.  I saved the pasilla pepper water.

Fast forward to today: I lined my pot with banana leaves, added the marinated pork into the pot along with ripe yellow plantains and filled the pot with the pasilla pepper water mixed with two generous tablespoons of brown sugar.  I also added a generous portion of salt since my paste had no salt in it.  Lastly, I doused off my creation with some rum and topped it off with more banana leaves.

The meat will slow simmer for 4 hours until it is shreddable at which point I will mix the shredded meat into its own juices and serve alongside with crema fresca, steamed corn tortillas, and pickled red onions mixed with lime juice and serrano chillis.

My version of a Bayless recipe.  A little different, a little richer, but hopefully just as satisfying.

Total cost of dinner (20+ servings): Pork shoulder roast $8 + banana leaves $2 + pasilla peppers $1.5 + red onions, lime, serrano $3 + tortillas $2 = $15.5.  Not too shabby...

Frozen banana leaves are available in a lot of Chinese supermarkets.  Flushing is by far the easiest access to banana leaves for me.  If you take the Q19 that runs along Astoria Blvd, it will take you straight into downtown flushing.  Most frozen foods sections will have banana leaves as it is a very popular ingredient in southeast asian cooking as well as southern chinese cuisine.

Each packet has so many large leaves that you'll really have to stretch to use them up.

Also, are you doing Bayless's Mexican sour orange/lime chicken soup?

If so, he says the best substitute for Mexican sour orange, due to their inaccessibility in the north eastern US, is to use asian kaffir lime leaves.  If you go to any large asian supermarket in Flushing, you will be able to find kaffir lime leaves in abundance in the frozen foods section as well.

I actually have been nailing quite a number of meals recently.  But this one I'm particularly proud of since my wife told me it was the best meal she's had so far this entire year (including eating at restaurants).  Of course, it was a special occasion (anniversary) so I decided to splurge a little more than usual.

3 course seafood

As always with seafood, simple is best.

Course 1: Langostino a la Plancha
Thick layer of salt on top of a skillet.  Fresh, Large U6 orange and white striped langostino placed on top of salt.  Cooked till just bright orange with slightly firm meat, very crisp and aromatic shell.  Served with homemade garlic and lime zest aioli.

Course 2: Sea Urchin and Yuzo Crostini
Fresh, whole Atlantic sea urchin - crack the top and remove the roe.  Topped with fresh yuzo juice (a Japanese citrus) and sea salt on a toasted baguette sliced on the bias.

Course 3: Jumbo Seared Sea Scallops
Dry sea scallops (colossal size) seared and topped with diced avocado and campari tomatoes and crisp garlic chips.  Served with lemon wedges.

Total cost: Langoustine $12 + Sea Urchin $8 + Yuzo $3 + Sea Scallops $ 12 + Lime, bread, avocado, tomatoes, etc. $5 = $40.
