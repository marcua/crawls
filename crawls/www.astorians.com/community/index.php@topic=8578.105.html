Paul frequently speaks over people's heads. When I hear him invoke Austrian economics, I am simultaneously thrilled, imagining people googling Menger, Bohm-Bawerk, von Mises, Hayek, and horrified, imagining the vast majority of people wondering what the hell he's talking about.

He has his moments, but he rarely speaks to people's gut-level fears, their coalitional identities, and, of course he has no interest in talking to them about free lunch promises. These are the three areas in which the other candidates of both parties comfortably cohabitate. Who among the Democrats is arguing for a new philosophy of governance? None of them. It's all about to what degree they will expand government, not whether, perhaps, it's time for Democrats to stop worshiping the cult of the omnipotent state.

The Republicans are the same way, except for Paul. Frankly, without Paul, the entire conversation in the Republican Party would be boring. I hope Paul stays in until he runs out of money, fighting in every primary up until the end. Frankly, the country needs to hear his ideas. There are still some people who aren't really paying attention to politics, believe it or not, and the longer he stays in this thing, the more people will be exposed to his perspective. The campaign has always acknowledged that it is operating on two fronts, the political race and the cultural/intellectual competition.

I disagree with Paul's stance on abortion, but I agree with him that the government should not allocate tax dollars to fund abortions.

I disagree with him, sort of, about immigration, because I *know* that he would advocate a far more open approach to legal immigration, however he keeps thumping his chest about illegal immigration in a way that I find upsetting. To my way of thinking it is absolutely immoral to consider that a person should have to endure restrictions on their capacity to lead a happy and peaceful life simply because they happened to be born in one place as opposed to another. Although illegal immigration may be against current laws technically, there is something called "the rule of law", which is the principle some laws are valid and others are not based on how those laws relate to individual liberty. Slavery was technically legal, but was a violation of the rule of law. Given the case of a person who lives in one region of the world, finds it unbearable or tolerable but not as good as it might be if he/she moved elsewhere, then moves to a place where he/she engages in peaceful, voluntary contracts with other people, I consider it completely inappropriate for any government to put up any obstacles to such behavior.

Paul talks about how some illegal immigrants make use of publicly subsidized programs at taxpayers' expense. But the problem isn't that illegal immigrants become a burden to our welfare state. The problem is the existence of the welfare state in the first place, regardless of whether the people using it are native born Americans or people from abroad. Paul knows this, of course, but I feel he overemphasizes his opposition to illegal immigration and underemphasizes his advocacy of open immigration.

Forget about graceful exits. I want him to stay until the bloody end and continue to expose the Republican Party for what it has has become: a big government party with a hugely powerful, big spending coalition of big businesses who looooove their pork.

Some Observations on Four Terms in Congress


Before the US House of Representatives, September 19, 1984

Mr. Speaker,
…

The most I can hope for is that someday a suggestion I've made is remembered: that the debate would shift to a different plane.
Instead of asking which form of intervention and planning government should impose, perhaps someday Congress will debate intervention versus nonintervention, government versus voluntary planning, U.S. sovereignty versus internationalism – the pros and cons of true liberty.
Today the debate basically is only that of deciding who will be the victims and who the beneficiaries.
I hope the hours of debate over the mechanisms of the political system orchestrated by the special interests will give way to this more important debate on freedom. The lack of this debate was my greatest disappointment.
...
Something has to give – we must choose either freedom and prosperity or tyranny and poverty.



September 19, 1984
Dr. Ron Paul is a Republican member of Congress from Texas.
