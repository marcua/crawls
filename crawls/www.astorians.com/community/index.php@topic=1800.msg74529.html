To loosely paraphrase a story from Bertrand Russell.

Image a teapot orbiting the sun.  It is somewhere beyond the asteriod belt, hidden behind a larger object.  There is no way that we can see it from earth.

It is POSSIBLE that such a thing exists.  However, it is not reasonable to expect that such a thing exists, and anybody who believes in it does so despite the preponderance of the evidence.

Such is religious belief.  It is possible that god exists, although there is no evidence for this.  As such, atheism is a reasonable alternative.

Alexa, I actually and truly am, among other things, a person whose positions with respect to mysticism are exactly atheistic.

A person who takes an atheistic stand is not asserting anything, he/she is responding to an assertion that is entirely beyond reason and experience. There is no assumption of knowledge in refusing to accept mystical assertions. The assumption of knowledge is solely made by the positive claimant, "There is such a thing as a [fill in supernatural entity]." The atheist simply replies, "Your claim about the existence of the supernatural has no basis in fact." What an atheist chooses to believe in... well, that would be something other than (though likely informed by) his/her atheistic stance. Atheism per se makes no positive claims.

The atheist, unlike the devout [fill in your mystical belief system of choice), not only remains open to, but in fact invites any and all new data about the world. As new data have emerged that progressively chip away at "what god is or does", devout mystics desperately find new ways of justifying their insistence on mumbo jumbo, but even most of them realize that there's a difference between "what they know" and "what they have faith in".

Imagine a teapot orbiting the sun...

It is POSSIBLE that such a thing exists.  However, it is not reasonable to expect that such a thing exists, and anybody who believes in it does so despite the preponderance of the evidence.

And yet, here we are millions of times more complex than a teapot, orbiting a sun. The same logic that drives mathematicians to propose that given an infinitely large universe a planet like ours, orbiting a sun like ours, with our exact chance for the rise of life is going to eventually occur also works with teapots. As a matter of fact given how simple teapots are, mathematics should dictate that there should be many many of these teapots orbiting suns, created by chance, and never seen by intelligent beings. Then again, string theory dictates that there's probably an entire parallel universe filled with nothing but teapots. All this is "provable".

I find it strange that when it comes to a planet and life, which is substantially more complex (billion billion billion times less likely to occur) than a teapot, we choose to believe it arose by mathematical chance, whereas if we encountered a teapot in space we'd wonder who made it.

Herein lies the heart of the debate for me I think. By no means do I think that the fabric of the universe came together exactly how religion dictates, but I also think there is indeed compelling "evidence" for the proposition of theories that involve intelligence as a force behind the universe.

I couldn't be atheist for this reason, yet I also have a hard time with the way conventional religion paints the picture of origins and destinations when it comes to cosmological things. And so I live in conflict as I've not yet found an answer that really works for me.

Coincidentally I do find atheism a suitable alternative, just not for me.

I think that you are mistaking probability.  Nobody knows exactly what the probability of life arising is.  Obviously it is greater than zero, or we would not be here. 

Secondly, given random mutation and natural selection (evolution), there is a mechanism by which life, in extreme variety, can arise.  There is no mechanism for teapots to spontaneously form.

But all of that is beside the point. I may have tried to explain Russell's point poorly.  Let me try again.

There is no reason to expect that a teapot actually exists orbiting the sun.  None whatsoever.  Simply because we can imagine one does not mean that one exists.  The same goes for god.  We have no proof that god exists, so it is not reasonable to state that he does.
