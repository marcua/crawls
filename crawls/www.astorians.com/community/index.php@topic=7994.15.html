I'm not going to take the time in this post to point out the problems with each of these proposals one-by-one. But here are some of the problems with her proposals, on principle, in general, based on current, up-to-date economic science.

1. Central planning of human behavior on a societal level is both extraordinarily inefficient and lethal to human progress. Why?
      a) There is a knowledge problem: specifically, there are no experts capable of knowing from one moment to the next what are the best ways for individuals to use their time, skills, and resources, the best ways for them to respond to adversity and opportunity, the best ways for them to develop and adapt, intellectually, emotionally, professionally as unique individuals. Furthermore, it is impossible for a central planner to implement policies that respect individual choice and experimentation; the nature of central planning is to substitute objective plans and priorities for subjective individual plans and priorities. Human progress is based on dynamic and spontaneous discovery and experimentation, which complying with "expert" plans discourages and destroys. Examples? See the world today. The rate of discovery and innovation is greatest in the countries whose governments are least interventionist.
      b) There is the conceit of expertise: specifically, once central planning starts, its systematic response to its own failures is to introduce more and more "expertise", in the form of more severe and ubiquitous interventions. Today, government involvement in health care has produced myriad problems (e.g., people don't think they are responsible for their own health care spending because employers are mandated to provide coverage, insurance companies are far too involved in common interactions between doctors and patients). Of course, Hillary's solution involves the introduction of more government experts and less individual choice. 
      c) Central planning versus PROFIT/LOSS system: when you and I approve of goods and services offered at prices we can afford, we buy them up, and both the consumer and producer win. Competition ensures that some producers will make money, while others will fail. Most entrepreneurs fail a couple of times, but they learn from their losses and adapt. The best of them end up succeeding in proportion to their ability to offer you and me something we value for less than it costs them to produce it. ON THE OTHER HAND, central planning government officials are not bound by Profit/Loss parameters. They regularly force us to pay for things and services we don't want at higher prices than we would ever be willing to pay voluntarily. They have no systemic profit/loss system to learn from. In fact, they actually have institutional incentives to spend more than they should on projects that are failing, because doing so becomes the basis for requesting budgetary increases. The current debt of the country, tens of trillions of dollars, is a reflection both of the electorate's willingness to tolerate the deferred costs of free lunch programs and politicians' understanding free lunch promises "work" for only one purpose, getting them re-elected.
 
2. What happens when a scarce resource is declared "free"? What happens when the government acts to distort prices?   
     a) Prices play a critical role in human society. They communicate information that is vital to our daily decision making about how we invest and spend our time and money. Many of us try to increase the price of our labor and/or products so that we can afford the prices of things we aspire to experience (home ownership, travel, hobbies, new socks). Some of us are happy to pay the price of an iPod, while others of us would rather pay the price of a car rental for a weekend hiking experience. Our responses to prices are completely subjective, based on specifics of time and place and individuality and culture. In this way, prices arise spontaneously, they are not planned by experts. They are a result of human behavior, not of human design.

What happens when prices are designed? When an expert says, THE PRICE OF LABOR CANNOT BE LOWER THAN $8.00 AN HOUR -or- THE PRICE OF HEALTH CARE WILL NOW BE $0.00.

     b) The "tragedy of the commons" is a description of how people overuse resources that are priced at $0.00. This should be intuitive to most of us, but people are often blinded to the problems that arise from it by the prospect of utopian results. "Everyone will have health care! How can that be bad?" Here's how. When the population of doctors are declared a public resource that can be used without cost, they become over-used and poorly compensated. The impact on a doctor's incentive to provide the highest quality of care possible is extraordinarily negative. Why aspire to be the best doctor if your time and expertise will always be valued at the same rate? Would you try to be the best at what you do if there were no increased compensation associated with greater knowledge and skill? In every country that has socialized medicine, there are 2 discernible phenomena: (1) the best doctors and researchers leave (brain drain) for countries where they will be better compensated, and (2) fewer people go into medicine and medical research, leading to a shortage of medical providers to a population that believes it is entitled to cost-free care... which leads to waiting lists for life-saving procedures and reduced access to medical breakthroughs (in France, for example, a huge percentage of men die of prostate cancer compared to the mortality rate in the U.S.).

    c) When the price of labor is set artificially high, the employers have 4 choices: not hire labor they could really use, making it hard for them to grow their business; hire labor off the books, which opens them up to fines and lawsuits, making it harder for them to grow a business;  fire labor to make up for increased labor costs, which is a tragedy for the business owner and the lost employees; or move business to a place that lets them hire labor at costs they can afford, which unnecessarily disrupts a business and harms the economic prospects of a community. 
      Who the hell is any one of us to presume to interfere with a voluntary, mutual contract between an employer and a laborer?  Some of us think that labor should have an OBJECTIVE price, but, as I've described above, that is not how pricing works. Costs of living and costs of starting different businesses vary across the country, the world. Employers need the flexibility of contracting for labor at lower costs, and low-skilled laborers need the job opportunities that arise from an unregulated, highly competitive industry. The particular circumstances of a person, his educational background and life experience and skill set, may mean that his labor is worth a low price to one employer or another AT A SPECIFIC MOMENT IN A SPECIFIC PLACE. But that person's labor price is not static. His economic opportunities will improve as he gains experience, especially if he applies himself, learns and improves, increases his prospective value to others.
   Shouldn't we all want as low a barrier to gaining work experience as possible, and as dynamic a set of opportunities for employees to increase their labor price as possible?
