I totally disrecommend QLIMG.  They make you wait one hour or even two, just to see a doctor who won't hear you long enough for you to describe your symptoms.  Sometimes you wait 1 or 2 hours just so the PCP can refer you to a specialist, because the rules say he has to be the gatekeeper.  That's a lot of wasted sick leave.  And even during the long wait, you can't catch up on your reading because the administrator has turned the waiting rooms into TV theaters.

My PCP flatly refused to let my eyes be examined by an ophthalmologist, even tho I begged him.  But my eyesight was failing, and I had to get some expensive glasses.  Then even the glasses weren't doing much good.  But it so happens that the optometrist also has the authority to refer you to an ophthalmologist, and when I finally got to see the eye doctor he said I had VERY BIG CATARACTS.  Fortunately there are good specialists associated with QLIMG and I got very good cataract surgery from a first-rate ophthalmic surgeon, Dr. Stephen Haug.

So occasionally I get lucky.  But in the balance, QLIMG sucks, or at least HIP sucks.  I want to change my plan in October, when my employer lets me.

Ahhh! A subject in which I am an expert! I've had bilateral total hip replacements, bilateral total knee replacements and various joints in both my hands either totally replaced or arthoscopically repaired. And oh, yes, I've also had a bilateral Carpel Tunnel Repair.

If your health insurance will cover you, find a doctor at the Hospital For Special Surgery. The place is like a freaking spa*. The doctors are all Board Certified in more than one sub-speciality. One of my orthopaedic surgeons sub-specializes in hips—just hips. Another in knees. Yet a third in spines.

My hand surgeon is the love of my life. When 4 other doctors tried to convince me that I just *had* to live with (practically) useless hands, he used FDA approved, self-made and designed surgical tools and gave me back my life. He has subsequently retired, but he left a staff of physicians behind who use his methods. This hospital is innovative in their approach to both pain and numbness, but they pay special attention to those clicking sounds—or as was explained to me—those tell-tale noises in our bodies that mean something is wrong and should be fixed before it becomes something big(ger).

*It is crappy enough to be in a hospital, no matter the circumstances. HSS makes you feel like you are in one of those very expensive resort-type spas. The staff is warm, friendly and very responsive. The meals are fabulous—and when you are able to get around—they are served in an atrium with a view of the river. I dread being thrown back in a hospital, but I do so with a little comfort zone when I know I will be staying at The Hospital & Spa For Special Surgery!
