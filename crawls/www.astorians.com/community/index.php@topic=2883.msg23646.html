Con Ed Says Repairs To Electric Grid May Take Seven Months

http://ny1.com/ny1/content/index.jsp?stid=1&aid=61584

August 04, 2006

In a report issued Friday, Con Edison says it could take until February to repair the damaged power system in Northwest Queens. NY1’s Josh Robin filed this report.

Some Queens residents waiting for normal power might have to wait a few months. Con Ed now says it expects work on its embattled network will last until February, seven months from now. That's according to a report from the utility to state regulators released late Friday.

The 69-page document says the company plans to have on average about 3 percent of its electric operations crew - about 100 workers - in the area through the beginning of next year, tending to the failed system.

Customers in the area have power already. Con Ed is just fixing what brought about last month's outage in an attempt to prevent a devastating repeat.

But many get their juice not from an electric grid, but 50 fuel-fired generators. Those will be there indefinitely, the utility says. Con Ed cannot say when those will be gone.

Customers can't spot the difference inside their homes between generator power and electric power. But outside, some complain about the large mobile units that belch fumes and are taking up that valuable New York commodity - parking spaces.

Asked about the timetable, a spokesman said: "The complete rebuilding of the network will take several months, but we are going to do it safely and expeditiously as possible to minimize inconvenience."

But Queens Assemblyman Michael Gianaris says February seems too long.

“The cover up is continuing. They tell us they don't know what's wrong with it, but they know how long it's going to take to fix it, apparently,” he says. “Look, I'm not an engineer, but it would seem to me in six months you can rebuild an entire network, much less just repair the damage that was done."

This report is Con Ed's second attempt. The state commission that oversees the utility rejected the first one, calling it “unresponsive and virtually useless.”

Whether this one is sufficient isn't known. A spokeswoman for the state authority would only say it has been received.

- Josh Robin

I believe a legitimate (i.e. not done by ConEd) investigation would find that Con-Ed's "demand reduction" efforts greatly contributed to causing the blackout.

First of all, I don't believe reducing voltage actually will reduce demand in a primarily residential area except maybe in the middle of winter.  In fact I think it increases it.  Very few devices used today are pure resistive loads that a voltage reduction will reduce the power consumption of.  The only ones that spring to mind are light bulbs, toasters, electric ovens (how many people in our area don't use gas?), and space heaters (in the summer?).    Electric motors (fans, A/C compressors, refrigerators) run less efficiently with reduced voltage and will actually draw more power.  And I'm fairly certain they are the bulk of residential electric load in the summer. 

On top of that, since it wasn't a supply problem, even if the power demand was unchanged by the voltage reduction Con Ed refers to as a "demand reduction measure", the current being pulled through the wires increases proportionally to the reduced voltage, which depending on exactly where in the system the voltage is reduced, likely means much more current had to be pulled through the feeder cables, causing them to fail.

The wear and tear caused by increased current from 33% undervoltage every evening for 3 straight months before the blackout I'm sure didn't help any either.
