For example, I hear something on a show I don't like....and I know that Home Depot advertises on the show. So I call them up and tell them I will shop at Lowe's if they continue to send money to the person I disagree with. I am voting with my dollars....and that seems to upset Bill.

So anyway, Home Depot calls up the station and says that they have heard from me and many others and they are not happy that I and others may go elsewhere with my dollars and the personality on the show I don't like is faced with a choice. Kempster, I have always liked you and 99.9% of the other folks here and certainly didn't mean to be personally offensive to you or anyone else.  I'm not literally calling out you or anyone else as a weenie.  Rather, I'm calling out a means of protest I find wrongheaded. 

As an uber capitalist, I am all for voting with your dollars, shopping where you want to, listening to who you want to, etc.  My problem in the case of the hypothetical radio host is the intention of these organized campaigns (and usually the end result) is to silence someone else's voice.  I am in favor of a plurality of free speech and a multitude of voices.  But who gets to decide what the "right" kind of speech is?  Take the recent example of Dr. Laura.  She used the "N" word, a word that most of us find deplorable and would never use in any context.  From what I understand, Dr. Laura says that she knows better and never uses that word herself.  Now, I don't know that to be true or not.  She could be the nicest lady in the world, or the most virulent racist.  So who should be the arbiter of free speech who decides who gets to express their views over the airwaves?  The group Media Matters has volunteers who tape conservative television and radio shows, hoping that the hosts will slip up and use "forbidden" speech so that they can lodge complaints and organize boycott campaigns.  They helped take down Imus, for example.  There is an equivalent though smaller conservative group whose name escapes me.  I am against both of these groups because their actions are dangerously close to an attempt to regulate the use of political speech.

If there is a market for Keith Olberman, let people watch him.  Ditto Bill O'Reilly.  If there are idiots in this world who want to hear things that many of us deem hateful, then why the heck not?  The cure should be rational discourse like you and I are having right now, not an attempt to pull the plug on thoughts you disagree with.  That any of us should be "thought police" is totally antithetical to what the founders would have envisioned.  And you don't win hearts and minds that way either.  If some guy is spouting hate speech and you pull the plug on his show, you lose the chance to engage with his listeners.  All you do by silencing their forum is to "validate" their repugnant thesis.  Whereas if you engage with them, you can at least try to change their minds.
