Blood Diamond
« on: December 05, 2006, 11:02:01 AM »
http://www.blooddiamondaction.org/

Last night I went to a preview screening of Blood Diamond. Jennifer Connelly was there as was the director, Edward Zwick. Oddly, this was hosted by Marie Claire - Jen is their January covergirl and they mention the movie in her interview. However, it was quite noticeable - both in the statements made by the hyperactive editor (in a much too tight skirt and flimsy see through blouse) who spoke at the event and in the story itself - that they are edging away from implying that buying diamonds might be an ethical issue NOW. The article talks about "The bad old days of diamond smuggling" (they're over now, of course!) and the editor stammered a little bit when she made reference to "how it used to be to buy diamonds." The director, however, made in clear in the Q&A after the screening that while some steps have been made there are still not enough controls in place. No one asked how (or even if) you can be sure whether the diamond you are purchasing is conflict-free. They did, however, ask what it was like to work with Leonardo DiCaprio, where the actress got her boots, etc.

I think the interview and screening were originally set up by someone who cared about the issue and then it was more or less smothered by someone who was worried about advertising dollars.

Anyway, the movie itself was pretty impressive although VERY violent. At one point I left and just sat outside for about 15 minutes to get a break from the gore and the constant tension. But if you can hack it, I say go for it.

I personally have made a big decision. No more diamonds for me! I'm stopping NOW.

Daisy, here's an article that will give you the basic background: http://www.worldpress.org/Africa/2193.cfm

Warlords force people - even children - to work in the mines. The diamonds are smuggled out of the country and at some point enter the stream of commerce as legitimate products. The warlords use the money from the diamonds to buy weapons.

The diamond industry, as a whole, is so crooked. From DeBeers to the retailers. Diamonds became the jewel du jour as a result of an advertisement that started many years ago—Diamonds Are Forever. Prior to that, colored gemstones were used for engagement and special occasion rings. Today colored gemstones are considered old-fashioned even though Princess Diana's engagement ring was a dark blue sapphire.

You can *never* believe a seller because by virtue of the question, "where was this stone mined?" you *will* get a politically correct answer. If you want a diamond, however, it doesn't make you a bad person.

As to asking for certification, that provides you only with information about the quality of the stone. Your certificate will tell you about grading, size, color, flaws, and give you the registered number of the stone—it will *not* tell you where the stone was mined.

One other thing, Diamond Nexus Labs only produce a diamond-simulant. Far inferior to a real diamond in brilliance and refraction. There is, however, a man-made diamond being produced in small quantities from Charles & Colvard called Moissanite. Many of the certifying agencies (GIA, AG, EGL) had to have special equipment made for them in order to distinguish between a real stone and the Moissanite. These stones are not inexpensive, yet they *are* far less expensive than their diamond counterparts.
