Title: Re: Ron Paul on Wall Street Bailouts
Post by: CW on March 21, 2010, 10:35:45 AM
I think it was a little over 2 trillion. The vast majority of that was secured lending where banks and other large financial entities could borrow for a short term against government, corporate and mortgage securities that they own. If the bank that borrowed the money doesn't pay it back, then the government keeps the securities they put up as collateral. This didn't get much press since it was unlikely that the borrowers would go under in 30 days (the max they could borrow for) and even if they did, the government would just keep the collateral so they'd get paid anyways. The only reason it got any attention outside the financial press is because 2 trillion is such an impressive number. Some people misleading use the $2 trillion number and imply that it was just given to the banks to bail them out like the TARP funds.

Even the TARP funds had the banks paying fairly high interest on the money. I forget how much but it was somewhere in the 8%-10% range. Most of the banks have paid that back with interest. The main losses on the TARP have been from AIG, Freddie Mac and FNMA. As they split up AIG, they'll probably get something back on it but it may not be the full amount. I'm not sure if the loans to the car companies were paid back or if GM/Chrysler will pay them back under the terms of the bankruptcy.

The debate of the role of a central bank in the U.S. goes back a LONG time.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_War

As far as Ron Paul, I like him for the most part but his economic policies absolutely scare me.
