Developer's love the 421-a because they get a lot of extra money for very little, it's a nice windfall. I can't see how they help the buyer's that much as the "savings" are capitalized into the purchase price.

I'm not so sure I'd agree that the abatement is rolled into the purchase price.  I think without the abatement, the developers would have to lower prices to draw buyers who could find a similar new apartment that is abated (read that as tax free).  This is why the demise of the 421a program is expected to slow new development.  I think all new condos sell for the same general market price of the day.  Abatement or not.  So if a buyer can save RE taxes for a few years or the full 25 yrs if it's a brand new building, then that's a big deal for that condo owner.  And as you know, condo common charges are generally much lower than coop maintenance fees because the tax is borne by the unit owner.  So the person who owns a condo and pays a fairly low common charge and is exempt from RE taxes on top of it has a very sweet deal for whatever amount of time.  Yes - the purchase price of a condo is generally more than a coop but believe me, with a 421a in place, it comes pack in your pocket very quickly.

You're absolutely right.  Today is a very usual time to be making these type of decisions.  But it absolutely depends on the rental that you're comparing and the property you're comparing it to.  Generally people who decide that they want to buy their first home are looking at a property that is a few notches up from their current rental apartment.  It's all about moving up in life.  Maybe more space and amenties, that kind of thing.  So in order to look at the comparatives, people really need to look at what rent would be if you rented the kind of apartment you're looking at buying.  Not the rent you pay now.  Maybe you are looking at it that way.  But even still, there could be factors with the building you're in that contribute to numbers coming out that way.  And maybe you haven't find just the right property at the right price.  It takes time and you'll know when it feels right.

Quote from: igpig on July 25, 2008, 12:16:08 PM
You pay upfront for the 421a as part of purchase price and pay less monthly.  In theory it should be equivalent.  In reality, it just complicates the calculations.  You can be better or worse off with a 421a property.  If two properties have the same price and one has 421a then you are better off with the one that has 421a.  Most likely though the property with 421a will have higher price and you need to do the math to figure out the better deal.

OK but forget for a minute new construction.  Maybe a developer (or their agents) will add some dollar amount to the purchase price to make up for the tax break.  OK!!!  Say they definitely do!  A new owner could be fooled into thinking that they're paying for all the brand new stuff and fancy amenities.  And those buyers are often so dazzled that they will pay no matter what.

But take a unit that was granted the 25 year tax break that is being sold by the owner 3 years in.  That owner isn't going to get away with telling buyers that their apartment deserves x dollar amount more because they're going to save money on taxes.  The market (a stable market for argument sake) forces them to list for the going rate for that neighborhood and according to size, and so forth.  They will not get away with asking a premium and then telling the new owner about how much they'll save.  So yes - a buyer of a brand new condo should realize that they're paying top dollar for a large number of reasons.  Frankly, the biggest concern I'd have with buying a brand new condo is the mysterious common charge.  I always see listings for new units with very low common charges.  But once people close and move in, they'll realize that they need to cover the as yet unsold units.  And maybe the dream team didn't consider the hallway lighting, etc.  More often than not the common charges are raised immediately once an on-site board is formed of unit owners.

So I guess my thinking is that buyer beware going into brand new construction.  So long as you can weather some unexpected increases, then it's fine.  But it's foolish to believe the glossy marketing hype.  And on the flip side, I think a shopper who stumbles on a condo with a good number of years remaining on an existing 421a has got themselves a nice deal.
