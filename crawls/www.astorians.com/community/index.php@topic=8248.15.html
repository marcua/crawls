Quote from: mcw on October 11, 2007, 12:25:09 PM
Is it illegal to ask, or to refuse to rent based on ethnicity?

Both.  The New York City Human Rights Law covers discrimination in housing, both public and private (but not a two family house in which the owner resides).  A brief overview and history of the law can be found here, and here are the specific unlawful discriminatory practices. § 8-107(5)(a)(1) provides that it is illegal to:

Quote
refuse to sell, rent, lease . . . such a housing accommodation . . . because of the actual or perceived race, creed, color, national origin, gender, age, disability, sexual orientation, marital status, partnership status, or alienage or citizenship status of such person or persons, or because children are, may be or would be residing with such person or persons.

The law has been interpreted to prohibit specific questions that elicit information that would be grounds for illegal discrimination (such as asking about ethnicity, national origin, religion, and so on).  You can't ask or be asked such questions in an employment interview, either.

MagicPie - from my experience (I worked at the NYC Commission on Human Rights in the mid '90s - I actually wrote the original version of the overview linked to above), the issues are many.  First is the time and effort it takes to bring a suit.  Here is an overview of what you have to do -- anyone can make an appointment with the Commission to complain of discrimination in employment, public accommodation or housing within the five boroughs, and someone will sit down with them and take down the particulars of their case.  If the issue can't be resolved, the Commission will investigate the complaint.  (Overview of entire process).  Most allegations of housing discrimination, particularly in a refusal to rent situation, are situations where there is not a lot of documentation, so it becomes a "s/he said, s/he said" situation.  Aside from the time issue, lack of documentation makes it difficult to prove, as you said.  Also, when I worked there, there was as much as a ten year backlog of cases.  Things may well be better now, but it's not necessarily an immediately gratifying process.

Probably the most successful case while I was there was against a realtor that may or may not be still in business in Park Slope.  It made the front page of the Daily News, and it was also covered in the NY Times.  The real estate office would use a code on the front of the rental notices that the landlord would fill out to indicate if the landlord did not want black or Hispanic tenants.  A prospective tenant was taken to see an apartment by a realtor who did not realise that the apartment was coded, and either did not know about the coding system, or was opposed to it (I can't remember the exact details now).  The landlord was present and said the apartment was taken.  The realtor was yelled at when she got back to the office and told, that's a coded apartment - why did you take that client to see it?  The realtor actually blew the whistle on this one.  Because there were physical examples of the coded listings, it was easier to prove than some others.
