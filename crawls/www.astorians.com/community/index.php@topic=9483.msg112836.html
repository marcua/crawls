The Steinway Shopping Experience
« on: February 14, 2008, 06:22:37 PM »
There’s been several threads on Astorians discussing the state of shopping in the neighborhood, especially on Steinway.

At times it seems to me that the discussion seems to overly focus on what Astoria doesn’t have instead of what it does. I’d like to point out that Astoria has four, prime, diverse retail strips: Ditmars, Grand, Broadway and Steinway. As well as big box retailers on Northern Boulevard and smaller shopping areas scattered throughout the area.

How many other neighborhoods in this city can lay a similar claim? How many of people come from places with no “downtowns” or one’s that are empty?

As an FYI, at the end of this post you’ll find a census of the stores on Steinway prime: the blocks between Broadway and 30th Avenue, in order from north to south (west side of the street in blue and the east side in red).

Steinway between 30th and 28th Avenues was, in my opinion, considered to be of lesser importance than the prime blocks, though you can find some varied businesses, including restaurants, clothes shops, nail salons etc., and a few of Astoria’s long-time enterprises including Schtaz’s paint, She Goes to Your Head hair salon, Joanne’s Bakery, and Gyro Uno.

Though this may be debatable to some, Steinway between 28th Avenue and Astoria Boulevard has actually improved with the rise of Arabic-owned businesses, though it looks like this stretch is again in transition. I counted approximately 8-9 empty storefronts, which I find troubling.

I deliberately left out Steinway between Broadway and 34th Avenue because it was never prime and always a bit of a hodgepodge (and a mess).


Though I think it’s important to support local shops, I make no moral judgments as to how and where people shop. And I’m not advocating  that one must shop locally to be a “good“ Astorian. I’m only hoping people enjoy what is on offer and perhaps think twice before making a complaint and maybe give Steinway merchants a chance at capturing some of your disposable income.

A statement like that is a pretty general one, don't you think? To say that there are no options for clothing or shoes in all of Astoria is like saying that there are no options for restaurants in Manhattan. Astoria is a pretty big neighborhood, in case you didn't notice. Plus there are plenty of "acceptable" clothing stores here in Astoria. But are they good enough for you should be the question. On 31st Street between Ditmars and 21st Ave you have Mimi's Closet. A small boutique granted, prices can be a bit high, but all of her dresses are one of a kind designs, as well as her handbags and shoes. Vintage and vintage style is what she sells. She also sells handmade jewelry, which are well within the price range of practically anybody living here in Astoria. Further down on 31st street we have The Blooming Co., located right past that Mexican restaurant whose name I forget at this moment. They also are a sort of vintage style, but they also have shoes, boots, hats handbags, gloves and both of these places would fit in perfectly in a neighborhood like Soho or the West Village, but chose instead to plant their roots here.

  On 31st Ave you have the Love boutique ( I think it's called) another fine establishment in the same sort of style. There are several others whose names and locations escape my mind at this very moment, but I'm sure if you googled "vintage boutique-astoria ny" you might come up with several hits.

If you consider Foot Locker to be a "quality establishment",that's funny ,considering that their salespeople couldn't sell a pair of ballet shoes to a ballerina. Granted I'm not against shopping in Manhattan, but when we have such hidden treasures here in our own neighborhood and how Manhattan has "many more viable options", it makes me wonder if people actually know anything about their own neighborhood.

a) aren't looking to get quality all the time
b) can't afford it
Just explore your neighborhood a little more before saying that all that is available here is "a joke".

I was a regular customer at the "Manila Hair Salon" on 36th Avenue for as long as I've been living at my present location, which is about twelve years. The shop owner was simply wonderful, and always gave a great cut at a very reasonable price. The last time I was there, though (about three months ago), he told me he would be closing the shop and moving to Jackson Heights because the landlord for the shop had boosted his rent to the point where he'd be losing money if he were to stay at his present location - WHERE HE'D BEEN FOR OVER TWENTY YEARS!
You would think that a landlord would want to hold on to a long-time tenant who'd been dependably paying rent for twenty years. I know I would! So now the shop has been gated up and vacant for the past three months with NO income for the landlord. All I can say is that that type of thinking on the part of the landlord is stupid and short-sighted. He lost a good dependable tenant - and for a still developing commercial strip like 36th Avenue to have one less successful business on it, it will only discourage other good entrepreneurs to choose the location as a place to start a business.
