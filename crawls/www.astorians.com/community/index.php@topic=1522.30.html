The cemetery behind St. George's Church has not been destroyed. It is still there, but it's hard to see now because it's above street level and there are a bunch of very unattractive plywood boards separating it from the construction zone.

What was demolished was the old parsonage building. It will be replaced by apartments, and the church says that the money made from those apartments will support the rest of the church grounds. As far as I know the cemetery itself was never slated for obliteration.

The building that was demolished had some historical significance for Astoria. Astoria was named for John Jacob Astor with the hope that by naming it after him he would give money to help develop the area. He gave $500 to a convent and that was it. That building that was destroyed is where Astor's 500 bucks went.

I've read conflicting accounts of that story, though. Some say Astoria was named for Astor before he gave the $500, others say he gave the $500 and thus the area was named Astoria. It may be semantic as to which events literally came first, but either way it's hard to believe that $500 had a major impact on development of the area.

Getting back to my original question, I called the church at their listed number to see what's up but there was no answer, so I guess I'll have to wait and see for now.
It seems the only way to get onto the cemetery grounds now would be to climb some mighty high fences on 14th Street, or possibly climb the fence around back. But that fence appears to be on private property, and while I considered climbing it anyway I figured I didn't want to get stuck in a boneyard without good reason to be there.

I like that cemetery a lot. Old, old markers of some famous New York characters, most notably the Blackwells. Roosevelt Island used to be called Blackwell Island.

this topic seems like it deserves its own forum, since we have numerous subjects going on here.

cemetery space in america was originally designated dual-purpose park + burial ground. as burial space in new york city fills to capacity cemetery owners and developers are exploring ways to support these grounds after they're unable to make their money off burials. in some cases they're literally reverting to that original dual-purpose, in other cases they're organizing tour groups and that kind of thing.
