I think there are three kinds of "moderation" that takes place on this board. One I would call self-moderation. This should be the most frequent and easiest form of moderating the board. It just means you make sure you are posting on topic (or at least related to the thread), and in the right category so that you are not being a pain to the rest of the community.

Then there's community moderation, this is the best type imho and the least "heavy handed." Community moderation is when someone says something off topic and someone else chimes in immediately afterwards and says, "that's a bit off topic don't you think?" and the thread goes back to normal. Another example of community moderation is if someone says "all liberals are going to burn in hell", and someone else responds back and says "I don't think that's a very nice thing to say".

Administrative moderation is when a moderator comes in and physically changes the thread. This is the least desirable, and frankly, since none of us currently get paid to do this kind of moderation I'd very much like to rely on the community to help keep things as organized as possible (again, not by physically changing things, but by helping to remind each other to stay on topic, or to not be racist etc). I think it's better and more healthy for the community when administrative moderation is avoided altogether. Administrative moderating is like babysitting and should only come in to play when the first two forms of moderation break down.

So my idea is that even though not everyone has the power to physically change others' posts, we do all have a responsibility here to help moderate the board and keep things organized. I don't like the policing (babysitting) model, and I'd rather that instead of getting reported posts all the time, that we all help each other learn what is appropriate and acceptable and what isn't. As I see it, we can define those rules as a community as we go along, and they may change. It's nearly impossible for moderators to keep up with the content anyway on this board, which is why I think it works so much better when people just respond and remind each other when a thread is getting off topic or when someone is becoming abusive.

I'd like to have as few hard rules as possible, and as much community involvement as possible when it comes to "moderation" and only have to make a moderator step in to physically address some problem when absolutely necessary. The "administrative" moderating isn't fun.

These are my thoughts currently anyway.

As I was thinking about this topic, something occurred to me.  As this board grows, so do the number of threads, and posts.  I know I often am too lazy to search back for previous threads about a topic I am interested, and know was on here.  So I'll start a new thread.  And as new people come aboard the site, they might not necessarily look for previous topics also.  The same topics have been discussed many times on this board, and in many threads.  For example, the new Staples that is going up behind Neptune Diner.  We have discussed this in great detail in the past.

So I guess I'm saying, that with so many people on here, and the board being difficult to stay up-to-date on, it's natural that threads are going to stray from their original topic.  There will be redundancy with topics being discussed in multiple threads.  And people will not search the boards for previous topics that might already exist on here.  I think it's just the nature of the beast.  The only way to really combat that would be for the moderators to enforce strict rules about staying on-topic in a thread, and if the conversation deviates, they would then cut out the tangent an start a new thread.  But I highly doubt the moderators have to time, or desire to micromanage the board to that level.

Just my $.02

I haven't been a user of this web page for very long, and what is more this is the first web page of this sort  that I have ever used.

But I want to offer my two cents, and you can take it or leave it.  Either way, I want to give a "big up" to whoever out there is running this page and to the people who have been very generous with their time and effort in posting such helpful comments -- this has truly been a fantastic resource for me and my wife as we try to navigate our new adopted neighborhood.

Anyway, as mentioned by a previous post, I did, in fact, change the topic of the "What are they building next to the Neptune Diner" from the subject indicated in the title to a discussion of Totonno's pizza.  If this is a violation of etiquette, I will not do similar things in the future.  But, I think there is a tremendous amount to be said for the ability for threads to reflect a stream-of-consciousness approach, rather than a hyper-organized and categorized model.

The analogy I would offer is that of a university library (sorry, I'm an academic, and this is what first comes to mind).  You may have heard that there is a lot of talk (especially on the part of Google) about digitizing entire university libraries and making the contents available to the public online (and searchable, etc.).  But what may be lost in doing so is the invaluable experience of "browsing."  I spend almost every day of the week in the NYU library, and I have learned as much from random books that catch my eye in the stacks as I do from the actual books I use for my research (even if the random books are off-topic, which is usually the case, I'm interested in so many things that occasionally pulling a random volume off the shelf and flipping through it can be a good time). 

I sort of see these threads in the same way.  It's true that if a thread becomes a catch-all for everything that's tangentially related to anything else, pretty soon you're going to have a discussion about Staples and pizza in the same thread.  But at the same time, I learned a lot about both topics in the space of four or five posts (and from a person whose posts I enjoy reading).  And besides, it seems like most of the time, people do create separate threads for distinct topics.  So I guess my attitude is sort of the anti-Google position.  There's so much information out there, let it fall where it may and let people discover it where they may- that's part of how knowledge is spread and horizons widened, and part of the beauty of human interaction.

But I do apologize for so flagrantly changing the topic.
