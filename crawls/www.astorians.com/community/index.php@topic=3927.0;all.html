I know most smokers feel like commercials and articles such as this are lecturing, but I found this to be quite interesting.

What happens to your body if you stop smoking Right now?

I think one of the main reasons it’s so hard to quit smoking is because all the benefits of quitting and all the dangers of continuing seem very far away. Well, here’s a little timeline about some of the more immediate effects of quitting smoking and how that will affect your body RIGHT NOW.

In 20 minutes your blood pressure will drop back down to normal.
In 8 hours the carbon monoxide (a toxic gas) levels in your blood stream will drop by half, and oxygen levels will return to normal.
In 48 hours your chance of having a heart attack will have decreased. All nicotine will have left your body. Your sense of taste and smell will return to a normal level.
In 72 hours your bronchial tubes will relax, and your energy levels will increase.
In 2 weeks your circulation will increase, and it will continue to improve for the next 10 weeks.
In three to nine months coughs, wheezing and breathing problems will dissipate as your lung capacity improves by 10%.
In 1 year your risk of having a heart attack will have dropped by half.
In 5 years your risk of having a stroke returns to that of a non-smoker.
In 10 years your risk of lung cancer will have returned to that of a non-smoker.
In 15 years your risk of heart attack will have returned to that of a non-smoker.
So, you have more immediate things to look forward to if you quit now besides just freaking out about not being able to smoke.

Quote from: erpotown on October 25, 2006, 01:00:49 PM

THE TRUTH: Your heart rate drops, not your BP. No scientist could have ever made such a statement. The average smoker goes longer than 20 minutes between cigarettes. Hypothetically then, their BP goes down between each cigarette? What about the smoker who has chronic low blood pressure? (And yes, there are plenty.) A person who smokes *and* has chronic tachycardia can feel the difference. The average person cannot.

Quote from: erpotown on October 25, 2006, 01:00:49 PM

THE TRUTH: People who stop smoking before age 35 avoid 90% of the health risks attributable to tobacco; however with each 10 years the percentage is decreased by 12%. That is the fact. Not the crap that you quoted. So, let's look at the average smoker. They are well over 35. The facts about smoking kept the 20–40 year old population from ever starting. People who quit smoking before age 50 have one-half the risk of dying in the next 15 years compared with continuing smokers. How good are the statistics for a person over 50 who quits?

FACTS:
*12 hours after quitting: The carbon monoxide level in your blood drops to normal.

*2 weeks to 3 months after quitting: Your circulation improves and your lung function increases.

*1 to 9 months after quitting: Coughing and shortness of breath decrease; cilia (tiny hair like structures that move mucus out of the lungs) regain normal function in the lungs, increasing the ability to handle mucus, clean the lungs, and reduce the risk of infection.

*1 year after quitting: The excess risk of coronary heart disease is half that of a smoker's.

This is not a discussion about smoking, it is about quitting. We all know what a lousy thing it is. Knowing the truth about quitting may make it easier to do for some people. Being fed lies like those that people were fed by the tobacco industry simply perpetuates the smoker's comfort zone. Just like knowing what to expect when you attempt to quit. Ask someone who gave up recreational drugs or drinking how bad it was for them to quit. Then triple it for what it is like to quit smoking. You need to be committed to the process to be successful. To read stuff like this simply sabotages the process for most people. The truth is always much more effective.

Sorry, I don't mean to sound so harsh, but this is a very personal subject for me.

Erpotown, I did not find your post offensive.  There was a time in the 80's when I was young (like 10 years old) and all the news about smoking came out, where because of what I would hear in school, I would come home and constantly nag my parents to quit, because I was scared of what would happen to them.  Now, I'm older and wiser (but not that old!) and I have stopped the nagging because I have long ago realized that smoking is extremely hard to quit, and a person has to really want to do it.  That might be a naive way of putting it, but I've had many a smoker tell me how much they enjoy it.  I've also had many a friend quit and pick it up again, months and years later.  My parents haven't quit smoking to this day.  Both my grandfathers died from smoking related illnesses, and it is hard not to picture the same fate for them.  At least they don't smoke in front of any children and usually try not to have me breathe it when I'm around them.  My father has this weird habit of quitting for the whole weekend, being an irritable mess the whole time, and then picking it up again on Monday.  It's frustrating because he never gets past the withdrawal period.  I don't hate smokers, I hate the smoke and I hate what it can do to people.   

I will never pick up the habit due to sheer fear that I'll never be able to quit.  You're supposed to look up to your parents, if they can't do it, how can I think I won't get addicted too?  Fear is good.  I'm scared of drugs too.

One of my brothers is still a smoker/chewer and reading this makes me wish that he could stop even more.
Stop just wishing. Become more proactive in his mindset about what he does.

Before I tell you what I *know* works, allow me to tell you what I know doesn't.

Don't ever tell him that it'll kill ya!
Don't ever say that you are looking out for his best interest.
Don't suggest that the addicting powers of nicotine are overrated.
Don't advise him that the psychological attachment to smoking is underrated.
Don't tell him it is unhealthy.
Don't remind him he has a monkey on his back.
Don't even mention heart attacks, or strokes, or heart rate, or lung cancer.
Don't tell him that you hate the smell of smoke.
Don't ever call him names like asshole or jerk.
Don't ever quote him facts, even if you *are* certain they *are* accurate.

None of these will have any effect other than to have him shut you out. As soon as you go there, he will stop listening; stop hearing you.

What works?

Every time you see him or speak to him, tell him you love him. Every now and then (like perhaps 3 times a year) tell him you love him and also tell him how important he is to you; tell him how painful it would be for you to lose him to a smoke-related illness—and then drop the subject. Don't engage in a discussion. Don't even think of encouraging him into a constructive conversation. In your non-smoker's mind you will see him as being defensive and he will feel attacked. But, the thought will remain. My sister loves me. I love her. I would feel awful leaving her with such grief. Step back; back off. Pray for him.
