Sad. This whole thread is very sad.

Not that anyone asked, but this is what I would do.

Pick a day, or evening, weekday or weekend—your choice—post the building with flyers. Nothing fancy, mind you, just plain and simple black and white.
Let's All Have A Meet and Greet
EACH OTHER
Day of Week, Time of Day–Time of Day
Pick a Common Area of Your Building

Come with a glass of iced tea. Yes I said tea—okay, cola—nothing alcoholic, not yet. If/when a few people show up, be prepared to invite them to your apartment, and have a very simple spread for them. Potato chips and a dip would be fine. Let them suggest going back to their place to bring over something more substantial. Perhaps even have a couple of menus that you can order from.

Yep, this is Mohammed going to the mountain, but what the hell, It serves the same purpose . . . people get to know one-another on a first-name basis. Enough of neighbors just nodding the hello thing. Make them make friends. In the end you may not like them enough to really socialize with them, but at least you know who the hell they are!

Blame it on my age, my gender—who cares what—my experiences are quite different than yours.

People smile, say hello and stop to talk to me on the street, in the supermarket, at the gas station—almost everywhere I go. I respond in kind.

When I recently had a problem, people came calling all through the night and into the morning to be sure that I was okay. Just as I would have had what happened to me had happened to someone I knew.

Perhaps, just perhaps, you are experiencing yourself. Perhaps you are not as friendly as you want others to be with you. Jennifer's noting that you said,
Quote
and the "bitch" said "yeah and?"
might be an indication of the way you are thinking. Comon, we know you are a friendly guy, just maybe not in the way others (your peers) perceive you. Maybe it is time to look inward? I'm not scolding here, just posing possabilities to help you broaden your scope of associations.

I know a lot of people. Not all of them are my friends. But there are always people watching my back. That gives me great comfort.

Jonathan mentioned that he has 3 apartments on his building. I have 140. Do I know everyone? Hell no! Do I know most, yep. When someone new moves in, we, the old timers, bring them a meal while they are moving in and welcome them to the building. We give them a folder with all the take-out menus from the hood, and tell them about the elevator rules, the basement rules, the building employees' telephone numbers, whatever they need to get settled and comfortable. People did this for me . . . twice. The first time in 1964 when I moved in, and the second time on Easter Sunday, in the middle of a freak snowstorm in 1970 when I came home from the hospital with a baby in each arm. My neighbors made us meals for a week so that we could get acclimated to our twins. I treasure those moments.

Okay, jumping off my soapbox, now.  :roll:

Shrugs. It's probably inherent to most big cities - not just NY and DC - if not elsewhere. We are quite lucky and have proved the odds wrong: we are good friends with our next doors neighbors. I would count them as yet more friends Astoria has given me.

Having said that, I abhor the rest of the people who share our floor (6 other units)... So 1/6, that's not bad, right?

I think that its just luck-of-the-draw. In some cases, you get lucky and find neighbors with similar interests or shared background who are amiable and willing to open their homes (and minds) to new friends. But most of the time, in a city of 8 million, you won't find a match. In that case, I would discount the rude woman as not really worth your time (unless she redeems herself somehow and shows that it was just an anomalous bad day) and move on to other neighbors. If not at least for the practical reasons you stated. However, I don't agree with inviting a whole slew of people to some kind of mixer. Unless your intention is to be the building mascot or something. The turnover rate is so high for typical tenants in this city that I think that's too much effort just to make acquaintances. I happen to favor the more personal appraoch, like the one you attempted-- just don't get discouraged!

Even though we're not friends with the folks on the other floors and other buildings now - we're all friendly.  I've brought the upstairs neighbors gallons of beer that I wanted out of my refrigerator after a party and they've brought me zucchini bread.  My top floor neighbor had a plant that she didn't think was doing well so she gave it to me and asked that I plant it in the garden.

"WHY DO YOU WANT THEM BACK THERE??"  He had a point.  We have privacy as long as the neighbors aren't using their yard.  If they start using  their yard we'll lose this.  And I think this is what a lot of neighbors fear.  If you start talking to your neighbors they might want to start hanging out.  And once you start hanging out - well... how do you get rid of them??  New York is a crowded place.  For some - home is the only place where they can go to be alone and get away from ALL THOSE PEOPLE.  Some people don't want their neighbors to be anything more than strangers. 

All that said.... I miss life in the house where I grew up.  Friends and relatives would drop by all the time.  Grandparents had their own keys, aunts and uncles lived near by.  There was always coffee brewing and cookies on the table.  I miss that.  But that existed in a world with a lot of SPACE and a stay at home mom and aunts and grandmothers. 


In the meantime - two buildings down there is a new family of 4 with two of the cutest little girls ever (3 and 5 yrs old).  They're always outside playing with either mom or dad or both sitting on the stoop (dad is actually usually working - he's the new building super).  Right now we're on a "hi" basis.  We've exchanged a few more words but not much.  I ordered cupcakes for my homeroom students tomorrow and I ordered extras so that I can bring them to my new neighbors.  I don't expect to become friends with these folk.  But it's a neighborly gesture and I can't wait to see the girls' reactions.
