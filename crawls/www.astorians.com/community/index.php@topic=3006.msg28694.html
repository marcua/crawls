Letter from Woodside (um dirk, its next to Astoria, but still in western Queens)

I live in Woodside.  Let me tell you a little bit about my commute.  Currently, I wait 10-15 minutes for a bus to take me to the subway.  When the bus arrives, many times it is too crowded to board.  So, I have to wait another 10-15 minutes for the next bus.  If I am lucky enough to squeeze onto that one, I end up in Long Island City waiting with a bunch of other poor saps for a train which usually arrives packed like a string of sardine cans - meaning that I have to let a train or two go by before I can take one to get to work in Manhattan. 

This is stressful, exhausting, and affects my quality of life.  Apparently, no one at city hall understands these problems, because instead of talking about fixing what already doesn't work, they are excitedly planning to pile tens of thousands more people along the train route.  I am all for "affordable" housing, however I will no longer be able to afford my own house if I can't make it to work on time.  Dumping more people into an already overpopulated area and forcing our poorly run transit system to absorb the impact will be an utter disaster. 

Where will the children of my new neighbors go to school?  Western Queens school districts are already among the most overcrowded in the city, and our area's Catholic schools are closing.  Will fire, police, emergency services and utilities be able to provide service to all the new arrivals?  Who decided that this was a good idea?  Certainly not the people. 

I was at the meeting at St. Mary's where the plan was discussed, and not one resident of the area thought it was a smart one.  The bureaucrats seem to think they know better than us, though, so it will be done.  They are supposed to be representing us, not making deals with the devil. 

While the Mayor and City Council are busy playing SimCity, the people who live here are the ones who are suffering.  Neighborhoods where constituents can outspend the real estate industry in political contributions don't have to worry about rampant overbuilding ruining their lives. 

So much for looking out for the little people.

Joanna [mod]
Woodside

Where to begin?  First, MaspethMaverick - I like your website.  It sounds like the Juniper Park Civic Association does good work and that's very commendable.  I know I've heard of Maspeth, but wasn't certain where it was.  I found this nifty map (posted below) that shows the neighborhoods of Queens.  I do concede that Maspeth is in Western Queens, but this map shows it bordering Woodside, not LIC.  Of course, this map also combines Astoria and LIC and has Northern Astoria as the Ditmars/Steinway area.  I've driven to Middle Village, which abuts Maspeth and it's a 25-30 minute drive, traffic permitting, not really what I would call close, but definitely closer than Forest Hills.  I still find it interesting that folks in Maspeth would get involved in Astoria, but then again, I’ve been involved in fights in the Village (both East and West), so maybe it’s not so strange.  I would be interested in hearing what the catchment area is for the Greater Astoria Historical Society is, and what the catchment area is for LICA?  Is it just Astoria or more expansive?

I definitely agree that the subways need to be improved, and should be running more often, both on the N/W, but also the R and 7 lines.  I wish they'd figure out what to do with the G - it really seems to get short shrift. 

I wouldn't characterize myself as only wanting to isolate myself or pretending that other parts of Queens don't exist.  I go to Woodside, Jackson Heights and Flushing regularly.  I get to other parts of Queens much less often.  I do, however, follow what is happening in communities throughout the city through the blogosphere and local community papers, though I tend to focus more on the local politics than development issues (just my area of interest).  While there are similarities between many neighborhoods, each neighborhood has issues and a dynamic that is particular to itself. 

For myself, I don't see overdevelopment happening in Astoria, at least not yet.  I see mainly in-fill development, building on vacant lots.  I don't even see that many buildings being torn down, not like they do in Bayside or Staten Island.  LIC is a different story entirely, but this is Astorians.com not LIC.com and I, personally, don't get involved in LIC issues (the Hunters Point area on this map).  I walk along the Astoria waterfront sometimes and have been over to the water a number of times this Summer.  I haven't seen any buildings going up along the water in Astoria, though there are some new construction projects a few blocks inland.  I think the highrises to which you are referring are in LIC. 

I've seen the postings about LICA on the board, and had actually been interested in them at one point, but the entire approach about beating us all up over attending LICA meetings, trying to force discussion of “over-development” in multiple threads (including ones about allergies – I’m still waiting to see over-development brought up in a food thread)  and trying to hijack the community response to hate crimes made me uncomfortable.  As such, I haven't checked them out.  Their website is interesting, but the approach by the "anti-development" (for lack of a better term) folks on Astorians.com hasn't made me more interested, it's actually made me less inclined to check them out.  I don't think that's the result that LICA is seeking.  The constant snide remarks, personal attacks, belligerence towards seemingly all of our local elected officials and even other local community groups, just seems really weird.  I guess I just don’t understand what people are trying to accomplish by this sort of behavior.  It’s really rather off-putting.
