In Fiscal Crisis, Mayor Considers Raising Property Tax 7 Percent

[url][http://www.nytimes.com/2008/09/23/nyregion/23tax.html?_r=1&em&oref=slogin/url]

Quote
Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg, worried about the effects of the global financial crisis on New York City’s economy, said on Monday that his administration is considering imposing a 7 percent property tax increase on homeowners in January.

The mayor said the move, which would generate an additional $600 million in revenue next year and require City Council approval, would help the city weather a worsening fiscal situation.

New York City is heavily dependent on the financial services industry: An estimated 10 percent of its tax revenues come from Wall Street.

When Bloomberg raised property taxes 5 years ago 18 percent, he claimed it was a temporary increase, and when the city's finances get better, they will lower them again. Well, not only haven't they lowered them, they will now increase them an additional 7%. This is for residents of condos and coop apartments, and owners of 1-3 family homes. why are there no increases again for apartment building owners and business buildings? Why must the middle class, many who are already struggling in this economy  keep having to bear the brunt when the city's economy less than ideal?

The drop in the value of homes has yet to be reflected, no such downgraded change in property values have been made by the city. They only use these figures for increasing taxes, they do not use them for decreasing htem.
This is sort-of true

 1) The assessed value of a 1 to 3 family building is based on the sales price of similar property over the last 3 years. It takes some time for a change in prevailing prices to change the assessment - I suppose if every building sold in the last year was given away, the assessed value of other homes would fall only by 1/3.  There is also a cap on the amount that the assessed value of one property can rise in a year.

 2) As I get it, the assessment is actually used only to distribute the burden - that is, the city budget determines how much money property taxes should bring in, and sets a tax rate to make the target.  If all classes of real-estate happen to change valuation by the same proportion, then there's no change to the end result. But if one class of property appears to be more valuable, then the owners of those properties pay more, and others less.

 3) Different methods are used to determine the assessed value of different kinds of property. For example, property that exists primarily to generate income is assessed based on the income, what are typically owner-occupied homes are assessed by recent sales.

There is a procedure to request a change to the assessed value; I don't know what's required or what the probability of success might be.. the city has lists of what properties got revised notices by month on the Dept of Finance Property Valuation page.
