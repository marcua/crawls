Young whippersnappers more narcissistic?
« on: February 27, 2007, 11:42:40 AM »
http://news.yahoo.com/s/ap/20070227/ap_on_re_us/self_centered_students

According to the article above, the very fabric of our society is being undermined by young people and their self-centered ways.

What do people think about this?  On the one hand, as we discussed a little bit before in the American Idol thread, certain aspects of popular culture do seem to encourage (or do they reflect?) greater levels of self-centredness, valuing of fame, etc.

On the other hand, it's a well-documented phenomenon that older generations have a tendency to think that younger generations are morally bankrupt and vastly inferior.

On the third hand, the study does seem to use a set of semi-objective measures (however much self-evaluation can be objective, which I don't think it can 100%) to measure these things.  But could the measures being used be calibrated according to an older generation's set of values that sees different things as markers of different qualities?  I.e., not that the younger generation is objectively more narcissistic, but that it engages in more behaviors that the people who designed the study--and who presumably have a different (not better or worse) worldview, and a different age-based social code for signifying certain qualities about themselves and reading them in others--view as signifying narcissism.

And you could also, for example, chalk up increasing belief in being "special" over the past century to a changing economy--e.g. fewer of us are among throngs laboring in factories or fields, and increased professionalisation of the work force,  hence more people feel more different from one another, and more autonomy in daily activities. 

I dunno.  I'm interested in people's thoughts.

I read an article in NYMag online a few weeks ago (and which I now cannot find) about a study which involved two groups of elementary school students.  The ones who were told that they were smart remained at about the same level when tested on academic skills at the end of the study; the ones who were complimented on how hard they worked, on the other hand, raised their scores.  The results seemed to draw a distinction between fostering a sense of entitlement based on innate ability, and reinforcing the importance of effort and work ethic.

Though I have a tendency to dismiss the differences between the generations, in this case I'll take what's behind door number 3.

In my opinion, it represents a general conciousness which has been moving in the direction of individuality and self importance since the mandated conformity of World War II and the attached social conformity of the 1950's (both of which were movements away from the increased individuality/narcissism of the progressive era, 1920's).

I see the output of media, as well as the change in labor management (the greater autonomy needed to give gen Xer's and gen Yer's in order to maximize productivity), as symptoms of this, rather than the cause.  Eventually we'll see a swing back in the other direction.

Although, I tend to think most 18-year-olds aren't really thinking in those terms, even if they know that that's the case.

Also, I'm not sure I buy Harlan's argument both because I don't think your average 18-year-old is sitting in his or her dorm room comparing the cost of his education to that of people ten to fifteen years older than him, and because college enrollment is rising not only because of a demographic boom, but also because college education is seen as necessary and/or available to people from a wider swath of the socioeconomic spectrum, and also because it is seen as a prerequisite to a professional-type job.  I don't think that these factors would contribute to a college student's greater feelings of worthiness.  

I also agree with Frappe's assessment of a changing national ethos.  We're not all working together and making sacrifices to defeat, e.g., Hitler, nor are vast numbers of college students' age mates being drafted.
