Pulled pork, why do you ask for recipes when you know full well that whatever you make up on your own will be more delicious than a recipe you get from someone else?
If I may be so impertinent, I would like to answer your question. Cooking is all about experimenting. You start off with a recipe and you continue to experiment with the ingredients until you perfect what you want to achieve. When I was a younger woman, if a recipe would say to use yeast . . . or worse yet, roll out the dough . . . I would chuck the idea right away. As I got more comfortable with baking and experimenting with ingredients and methods, and was lucky enough to have a family that enjoyed even my worst mistakes, I developed a repertoire of hundreds of really great dishes. I have finally started cataloging them so I can pass them down to my children who are always calling and asking me how to make this or that. I was raised in an Orthodox Eastern-European family environment as was my late first husband. I used to stand in both my Mom's and my Mother-in-law's kitchen and write down what they were doing in the hopes of preserving their recipes. My late second husband was 100% Italian. I did the same thing with his Mother.

I've only recently stopped baking certain foods that I found I could buy less expensively than the ingredients were costing me. There is one caveat, however: they have to be as good, if not better, than mine!

For example, I now buy my biscotti from a bakery in upstate NY. She makes some of the most amazing I've ever tasted.

Pulled pork, why do you ask for recipes when you know full well that whatever you make up on your own will be more delicious than a recipe you get from someone else?

GFB, fair question from a friend.  So the answer is LWP, NYMAC and Edava are all correct. I am a chef and can riff on any idea and create a dish for 2 or 200 from scratch. Baking is very constraining for me as a concept. It is science and not art, at the most basic level. Of course baking is an art in the hands of the right people.  With cooking I never want to cook the same dish twice because it would not feel as creative as the first time. Also why I am not a restaurant chef where it has to be perfectly the same every time. Yes I am consistent with my professional cooking but personally I want to create a new masterpiece or at least a new idea each and every time.

So back to baking, I ask for advice because I want to start with what people see as a good recipe. I just love to look at the recipe and see a "why and how" of it.  Baking will always be something I appreciate from others more than myself. 

Anyway, just a glimpse into how I see it as a chef if anyone was interested.

My mom would make monkey bread, it's fast and easy to do and fun to pull apart with your hands..

Monkey Bread

 INGREDIENTS

    * 3 (12 ounce) packages refrigerated biscuit dough
    * 1 cup white sugar
    * 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon
    * 1/2 cup margarine
    * 1 cup packed brown sugar
    * 1/2 cup chopped walnuts (optional)
    * 1/2 cup raisins

DIRECTIONS

   1. Preheat oven to 350 degrees F (175 degrees C). Grease one 9 or 10 inch tube pan.
   2. Mix white sugar and cinnamon in a plastic bag. Cut biscuits into quarters. Shake 6 to 8 biscuit pieces in the sugar cinnamon mix. Arrange pieces in the bottom of the prepared pan. Continue until all biscuits are coated and placed in pan. If using nuts and raisins, arrange them in and among the biscuit pieces as you go along.
   3. In a small saucepan, melt the margarine with the brown sugar over medium heat. Boil for 1 minute. Pour over the biscuits.
   4. Bake at 350 degrees F (175 degrees C) for 35 minutes. Let bread cool in pan for 10 minutes, then turn out onto a plate. Do not cut! The bread just pulls apart.
