Most politically minded people are at best, slacktivists.  For example, in the primary and special run-off election, hardly anyone bothered to show up and vote, even though the primary is when there is real political change.  In the general election, everyone votes, but by then, the outcome is already assumed.  On average, people do not campaign or donate for candidates they like, or join a political club, or even attend a community board meeting.

The local political machine and the local media are in perfect lockstep.  Even though print media is dead for anyone with a computer, the base of voters tends to be seniors and immigrants and less likely to be wired.  I have said time and again how the Western Queens Gazette has never reported anything critical about the elected leaders, police activities and officials and local organizations in Astoria.  It presents a perfect Potemkin village where everything is right and just and we should just be happy to live here.

Lynn Serpe and Jerry Khan are earnest, but they have no chance of winning.  The problem is that people vote for their favorite team; if they are registered Democrats, they vote Democrat, if they are registered Republicans, they vote Republican and make no real effort to research the issues and the candidates.  The irony is that Astoria is overwhelmingly Democrat and generally about as conservative as Republicans.

Bill Thompson and his campaign is so anemic that he comes off as an empty suit.  Bloomberg's campaign is a marketing juggernaut and it is likely he will take the third term.  A few weeks ago, I asked a Bloomberg campaign worker canvassing inside my apartment building and taking notes of all the names on the mailboxes to leave.  I have since received a whole collection of Bloomberg leaflet mailings.  It speaks to how money buys political office.
