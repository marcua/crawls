Noisy Streets -- not sure what you mean?
Coming back home late from partying -- we have our lights on a timer.  Birds need a fairly regularly scheduled amount of daylight/nighttime.  If we know we're going to be out late, the lights go off around 9ish, and the bird goes to sleep.
My parents are also in Jersey, and have several parrots, so if we're going away, the bird will usually stay with them.  Failing that, I would board him at Birdcamp, which is in Manhattan, around 53rd & 2nd Ave.  birdcamp.com
Also, we buy all our food and supplies there.  They know what they're doing there :)
For an avian vet, I like Dr. Quesenberry at the Animal Medical Center of NY -- amcny.org  I was using a vet in Astoria for a while, but birds are really a specialty... didn't find anyone in the area that was knowledgeable enough.
We haven't had any complaints from the neighbors.
We told our landlord we were getting a 'small bird' and he waved and said it was fine.

Post by: DancNspin on October 12, 2009, 11:00:22 PM
Not sure what you mean, but all feathers start out as blood feathers.  The below is from http://www.peteducation.com/article.cfm?c=15+1829&aid=2776 (http://www.peteducation.com/article.cfm?c=15+1829&aid=2776)
Like hair, feathers develop in a specialized area in the skin called a follicle. As a new feather develops, it has an artery and vein that extends up through the shaft and nourishes the feather. A feather at this stage is called a blood feather. Due to the color of the blood supply, the shaft of a blood feather will appear dark, whereas the shaft of an older, mature feather will be white. A blood feather has a larger quill (calamus) than a mature feather. A blood feather starts out with a waxy keratin sheath that protects it while it grows. When the feather is mature, the blood supply will recede and the waxy sheath will be removed by the bird.

Although an adult bird will typically replace all of its feathers during a molt, the loss of feathers is staggered, often over several months, so the bird has enough feathers for flight and insulation. A molt is usually triggered by the change in day length or may occur after breeding. Some wild birds, such as goldfinches, who molt twice a year, change from a bright plumage during the breeding season to a more somber plumage for the rest of the year.

Post by: DancNspin on October 12, 2009, 11:41:58 PM
From what I can see of the article you posted, they are talking about a broken feather, and that it is better to pull out a broken feather since design of the shaft causes more blood loss.  Is this feather broken, or is it whole?   If it's a whole feather, I would leave it alone.   Only if it's sticking out and you're afraid it will truly present a problem of breaking in the future, would I maybe call your local vet and see what they say.  I'm doubting that a place like Birdcamp would want the liability, but it's possible and doesn't hurt to ask.  It would probably only take a few minutes in any case.  You could also do it yourself, but you would need an extra set of hands and some styptic pencils on hand (usually a good idea to keep some styptic pencils around anyway).
