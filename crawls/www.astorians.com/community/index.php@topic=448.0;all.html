It is amazing how cooperative people can be when you just ask.

Last summer on my vacation to the beach, an idyllic weekend picked up with a group of us enjoying a nice, calm, quiet summer evening in a little beach town.  The following day we'd hoped for more only to discover a large family, including once loud, outside playing obnoxious child had settled in next door.  We complained that evening at the racket  and into the next, amongst ourselves, until another "hardened" lifetime New Yorker showed up.  He'd been there under an hour, when upon noticing the volume level of the offending child, he calmly walked out to our front porch stoop and yelled "WILL YOU SHUT THAT KID UP?!"

Maybe it wasn't the most polite way of asking but it worked.

The rest of the week was as quiet as the first day.  The kid playing outside, enjoying summer in lower decibles.

It seems like there's something inbetween the extremes though.  That instead of letting a problem irritate you to the point of yelling or not asking for a conversation to be moved outside for fear of seeming impolite, we just get over ourselves already and learn to say "would you please take that outside."

  I must agree with you about certain people who dread the void or cacaphony of their own thoughts.  I hope that they are balanced in numbers with those that can listen to themselves and to each other.
  I don't believe that this is the fault of the individual however.
  Something is leading me to believe that there is an influence at work here, pervading society itself (not just here in the States) that finds it in it's best interest to transform each and every one of us into an ideal consumer.

  An ideal consumer does not choose.
  An ideal consumer does not think.
  An ideal consumer feels only existential dread when not consuming the array of products (eatable, watchable, readable) that are layed out before  us at very small cost to those who would reap the benefits of our hard labour.

Noise can be used as a weapon to destabilize our thought processes.  And, in addition to literal noise, there is semantic noise.  This kind of noise is even more pernicious in that it is able to invade our very souls as it were, becoming internalized, almost a part of us, telling us what we think we want, what would be good for us and what we should avoid at all costs.

Someone who says to me, "I can't hear myself think" is in a very bad place indeed and would do well to get out of the situation as soon as possible as if his or her life depended on it, just as if someone that were drowning couldn't feel themself breathe.

Well, that's my take on it anyway.  But far from speaking from a soap box I, myself find myself entangled in this very same pervasive kind of noise which is internal as well as external causing me to feel restless and unsatisfied, prohibiting me from really just existing, being in the moment and living life on it's own terms without all the extraneous trivial garbage.
------------------------------------

Concerning the ipod,  you are right, it is not a radio.  However, you can download from the internet, radio shows, songs, audiobooks, lectures, etc. (this can even be done automatically) by connecting the device to a computer.  The storage capacity for these little things is enormous.  I personally don't use earphones but rather hook it up to those cheapo $10  pc speakers to play in my house while I'm working on the computer.
To play those audiofiles on my comp while I'm working would eat up too many resources and inhibit my work.
I also take the ipod along when I have to go on a trip so it acts as a sort of portable audiolibrary.

  Internet radio had been around for a long time with a lot of great stuff.  What ipods have done is draw attention to this medium to many people who didn't know it even existed.  Not only that, but the popularity of "podcasting" (I know it's a funny term) has inspired many people to take the media into their own hands and experiment in internet broadcasting themselves.  This is a great developement and will do wonders for a more accessible media.
