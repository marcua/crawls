Hi Astoria, my name is Hank I am 72 years old. I grew up in Astoria many years ago. I lived at 23-20 32nd St. which is between 23 Ave. and 23 Rd. It was my maternal grandparents home and we lived in the downstairs apt. We lived there until 1951 when we moved to 38th Ave. and 30th St. when the house was sold after my grandparents passing. We lived there until 1955 when I graduated from LIC High School and we moved to New Jersey. I still hung around with my friends in the Ditmars/31st Street area since LIC was dead.

Using Google's Street View I see that the Ditmars theater is now a post office and the Grand Theater is now a Key Food market, they were great places to spend a Sat. watching a bunch of cartoons, serials and a couple of current films. A bargain at 12 cents as was the Astoria Pool at the same price.

Hanlon's bar is still there but the rest of 31st St. is new to me. There was a small bowling alley of about 16 lanes beneath the East side El exit at the Ditmars station, one level below the street level when you came down the stairs. It closed in the Summer since there was no A/C and the wooden alleys warped from the heat. There was also a pool hall across the street one flight up above the street at the West side exit stairs. Neither of them were the family type bowling alley or pool room of today. They sold beer to anyone with the price and there were always big money games going on among the gamblers. Part of growing up.

The pool hall was above a night spot on the ground level where our local hero Tony Bennet sang under the name Joe Bari. Tony lived on 33rd st. right around the corner from me and his cousin was my best friend, Tony's brother John took us fishing. Tony was Mr. Cool with the first pair of mirrored sunglasses us local boys ever saw.

At the South end of 32nd St. at 24th Ave. the street went up a hill as it does now but it was not paved and ended up in a steep hill going down toward 31st St. where there is now a parking lot and a store, that was called Chinky Hill and was the neighborhood place for sleigh riding. The very un-PC name was a mystery since there were no Chinese families in the entire area. I since found out there were Chinese farms up there with the farmers selling their produce to Chinese restaurants in Manhattan. There was also a pearl button factory there before my time but the whole area was still littered with mother of pearl pieces that were what they used for the buttons. My grandparents told me about that so it was probably there in the 1930's. It might be the old building on the West side going up the hill, that was there when I was a kid but was never anything but an empty old building.

My last visit to Astoria was twenty years ago and I stopped in Bohemian Hall for a couple of beers and some memories. That was a busy place with parties, and big celebrations on Memorial Day, July 4th, Labor Day and any other reason for getting together to eat, drink and dance the polka. My moms family were Czech immigrants and Astoria had a large Czech population at the time. 

This probably belongs in a nostalgia forum but I could not find one. I found a lot of interesting info on my old home town while doing some research on my ancestors. There is more than meets the eye in our town's history.

Thank you for the responses, I see Astoria is still a friendly place.

Naturally I did hang out with a couple of Greek kids, plus some Italians, Irish, etc. it was a mixed bag especially during WWll when we had kids in P.S.85 from India, Africa and other countries who's parents were able to escape the war. Not near the park though, usually on 31st St. in a soda shop named Ringen's or the bowling alley or pool hall.

Chunky Hill is a new one to me. The hill was featured in a couple of films one being Goodfellas where it served as the neighborhood Henry Hill grew up in and met all his gangster buddies. Plenty of Astoria shots in that one. Another is an independent film that was written by a former local kid titled A Guide to Recognizing Your Saints. It never made it big but has Chaz Palminteir and Robert Downey in it and is an interesting story of growing up in Astoria, great shots of Astoria Pool at night and again Chinky Hill serving as home base for the principles.

All my old pictures are of family with many taken at 23-20 32nd St. some back when it was called Park Place or 3rd Ave. under one of Astoria's various old street naming schemes. 23rd Road had the exotic sounding name Pleasure Place, 23rd Ave. was Potter Ave. and 31st. Street was called DeBevoise St. or 2nd Ave. depending on the time frame. My favorite site for old Astoria pictures is http://astoriahistory.smugmug.com/  You can spend hours looking at old pics and seeing how empty parts of Astoria were with active farms even in the 1930's and early 40's. Hello again. I hope everyone had a nice weekend.

Here are a couple of more sites for historic photos. The first is one you may already be familiar with: http://www.forgotten-ny.com/ lots of interesting facts. Another is the extensive NY Public Library site that has a huge collection of photos of all types, if you do a search for Queens NY there are many of Astoria. A library card is not required.
http://digitalgallery.nypl.org/nypldigital/index.cfm .

I did another Street View visit to the Ditmars area and found some things had not changed. The funeral home near Immaculate Conception church is still there as is LaGuli the pastry shop. The flower shop next to the church used to be a well known ice cream parlor named Jacobi's. It was a busy family place with more adults than kids as customers. The N.E. corner of Ditmars and 31st St was the home of Woolworth's with a McClellans 5 and 10 just East across 32nd St. Across the street on the South side of Ditmars was a pizza joint with the best pie in town, thin crust, oily, and loaded with cheese. You flipped the pointy end back to keep it from dripping in your lap.

Over on 23rd Ave. and 31st St. there was Marrotta's pharmacy on the N.E. corner, the Marrottas lived in the apt. house on 32nd St. near 23rd Rd., me and their son Arty went to P.S. 85 together. On the S.E. corner where there is now a restaurant was Beebe's Radio shop. Mr. Beebe put a 10" TV in his window and piped a speaker outside so the neighborhood could enjoy the new wonder of television, which he also started to sell in addition to fixing radios. Adults brought a chair and us kids sat on the ground and watched the few movies and programs that were on at night. An averaqe night before it got cold would see about 10-15 people out there.

Just East in the middle of the same block was Joe Pospisil's bicycle repair and rental shop. Joe was probably responsible for 98% of the local kids being able to ride a two wheeler. He rented Schwinns and Columbias for ten cents per hour since very few kids had their own bike. If you didn't have a dime Joe would let you make some money sweeping out the store or taking out the trash, a great member of the neighborhood who was like every kids uncle, he even sawed off the handles on worn out brooms to make us stickball bats.

The single house across on the North side of the street was the Marino family who owned a fish store next to the house. I went to school with their son Andy. East of Marino's where there is now a tax preparer was Sam's candy store. Sam Abramson and his wife and two kids lived in back of the store where they sold candy, cigarettes, razor blades, shoelaces, yo-yos, newspapers, magazines comic books and anything else Sam could hang up or squeeze into his little shop along with the 6 stools for someone who wanted coffee or a Coke, ice cream soda, egg cream, tuna on rye etc. You were lucky to get a seat though because at least two stools were always used to store the daily paper. Sunday morning my folks would give me a quarter to get the NY Daily News,(5cents) the NY Daily Mirror(5cents) and the Journal American (10cents) Sunday editions. I made a nickel on the deal. I went to school with Bernie Abramson who was the only Jewish kid in the neighborhood but never got any crap about it.

See ya later for stickball under the railroad trestle on 33rd St. or a game of marbles in the empty lot.  :-)

Thanks for the kind words Wooly.

Bohemian Hall was a big part of neighborhood entertainment and events back in my time of the 40's and early 50's. The large Bohemian population supported the activities and I think everyone of any nationality enjoyed the celebrations out in the beer garden on Memorial Day, July 4th and Labor Day. There were many dances and dinners and parties for many easily found reasons by a group who liked to eat, drink and dance the polka until the wee hours of the morning. I was only five or six and being carried home by my Irish born father who was singing along with my Czech mother and grandparents is a nice memory, him singing in Czech with a brogue still brings a smile to my face. He fit in well with these fun loving Bohemians.

Bohemian Hall also hosted a Sokol organization. A carry over from the old country that promoted exercise programs and other activities for the boys one and all regardless of national background. This was a very serious group and to the leaders and instructors exercise and health were not to be taken lightly. We learned and practiced rope climbing, broad jumps, high jumping, the parallel bars and other competitive skills along with running, push ups, pull ups etc. Once per year we went to a meet in West Jersey to compete with other Sokol groups. One year of that was enough for most kids.

I was in Astoria about twenty years ago and stopped in to Bohemian Hall for a beer. I had not been there in at least 30 years and everything was the same. The bartender let me wander around outside and downstairs and it brought back a lot of memories. There had been a two lane bowling alley downstairs that had been removed, a major renovation.

I'm glad someone is enjoying the Astoria history lesson, I have many items of interest to me that others may enjoy if you are a history buff as I am. I will gladly post items as time goes by and I'm more than happy to keep Astoria's past alive.

I am opening a separate topic for a longer newspaper item from 1922 regarding the Chinese farms on Chinky Hill so as not to clutter up this thread with something not everyone may be interested in.

Hi Tiger welcome to the discussion. I was born in 1938 and lived at 23-20 32 St. until about 1951 when we had to move to 38th Ave in LIC but I continued to hang out at what we called Ditmars which was actually the area on 31st St. between 23rd Ave and Ditmars Ave., I guess the el stop defined the neighborhood. My family hijacked me to Jersey in 1955


I rely on Google's Street View for current conditions so based on that I will try to answer your question using compass points for locations. I remember the N/E corner presently occupied by a Lefkos Cafe was home to Ray's Pharmacy owned by Ray Marrotta who lived in the apt house on 32nd St. and 23rd Rd. I went to school with his son Arthur. The S/E corner shows a Gyro Souvlaki restaurant and that was home to Beebe Radio only it was a smaller space which did not extend as far East as it does today.
I had posted that Mr. Beebe had put about a 10" standard for the time TV in his 23rd Ave window and piped out the audio so people could watch this new miracle that was going to ruin his radio sales and repair trade. I went to school with his daughter.


The audience started to gather after supper and the corner was soon full of folding chairs and kids just sitting on the sidewalk watching whatever came on. TV also ruined sitting in front of your house and having the neighbors stop by for a glass of beer or an ice tea.


The S/W corner where the bank is was an empty lot as I recall. Sadly I have no recollection of what was on the corner where the present day pharmacy is so I hope that was not your main question. A bit North of that pharmacy was Phil's deli who served great hot dogs covered in cole slaw, Phil did not believe in sauerkraut and I grew up thinking slaw was the natural topping for a dog. A bit West of the pharmacy was a small shop that sold fresh ground coffee, I see there is still a coffee shop there, continuity is great.


Enough for now about one corner in my enjoyable childhood in the Astoria neighborhood. Glad to answer more if I can. Hank

I too am an old newcomer from Astoria.... I grew up on 42nd Street between 28th & 30th Avenues (a few blocks from St. Joseph's) where I went to elementary school.  I now live in Long Island but have very fond memories.  I get very emotional when I'm there -- since I've lost my grandparents (where I spent most of my time), my mom and best friend who lived on 41st Street.  Life was so much easier then.  Walking home from school, and then running out to play until Baba called for me hanging out of the kitchen window telling me supper was ready.  We could entertain ourselves just sitting on the stoop talking to our friends, or sitting on the fire escape listening to music.  Waiting for Mr. Softee and his wonderful jingle to come down your street on summer nights.  And what about the rides on wheels!  Astoria Pool.  Riding your bike all day.  Making club houses out of old boxes people were throwing away.  Going EVERYDAY to the bakery for fresh bread... and the BEST apple cream cake from the Steinway Bakery.  Having all of your grandparents and parents friends living withint blocks from us - stopping by for a cup of coffee.  My mother was from Yugoslavia and my father from Italy and that was mostly what the population consisted of, as well as Irish and Greek.  Even to this day, when I pass my old apartment - I can see lives gone by.  It makes me sad to think that my children will never really understand how beautifully simple life was.

Hi Pat, the hill was an empty lot during my time which must have been just before the time you speak of since no one seemed to have any interest in what was going on there.


I graduated from P.S. 85 in 1950 and then went to JHS 141 as you did. Later to LIC H.S. since we had moved to 38th Ave and 30th St. and the rules were if you lived West of the el you went to LIC, East of the el you went to Bryant.


I have posted a lot of info here about the hill since it always seemed a mystery as to it's name and why the then unpaved street was littered in mother-of-pearl. My grandparents said there was a button factory up there and that was the reason since prior to plastic  buttons were made from mother-of-pearl but they had no idea on why it was called Chinky Hill. The button factory employed mainly Czech immigrants from the neighborhood since a Czech had invented the machine used in button making, my mom's family were Czech so that came full circle researching their past in Astoria, it was an interesting trip.


Our landmark Chinky Hill has been in a couple of films including the first part of Goodfellows where it doubled for the Bklyn neighborhood where the story actually took place, lots of shots of P.S.85 in the background. The other film where it played a big part is A Guide to Recognizing Your Saints with Robert Downey Jr. among others. The main character lives in one of the older houses on the East side of the street going up the hill and a lot of the action takes place on 31st St. and 24th Ave as well as Astoria Pool. It's great to see the old neighborhood whenever I feel like running the DVDs. If you haven't seen Saints it's worth buying just for the trip back to Astoria.
