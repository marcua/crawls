Its a beautiful day today, cool, clear and crisp outside, just like 9/11 2001.  Im feeling sad and down.  Watched the French brothers documentary on 9/11 last night instead of the ABC show.  Very sad and upsetting.  Just like 2001, im home working on the computer and watching events on the news.  Went out did some errands but feeling sort of useless. 

Everything is so political now and partisan and mean.  I came away from last nights show thinking that this was one event (horrible huge and terrible) but one event. Look at all the life, effort, politics and money spent on this and so little has actually been accomplished.

The other feeling I came away with from last nights show and todays events is a sense of love, power, courage and spirit in those who have worked to help each other just because we are all human. Maybe that is the best thing to hold on to today.  9/11 was tragic yet also showed the goodness of the human spirit.

Nothing like what has ever happened before will happen again. We won't let it happen because we know better. We the common and uncommon people will watch with delight or dismay the actions of the elected and unelected officials taking steps to provide the best solutions they have available while adhering to the compass that is their agenda. We are not helpless to act and react. We have no need to be passive spectators. There is a hole in Pennsylvania where the remains of the first awakenings of our resolve lie buried these five years.

Someday, there may come a time in which a new and unimaginable act of horror may surprise us. It may suck the air from our lungs and sting our red and swollen eyes and confound us with how to explain what happened to our children, but it will not be because we did not try to stop it. We will look to place blame and make sweeping changes, accusations. The compass that is our agenda may find a new magnetic north, but first we will have come together again and helped each other and comforted one another.

All the while life will have gone on and more beautiful days will have come and gone. We will still be here for each other. The beauty of that will not fade.

I'm having a difficult time with all the media coverage and pomp that surrounds this date now.  I get a sense from the rest of the country that it is a distant memory to many of them.  They have no problems with all the movies being made, and docu-dramas, etc...  But as someone who witnessed it, it's still very fresh in my mind.  I feel a lack of sensitivity regarding this date.  Particularly from politicians to use as election fodder to boost their poll numbers.  Us New Yorkers experienced it.  It's something we'll have to live with the rest of our lives.  And most of us will have extremely vivid memories of it.  I feel very isolated as a New Yorker today from the rest of the country, because I just don't believe the rest of the country fully grasps the magnitude of the events of that day and what it was actually like to be there watching those towers burn.

But with that in mind, I also feel that it is time to try and move forward, in a positive way.  To stop treading so lightly.  Moments of silence and reading of 3,000 names rings hollow to me.  Names just blend together, especially a full 5 years after the events.  Perhaps we should do more to celebrate the opportunities that lie ahead of us for the memorial services.  It seems you could simply play the video tapes from the last 3 years memorial services on tv, and nobody would even know the difference that it's not live.  We have a tremendous opportunity to move forward, and instead of dwelling on the terrible day that occurred, look forward.  It seems a more fitting way to honor those who have died.  I fear that in 100 years, when all people directly involved and their children are all dead, we're still going to be reading the names of the victims and it will be meaningless. And each year they do the reading of the names, I feel it loses meaning, and with it, the overall impact of the date.  As a city known for constant motion, I feel we've shown a lack of it during these 5 years.

Hope, love, desire, dreams, PEACE!

9/11 was a strange day for me because I was in Tel Aviv, which in 2001 was hit by a few terrorist attacks itself, and I was in the Israel's tallest high rise for a job interview. I get downstairs where there is a small shopping center, it's afternoon Israel time and I'm watching live on television on CNN what happened here... it was very surreal to say the least

In the meantime I've come to grad school in NYC and as a grad student, I worked with the FDNY Liberty project, which provides grief counseling and intervention for firefighter families. I was involved with the support project for the 9/11 families and I transcribed the intimate stories of several victim families. I know that five years later, many of the wives don't want to hear the memorial speeches or the commercial movies, or anything else. This day is hard enough for them, most of them want to be left alone and grieve in silence.  For their children, on the other hand, the memorials have been a real booster, helping preserve their fathers' memory as persons that put their lives on the line.

Then, last year, I was working on a project with the OAG Healthcare Bureau. My partner from the project eventually described for me the havoc and fear of being in the vincinity ground zero on that day. Her personal story made it all the more real for me just how tragic this event was.

I hope that the federal government begins finally to accept responsibility for all the other victims, those that continue to suffer from dibiltating chronic illness due to the cleanup in the aftermath of the tragedy. They too are heroes and they too need to get our praise and recognition.

I'm also finding today more difficult than I perhaps expected to. And absolutely, I felt a vague uneasiness as I walked out this morning as thought automatically, "what an incredibly beautiful day." Five years is a benchmark as far as anniversaries, certainly. I'm sure that's another reason today feels harder. I've spoken to my mother, had friends come down to my office to reminisce at work, and we all seem to be feeling this in a similarly strong way this year, and have found ourselves surprised by it. Strange, because I'm ashamed to say, I barely remember last year.

Yet, not only can I remember that day and each moment of it with aching clarity, but every day that followed it for a week and more. I remember coming out of the subway and standing in a doorway on Second Avenue and 43rd Street on Friday morning, (9.14.01) watching the rain fall, and for some reason, the rain, the torrential violence of it broke me down for the first time since the events took place. And the following day, 9.15.01, driving in to the city to bring in donations, etc. and being the only other car on 23rd Street and Fifth Avenue at 11:00 a.m. except for the fully armed military truck next to me at the light. Sitting there looking straight down 23rd Street with nothing but NJ in front of me. The chills two months later when the plane crashed a few feet from the home where my father then lived in Rockaway and thinking it was happening all over again and that this time I would not escape "unscathed." As I am perhaps one of the few natives who did not have a direct loss of a friend or loved one that day.

I can remember one month, two, six, and one year later, equally so. The next few, not so much. I suppose this is not so uncommon, either. I don't know. It's not meant to  be callous, I certainly know that I sat and reconsidered the day and the events at least for a period of time on each of those anniversaries.  But, as I said, today (and actually, this has been true for much of last week, as well), I am feeling a similar melancholy that I experienced in the few months after the original day. It's more acute, certainly. A part of that has to do (from a very personal perspective) with looking back and thinking, "Five years is a long time. What have I done in five years? How has it all gotten past me like this?" And the rest has to do with not only the scar of the day, but all the other evil that has come from this administration during that time.

As far as not dwelling or not moving on, well, as I see it, I've had plenty of good come to me since that day. I do not write the above to dwell on the morbid. I celebrate the good as often as I can every other day of the year and try to recognize as much of it as I can when it happens. I would do that with or without 9.11.01 to give me perspective. And yes, I will even do that today as much as I can. But to recall the events on the day they occurred, and allow them to remain powerful has nothing to do with not getting on with your life for me, personally. It is only when you let something like this overwhelm your thoughts and actions (be it 9/11 or any other personal strife) that you have ceased to live.

So. Essentially. Yes. Sad. More than anticipated.
