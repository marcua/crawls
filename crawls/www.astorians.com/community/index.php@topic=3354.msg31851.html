first off, I have to agree with Jennifer- what the heck kind of spell check let you get away with "responce" ?

but back to your original point. It takes time. I have gotten to know a lot of people in my building, and would definitely feel comfortable knocking on more than a few doors if I needed help. But it took a while. I grew up in NY and it never occurred to me to go up to my neighbors and say, "hi, I just moved in". But people did come up to me and ask, and now, if I see someone new, I will go up and ask "hey, did you just move in?" and introduce myself. Of course, my building is a co-op and I think the residents have more of a vested interest in what is going on (my cousin stayed in my apt for a week shortly after I moved in and my neighbor went up to her the first time she saw her and asked who she was and if she was subletting my apt (subletting is not looked upon favorably in my building), now she knows I travel a lot and is not surprised when she sees a catsitter at my door... I also make a point of letting her know if I am going away).

I think the other issue in meeting neighbors is very much a question of timing. I see people I never knew lived in my building when I leave for work 10 minutes later than usual...

We do also have an annual X-mas party :)

It's luck of the draw - some people want to be friends with their neighbors and others don't.  I don't want to hang out with my neighbors b/c like Jonathan said - we don't have anything in common.  But when there was a teacher upstairs who used to live in Seattle (i'm a teacher, used to live in Seattle) well hey... of course we bonded (but we didn't see each other very often). Even though we're not friends with the folks on the other floors and other buildings now - we're all friendly.  I've brought the upstairs neighbors gallons of beer that I wanted out of my refrigerator after a party and they've brought me zucchini bread.  My top floor neighbor had a plant that she didn't think was doing well so she gave it to me and asked that I plant it in the garden. 

When I started talking to the next-door-neighbors and asking why they don't use their backyard (that is adjacent to ours) Jonathan thought I'd lost my mind!! "WHY DO YOU WANT THEM BACK THERE??"  He had a point.  We have privacy as long as the neighbors aren't using their yard.  If they start using  their yard we'll lose this.  And I think this is what a lot of neighbors fear.  If you start talking to your neighbors they might want to start hanging out.  And once you start hanging out - well... how do you get rid of them??  New York is a crowded place.  For some - home is the only place where they can go to be alone and get away from ALL THOSE PEOPLE.  Some people don't want their neighbors to be anything more than strangers. 

All that said.... I miss life in the house where I grew up.  Friends and relatives would drop by all the time.  Grandparents had their own keys, aunts and uncles lived near by.  There was always coffee brewing and cookies on the table.  I miss that.  But that existed in a world with a lot of SPACE and a stay at home mom and aunts and grandmothers. 


In the meantime - two buildings down there is a new family of 4 with two of the cutest little girls ever (3 and 5 yrs old).  They're always outside playing with either mom or dad or both sitting on the stoop (dad is actually usually working - he's the new building super).  Right now we're on a "hi" basis.  We've exchanged a few more words but not much.  I ordered cupcakes for my homeroom students tomorrow and I ordered extras so that I can bring them to my new neighbors.  I don't expect to become friends with these folk.  But it's a neighborly gesture and I can't wait to see the girls' reactions.
