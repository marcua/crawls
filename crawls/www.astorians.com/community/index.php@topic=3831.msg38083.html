So I guess CBGB's has finally shut its doors.  It was on the verge of closing for so long, that I became used to the situation, and never bothered to pay it a visit while I had the chance (that was a mistake).  I am such a big fan of a lot of the bands that came out of there in the 70's and 80's (espcially Queens' very own Ramones), but I arrived in NYC only in 2001, and at that point was not about to shell out a bunch of money to see bad punk rock.  It has only hit me now that the quintessential CBGB's experience WOULD have been to go hear some bad punk rock.  Well, a lesson learned too late.  At least I got to see the Bouncing Souls in Washington Square Park at a free show to "save CBGB's" last spring- they played "Argyle," and everyone jumped around an moshed like crazy.

does anyone have any CBGB's experiences they want to share?

I think this enforces my point that music is dead.  And my understanding is that CB's wasn't pulling people in, and wasn't for ages until people heard that it was closing, and as I said, it's a shame.  It is a landmark, sure.  It has nothing to do with the place not being a jazz club.  As a matter of fact, they had jazz there in one of the rooms. As far as Detour, it actually has people still showing up, and would be turned into a restaurant when it's closing.  But the landlord is trying to screw them out of the lease so they can jack their rents.  My point is that most places that close get turned into a cultural void, draining the city of yet another place to see/play music.  CB's is a very unique situation.  The building is owned by a homeless advocacy group, and isn't looking to just make a profit, as most landlords do when they close music venues.

I was responding more to the fact that Dirk automatically assumed that the place was being turned into an elite expensive condo complex, which simply isn't true.  So I don't feel quite as bad about a place closing if the building will be put to good use, and the landlord actually is trying to do something responsible.
