Core Staffing

http://www.employcore.com/

They paid fairly well, and were responsive. It helped that I was putting myself out there as Mr. Go-To Guy, friendly, hard-working, and all that hoo-ha.

Like most temp agencies, if you don't have mad skillz with MS Office, typing, etc., they'll try to get you to work on the cheap. Remember, their first loyalty is to the client -- the companies hiring the temps (and paying the bills). So, if you are totally professional, punctual, etc., even in the face of less-than-pleasant assignments, it makes the temp agency look good, and can put you in good stead for better assigmments (and more money).

Also, scour this website: http://www.panix.com/~grvsmth/redguide/intro.html

Hey Merm, I just saw your post.  I never actually wound up going to any temp agencies, despite all the great recommendations on this thread (*hangs head in shame*).  My situation is a little complicated because I'm a student from the Boston area although I now live in New York, so other options that I could have taken advantage of at school, like being a teaching assistant or research assistant, are now off-limits or inconvenient because of the distance.  So what I did was network like crazy with local contacts that my professors provided - I'm an art history grad student, so this mostly meant museum people and local professors.  It took a good 3-4 months to hustle up a reasonable amount of freelance work, and now I have 4 jobs!  I work retail one day a week, and I'm also doing a translation project, scanning slides, and compiling digital images.  I would strongly recommend that you capitalize on any specialized skills that you've developed as a student - any verbal/computer/language skills.  The work will be more interesting to you and will likely pay more.  Don't be embarrassed to let people know that you're looking for work - if they keep you in mind, they'll think of you if an opportunity comes up.  You can also try tutoring or proofreading.  I actually took a two-day course on legal proofreading as a back-up.  The info for the course that I took is here: http://www.learnproofreading.com/index.html.  The instructor is very helpful about giving advice on finding proofreading jobs and on how to format your resume.  She also teaches the same course at NYU.  The last thing is that if you don't want to appear overqualified, don't shoot yourself in the foot by having a too-long resume - keep it to one page only.  I hope this helps - let me know how your job search turns out!  Sigh, now if only I had time for my own work...

Oops, I just re-read your post and saw that your field is corporate communications.  My impression is that temp work is a great way to break into industry work, so I would definitely give that a try if you're looking for something full-time.  I should have clarified that I was looking for part-time only, so that was how I wound up with all these freelance gigs - although now I'm getting perilously close to the full-time mark.  Good luck!
