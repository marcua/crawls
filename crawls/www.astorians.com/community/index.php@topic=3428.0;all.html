Korean barbeque is very meat-centric, mostly beef.  You sit at a table with a fire in the middle and they bring you the meat (and numerous condiments).  It is not vegetarian friendly.  Mongolian barbeque is veggie friendly and you choose the specific ingredients for them to cook (reputable places clean off the cooking surface between each sauteeing).  I always thought of it as a west coast sort of thing; didn't know there was Mongolian BBQ out here!  Yum!

a brief description of Mongolian and Korean BBQ
« Reply #21 on: December 25, 2006, 11:48:21 AM »
Hi everyone, here's a description of the two based on my personal experience and from what I've heard while living in Beijing, Taiwan and the US.

Mongolian BBQ and Korean BBQ are completely different.  Interestingly, "Mongolian" BBQ found in US restaurants, actually has its origin in Taiwan, although it can be found all over mainland China nowadays as well.  It actually didn't originate in inner monglia as the name would suggest.  When you eat Monglian BBQ, you choose your vegetables, meat, sauce, etc. and it is all thrown onto a large hot skillet (traditionally, the skillet is the size of a small coffee table--it's very large).  Then it is thrown back into the bowl and each individual person has her own bowl  I live in Beijing for half the year (and astoria the other half) and Mongolian BBQ has a mainly touristy side to it.

In Korean BBQ, there are two types of skillets that I know of.  One is a rectangular one that is slanted with a space for the oil and fat to drip out into a hole in the table into a catch.  Another one is on a round flat skillet.  Meat and vegetables are brought out and everybody eats it family style.  Depending on how traditional, the server will come out and place the  meat on the skillet for you and flip the meat and then cut it when it's ready.  Kimchee is sometimes put on the skillet at the bottom of the rectangular skillet and it catches alot of the grease--unhealthy but incredibly tasty.  It can be served with rice depending on what you want.  You can also request that they grill it for you (especially if you are in suits and you don't want to smoke to get over your clothes).  Usually, it is meat based but you can order mushrooms, onions, etc. but not much greens besides the side dishes.  Lettuce is sometimes brought out with the galbi for you to roll (I've heard that traditionally women don't roll it but men do--this may be a regional distinction as not all of my Korean friends agree and my Korean friends are not Korean-American but Koreans living in Beijing for school).  The greatest concentration of foreigners in Beijing are Korean and everytime I eat Korean BBQ with a different group of Koreans, the names are different, the traditions have some slight variation, etc.  So what I've described is just the very basic types, I'm sure there are many many different kinds and methods to Korean BBQ.
