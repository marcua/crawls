« on: December 10, 2007, 07:55:26 AM »
I have a question for those of you who are better versed than I am in city planning regulations.
 
When the Department City Planning published its "Plan for Long Island City: A Framework for Development" in the mid-1990s

http://www.nyc.gov/html/dcp/html/pub/lic.shtml

It was my understanding that most of the proposed development was to be concentrated south of Queens Plaza, specifically along Jackson Avenue and that changes in zoning were to be implemented that allowed the area to be changed from primarily manufacturing to mixed-use developments that combined commercial, manufacturing and residential.

Can someone tell me what happened to the commercial development and why is there so much residential construction on Queens Plaza north?

--When were the zoning regulations changed form manufacturing/commercial to residential (on QPNorth)?

--Was this part of the original plan or were regulations amended somewhere along the line to focus on residential?

--Also, how is it possible that developers are erecting hi-rises, which appear to be hotels, much further north into existing residential areas of Dutch Kills?

I read an article recently saying that Dutch Kills residents were rushing to try a close a loophole. More power to them I say but can someone tell me how this loophole appeared – was it magic?

http://www.nydailynews.com/ny_local/queens/2007/11/19/2007-11-19_neighborhood_says_long_island_city_build.html

I’ve been following the plans to redevelop LIC (obviously not too closely) and had thought the plan had some good points (though I did have some misgivings)

The focus on commercial development meant a change from mainly manufacturing to office but at least the character of the area would be somewhat maintained as well as its role as center for jobs and employment.

Why is it then that every building that seems to be going up is residential in nature? Aside from the additional buildings around Citicorp are there any other commercial buildings going up or existing buildings being redeveloped as offices?

I don't know when/if QP north was added to the plan, but it is only a very small portion of QP north. If you look at the executive summary for the rezoning http://www.nyc.gov/html/dcp/html/lic/lic2.shtml You can see it is pretty much only 1 block on the northside that made it in.

As for why residential vs commercial/office I believe it is primarily for a few reasons.

1)Quicker returns. When a condo building is completely sold out the developer has recouped all their money and can call it a day. With office buildings it is very different. The developer on office space, in the case of companies like vornado and Extell, owns and manages the building after they build it. They lease the space out instead of selling it, so it often takes longer to realize their return.
2) Desirability. The market for office space outside of the CBD is pretty limited. For many reasons (transportation being a major one) LIC just isn't that attractive for office space.

The reality is that city planning failed terribly in their efforts to create a fourth business district. The only major tenant, Met Life, is running shrieking back to the city. I have a good friend whose wife works there, and she says that everyone hates the location. When I mentioned to her the real estate types who are trying to market the area as an upscale yuppie housing, well, we don't have to repeat her comments on a public board.

This area is served by excellent transportation. And the central business district in Manhattan, has not only expanded beyond its tradtional bounds, but into other areas, in Brooklyn and Jersey and even Westchester and Conn. All are now considered attractive communities and prime commerical space.

In Queens, the story is different. The effort to attract commerical tenants has backfired terribly. It is just about finished. Even MOMAQueens left with nary a backward glance. Now desperate planners are falling back on making the area purely residential.

City planning is spinning its wheels, and like with the failed 2012 Olympics effort, their creativity is limited to one tired half-baked blockbuster idea after another. They can put in four 50 story buildings for Silvercup and want us to believe that the traffic can be handled with fine tuning one traffic light. When the folks in Dutch Kills asked who approved 14 hotels in their tiny community, everyone, from the mayor's office, to city planning, just shrugged. Afterall, they don't live in Dutch Kills.

The point is that the entire effort at making LIC an attractive area is sputtering - the folks over a Arris just discovered they face an active railyard. Simple amenities, as dry cleaners and shopping, just doesn't exist in the community (oh yes, we hear stories of them getting ... one!) Residents and visitors alike on their more candid moments, liken the area to Newport in Pavonia near Jersey City. Highrise after highrise with no street life, no community, no soul.

And what of the long term residents? Cracked walls and windows facing 15 story buildings will be a way of life for the people in Hunters Point and Dutch Kills from now on. Too late, they discovered what happens if you bring development in. You lose your community.

Their experience is a cautionary tale for all.

With the exception of the immigrant communities of Flushing or Elmhurst, development is scorned throughout the city (Ratnerville, Greenwich Village, West Side, East Side, Lower Manhattan, Greenpoint.) In the case of most of Queens, communities are frantically trying to downzone. Every effort to develop places, from Willets Point to downtown Jamaica, is met with spirited oppostion. Like a game of musical chairs, only the one left standing will lose - they will have to find the place to fit in the million that the mayor claims he wants here by 2030.

Only in western Queens is it welcomed - by the politicians, press, and community boards.

All this space, coming on the market just as the real estate bubble is about to burst, should make for some interesting situations.

The biggest difference in my opinion between DUMBO and LIC is that as you say, the DUMBO area is smaller and also I think manufacturing started to decline long before it did so in before LIC, leaving property open for re-development sooner.

Second, and more importantly, a large portion of land in DUMBO is controlled by a single real estate concern owned the Walentas family. They’ve been the driving force in converting former industrial spaces lofts to residential. The Walentas essentially created the brand “DUMBO” and have tried not only to convert structures to residential use but also to create a neighbourhood.

Long Island City is bigger, more diverse and until relatively recently, manufacturing was still vital to the area. It was never really ‘abandonded’ and open to for gentrification like other former industrial areas in the city that went into decline and were re-colonized.

There are more competing interests in LIC, which definitely has more players in the pot: manufacturers fighting to keep their space and maintain LIC as an industrial base (though that battle is probably now lost), existing residents, the city, and various real estate companies who see virgin territory ready for exploitation.

And unlike DUMBO, LIC has no single “vision”, for lack of a better word, so development is proceeding in a hodgepodge fashion. The master plan to create the fourth business district supposed to establish a sort of unity as to what should be built, but as you can see from what’s going up now it’s apparently “developers gone wild.”

From what I’ve read and seen, in my opinion the Walentas have done a decent job in preserving some of DUMBO’s historic buildings and have been somewhat successful in creating a neighborhood feel by keeping rents reasonable for small mom & pops type stores to operate. That’s a plus in my book.

But they aren’t perfect, being real estate guys after all. They’ve floated various plans to erect hi-rises in DUMBO that so far have been successfully thwarted by local residents seeking maintain the character of the area. It’s an ongoing situation so we’ll see what happens.

People and private market forces create the need for urban planning, not the other way around.  Comprehensive plans and zoning can only attempt to channel these forces in a beneficial way.

The push for the 4th CBD in LIC just won't work because the people aren't there for it.  Its been said before, but people dislike working in LIC.  I work in downtown LIC, and I love it, but only because I'm an Astorian.  My comrades from Brooklyn, Jersey, the Bronx, Manhattan and even the Island despise working here (and the folks from the island will hate it more once the East Side Access Project is finished and the Hunterspoint Station is closed).  Its takes longer to get here and the neighborhood amenities are not like what they were accustomed to in Manhattan.  This lessens the available workforce that a company can draw from, thereby making it less attractive for offices to settle in.

If offices really found this an attractive place to settle, it would have evolved into a 4th CBD regardless of any sort of planning.  Since the potential workforce draw is sparse, so are the businesses which thrive here: residential buildings, warehousing, light manufacturing.
