Gleason,  Gleason, Gleason what are we to do with you my friend?

The community you describe sounds more like a museum piece than the alive, vibrant, and diverse city neighborhood in which we currently reside and though you may prefer to live in a big city equivalent of Colonial Williamsburg -- I do not.

Many of your points are valid but you seem to rail against ANY change, which is both unfeasible and will, in the end, result in the decline of our neighborhood.

Who here has argued in favour of building 'broader bigger and better? Not one person o my knowledge. But if the neighborhood doesnt change and adapt, people will leave it and youll get your wish: a place frozen in time, but with no one around and buildings falling in disrepair. Think it wont happen? Tell that to the people of 1970s South Bronx.

When I read your postings, many times my first reaction is to nod in agreement but then something happens a switch is flipped or something.  Your arguments start to wander and become diatribes lumping current problems with societal changes that started, and sometimes ended, decades ago and Im left to wonder: "what are you on about?"

And on several occasions what are you on about turns into is he really saying what I think hes saying because Im left feeling very uncomfortable by what I read. Its one thing to moan about the powers that be, the real estate industry or the city, etc. I draw a line though, when the criticism ventures into discussion of the type of people who live here. Its an easy out when one is frustrated and I dont like it.

Im with you 100% on one issue -- erecting 1015 story towers next to one-, two- and three-family houses in areas such as Dutch Kills is unacceptable and must be stopped. But how about a smaller apartment building? Thats not out of character to whats already there and to me seems to be a reasonable comprise (if only a developer could be bothered with smaller projects such as these). But something tells me that youd still be unhappy.

I share many of the same frustrations you do as I look around as see whats happening but theres a difference between change, appropriate development and overdevelopment and to argue in favor of constructing in-scale buildings in on reasonable sites doesnt make me or anyone else Anti-Astoria.

As you can see from my other thread Im upset at the direction development has taken in LIC. A mixed-use commercial hub was proposed. What we are getting are luxury condos built in inappropriate locations -- and its my fear this will further impact further north into more residential areas.

I should stop now but at the risk of becoming diatribic myself Ill venture to address some of your specific points since you offered a dare:

Con Ed:
Yes the power failure is Con Eds fault combined with a flawed concept of privatisation and deregulation thats been crammed down our throats.

Increased competition was supposed to unleash the power of the market and result in lower energy bills. My Con Ed bill isnt any lower, is yours? In fact its been rising for quite sometime even before the current oil crisis.

Deregulation has enriched the moneypeople at the expense of the consumer. Its allowed Con Ed to shift its burdens and responsibilities onto other companies (which they deny and try push back on Con Ed), and to stop investing in maintenance and essential upgrades. I think we can all agree that increased development will aggravate the situation and I have no ideas on how it can be fixed.

Old Astoria and landmarking:
Do you know who I blame the owners of those grand houses who fought against establishing a landmark district and then sold out to developers knowing full well their properties would be torn down and monstrosities built in their place. You can say it was their right to do so and you can blame the city for not trying harder but lets face it if residents wanted the landmarking it would have been implemented.

Everyone in JH thought the world was going to end when the historic district was finally established but guess what? The exact opposite happened. The neighborhood has dramatically improved and the current thinking is that much of it is a result of landmarking and the leadership of committee. Lets hope the residents of Sunnyside Gardens see similar results and provide an example to the remaining owners in Old Astoria so we can save what little is left.

Schools:
What are you on about? I agree that District 30 along with many others in the city, especially Queens are bursting with students. But this is not a new issue and its not being ignored. Overcrowding has long been on the agenda and the Board of Ed has responded by building more schools, though not at a speed to everyones liking. What more do you want --mandatory birth control or a one-child policy? Or should we solve the problem by building more luxury condos who owners will likely not have children or, if they do, can afford to send them to private school?

Hospitals:
What are you on about? Boulevard Hospital was closed in what the late 70s, early 80s? I know because my parents fought hard to keep it open. Im lost here. That battle was over nearly 30 years ago and youre still not over it? And how is the current structure of the healthcare system the fault of current development?
 
And if I may add: Astoria General has always been a bit of a dump but I have been to the emergency room relatively recently and to me its never looked better. (Though I still would go into Manhattan if I had the choice)
 
Traffic and SUVs:
What are you on about? Traffic lately? When has Astoria not been beset with traffic problems? The early 1940s? I wouldnt be surprised if the complaints started rolling in as soon as the Triborough and Grand Central were opened.

Also two and three car families are not exactly new or news. Even if the population remained the same over the past 10+ years Astoria would still have a traffic problem with more cars per family on the road and its not as if oversized SUVs and inconsiderate drivers are endemic to only Astoria.


Ouch. I think I may have hurt myself. Sorry for the long post. Im done (for the moment).
