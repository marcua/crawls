The Fox Family Made for TV Movie proudly presents:  George's Lesson, staring Jason Schwimmer, Sally Fields and Dan Castellana.

George yearned to be a singer.  His voice had a deep resonance which exuded an erotic masculinity.  During childhood, while he battled the ravages of mental retardation, his parents, encouraged his singing, hoping it would give him a foundation for recovery.  The tears which slipped out of his grandmother's eyes while he sang "happy birthday" to her on her last year on earth would become a permanent piece of heart wrenching family lore.

As George grew, he could not socially keep up with the other kids his age.  He did mainstream, but he was teased constantly in school, especially after being caught singing "I Feel Pretty" in the school bathroom.  Though his talent was obvious to anyone, he began to feel the weight of an unflinching world that demanded perfection.  He was rebuffed in his attempts to join the choir, music teachers would ignore him, and even his dad gently told him to "forget his singing and get a job at the A&P"

But George kept at it.  He went to music producer after music producer.  Some were harsher than others, but their messages were the same:  "You're good kid, but you'll be laughed at."  No matter how well he sung, no one wanted to listen.  The next few years would sow a nadir only the aisles of the A&P would ever fully understand.  Only his mop would hear his mellifluous chimes.

Fate would find him on a long line one September morning with his always supportive mother.  She couldn’t bear to see her son suffering and she waited all night with him so he could audition on American Idol.  Though barely able to stand up straight and hampered by the steady stream of mucus which slipped down to his mouth and was thereupon spit out onto all but the most dexterous of passer byers, his version of “Girls Just Want to Have Fun” flabbergasted the producers.  The stone cold silence which followed his last notes was broken a moment later by the thunderous ovation by those who were trying to escape his stead only a few minutes before.  But the world doesn’t always operate on merit and after a few minutes of conferring, the producers shook their heads and turned him down.

His mother called up the very next day and asked why he wasn’t given a chance.  She was hung up on several times.  She finally got through to talking with one junior producer, who told her that due to a legal decision which the show’s lawyers made due to a strident posting in Astorians.com, people of his handicap were not permitted on the show.  He should try Jerry Springer instead.

But George had one thing on his side.  Laura, a big wig at the network, was in the bathroom reading the articles of Playgirl when she heard George’s voice through the ventilation ducts.  She connected the smooth butter like voice with the strapping square jawed men in the magazine and fell in love.  The memo she wrote to the producers was curt and precise: “Either he’s in, or you’re out”.

The rest is history.  George’s winning of the American Idol contest, his marriage to Laura and his eventual ascendancy to the Presidency of the United States of America.  America had learned a lesson she would hopefully never forget.
