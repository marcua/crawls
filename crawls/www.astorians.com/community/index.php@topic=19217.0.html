I don't know what the PRE is, but if you're living in a building that is rent-stabilized or controlled, it can only go up by a certain amount set by the rent board: http://www.housingnyc.com/

If you live in a house, they can do whatever the hell they want, which is what happened to me once, where the landlord never fixed anything and then decided to raise the rent $200.  I ended up moving out.  Sometimes I think in the houses, you are better off with the landlord living there as well, rather than with an absentee one, because at least then, they'll hopefully care about the house just as much as you do, and will fix things quicker.  That has been my experience.  A good landlord shouldn't want to lose good tenants who take care of the place, don't wreck it, don't have parties all night long, and who pay the rent on time.

You should definitely ask why you are only being offered a 6mos lease.  That could mean they are looking to sell or have family move in or something.

It's my understanding that new buildings, i.e., built after 1974, are generally not covered by rent stabilization guidelines, unless the building receives a tax abatement -- which I thought the Pistilli buildings do. Maybe one of the owners can chip in here and clarify.

See this link for Fact Sheet #1: Rent Stabilization and Rent Control: www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/dhcr/DHCR1.html

From the fact sheet: "Rent stabilization generally covers buildings built after 1947 and before 1974, and apartments removed from rent control. It also covers buildings that receive J-51 and 421-a tax benefits."

If the building receives a tax abatement I think your apartment is subject to the same regulations (automatic 1-and 2-year leases, regulated rent infreases) as a rent stabilized apartment.

Quote from: Rane3 on August 31, 2010, 04:15:30 PM
I don't believe they have a tax abatement. How would one check such a thing?

From the J-51 /421a FAQ page...

If the building was rehabilitated or converted from another use, the abatement is known as a J-51 tax abatement. If it is newly constructed, the abatement is known as a 421-a tax abatement.

www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/faq/421a-j51.html?/guide/

To find out if your building is a part of the J-51 or 421-a programs call the Department of Housing, Preservation and Development at 212-863-5517 (J-51) or 212-863-5421 (421-a), or call the NYC Dept. of Finance at 311. If you have any further questions about the expiration of tax abatements call the NYS Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR) at 718-739-6400 or 212-961-8930.
