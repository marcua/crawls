I couldn't agree with annie more.  Suburbs to me represent static living.  Many are dysfunctional.  Most of them require a car to have a real, functional life.  It IS possible to manage around the limitations, but it takes a lot of tolerance and willingness to adjust drastically.  And in a region that has less than 4 season availability, even these adjustments don't work.  Believe me, I did this - I lived on Long Island/Suffolk County without owning a car for 4 years.  I was lucky enough to have some things within walking and biking distance, but not everything.  And these places were not truly integrated into the community as is the case in NYC/Astoria.  I was not able to function without some access to a car (bus system was a joke) during my entire time there.  I'm sorry, but you need a car in the suburbs.  To me, this is dysfunction on many levels.

Re: urban vs. suburban (and exurban?)
« Reply #61 on: August 18, 2006, 12:15:24 PM »
I grew up in a fairly affluent suburb of Rochester, but my family was far from affluent.  But I had a relatively possitive experience.  Maybe it's because of my family, or friends, but I was exposed to many cultural activities and experiences.  Certainly not as many as here, but I think I got a pretty good background of experiences to set me up for city living.  I searched out and found some really amazing jazz musicians in Rochester.  Ok, it was in the city proper, and not in the 'burbs.  But downtown Rochester is just a stones throw from the burb I lived in.  But my teachers were usually very encouraging, and gave me great advice on things I should check out.  And the friends I associated with, most of whom I'm still very close with, all seeked out great music and art to better themselves.

So I think it has a lot to do with personal experiences that shape our oppinions of the burbs.  And one last note...
I grew up playing soccer and lacrosse.  Wouldn't have been possible in a city school.  Being able to ride my bike to the school or park on a daily basis to play soccer or baseball was a great experience that I can't imagine living without.  I wouldn't have been able to do that stuff in the city.

So the city I would agree is about the best place I could be at this stage in my life.  But growing up until the end of highschool, the burbs was just fine with me.  And there are plenty of people in the city as well that have no desire to seek out the wonderful cultural benefits of the city.  So again, it has just as much to do with the person as it does with the place.

I agree with you completely.  It's entirely dependent on your experiences growing up.  Wherever you grow up.  I have met many NYorkers born and raised who can't wait to leave this city.   And as far as culture goes - look at Beacon now - I WISH I had access to that growing up.  All those galleries and artists everywhere.  It is 10 times more different than it was years ago.  Art was a class in high school back then.  I had many encouraging friends and teachers, and I wouldn't be where I am today without them.  And with more people moving there from the city, I have noticed a lot more open-mindedness come into Beacon as well.  I'm not sure the gossiping will ever change though.  Yeah, you need a car.  Things close by 9pm.  If you don't want to rely on a car and go out late night, you live in a place that has a transit system nearby and more of a nightlife.   It's pretty simple.  Which is why I'm in Astoria.   :-)  But that said, one of the biggest reasons I am happy I grew up elsewhere is that I know how to drive.  And it's all a part of what has led me to the person I am today.

As far as what constitutes suburb and city at this point, I could care less.   The big box stores tend to remind me of upstate, so that's my biggest comparison.  I'm just happy to live in a place I love, and that fulfills my needs at this point in my life.   :-)
