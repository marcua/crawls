Reporting a cab driver
« on: May 26, 2009, 03:44:57 PM »
I had a horrible cab situation the other day and I'm wondering if I should go through the whole reporting process.  If so, does anyone know how to get that started and what would be required of me?  For example, would I have to appear in court?  I don't know if it's worth all that, but it was really awful and still makes me upset to think about it. 
 

Background story - read if you want:  I hailed a cab in Manhattan to take me to Penn Station.  I had a suitcase with me.  When one pulled over (well, really just stopped in the middle of the road), the driver didn't get out to help me with my suitcase.  I climbed in and asked him to take me to Penn Station's LIRR entrance.  He responded with "I don't care where the f*** you're going", but started driving anyway.  At a red light, I asked him if he planned to take 6th Ave and he wouldn't respond (he was reading the newspaper (!!!) and had talk radio on so I figured he didn't hear me).  I asked again, "Are you planning to take 6th Ave?"  He puts the car in park, turns around and stares at me, refusing to drive or answer me.  The light turns green and there are other cars honking behind us, but he still won't drive.  Now the meter is going up and I'm getting pissed.  Finally he screams, "It is my job to drive this cab, and YOU do not tell me how to do my job!"  The meter was up to a few dollars, but I asked him to pull over so I could get out.  Once I said that, he zoomed away, turned down 6th (the way I didn't want him to go), and wouldn't pull over.  He kept ranting and screaming about how gas costs money and if I got out of the cab, I wouldn't pay him.  I was honestly scared for my life.  Then, a block from Penn, he gets caught behind a fire truck and a red light and I ask him to stop the meter so I can get out.  The fare was up to $6.50.  I had long ago decided I would not be tipping this driver, but all I had was $7, and I didn't want to give him my credit card.  I gave him $7 and asked for change.  He said he didn't have any and the next thing I know, he's driving three blocks out of the way, past Penn.  I ask where he's going and again ask to be let out, but he replies, "going to get you change."  Now I'm shouting at him to just let me out, and he FINALLY does.  I wrote down his cab #, but I was in so much shock at the whole experience that I totally forgot to write down his name.  This story might sound tame, but this man was screaming at me the entire time and not letting me out when I asked to be let out... all because I asked him if he planned to take a certain route, which ccording to that "taxi rider bill of rights" thing, the passenger has the right to direct.

Help! I really don't want to leave this situation alone, but I don't really want to have a day off of work to appear in court either.  If I have the cab number, what can I do?  Thanks in advance.

I agree about filing the complaint. He was probably having a bad day, but that's no excuse for treating you like that. You do have to appear in court, but I think it's worth following through with it since he was so nasty to you. The complaint form allows you to select the day/time you would be able to appear, so it wouldn't take a full day, if that helps. The medallion number, along with date and time of day of the incident, will allow them to identify the driver.
Here's the link for filing a complaint along with more info about what's involved: http://www.nyc.gov/html/tlc/html/passenger/file_complaint.shtml

I don't know about anyone else, but I was never charged a thing for my hearings.  I realize you don't want to see this guy again, but if you let him get away with it, there's a good chance he'll just do it again to someone else.  It's not court either, rather it's an office building down on Rector Street, where you go into an office where a judge sits at a desk and listens to both sides of the story.  If it makes you feel better, you can bring a lawyer, because the taxi guy will have his own as well.  In my experience, it seems the judge encounters this kind of thing all the time, and while impartial, they are fair and don't take sides.  At my first hearing, the judge was actually very rude to the driver because he pulled a fast one by saying he wanted a lawyer, when he had months to get one beforehand.  So the first hearing was postponed.  It is a pain in the ass, but I'm satisfied with her ruling when it was all said and done.   Just don't look at the driver, look at the judge the whole time.  That was how I dealt with his trying to give me dirty looks.
