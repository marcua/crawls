Title: Re: Internet-based Banks: Good or Bad?
Post by: merm on August 20, 2006, 10:30:57 AM
I wouldn't pay off a student loan with a credit card. Credit card companies can raise your rate. Besides, student loan interest is deductible on your taxes, and credit card interest is not. Credit card companies can not be trusted.

If you have a decent credit rating I've seen generous offers from Citibank Dividend card on balance transfers.

Also, if you're looking for a loan to consolidate your loans check out www.prosper.com . It's a peer-to-peer lending thing that I've been doing for about 6 months. I really like it.

Another thing to keep in mind. If you do a balance transfer, transfer the balance onto a card that has a ZERO balance. Then DO NOT USE that card for any purchases until you get the balance paid off. Here is the reason why. Your credit card might have a normal interest rate of 12% for purchases. And let's say you have $1000 balance on the card at that 12%. Then you take out a balance transfer for 0% on another $1000. Your total balance is now $2000 ($1000 at 0% and $1000 at 12%). Seems like a good deal.

Well, when you make payments the credit card company won't put any money on your 12% portion of the bill, they only apply your payments to the 0% portion of the bill until that full 0% is paid off. Only THEN will they allow you to start paying on the 12% stuff. This is the way that credit card companies "lock in" a high rate on you, is by "offering" you a 0% rate. In reality all they are doing is offering to screw you over. Don't fall for it.

If you are doing a balance transfer, get the balance to ZERO on the card. Wait until next billing cycle (because they take your "average daily balance" and use that trick to charge you more interest) and THEN transfer the balance. Then DO NOT USE THE CARD FOR PURCHASES (since the purchases at the high rate will go in behind your low rate stuff - see above). I just sign up for auto-pay with the minimum deducted from my checking. That is the ONLY way that a balance transfer at "5%" is ACTUALLY 5% on a credit card.

Just my $0.02

Moving money should *never* involve fees. If your institution requires a fee paid—get out of that institution. I personally use Citibank (only because of an almost half century history), ING, Fidelity, ML, AMEX and eTrade.

Higher rates are genreally obtained by having higher balances. The more you spread your money around the lower the rates will be.

Having any monies just sit and not earn appreciable interest is not a very good idea.

ALWAYS leave your money where, at least, the original principal will be insured. Otherwise it is *not* a banking situation—but rather an investment.
