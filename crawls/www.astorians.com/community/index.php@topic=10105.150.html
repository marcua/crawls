That LED sign used to be my nemesis.  I went to high school in the city, and would take the train from Ditmars each morning. 

One morning, I made sure to get to the station especially early, as I had day one of a two day Spanish final...my first class at 8:15 am, and I wanted to be sure I got there comfortably on time.  I got to the station, and there were two trains at the platform, so I got on the train that the sign was indicating, along with everyone else.  After about 5 or 10 minutes with no announcement, suddenly some people notice that the LED sign is now pointing to the other train, across the platform, and those who were still coming up the stairs were getting on that train.  So most people got up and went in that train as well.

A few minutes later, and everyone got into that train, the LED sign changed back to the first train, and those who were coming up the stairs started going onto that train, so we followed them back to the first train.  This was accompanied by an announcement, in the second train, saying that the train across the platform would be the first train to leave.

After sitting in the first train again for a few minutes, and with time on my end getting dangerously short with my final looming, suddenly the LED sign changed back again to the second train...a handful of people coming up the stairs rushed in, made it into that train, but the rest of us on the first train didn't get out in time...the doors closed and the train pulled out of the station...no announcement! 

I really let the conductor have it as the train was leaving the station.  Of course, it took another 10 or so minutes for the next train to leave, and coupled with the slowness that is the Lexington Avenue line, I missed more than half of the first part of the final.  Even better, I wasn't given the opportunity to make it up, so of course my final grade reflected the MTA's incompetence that morning

Long overdue to have the nutty N/W dichotomy at Ditmars in the morning rush noted.  Used to be they'd automatically send one train out after another, but there's very little rhyme or reason now as to which train goes first.  The only hard fast rule I can sense: a vast majority of the time, if there's an N and W in the station, the N will leave first.  Get the impression they have to fire out two of them for every W in the morning rush.

Another good rule: let's say one train is just about full and getting ready to leave, so you decide you want a seat and wait for the next.  When the next pulls in, and you get on, if the conductor states: "Doors will close on this train until the next scheduled departure time" ... get off.  Because from what I've seen, a vast majority of the time, that train will sit there and let the next load up and leave first ... often while keeping its doors closed and trapping those unfortunate people inside who hopped on early.  (Although that nice older white guy who cleans the trains will often pop a door open, sensing the insanity of his bosses.)  Of course, even if the doors pop open on that "first" train to let people out, it will always be only after the leaving train has already filled with passengers.

It's a ludicrous situation that these *ssclowns have done nothing to address -- get the impression they think it's funny when you see the quizzical looks on their faces as they walk to their trains.  They could EASILY announce which train will be leaving first as each train leaves.  Then again, how many times have I sat there to hear the station manager clearly intone over the loudspeaker: "Conducter, please close the doors on our train" ... only to have the doors sit there open for close to a minute afterwards.

I've toyed with the idea of leaving for work an hour early and hitting the gym.  I've noticed the radical difference between trains leaving at, say, 7:15 as opposed to 8:15: day and night.  The early trains are much less crowded and seem to leave on a rational schedule.  The after-8 trains have become totally nuts: leaving on irrational time schedules (how many times have you sat on a train filling to capacity with passengers at Ditmars, seemingly minutes behind schedule), packed to the gills by 30th Ave, just a gnarly ride these days in light of the increased # of passengers using it these days.  Man, I can remember when 36th Ave was a ghost stop -- it's getting to be like Broadway in terms of passenger load. 

All I know is these discussions on giving seats to pregnant women ... most mornings, the train is so uncomfortably crowded that I don't even look up from my seat, recognizing that it would be an enormous hassle to give my seat to anyone.  Much less the pregnant woman on crutches who's carrying six bags who always seems to get on my car and hover directly over me.  Hitting on pretty girls, not a problem either.  I judge women by their knees these days on the train.
