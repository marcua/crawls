I got a pretty good recipe for vegetable tian out of a French cookbook. Basically you saute in olive oil some sliced onion, eggplant, red pepper, and garlic (to your preference - this time I made it there's way too much onion and eggplant for me) until the eggplant is golden, then pour it all into a shallow baking dish.

On top of that, put down thin slices of raw zucchini and plum tomatoes in alternating rows until the whole thing is covered. Sprinkle salt, pepper, rosemary, and thyme to taste. Bake at 375 for 25 minutes. Then, sprinkle on 3 TBSP of bread crumbs and some parmesan cheese (I used feta this last time and it worked well). Drizzle olive oil over the top. Bake for about 10 more minutes or until sufficiently browned. Then you eat it with rice or pasta.

Aside from all the chopping, it's pretty easy, cheap, tasty, and healthy. It can probably be cooked at a higher temperature. I think I'll try that next time.

Astorians,
  It's been a long time since I've posted here but I thought that I'd give this thread a bump and try to revive it.
So, what are you making for dinner tonight?
  A few weeks ago I spent, as the Manitobans say, a fair bit (actually a large bit) of time down on Mott and Mullberry Street in Chinatown taking in the sights and tastes along Grand Avenue, and the environs north or Canal Street.  Alligator hooves in a food court/market between Mott and Mulberry, Beef Pen**s available by the pound on Elizabeth Street, and indescribable seafood on every corner, G-d, don't you love NYC?
  Anyway, I took home an amazing Chinese soup from a stall that I don't remember how to find again and this is my version of an amazing mushroom soup.
  While I have been trying to recreate it, I've added my own touches.  For most Astorians, this soup might be cost-prohibitive, since they do not have the ingredients in their pantry.
  None the less enjoy

Mott Street Chinese Mushroom Soup

Ingredients (in proportion)
5 dried Shiitake Mushrooms (buy the more expensive variety they have more flavor)
5 cups Water
1 bunch green onions (finely minced)
2 cloves of garlic (finely minced)
1 bouillon cube (I use Bao Long Vi Bo Kho cubes from Viet Nam)
1 tsp black pepper
1 tsp sea (or kosher) salt
1 Tbsp Soy Sauce
1 Tbsp Sesame Oil (I prefer Sesame Chile Oil)
2 Tbsp Black Bean/Garlic Sauce
1 Tbsp of coarse ground Chile Powder (I use either Korean of Vietnamese--piment miett or Korean--Gochutgaru)
20 Raw peanuts (goober peas), shelled no need to discard the husks
2 oz Tofu of choice (I tend to use flavored pressed tofu such as su ji or spiced pressed tofu with soy sauce, but any tofu will be a welcome addition) cubed into small pieces
2 Handfulls of Bean Sprouts
1 handfull of Bamboo shoots, sliced into short threads

Directions
(add water as needed to maintain a soup)

1. Add green onions, garlic, black pepper, and salt to 5 cups water. Bring to a partial boil. Allow to cool to the

temperature of warm tap water

2. Add 5 Shiitake Mushroom to warm water and allow to soak for 30 minutes (or until rehydrated) to create a basic broth

3. Remove Mushrooms, stem as needed, and return to basic broth

4. Add Soy Sauce, Sesame Oil, Chile Powder, Black Bean Sauce, and peanuts to broth and simmer for 30 minutes

5. Add Tofu to the broth, continue simmerring

6. Add Bean Sprouts and bamboo shoots, continue simmering until the peanuts have the texture of a frozen pea.

Cypriot Sour Trahanas Soup

Bought a package of sour trahanas from Mediterranean Foods out of pure curiosity & did some experimenting - good results if you like a tangy, yogurt-like sour-ish flavor to a very hearty soup. 

Dump bag of trahanas into bowl & covered with tap water & set aside.  In large stock pot, boil a dozen chicken legs in salted water with shallots, celery & carrots for about 1 1/2 hours until broth is rich & meat was falling off the bones (skim as needed).  Remove chix legs from stock and add in trahanas (which by now should have absorbed water & become like an oatmeal consistency).  Stir to combine & heat on low stirring often to prevent sticking to bottom of pot.  Shred meat & add back into soup.  Add salt/pepper to taste & ladle into bowls.  I like to top the bowl with a small pat of butter & sprinkle with dried mint - really wakes up the flavor. 

My guess is this will be even better tomorrow!
