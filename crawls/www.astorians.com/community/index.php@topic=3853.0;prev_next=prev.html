Quote from: rdc on November 07, 2006, 11:52:44 AM
Yeah I have a hard time trusting a Diebold machine.  If my district (whichever one votes at the school on 21st/Ditmars) moves to electronic voting I'm moving back to absentee ballots.

Just a note that there are a number of companies that make electronic voting machines, that some of them (including some of the Diebold machines I think) are just interfaces to make paper ballots, and that electronic voting is, at least in theory, important to allow the disabled to vote in privacy. Also, when they're working correctly, electronic interfaces are less prone to error (undervoting, overvoting, etc) than some of the old technologies, especially punchcards. Personally, I'd like to see voting machines that print the votes onto a piece of cardstock that then gets read by an optical-scan machine.

As for absentee ballots, they're fine, but ballots get lost in the mail, and you lose some privacy. I think their error rate is relatively high for these and other reasons.

The New York Times had a cool op-ed yesterday about requiring 99% statistical significance for an election to be certified. All voting systems have random errors (on the order of a percent or two) for various reasons, technical and otherwise, and you can estimate the margin of victory needed to have confidence in the result even allowing for some random error. The idea is that if that level of significance is not reached, you have to do the election over again! This is a fantastic idea, although you have to be a bit of a stats nerd to appreciate it, I think. :)
