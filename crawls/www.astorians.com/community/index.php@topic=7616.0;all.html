Booksellers: Public or Private Interests?
« on: August 21, 2007, 03:13:32 PM »
Barnes & Nobles recently announced it shall not regularly carry the If I Did It book by O.J. Simpson, citing lack of public demand.  Borders shall carry it, but shall not promote the book.  Given the public interest in the matter, I can comfortably suggest that both companies have made their decisions for reasons other than the profitability of the book, but it political, moral or pressure from interest groups/government.

As the giga booksellers come to dominate more and more of the book market, making it practically impossible for smaller bookshops to remain viable (especially outside of the large, cosmopolitan cities like NYC), what responsibility do these booksellers have towards the public?  As a privately held business with marketshares that are large enough to affect the operation of the market itself and alter the course of the invisible hand, can they promote or retard any titles it deems fit based on reasons other than profit?  Would concerns of the defacto censorship that occurs due to such be superior or subordinate to the rights of private interests?  OJ might be in the company of Helmsley when it comes to public affection, but what if this defacto censorship extended to political issues?  Does collusion with the other giga booksellers impact this?  Further, does their collective monopoly of the market require that they loosen their profit expectations, since their marketshare precludes the market from "taking a chance" on more shaky titles?

Be interested to hear what everyone thinks on this.  I'm on the fence with this one.  Its raining and I'm burning time at work.

I work in publishing so of course I've been hearing lots about OJ's "fictional memoir".

One thing you need to understand is that B&N and Borders simply DON'T carry every book that is published, period. No bookseller does. Every season they decline to purchase books that we publish simply because they don't feel it fits their market, or because there are too many competitive titles and they decide to go with something from another house, or simply because the buyer doesn't like the book or the author. While the OJ book is making more waves because of its content, it's certainly not news and it's not censorship. Censorship would be the government saying it can't be published at ALL--or that anyone buying or selling it will be fined. A bookstore (which is a business) is making a decision on what they think will sell--what will bring them profit. That's their bottom line. And if anything, independent stores are much more picky about which titles they purchase, and often based on the owner's personal biases--that's more along the lines of censorship than a huge chain hearing public outcry (and there WAS public outcry) and refusing to carry something they feel is a bad business decision.

Also, did you know consumers can purchase books directly from the publisher? So even if every single chain and Amazon refused to carry this book, there wouldn't be censorship--the publisher could still create and advertise the book, and customers could get it; the bookstores would simply refuse to be the middleman.

I'm interested as to whether B&N will stock it if it becomes a bestseller--that might show you a little more about the true motives.

Excellent arguements.  I'm still not sure which way I would go, because though I'm a big fan of all points of view being heard, I'm a bigger fan of private interests and the government staying out of our business.

I wouldn't suggest that B&N carry all books, but I think there is a reasonable certainty that this book shall be a moneymaker.  The question certainly shall be compounded should it become a bestseller.  I further agree that there are other booksellers out there like Amazon.  My knowledge of the book industry is limited, however, is it fair to say that B&N refusing to regularly carry the book, and Borders refusing to promote it would atleast moderately affect sales?   I'll sometimes go into a B&N, get a coffee, briefly read through titles and then go and buy the titles I liked from a place like Amazon.

Censorship is a tricky word, which is why I keep using de facto censorship, because clearly, it is not a government ordered censorship.  Its a possible de facto censorship due to the actions of private interests when their marketshare is so great that the invisible hand no longer feely operates.  In the 1950's the red scare created a form of de facto censorship due to pressure groups.  Private interests, movie studios and television networks, which controlled a marketshare akin to the giga booksellers of today, blacklisted actors, artists, writers, et cetera due to the public pressures.  More importantly, they had a chilling effect on those not blacklisted.  Was it acceptable to say "Hey Jules Dassin is not censored, he's still making movies in Europe which you can see if you try hard enough"?  Was it acceptable for the private interests to use the excuse of the bottom line, or was there a public interest at stake?

Granted, there is one hellava gap between the red scare and OJ Simpson and maybe titles that are racist, pornographic and into touture.  However, I do not see the specific content to constitute an acceptable excuse, whether its garbage or not (arguments which regard the public interest, like forbidding the sale of pornographic material near schools, is a different matter).  Once the de facto censorship of one thing is found acceptable, it makes it easy for others to follow.  What if Rupert Murdock acquired B&N and Borders?  By no means is that a far-fetched scenario.  Some regions only have B&N to purchase books from.  What if their only newschannel was FOX?  Can we say that there is no public issue here, simply because anyone who wants to may go on the internet for their news?

I have to find the references for this, but as an urban planning student, I came across some cases where an interest group argued that the mall effectely replaced the old downtown as the region's marketplace and center of human activity.  Forcing them to hold a demonstration on some little used road where no one would see them would be a form of de facto censorship.  Even though there was no absolute ban on them marching anywhere, the judge agreed that even though the mall was a private interest, it had an obligation to allow such, due to its market dominance (of human activity).

Good point on Walmart though.  Have people raised issue in the past with them on that?  I thought I also heard about a major pharamacutical chain refusing to carry birth control?

De facto censorship is definitely dangerous. However, I don't think a chain bookstore has any responsibility to the public other than profit.
