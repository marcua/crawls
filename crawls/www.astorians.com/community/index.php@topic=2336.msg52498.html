Just to update some of NYC MacUsers' info:

The four defendants were charged with 6 to 8 counts, ranging from C felony assault (assault with a weapon charged as a hate crime) to A misdemeanor possession of a weapon. They have all since been released on bail. Some have retained private counsel, others continue to be represented by members of the 18B panel. NYC doesn't have a "public defender" -- the majority of cases in NYC get picked up by the Legal Aid Society (I'm a LAS attorney), a private non-profit that contracts with the city to provide indigent defense. Not sure why LAS didn't pick up any of the defendants, unless there was a conflict of interest.

Anyway, to check the status of this or any other criminal case, go to WebCrims at the NYS Court website:

http://iapps.courts.state.ny.us/webcrim_attorney/Login

Log in as a guest and go to case searches. You can search by name or docket number.

And to check the status of any Rikers Island inmate, go to the inmate lookup on the Department of Corrections website:

http://www.nyc.gov/html/doc/html/home/home.shtml

I've lived in Astoria on and off throughout my entire life and this is only the second crime in the neighborhood that has had me totally creeped out -- the first was when I went to arraign a robbery that happened a couple houses down from my own. I'm glad they caught these guys, but it's only half the battle. Even though the top charge is a C felony, the odds of there being a finding to that count is pretty slim unless the case is taken to trial -- it will inevitably be pled down to the E felony or the A misdemeanor and the perps will either get 5 years probation or a light jail sentence. Sad, but all too unfortunately true.

My understanding is that at least some people who study criminal behavior say that the major deterrent factor in most crimes is not the severity of sentence, but the odds of getting caught and convicted. If you think there's only a 1-in-20 chance of getting arrested for (say) beating up someone on the street, it's not going to matter much to you whether the crime is worth 10 years in the pokey or a slap in the wrist, as it's not going to happen to you. (Especially if you're 19 and therefore immortal.) So I'm very heartened that they caught these guys (assuming they're the right guys, of course), as the arrests themselves are a major factor in reducing future crimes in the future... Thumbs up for good police work.

Quote from: NYCMacUser on February 04, 2007, 11:37:28 AM
What kind of say will the victim(s) have in any plea deal?

Typically, the victim will have some say, but ultimately it's the decision of the players (DA and defense) involved. More often than not, though, the victim is willing to go along with a plea deal because they can avoid having to testify and be subjected to cross-examination. Also, trials are time consuming -- most witnesses don't have the time to come back to court repeatedly. I imagine it's a very emotionally draining experience as well.

In juvenile cases (I practice juvenile defense -- yes, I represent the scourge of the earth: teenagers), the victim has absolutely no say in plea negotiations and limited say at sentencing (via a victim impact statement). Not sure which system is better, though.
