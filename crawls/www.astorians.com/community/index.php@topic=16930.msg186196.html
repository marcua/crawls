Whether people mean to or not, the fact that 20 somethings now find Astoria desirable has changed the neighborhood. The influx of people willing to pay very high rents to live in areas they deem desirable has jacked up rent prices in neighborhoods that you used to be able to afford to raise a family. The fact that in order to have the same standard of living that I had growing up means that I would have to move an hour or more from the place where I have roots and family even though I have a good job is sad. It's a different time and a different place. Places change, yes. The NYC of the past was not ideal by any means. What I really lament is the decline in the standard of living for the middle and lower middle class. I think people are taking out their anger on the fact that they are priced out of the neighborhoods that people used to be able to go to raise families. Manhattan used to be where people lived when they were of a certain age group and lifestyle and people moved to Queens, Long Island and Westchester to raise families. Now those 20 somethings are priced out of Manhattan which has been scooped up by the uber-rich and those 20 somethings who are not uber-rich but have plenty of expendible income have moved to Queens and Brooklyn.  Long Island and Westchester are very expensive and now Queens, the last bastion of middle class affordability is going the way of Brooklyn (given, you can go further out in Queens and it is cheaper, but not by much). There really aren't any middle class enclaves anymore. I think that's more of a sign of the shifting demographics of the country in general and the shrinking of the middle class.

I don't buy the "march of history" idea; that was discredited in 1989 when the Berlin Wall came down.
People make choices and there is nothing inevitable about local politicians like Vallone Inc. selling the neighborhood out to real estate developers, which would be resisted except that the 20 Somethings in question don't believe in neighborhoods; they revel in anonymity and they romanticize the bleakness. Why, one proudly proclaimed that she had "left everyting behind" in the Midwest to come to the big City. It's what motivates people that matters. Go to Jackson Heights, Flushing,  or real immigrant communities and just about everyone who comes over here from India or Hong Kong, Colombia or wherever, wants to bring their families over. What we're dealing with here in Astoria is not that; it's self-centeredness, only the irony is that they don't yet have selves to center. They are, for the most part , little collections of trends. The Greeks and Italians and first-wave immigrants who made this place a desirable place to live lived for family and tradition and the continuity of generations, not some abstract and idiotic notion of Self.
