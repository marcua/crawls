Title: Re: Turning on the Heat
Post by: purplecurlygirl on November 06, 2007, 09:36:30 AM
http://www.oag.state.ny.us/realestate/habitability.html

Heat must be supplied from Oct. 1 through May 1 if the temperature outside falls below 55 degrees between 6 a.m. and 10 p.m. (inside temp maintained at 68 degrees) and if the temperature outside falls below 40 degrees between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m., the inside must be maintained at 55. This law applies to multiple family dwellings, so your building qualifies.

You need to call your landlord or superintendent before you start withholding rent--they might just be waiting to hear from you to save some $$. You can also call the Attorney General's Office to find out your next steps.

DO NOT just stop paying rent. If you have to withhold rent for problems of habitability, there are specific steps you must take. You must continue to pay the rent into an escrow account, you must document that you have attempted to work with them to correct the problem, etc.

A multiple dwelling is defined as a "building with three or more residential units."

From the NYC Rent Guidelines Board (http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/dhcr/dhcr15.html):

Quote
By law, building owners must provide all tenants with the following levels of heat (During the heating season, October 1 through May 31):

    * Between 6 a.m. and 10 p.m., heat must register at least 68 degrees Fahrenheit when the outside temperature falls below 55 degrees;
    * Between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m., heat must register at least 55 degrees Fahrenheit when the outside temperature falls below 40 degrees.

Title: Re: Turning on the Heat
Post by: purplecurlygirl on November 06, 2007, 01:53:46 PM
Quote from: paratactical on November 06, 2007, 10:56:24 AM
Should I be setting up an account or just calling 311? I wasn't here last year, so can't vouch for that. I'm just trying to figure out what I should do. I'd hate to have sent him rent (he hasn't cashed the check yet, so I could void it through my bank) and then not get heat until he's worried about the money coming in December.

Before you set up an escrow account, you need to document that you've taken all reasonable steps possible. You need to call 311 to report it, repeatedly even, and you need to talk to the Attorney General's office before withholding rent. It would be better if you could get some of the other tenants in the building to withhold rent WITH you (through separate or the same escrow accounts). Definitely do NOT stop payment on a check you've already sent him--that's antagonistic behavior and will not put you in the best light when you need to be seen as a good tenant.

1. Call 311. Call every day while you're sorting out the rest of it.
2. Call the Attorney General's office and ask for guidelines on withholding rent in this situation.
3. Talk to your fellow tenants and see if anyone is interested in following those guidelines. Regardless, encourage them to call 311 with complaints.
4. Contact your landlord via certified mail, return receipt, mentioning the heating requirement law. Provide documentation that he is not meeting the law (one way to do this is to get an indoor/outdoor thermometer that shows the time and take pictures of it). Mention that you will be taking all steps legally available to you to restore the apartment to legal standards of habitability, up to and including withholding rent. Include that other tenants will be joining you in this, if applicable.
5. Follow the steps the AG office gives you for legal withholding of rent.

I am not a lawyer. But at a previous apartment, these steps worked for me up to number 5--after I sent the letter, the heat came on.

Post by: girl775 on November 06, 2007, 02:01:29 PM
I agree that 311 should be called. The problem with calling 311 is that a housing inspector needs to be sent out and I don't think they give you a time (or even a day) as to when they're coming. If you have relationships with other neighbors, perhaps one that's home during the day, it would be best for them to also call 311.

The heating requirements do not vary by the number of units in the building, but what does vary is your right to a renewal lease. It's easier for a rent stabilized tenant to make these kinds of complaints because they cannot be evicted for making these types of complaints. In an unregulated apt. building, the landlord doesn't have to give you a reason to not renew your lease (or to terminate a month-to-month tenancy). So, sometimes there's a fine line to walk because you may want to stay in the apartment in the long-run and the landlord may be vindictive. I like the idea of anonymously sending the heating season rules to the landlord (there's probably a flier on the HPD website).

Don't withhold rent until all your other options are exhausted and document everything.
