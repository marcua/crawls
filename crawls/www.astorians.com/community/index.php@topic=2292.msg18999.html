Switching to Green Power Electricity
« on: June 04, 2006, 01:02:16 PM »
A couple months ago we came across the Green Power option listed on ConEd's Website.  You can even enroll online.  It does cost a few bucks more a month but at least it is 100% renewable energy.  They use a combination of wind power and hydro-electric.  Of course I would prefer 100% wind power because of the environmental effects of dams but at least it doesn't use any fossil fuels.   If you sign up, you get a 25.00$ rebate on your energy bill plus no NYS sales tax on your bill. 

It seems like a good alternative that everyone can do to decrease use of fossil fuels.  If anyone figures out how to switch to 100% wind and solar power please let me know. 

Check out the option at www.NewWindEnergy.com/nyc

I just signed up for 100% Wind Energy.  Thanks a bunch Mark!

So for less than $100/year, I save the earth. 


Look How It Works:

- $25 Refund right off the bat
- No NYS Sales Tax (Exemption) - thats 4.00% off your bill
- 100% Wind is $0.19000/kwh and the rate last month was $0.198635/kwh**


** I did note some sort of Adjust. Factor Credit on my bill of $0.034157/kwh but I'm not sure what it is or when it will go way.
So lets do the calculation to see how much more it will cost using a conservative assumption that includes the credit..

With the credit cost/kwh = $0.164478
100% Wind Green Energy = $0.1900/kwh
Difference=$0.025522 or 15.55%
Less NYS Sales Tax Exemption of - 4.00%

Total Bill Increase w/ Green Power - 11.55%

That Translates into an additional $7.83 based on my last months bill (11.55% x $67.82 elec)



Wow.  I feel good. 8-)

Those numbers don't look right to me. I think the costs you might be looking at are the supply + delivery costs. You need to be looking at the supply costs only, delivery costs will be in addition to the supply.

When you get a quote for power (even through ConEd Solutions Green Power) from any ESCO they are only quoting you the supply costs. ConEd then adds the delivery costs to that when you get your bill.

So, to compare apples with apples you need to get the supply cost for the green power, and supply costs for the non-green for the same month's period.

I'm VERY interested in finding out more about this. If the cost is close I'll switch too.
