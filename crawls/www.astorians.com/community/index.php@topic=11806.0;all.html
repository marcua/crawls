We blew most of the contents of our wine rack -- at the very least, a half case of very good French wine that I haven't been able to find since!

I was on the 21st floor of a building on Madison and 51st.  Thank goodness I wasn't in my regular office on the 46th floor a few blocks away (I evacuated from that office once, when there was a building fire shortly after September 11.  Needless to say, I never want to walk down 46 flights of stairs again.)  It only took me a couple of hours to get home -- someone walked with me as far as the Queensboro bridge, and once I got across I miraculously caught a Q19A at the foot of the bridge.  The a/c on the bus wasn't working, and the person sitting next to me was complaining about it.  I wanted to say, hey, you're on a bus, quit your kvetching!

We hung out in our neighbour's backyard, our downstairs neighbour providing the guitar music and I providing the wine.  We sang our way through song after song, lit by flickering candlelight.  Around 10:00, headlights appeared around the corner of our house.  After almost freaking out, we discovered it was my in-laws (who live on Long Island), who had endured a 4-hour car ride from their office in Lower Manhattan (fortunately, their friend drove a Geo -- good gas mileage!).

Around 2am, we went out into the street and tilted our heads back, marvelling at the brightness of the moon and the plethora of stars.  Everything was magically quiet - no a/c's humming or other background electrical noise we automatically internalise and don't even notice until it falls silent.

We found beds for everyone, and after a fitful, hot sleep, woke up to fans whirring - the electricity had come on about 5:15.

Friends of ours on the Upper East Side didn't get their electricity back for three days.  They have a balcony, but they had trained their dog not to go on the balcony.  So now, no matter what they did, she wouldn't go on the balcony.  This meant that for three days, they had to walk the dog down and back up 21 flights of stairs, several times a day.

At that time I was working for the factory outlet division of a retailer, so my whole department was up in Woodbury Commons.  We had finished a walk through at our store, and were shopping when everything started going haywire, alarms going off, lights flashing, the whole 9.  So we jumped in the car and headed back to the office in NJ, listening to accounts of what was happening in NYC, myself politically incorrectly mimicking the stories we were hearing.
Once we got back to Jersey, and there was no power there either, the other guy who lived in NYC and myself got in his car that didn't have an air conditioner, and headed to the Holland Tunnel, as it was reportedly open.  It wasn't, so we turned around, headed north, to the Lincoln Tunnel, which was closed, and finally to the GWB, which opened as we were driving there.  He lived on like 91st and York, so I got off there, and hoofed it back.  About 6 blocks after I got out of his car, my ipod died, than a block later my phone died.
I walked over the 59th St Bridge, back home, all in the dark, somehow without even enough money in my pocket to buy a water.  When I finally got home, I lit a bunch of IKEA tea lights that I had, took the coldest shower ever, which felt amazing, and then tried to get a hold of my friend who lived like 4 blocks away at the time.  Having no cell phone, no buzzers, no nothing, I gave up after like 10 minutes, convinced he wasn't home (he wasn't, he was at his girlfriend's house).  I had successfully scrounged up enough change to get a few hot dogs at Mano's Papaya, which was still serving food somehow, but when I got there they were closed.  So I went home, ate a huge bowl of cereal and went to sleep. 
I woke up to my alarm going off at like 5 AM when the power came back on, and later irate phone calls from my then girlfriend because my phone had died and she was worried.

The power went out that afternoon and I thought it was just my motion sensor acting funny in my studio (Stony Brook University Music Department on Long Island).  I was practicing and a lot of time I'd be so focused on tiny movements that I wouldn't move the rest of my body much; therefore the lights would go out because the motion sensor didn't think anyone was in the room!

So, I left my studio and walked out to the little stream and waterfall area to try to call my teacher Sally and I couldn't make any calls!  She arrived and we decided just to skip our meeting and just go home.  Sally drove me home (I lived a 5 minute drive from campus), and it took about 20 minutes instead of the 5.  She got home ok.

My two housemates got home and we were all "well now what?".  No internet, no TV, no stereo.  We went across the street and bought some warming beer and a pound and a half of salmon - the fish guy sold it to us for way cheaper as he was trying to offload what he had. 

We wanted cold beer so we quickly pulled out ice packs from the freezer, put them in my gigantic 32qt stock pot and put the whole thing in my housemate's car and turned on the A/C!  It didn't do much for cooling down the beer.

We ended up spending time with our friends up the block who came down for the fish, and one of my housemates went to go fetch another friend who was a little ways away.  We grilled the fish with sea salt, olive oil, and fresh herbs from the garden.  We ate it with warmish beer.  We listened to music blaring from my housemate's car.  We just hung out with each other and it was a lot of fun!  I remember how quiet it was, too. Lovely.

A little before midnight, the power came back on.  That was just about the time we all headed to bed.  So, it worked out really well for us.  I have fond memories of that blackout.

Aah, the blackout.  The memories.

I was at work in the middle of doing a sensitive flash upgrade on our CEO's laptop that probably would have hosed it had the power gone out about 20 seconds earlier than it did.  I finished it and just a few seconds later I heard someone down the hall say "Hey, did the power just go out?"  I went into the server room (I work in IT) and all the backup batteries were bleeping at me letting me know we'd switched to backup power.  After we got some news that indicated this blackout wasn't going to be over anytime soon I shut the servers down one by one.  Some people were listening to the radio freaking out and thinking it was a terrorist attack because it had hit the whole Northeast and then some.  I just hung around until it became clear all we could do was make our respective ways home and I did just that.  My little Swiss Army knife with the flashlight came in handy several times. 

It took about two hours to walk home to Astoria from my office near the Empire State Building and I was oh so glad to be wearing comfortable shoes that day.  Email and web browsing worked on my cell phone so I used it to dash off a few messages to family while I still had juice and reception.  The only kicker was that I managed to somehow lose my wallet on the way home.  I think I may have dropped it when crossing the Queensboro Bridge.  That was a serious drag.  But I did make it home before sunset, which allowed me to take a shower and cook a quick dinner while it was still light.

Lit some candles and tried to read a book but just passed out all sweaty.  Didn't have anything exciting in the fridge or I would have scarfed it up -- during a later blackout I remember eating ice cream with my roommate by candlelight and that was kinda fun.  One of my neighbors got up on the roof with all his beer and proceeded to get really hammered.  He invited me up but I wasn't so sure I wanted to get wasted all the way up there - that might not have worked out so well when I had to get back down.

I think I got a bike not long after the blackout so I could get back into the city even if transit was down.  Nice to know that's always an option.

I was at work in Harlem.  When the power went out my boss said it was no big deal and the power would soon return, so why pay it any mind.  But then I heard on my Walkman (didn't have Internet radio yet) that the power was off all over the Northeast and nearby parts of Canada, so I decided it was time to scoot and get home somehow.

A number of coworkers felt the same way, and a bunch of us who lived in Queens or Brooklyn walked down to 125th St. to get the M60 bus if we were lucky, or cross the Triboro Bridge on foot if we weren't.  When we got to 125th St., the M60 bus that came was jammed.  Forget it, there was no way to get on it.  So we walked east on the south side of 125th St. 

Then, when we got to 125th St. and 2nd Avenue, we got lucky.  Amazingly lucky.  An empty M60 bus pulled over and took us on.  So not only did we get to ride to Queens, but we all got seats.

I don't know how the Brooklynites in our group got home, or how my boss made it home to the Bronx.  Shame on me for not asking  :oops:

When I got to 30th Avenue, Jack's Bargain Stop was about to close, and there were crowds of people begging to get inside to buy flashlights and batteries.  I had both, but the problem of supper still remained for me.  I know that when the power is off, mild refrigeration still continues in the fridge, but only if the door is kept closed.  So I could forget about anything in the fridge.  I bought a shish kebab and a bottle of water from a street vendor, had the vendor wrap the kebab in foil, and ate it in my apt.  (If I hadn't had that recourse, I suppose I could have opened a can of tuna or beans.)

When I woke up the next morning, I turned on the lamp over my bed to see if the power had come back on.  It had.  But I took Mayor Bloomberg's advice and stayed home in Astoria.  I assumed the milk in my fridge had spoiled, because milk has a dreadfully short shelf life in the summer, so I went to Michael's for my morning coffee.  Whatever they had, milk or reconstituted powdered stuff, was fine.

Thank you for the stories about the parties, the new apartment and the romance!

I happened to be home when the power went out.  I had taken a few days off to do work around the apartment, and I was in the middle of assembling a file cabinet.  I thought I had blown a fuse because I'd been running the AC all day, but when I went into the halls I noticed all the lights were off.  I went into the courtyard with my portable, battery-powered radio and listened to the news, and that's when I found out just how bad things were.  I can't remember what I had for dinner--probably something I scrounged from the cabinets--and then I went to sleep pretty early.  My power came back on around 5 AM.  I'd had a minor surgical procedure a couple of weeks earlier and I was due to get my stitches out that next day, but when I called the doctor's office no one answered, so I assumed they didn't have power yet.  I had to live with those stitches for another week.

Can't believe it's been 5 yrs already!  I was working on the 7th floor of a bldg at 52nd and 6th in midtown.  We sat in our office for about 10 minutes until we noticed that people were spilling into the street from the surrounding buildings, and we left.  A group of about 15 of us headed out to a coworker's apt near Grand Central because she had a landline phone that we used to check in with our families.  When we realized that the outage was going to last a while, I decided against trying to get back to Astoria and took up a friend's invite to stay at her place in Hell's Kitchen.  We crossed through Times Square just after dusk, and it was simply surreal to see it dark.  We also managed to stumble upon a pizza place selling fresh pies, so we bought one and then settled in a park across the street from her building.  She brought the wine she had in fridge to drink with our pizza, and we befriended an elderly couple who was kind enough to share some homemade chocolate chip cookies with us.  The wife was in a wheelchair, and without use of the elevator they were stranded downstairs.  We chatted with them for a couple of hours before we went to sleep.  The next morning a friend of a friend gave me a ride back to Astoria. 

Though it was definitely an inconvenience, once it became clear that it didn't occur due to a terrorist act, I wasn't too bothered by the blackout.  To me it is one of those quintessential NY experiences that I will always be able to tell a good story about.  On the other hand, you had to feel for all the business owners (restaurants, grocery stores, etc.) who were most affected by it.
