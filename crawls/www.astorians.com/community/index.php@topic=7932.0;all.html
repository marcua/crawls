I don't understand why people are so upset at Frappe's post. This is a "rant & vent" forum; he appears to be venting. He also said he was on the train that hit that person. I think it is completely reasonable for him to be upset.

I also don't understand some of the criticisms. Everyone knows that the person who committed suicide was suffering and was in a terrible state, but this doesn't mean that all anger directed towards that person is somehow cruel or inhuman. I think it is also unfair to suggest that we ought to "think of what this poor person must have been going through in order to do something like take there own life," without also acknowledging what those people on that train went through. We can and should make room for those people to express their feelings, and many of those people, like it or not, will feel anger. Just imagine how terrible it would have been if you were a parent sitting with your children in the front of the train. I think most people in that situation would feel a deep sense of anger and frustration afterwards. That's completely natural. Why belittle or criticize those feelings?

Also, I think it's wrong to take Frappe's suggestion to imply that it's possible to commit suicide without affecting others. I think his point has to do with the degree to which it affects others. All suicides affect others. If you jump off of a building and land on a group of tourists though, it affects others in a much more egregious sense. I think this was what he had in mind concerning this particular suicide.

I think everyone should keep in mind that Frappe was on a train that killed someone two days ago. If he is upset about it and expresses his anger in the appropriate "rant & vent" forum, why not just see his post as a way for him to say what's on his mind and to get it off his chest?

I have had several people in my life commit suicide.  A cousin hanged himself after going through an ugly divorce and losing what he believed was everything in his life; a friend who had epilepsy and struggled with his seizures, the side affects of the medications that he took to control the seizures and a family who treated him like a reject because of his condition; a successful high school athlete and future minister who was gay, but was unable to tell his family because he grew up in the same backwards little town that I ran from as soon as I turned 18; and a struggling musician who battled with alcohol and drug addiction, until he finally decided to take a lethal overdose. 

In each situation, I had the mix of emotions regarding their loss.  There was anger, bitterness, and deep sadness.  I knew most of their families and mourned with them.  I found that being angry or bitter didn't really help and I struggled to empathize with what they were going through so I could identify other friends and acquaintances who were going through the same thing.  Through this experience I gained invaluable knowledge and compassion for people who are suffering to that extent and feel hopeless. 

I've never actually witnessed someone kill themselves and I'm pretty certain that it's a traumatic experience; however, based on my own personal losses, I tend not to judge the individual who has committed suicide without knowing more.  But then again, each person has their experience and their own opinion regarding this topic and I can't judge how they feel either.

Clinically speaking, suicide is rarely spontaneous. People who commit suicide have in fact planned it and are usually coming out of a depression. When an individual is so depressed and distraught, they can rarely move, get out of bed, or leave the house. It is when a little bit of energy is mustered, that suicide actually occurs, and usually with a specific plan.

There are certain suicides that are in fact a cry for help: notes that are left to be found, attempts at overdosing on nonlethal substances, cutting yourself in a way to get a lot of blood and effect, but not as lethal, etc.

However, jumping in front of a train is not a cry for help...there is no one to hear it. It is a violent act of self-injury. I'm not judging either way, and it is upsetting that an individual may feel that a situation has come to a point that it is no longer salvageable. It is unfortunate that others had their lives inconvenienced for a couple of hours, but on the bright side, a couple of hours of a delayed train in a life that you don't feel suicide is the answer...not so bad.

While not a cry for help, certain more violent suicides may be attention seeking, and wishing to cause pain to others...the more violent and disturbing, the more guilt the mourners may feel. Sort of a "they'll be sorry when I'm dead" scenario. Other more violent suicides may be by people that are so ambivalent, they are not confident they will be able to carry out the suicide, and to it in a way that leaves little room for error (jumping off the 20 story building, gun in the mouth, etc.)My two cents on the topic.
