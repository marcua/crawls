Title: Re: I want to open a bookstore
Post by: attila the hun on August 04, 2006, 10:04:38 AM
Good luck, but I would be very, very cautious on this.

Here are a few things to keep in mind.  The American Booksellers Association, the primary trade organization for independant bookstores, had 5000+ members about 10 years ago.  That number has dropped to about 1700 members today.

Finances for bookstores are tough.  For inventory alone you will need to be spend a lot of money.  As an example, most general bookstores try to turn their inventory twice a year.  This means that if you have $1 million in sales per year, you will need to have $500,000 in inventory at any given time to sustain this.

That $500K (at retail) will cost you approx $300K (at wholesale), and most publishers and wholesale companies will only extend 30-60 days of credit.  The back-of-the-envelope calculation means that you will be able to float $100K (at cost) of inventory on publishers credit (assuming an inventory turn of 2, $1 million in annual sales, and 60 day credit for all of your inventory), but the other $200K (at cost) of inventory will need to be paid for before you sell it.  In other words, out of your pocket. 

This is just the inventory cost.  You will also need to pay for real estate, wages, fixtures, cash registers/computers, advertising, legal fees, etc.

Mirki2's suggestions are more suited to a college bookstore business model.   I do not htink that this is what you are thinking about, but it is also not terribly suitable to Astoria, as there is no easy way to connect with the student population as a whole, and any students in the area will be attending a number of different institutions.  Plus, the college bookstore market is highly seasonal and operates on much lower gross margins than trade bookstores.

Title: Re: I want to open a bookstore
Post by: attila the hun on August 04, 2006, 10:27:56 AM
No problem.

University towns are generally good places for bookstores.  When I read your original post, it sounded like you were talking about textbooks.  This is a viable business in and of itself, but it has an entirely different set of dynamics from a trade book store which is what the OP was talking about (I think).  As Astoria does not have a sizable university/college (like UC-Santa Cruz), a student bookstore would simply not work. 

Now, a used bookstore in general might.  The con to this is that pretty much all of your inventory must be paid for up front, and your selection will vary depending on what is available to be purchased.  But, margins are generally pretty high, as you are purchasing the books at 10% of cover price (approx), and selling them at 50% of cover price (again, approx).  This gives you an 80% gross margin on inventory. 

Once a used store is up and running, you can start to obtain inventory from your customers, paying them in store credit to be used on future purchases.  This will help ensure a steady supply of 'new' inventory, plus a steady supply of returning customers who want to use their credits

People browsing in used bookstores are generally more tolerant of dated/utilitarian fixtures (look at the Strand compared to B&N for an example), so one would save $ in the initial setup process of the business.

I am not sure what an industry average inventory turn might be on used books though, and acquiring your initial inventory might be tough.

Title: Re: I want to open a bookstore
Post by: attila the hun on August 05, 2006, 03:51:26 PM
GenXnyc makes several excellent points, although I will offer a slightly different view for some things.

An ecommerce capable website is probably not critical.  If you are selling used books, simply set up as a seller on half.com (aka ebay) and/or abebooks.com.  This is an inexpensive way of getting huge distribution on the internet.

For new books online, simply realize that you will never be able to compete with amazon or bn.com.  Setting up your own site using yahooshops or a similar service will actually be a huge timesink.  (jacket art, blurbs, navigation, etc)  Now, having a website is critical for advertising purposes, but you want to put as little effort as possible into this.  For a good, easy, cheap option, checkout www.booksite.com.  For the record, I am not affiliated in any way with booksite, so this is not a self-interested plug.

All bookstores have return options with publishers.  This is generally for credit only on future purchases, and may be at a penalty.  The books must also not be shopworn (as Genxnyc describes).  Do not view this as a get out of jail free card if you make bad buying decisions.  You will need to pay freight on incoming shipments, out going shipments (for returns) plus the labor involved in initially receiving the books, then pulling them for return, plus all of the accounting.  Returns are very costly for the bookstore, even if you get full credit.

Be careful on the discounts that you offer to people.  Discounting is a valid way of attracting customers, but each and every penny of discounting comes from your gross profit margin.  You will also need to be very, very careful about your budget in terms of wages.  Go to the American Booksellers Association and get all of their literature.  If I remember correctly, they offer sample guidelines as to what percentage of overall sales you want to spend on rent, utilities, wages, freight in, freight out, credit card fees, etc. 

Community events are a great way to build a loyal following, and hopefully some paying customers.  One thing to watch out for is to not be too obscure in your offerings.  As an example, most bookstores need to carry poetry, or else they loose credibility with their customers.  Overall though, poetry is not a category that sells any books. 

Sidelines.  Do it.  Coffee sales, I Love Astoria T-shirts, greeting cards, whatever.  You will make more money from all of these things than you will make from the books themselves.

Title: Re: I want to open a bookstore
Post by: danielle1028 on May 13, 2007, 08:07:38 AM
I currently work as a publicist at a major publishing house and it is tough.  I have thought about opening a bookstore in this area, but the start-up costs are astounding and I work in publishing.  But what makes an indepenedent bookstore successful, here are some things I've learned from various booksellers across the country:

Know your customer, really know them.  Know their names, their preferences and most important their email address.  Which leads me to the newsletter.  You want an extensive email database to send out information. 

You're not going to get famous authors, there are too many established indies and the chains in the city for you to even think of getting a big name.  Don't rely on this.  You can do authorless events.  There is a great childrens book called Fancy Nancy.  Hold a best Fancy outfit contest for girls aged 4-12 and they win a book.  You have to be creative with your events.  If down the road you do get a big name, sell tickets that include the price of the book.  Publisher's love this because we can quantify how successful an event is.

And I totally recommend alcohol.  It loosens people up and they end up buying more.  Pair it up with a wine book or a travel through Tuscany book.

You have to be the go to place for people to talk about what is going on in the community.

BOOK GROUPS!!!!  I know one indie that has over 200 book groups in their store.  You will need one staff person to organize this, or someone from the community.  You can offer suggestions, a place to meet and the possibility of getting in touch with the author if it is a mid-list or debut author.  And even better, you know what they like b/c of their book group preference and can suggest other reads.

You are lucky enough to live 20 minutes away from all of the major publishing houses.  Make friends.  Call their customer service numbers and see if there is someone that will talk to you about starting a new bookstore.  Call other indies from around the country - Schwartz (the midwest), Tattered Cover (THE indie bookstore, located in Denver), BookPeople (austin), etc.

Search for and join NAIBA - it's a regional organization for independent booksellers in the North Atlantic.

Don't be a literature snob.  There is a reason why Mary Higgins Clark, Janet Evanovich and Nora Roberts are on the bestseller list - THEY SELL.   Get a subscription to Publishers Weekly to see what is doing well and what trends the industry is facing.  To that point mass market romance readers are rabid readers - up to 6 to 8 paperbacks a month, we like them.

Publicity - this is a great story... pitch it.  And annouce every event or sale you have.  Make friends with local reporters, create a media list.  Publicist will ask for this when they call your store to book an author, if you don't have it, it means more work for us and we don't like that.

I'm getting wordy, so here a couple more and then I'm out:

A lounge/event area
