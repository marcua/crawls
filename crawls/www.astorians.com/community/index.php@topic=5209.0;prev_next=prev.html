Just want to begin a general thread on volunteer experiences.

Wanted to consider this a while before posting, because, I have sometimes found myself disappointed in  my volunteering experiences. I don't know whether this has more to do with expectations being too high, or not. But, first the good:

My company is very philanthropic, several times a year we participate in "Skip Lunch: Fight Hunger" and our division is often one of the highest in the city as far as donations. That's run by City Harvest and they seem to be one of the most productive and pro-active organizations in the city, they do a tremendous amount to help feed people of all ages and all over NYC.

Then there is our "Adopt-a-School" program that we take part in twice a year, creating a school library in a low-income public NYC school, organizing literacy classes, and donating books to individual classrooms. This is perhaps my favorite thing to do, it's truly an incredible thing to teach someone to read and the kids get such pleasure out of having their own book, so many of them have never had more than a beat-up textbook, that when you tell them the book they've just learned to read is theirs to keep, they are just absolutely thrilled. Lots of hugs involved. These initiatives are all the better because you can't take away the ability to read once its been given, so, as far as volunteering goes, it can't be beat.

Shoreline cleanup in the Rockaways, I believe it was through the NYC Parks Department, I did this years ago. We organized large groups to basically go up and down the beach and pick up garbage, perhaps an exercise in futility, but at the time the shore was being bombarded with refuse from illegal dumping and about 100 of us picked up somewhere close to a quarter of a ton of garbage in a day, which was then properly disposed of (some of you may remember when there were lots of medical waste and needles appearing on the beaches and they had to be shut down).

I have been disappointed in NYCares, though I really like the idea of what they do, and I have had good and sort of bad with them (it's still worthwhile, no volunteering is completely without merit). I've had two locations that I felt weren't managed properly and one location that left me scratching my head as to why we were there as the school was pretty darn incredible as far as the resources (in my experience of NYC public schools), so I felt the hours and bodies could have been put to better use elsewhere. On another occasion it was simply too much work for too small a group (and I later heard that there was a group nearby that had too little to do for the number of people they had, and that was frustrating). However, this isn't NYCares fault, it's the fact that they are limited to go to places that request assistance, and I imagine, as is so often the case, the neediest places aren't aware of the options available to them.

And finally, through work again, in conjunction with an organization called First Book, which is a terrific group, but, again, I was disappointed in the leadership at the site, as it was less than stellar. What my company does for First Book is donate books that would otherwise be scrapped and destroyed to schools all over the country. Teachers get the list of books and send in their requests. We as volunteers were responsible for putting together the pallets to get them ready for shipment. What wound up happening was we had to stand around for an hour before they were ready for us and when we were finally set to start we realized they had absolutely no rhyme or reason to how the titles were placed, and we (the volunteers) wasted lots of time reorganizing the books so that we could get most of the orders out on time (the warehouse space to house the books is donated, too, and usually only for the day, so whatever isn't completed in time doesn't go out, and that's just unacceptable in my opinion).

I know these places are overwhelmed and understaffed, but, it seems to me that they should try to perhaps scale back so the work they do is more useful and concentrated rather than scattered and half-complete.

In the end, as I said, it's all worthwhile even if it doesn't "fix" everything, and I've only ever had experiences with people who did truly mean well, even if they weren't the best at getting things together. I've luckily never been a part of an organization that wasn't honestly trying to help.
