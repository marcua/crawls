I would not advise your daughter to carry mace or any other "self defense weapon" that can be taken away from her and used against her. I'm a small woman myself and once I was shown how quickly I can be disarmed, I stopped carrying anything like that.

Instead, I recommend self-defense classes. The New York Jiu Jitsu Center offers free rape self-defense seminars once a month (call for dates). The best thing about these classes was the understanding of how to handle situations BEFORE they escalate to the point that a woman needs to physically defend herself. The sensei (a woman) focuses on language to dissuade a potential attacker or simple harrasser from bothering her. I also recommend the book The Gift of Fear by Gavin de Becker.

In this particular case, since we don't know what your daughter finally said to get the guy to leave her alone, it's hard to tell whether the guy was actively threatening or simply a creep who wasn't getting the picture (yes, I agree that an harrassment is implicitly a threat, but there's also a difference between Ignorant Creep and Guy Testing Her Boundaries To See If He Can Rape Her). In the future, she should try saying whatever it was louder and sooner. I think going to the police is a bit of overkill at this point but if the same guy talks to her a second time, she should go talk to them.

I do think this type of behavior is unusual for Astoria, and actually for New York City in general. I've been catcalled or had guys attempt to strike up a conversation when it was clear I wasn't interested, but I've very rarely been followed and never in broad daylight.

Now imagine a clean-cut, decent-looking white guy with a charming voice asking the same things: asking whether she's from the area and whether she comes to the laundromat often.  Admit that this suddenly seems like a very different and harmless set of circumstances.

Uh, no.  That is BS.  First off, who goes more than a few blocks to do laundry?  Sure, it's possible that someone might be visiting a friend, but the vast majority of people in a laundromat are from nearby.  And being followed into the laundromat from the street is just creepy no matter who is doing it.  And if the guy is innocently trying to strike up a conversation in the hopes of getting to know her, and does it this way, he is an idiot and should know better that it's probably not going to be well received.

IMO, the best weapon in this sort of situation (daylight hours, other people around) is common sense.  If it feels wrong, don't ignore the feeling - anyone is within their right to respond loudly and assertively if they do not trust the situation.  Taking a self-defense class is also a good idea.  But honestly, a woman in NYC protects herself the same way she protects herself anywhere - there's nothing special about protecting yourself here.  Stay alert, use your common sense, and be as strong physically and mentally as possible.  Don't be afraid to speak up and tell someone to back off, and don't think you have to do it politely or quietly, either.  What others said is true -- just about any woman is going to get unwanted attention at some point, and really the best way to deal with it is to find a response that you are comfortable with.

Unfortunately, I don't think the police care.  This sort of thing isn't even on their radar.  It is honestly minor enough that even if they take the report (no guarantee; I know people who have tried to file a report after being accosted or nearly assaulted, and the 114 refused to take the report), it is highly unlikely that they will do anything.  The only possible benefit to filing a report might be to establish a pattern, but even that isn't guaranteed.
