Jonathan,
I have made this drive too many times to count and each time it was phenomenal. I'm envious of you two!! I have way too many recommendations...

Let me emphasize this as my biggest piece of advice: make concrete bookings at the various B&Bs along PCH between Santa Cruz and San Luis Obispo. I decided to do "free form" a couple times and ended up without a place to pause along that long strech of scenic cliff views... nowhere to stop for the night except a turn-out overlooking the palisades (slept in the car-- fun, yes. comfortable? yeah right.)

Here are my Best Of's, starting with SF and going south to my homeland of LA, going on PCH for the most part-- until you hit Santa Barbara --and then change to state highway 101... trust me. PCH turns into some back roads that although interesting and adventurous, veer pretty far off from the gorgeous coastline... There's SO MUCH but I dont want to bore everyone. Here are the major sites:

Annie's Top Ten

*Forget about Pebble Beach and the 17-mile drive. Its lame and you have to PAY like $20 for it at the toll booth (I am NOT all about snooty private beaches)!

1) GILROY! "Garlic Capital of the World" Roll down your windows and make a pit stop here for some Garlic Ice Cream... Christopher Farms was the old company that called Gilroy home but now ConAgra has bought the brand. Ew. BUT if you're lucky they'll be having the annual Garlic Festival (I forget when it is, exactly)...

2) Casa de Frutas, Hollister: It looks like a weird fruity truck stop and park in one, and indeed it is. You're still in Garlic Country so enjoy the garlic-roasted pistachios (you wont believe how cheap it is!) and the homemade wines, preserves, and of course FRUITS and vegggies! They have the most extensive selection of chocolate-covered apricots I've ever seen. Stock up on snacks for the road trip. Check out the live peacock birds roaming the grounds, too.

3) Half Moon Bay-- some of the best surfing in and around Santa Cruz! Great breakfast spots and taquerias!

4) The Mystery Spot (a SC institution for goofy kitchy fun... affixing the signature bumper sticker is a mark of hokey sagacity. Seriously.)

5) BIG SUR! There's no entrance fee, no reservations... just miles and miles of the most beautiful coastline. Everyone can be an Ansel Adams here. Be sure to look for all the sea lions (they're not seals). 

6) Monterey Bay Aquarium (and neighboring Carmel): This is a STAPLE of the drive. Its my favorite oceanographic center in CA (better than the Long Beach one, however it is smaller)... they have this exhibit of the zonal habitats that is HUGE and they pump seawater into it continuously and there's a real live kelp forest... aw man I'm getting homesick! More adorable sea lions.

7) Hearst Castle, San Simeon: again, it costs extra to actually go up there but its pretty ridiculous and pretty interesting to consider what the media tycoon did with his little patch of utopia. Yet more sea lions (all on Hearst property, mind you)

8) Mission San Luis Obispo: SLO as we call it is a great college town with AMAZING burrito stands and some really sweet laid back people. Not as hoighty-toighty as Santa Barbara (yet?)... the mission is what used to be crowning jewel in the California Mission system, all connected by El Camino Real (as you will see signs for)... this pick is a sentimental throwback to my geeky school field trip days, but the spanish architecture (yay adobe bricks) is really wondrous and the gardens are great when in bloom...

9) If you continue on PCH toward Santa Barbara, you'll get an priceless experience: seeing a smidgen of the state's fertile central coast. Did you know that this region produces upt o 75% of the entire country's fruits and vegetables?? Big tip: stop at any fruit stand, strawberries will be in season and the miles and miles of strawberry aroma along the highway will be irresistable! This is the southern end of the agricultural belt known as the Grapevine (expands vastly inland). Juxtapose the simple rural life here against what you're about to see in Santa Barbara!
If you switch to the 101 then you'll see more awesome coastline and surfers and Carpenteria, Summerland. If you have time, try taking a diversion to Ojai... its a cute artsy commune like town of pottery kilns and hippies and great farmland and cheeses.

7) Santa Barbara: there's some really ritzy shoppping here. Try El Paseo Nuevo along the historic State Street downtown for some good touristy sights... if you're feeling mroe well-heeled, try going toward the Mission (yes, of course SB had one too!) and eating at the small taquerias on the hill. Parking is expensive downtown (its looking more and more like LA) so I would park further out and rent bikes, its great. Warning: don't wander into Isla Vista or Goleta or else you might get hit with a kegger or otherwise shiftless UCSB stonerstudents (although there is a good cheapo burrito place there)

pass through Ventura and Oxnard, CA where they also have annual strawberry festivals... if you're a surfer these parts can be akin to Mecca in the late summer...

Dont bother stopping in Camarillo. Notable only for its Factory Outlet shopping nightmare strip malls and for the fact that Charlie Parker was institutionalized here in the early part of the last century.

10) finally, The Getty Museum, LA: its up on Mulholland as the 101 segues into the 405. Yeah its a sappy vote but its free and if you havent been there, its  really SoCal's finest museum (sorry, fans of MoCA)

Oh wait, and I didnt even talk about Solvang or sheperd's bread or Pea Soup Andersens!!!!

Too much.

I miss my motherstate.

For more extended raves (and rants), email me!

see you sometime soon (I can show you pictures for better illustration, of course)
annie

Is it still what it used to be and is the food stilltop notch?  do i need to pack a suit?

Food is still top notch.  No need to wear a suit, at least upstairs.  It is Berkeley, and I don't think anyone will get on your case about not wearing a suit.  Wear nice clothes, though.

I love the Laura Chenel warm goat cheese salad.  It was at Chez Panisse where I finally understood how amazing fruit desserts are.  I had a chocolate cake (incredible!) but found the fruit-based desserts to be even more amazing.  It's just truly wonderful food and absolutely worth a visit.  The Cheese Board across the street is excellent, as is their pizza a few doors down.  Masse's Pastries, on Shattuck, but on the next block from Chez Panisse, is also a favorite of mine.  I think it's in the old Chocolat space.  A few doors down from Masse's is Black Oak Books, a wonderful independent bookstore.  Around the corner is The Walk Shop which has great shoes that are all geared toward walking.  Juice Bar Collective is on the same block, and will make you a good hippie juice drink.  On the corner of Walnut and Vine, next to The Walk Shop, is the original Peet's, which would later spawn Starbucks.  Oh, and next to Chez Panisse is Cesar, which makes wonderful tapas and excellent drinks.

For good Indian food, try VIK on Allston and 6th Street, I think.  It's chaat in a warehouse setting.

Apparently there is good gelato on Shattuck, too.

Great breakfast in Berkeley is the Homemade Cafe on Sacramento Street and Dwight.  It is my favorite breakfast place in town.  Also good is La Note on Shattuck.  Very French.  Delicious.

Good place to hike in the area is in Tlden Park and at the Sibley Vocanic Preserve.  Nice Botanical Gardens in Tilden and also on the UC Berkeley Campus.  Good bubble tea across from campus on Bancroft, called Quickely.  Cheap Vietnamese food nearby on Bancroft, too, at Le Petit Cheval.  3 dishes for $5, you can't beat that!

I miss that town.

Other nice places to eat in Berkeley are Lalime's on Cedar, I think it is.  Fanny's is another breakfast place on the corner of San Pablo Ave. and Gilman/Cedar.  It is part of the Chez Panisse scene.  Next door is Kermit Lynch Wines, a nice wine shop.  My favorite place, though, for wine is North Berkeley Wines on Cedar.  There are two good taquerias, Cancun and La Cascada; I'm a La Cascada girl myself.  Wonderful salsa bar.  I also enjoy the green mole cheese enchiladas at Cactus Taquera on Solano.  Britt-Marie's on Solano in Albany is a really nice wine bar and they make a good spanikopita and good roast chicken.  Glasses of wine run around $6 or so.  Good selection.  I remember sitting at the huge bar and laughing til I could laugh no more, the bartender at the time was so funny.  Two good bookstores on Solano, Pegasus and Half Price Books.

There is also great Ethiopian food in town.  Lots of people like The Blue Nile, but I prefer Ethiopia Restaurant on Telegraph or Cafe Colucci on College, or even Asmara in Oakland.  Speaking of College Avenue, you should go to Market Hall in Rockridge, under the Rockridge BART station.  Lots of great foodie things there.  Good coffee and Grace Bakery is there - the chocolate mint brownies are awesome. Oliveto is there and it is wonderful.  You can sit downstairs for wine/drinks/coffee/tea and tapas of sorts.

Four words: 
Berkeley Bowl
Monterey Market 
Two incredible stores.  MM is a little more downtempo and has things that are often not marked and you wonder what they are.  Exotic Asian fruits and such.  You can stop in at the Hopkins Street Bakery while you're there, or sit on the patio and relax at the cafe on the corner of Monterey and Hopkins.  Berkeley Bowl is just a marvel.  So much good stuff there, it's hard to fathom.  30 different kinds of citrus; local bread; great wines; cheese; OMG.

Kirala for sushi.

LoCocos pizza is pretty good.  Zachary's is Chicago-style, if you like that sort of thing.

Mr. Mopps is a fun place to buy kids toys.

I'll try to think of more and perhaps adjust this post with linkage. 

Meg

P.S. Important to make a reservation well-ahead of time for CP.  And on that note, you might consider the French Laundry up in the wine country.  Pricey and exclusive, I know, but if you can get a reservation and you eat there, you life will be changed forever.
