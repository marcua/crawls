Going Local on a Global Scale: Rethinking Food Trade in the Era of Climate Change, Dumping, and Rural Poverty

Spring/Summer 2005


by Kirsten Schwind
Food First Program Director

Trade Fuels Climate Change

Advocating for local food requires reexamining the deeply held economic theory of competitive advantage, which holds that each region should specialize in producing only what it can produce most cheaply, then trade with other regions for everything else. However, traditional economic calculations do not account for the true environmental cost of trade. For example, the potentially cataclysmic impacts of climate change mean that the environmental costs of transporting goods long distances are much higher than previously thought.

Most food travels hundreds, even thousands, of miles from farm to plate,[1] and the fossil fuel transportation infrastructure we rely on for all this trade emits greenhouse gasses that are contributing to climate change.[2] Climate change is raising sea temperatures and flooding coastal areas, and has the potential to increase crop failures, cause mass extinctions, and spur more destructive weather patterns such as hurricanes—all with profound implications for agriculture and human habitation.[3] Since the full consequences will not be felt for years after the greenhouse gasses have been emitted, it is exceedingly difficult to predict and price future ecological damage and add it to the energy costs of today’s food system. Thus even prices that are adjusted to include current energy subsidies or minor “climate change taxes” are not reliable indicators of the ecological and social price of fossil fuel–driven global trade.

Buying local food can make a big difference to the environment. For example, in 1920 Iowa produced a wide variety of fruits and vegetables, but now most of its fruits and vegetables are shipped from elsewhere. If Iowans bought just 10 percent more of their food from within the state, they could collectively save 7.9 million pounds of carbon dioxide emission a year.[4] The Japanese environmental organization Daichi-o-Mamoru Kai (the Association to Preserve the Earth) found that if Japanese families consumed local food instead of imported food, the impact would be equivalent to reducing household energy use by 20 percent; the biggest impact would come from eating tofu products from soy grown in Japan instead of in the US.[5] And researchers in the UK have calculated that purchasing local food has a greater positive impact on the environment than buying organic food that is not local.[6] While some food trade is inevitable, such as tropical products like coffee that are staples in colder climates, a surprising amount of trade is duplicative and ecologically wasteful. For example, Heinz ketchup eaten in California is made with California-grown tomatoes that have been shipped to Canada for processing and returned in bottles. In one year, the port of New York City exported $431,000 worth of California almonds to Italy, and imported $397,000 worth of Italian almonds to the United States.[7] This sort of unnecessary trade mortgages our children’s planet for profits today.

1 See Brian Halweil, Eat Here: Reclaiming Homegrown Pleasures in a Global Supermarket (New York: Norton, 2004) for an overview of studies on food miles. This very readable book is an excellent overview of the richness of the local food movement in the United States.

2 Hansen, et al., “Earth’s Energy Imbalance: Confirmation and Implications,” Science 2005 0: 11102522.

3 Canadian Department for International Development (DFID), et al., 2002, Linking Poverty Reduction and Environmental Management: Policy Challenges and Opportunities.

4 Rich Pirog, Timothy Van Pelt, Kamyar Enshayan, and Ellen Cook, 2001, Food, Fuel, and Freeways: An Iowa Perspective on How Far Food Travels, Fuel Usage, and Greenhouse Gas Emissions, Ames, IA: Leopold Center for Sustainable Agriculture, Iowa State University.

5 “Consumption of local food helps cut CO2 emissions,” March 2, 2005, Kyodo News.

6 J. N. Pretty, A. S. Ball, T. Lang, and J. I. L. Morison, 2005, “Farm Costs and Food Miles: An assessment of the full cost of the UK weekly food basket,” Food Policy 30:1–19.

7 Katy Mamen, Steve Gorelick, Helena Norberg-Hodge, and Diana Deumling, 2004, Ripe for Change: Rethinking California’s Food Economy, International Society for Ecology and Culture.

See lanseaux's post above for reference.

It is possible that chains would only bring in solely outside food, but that is entirely dependent upon the kind of supply chain they have.  I would suspect that most are made through an arrangement with a Sysco type company which probably does bulk purchases from the cheapest vendor it can find and then handles any shortfalls with local purchases.  Even so, the delivery driver for the food distribution and the warehouse workers are still part of the local community.  These types of jobs would not be available so the tax base is expanded locally in a way not possible with local only companies.  It's merely a shift in the business demographic of the community.  Less of one kind of worker more of another.  Without seeing some hard numbers based on a rigorously crafted study, I'd say it's six of one half dozen of the other.

The chain, using economy of scale, is able to lower the cost and in many instances provide cheaper goods and services.  Sticking with food, you'll be able to get more pounds of groceries from a chain than a local market.  This means Jack factory worker barely beating minimum has more money to pay other bills.  Now those dollars are freed up for him to spend locally on his pint at the pub or slice of pie.

I think the organic community needs to focus on the non monetary aspect because that's where the benefits truly lie.  Unless someone can develop a megaorganic farm that can lower costs like the big non-organic farms do.  Based on my farming knowledge (I had an interest in commercial farming for a long time so I do speak with some knowlege) the organics can't compete in that way.  Too much crop is lost to pests and the bulk weight produced is less because it isn't chemically enhanced.

Short of government legislation that would level the playing field, organics will always cost more pound per pound and I'm still back to the dubious aspect of more money actually being spent locally with locally grown foods.  I'd stick with health benefits, environmental benefits, and quality of taste as the reasoning.

But again, if there are some numbers out there someone can point me to, I'd be willing to read and ready to be wowed.

see megc's post for reference

As for fossil fuels, it sounds good in theory, but I doubt that if people stopped trucking food more than fifty miles the levels of emmissions would go down measurably.  I'd suspect that more short hauls would be more polluting.  Using purely speculative logic, when your vehicle is driving at highway speeds for long distances, it is doing less polluting than a vehicle that drives around the city with starts and stops all day.

I would also supsect that many localities would not have a good variety of fruits and vegetables.  Would this impact health as people in places like remote North Dakota and desert communities in Nevada had no access to foods like oranges or rice?  Do we make exceptions for them and if we do then how much does this detract from the overall reduction of emmissions we're trying to receive.

My problems with statistics that offer stats like "if only we hadn't imported those almonds the emissions would have dropped by ____" is that it is all speculative and assumes that everything happens in a vacuum or that there are no other consequences.  These types of studies drive me batty as they are very provacative, but don't really offer us any solutions or alternatives.  People in the desert will still eat watermellon and there is no reason they shouldn't.  I'm sure there's an environmental consequence for trying to grow rice or watermellons in the desert that isn't emission related but is possibly just as damaging.

I think everyone's heart is in the right place, but I don't think the economics backs it up and the studies listed so far are specious at best.

I'll have to tackle these one at a time over time.  Too many irons in the fire.

I've examined the Maine study, but I can see a couple of flaws in the presumptions of the case.
1)  Relative percentages are valid in this case.
2)  Exact amounts equal exact goods and services.

1) In this case, I'd say that economies of scale mitigate the effect of charitable giving in that the impact is much more while giving a smaller percent.  If you give $100 a year annually to charity, if you get a 10% increase in salary, I'd argue that it is a rare individual who also bumps the giving a corresponding $10.  The fact the big box store gives 1k per million of sales is likely to result in a much larger total amount to charity than the eight businesses held up as the local business sample.

2)  Here is where the equation really breaks down.  $100 at a big box gets a lot more than $100 at a more expensive small business.  What this means is that I have more money to spend locally when I'm done shopping at the big box store.  Even presuming the money spent there goes away at the exact rate listed in the survey, it does nothing to take the savings factor into account.  To give a quick example using fresh direct here in the city.  Family of four buys 2lbs of salmon filet, 2 bunches of broccoli and a half gallon of milk for a simple dinner.  Family 1 buys only organic (simulated the big box vs. small local guy scenario)  and family 2 buys farm raised less healthy fish and veggies and skim milk.

ALL PRICES LISTED AS OF TODAY
cost to family 1 = $23.98 ($11.99/lb) + $7.98 ($3.99/bunch) + $2.99 (the cheapest skim available)
$34.95 for a modest healthy meal

cost to family 2 = $14.98 (most expensive farm raised available vs. $13.98 for cheapest) + $3.98 ($1.99/bunch w/chemicals for free) + $2.59  (cheapest skim)
$21.55

A difference of $13.40/day to the family opting for the cheaper products.  If both families eat similar meals on a daily basis with similar price differentials (assuming some meals will be more expensive and some will be less expensive but averaging out about the same) for just 15 days out of the month--a full half a month eating out.  The extra money for the more frugal family adds up to $201 more dollars to spend.  That's a lot more money to be spent locally.  Now we could get into the whole what if they go to other chain stores, but then we'd need that character from the television show numbers because this stuff gets extremely complicated very fast which is why most studies are too simplistic to get a handle around it.

However, when you are the head of a household in charge of feeding/clothing your family, you will need a lot more arguments than "keep it local" when faced with cold hard reality of how much money you are saving each month at the big box and buying farm raised products instead of organic.  I'd suggest targeting the audience and play up the health benefits.

However, when you are the head of a household in charge of feeding/clothing your family, you will need a lot more arguments than "keep it local" when faced with cold hard reality of how much money you are saving each month at the big box and buying farm raised products instead of organic.  I'd suggest targeting the audience and play up the health benefits.


There are a few things going on here...

Firstly, one has to be really careful of interchanging "organic" with "local".  I think that both of these things has its merits.  Organic food is really important to some people and they would rather buy organic from Australia than low-spray from upstate.  I my history in this job and field, I have only once had someone try to argue that organic food isn't more expensive.  It simply is.  Yes, if you weigh the "hidden costs", it evens out a bit, but as the one balancing a checkbook for a family, you are lucky if you are able to weigh those things, too.  In a grocery store it's very easy to price one next to the other and know that you are paying to have someone pick squash beetles from each and every leaf.  (Which doesn't seem like that much more when you think of how much you would want to be paid to do that.)  

Buying locally means something completely different and isn't always more expensive.  However, buying locally isn't as easy as buying organic... unless you shop at Whole Foods or Fresh Direct, you aren't able to know exactly where your food comes from.  (And while I don't really know, it doesn't seem like either Fresh Direct or Whole Foods is known for low prices.) Buying locally often means farmers markets or CSA sites.... and while I obviously think that these work, they're not always as easy as stopping by the corner grocery to pick up some broccoli.  However, CSA and buying from Farmers Markets aren't always that expensive (with CSA being more affordable).  I think that CSA is a remarkable thing because it allows one to fit organic, local foods into their diet without breaking the bank.  There should be one in every neighborhood, perhaps especially lower-income neighborhoods with higher rates of chronic illness.  

When I first became aware of local and sustainable ag it was after hearing about the way that monoculture and the World Bank had affected the Gambia.  (The facts on this are a bit foggy in my brain.)  The way it boiled down, for centuries the Gambians had been sustainable farmers and then they were forced to raise their GDP and began only growing groundnuts for export.  Then they were starving.  On a different level, that's exactly what we are doing, only regionally.  It seems like a slippery slope.  With a fuel crisis, civil war, major catastrophe or the like, we'd be limited to buying from our general area.  And by the time that happened, we might have eliminated all of our local small farms and community gardens.  (Reference the Cuba's Diet article from April's Harper's.)  

and yes, people in the desert will eat watermelon.  I eat avocado.  You probably eat pineapple.  And there isn't really any reason that they/we shouldn't, seeing that they/we can.  (I'm smirking a bit writing that, though, since there's no reason we shouldn't practice eugenics/map DNA/clone pigs/walk on the moon.  I mean, we can, can't we?)  For me, this is partly a moral issue and partly about stewardship.  If we let our systems get completely out of hand, we then have to live with them, come what may.
