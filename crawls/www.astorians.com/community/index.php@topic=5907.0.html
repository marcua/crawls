The Anti-War Movement
« on: April 10, 2007, 09:34:27 AM »
http://news.yahoo.com/s/ap/20070410/ap_on_go_ca_st_pe/us_troops_iraq;_ylt=Ao0gheqlt43xhTVDEQibETrMWM0F

When I read that the administration is using a backdoor way to up the troop levels, I was ready to pop a vein and badmouth them.  Then I got to thinking that I can't treally blame them.  Why ought they?  The anti-war movement is dedicated yet appallingly small, given the unpopularity of the war and using the 1960's as a comparison.

So what's the theory on why such an unpopular war hasn't spawned an anti-war movement akin to the one a generation ago?

I'm guessing that the infrastructure for social mobilization which existed from the civil rights movement was co-opted to anti-war movement, as well as the hippie backlash against the uber-conformity of the 1950's, but there has to be more than that.

On this thread, please, no long cut and pastes.  Discussion and links only.

Frappe --
You say a generation ago but keep in mind the Vietnam war was two, almost three generations ago – it was a different world then.

Here's my thoughts:

1) People had a more direct stake during the Vietnam war, i.e., the draft. Nothing more mobilizing than the prospect of fighting and dying in an overseas war by force. Today’s all volunteer military negates this.

2) Demographics – The US of today is much older than the US of the 1960s. The anti-war movement of the 1960s, like many others, was born from the idealism of youth.

3) Today’s teenagers and young adults are much more conservative than their parents and grandparents.

The draft is definately the number one reason more people aren't up in arms over the war.  During 'Nam there must have been a fear among men that they would be called up and forced to fight and maybe die in a war without a clear sense of purpose like WWII had.  

Also I think that media and the co-opt of counter culture for commercial marketing and the idea of packaging youth as this tangible thing you can be by buying the right clothes and listening to the right music has made a generation of cynics.  All that marching and protest and for what?  We still live with a growing military complex, corporations have become more powerful than since the trust busting of the 1900's and voting turn out is lower than ever.  A lot of young people look back and don't feel that a lot really got accomplished, and I happen to be one of them.  We've made things slightly more fair for miniorities and women (not gays though) but things for people on the whole have not really gotten better.  So why get up early and march and maybe have to post bail?
