Very strange thing happened to me yesterday.  Has this happened to anyone else?

I went into the Dunkin Donuts in that strip mall on 45th and Ditmars.  I tried to buy a medium latte.  the big sign with prices said $2.49 for a medium latte.  the cashier rung it up as $2.89.  I pointed out her error (only noticed because i had already taken out $3.00 to pay and she wanted $3.14, which would be a pretty hefty tax on a $2.49 purchase.)  anyway, she insisted that the "real" price was $2.89 and wouldn't even agree to turn around to look at the sign hanging above her head with the big $2.49 price listed.  I stood there a bit dumbfounded saying "but your sign says $2.49."  When it became clear that i was not going to produce more money, she took my $3.00 (she actually owed me change) and shoved it in the drawer and told me to leave.  she would not give me a receipt. 

I was in a rush to get to work so did not ask for the manager (plus there was a long line behind me at that point).  Anyway, i wanted to know if this has happened to anyone else.  If so, I think I should report it, as it is illegal to charge a price higher than the one posted on your sign.  But, if it was an isolated incident, i will just chalk it up to a bad morning and let it go.

Rebb,
  I'm sure that both the city and state have laws against advertising a product at one price and selling it another.  However at many franchise chains, many of these advertisements have a small print disclaimer such as "at participating stores, etc."
  Personally, I wouldn't have been so accommodating.  When I patronize an establishment, I expect a minimum level of customer service and don't hesitate to report insubordinate employees to the manager.  I think that it is not only in my best interest, but the stores, and the employees as well.
  You benefit from getting it off your chest, the store benefits by being alerted to bad customer service, and the employee benefits from being called to task for unacceptable behavior.  The employee needs to be taught the responsibility and people skills required to deal with the public.  This may sound harsh but, come on, if you can't do a decent job working at a "no brainer" fast food job, how can you ever expect to succeed in more responsible positions in the future.  I've worked fast food and I know that it isn't the most rewarding job, but you are paid to provide a service.  If don't want to provide the service, then find a job that doesn't require you deal with the public.
  Whenever this happens to me and I'm in a hurry, I simply say, "Thanks, but no thanks!" and walk away leaving the product on the counter and the cashier with an over ring that the manager will have to take care of.  But of course, if you really, really need your latte...That's a different matter. Whats an extra 40 cents plus tax but an extra 15% surcharge to get your blood flowing in the morning?
