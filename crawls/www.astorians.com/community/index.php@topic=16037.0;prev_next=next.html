Back in the day, I had 800+ songs in my Napster collection. I don't think any of those took $$$ away from music companies, because my collection included:

-Stuff I already owned, but didn't have handy
-Stuff I longed to own (rarities) but couldn't find anywhere
-Stuff I already hear on the radio for free
-Stuff I heard good things about and wanted to hear before buying the CD

What made me nuts was how the music companies complained about how bad Napster was for the artists. Such B.S. It merely cut out the middleman (the music companies.) With Napster, bands could get instant exposure, and people could go out and buy the album with the lyrics, liner notes, photos, backstories etc.

Before mp3s, there were cassette tapes. More clumsy, but still provided free music for the taking. With the internet, artists can market themselves directly and Big Fat Corporate doesn't get to screw everybody over.

That's my take on it, anyway!

But what about the claims of artists seeing declines in profits and the substantial drop in music sales world wide? Couldn't that be a direct result of the internet music sharing trend? And if so how can these artists continue to make money for creating the music that we all want to listen to?

No, downloading music is not the same as listening to it on the radio. And like I said, internet music sharing is good for some, bad for others. The drop in music sales is undoubtedly a result of being able to download music for free online. Because while some of us do care about supporting the artists we like, many others don't give a crap. I would guess that this is especially true for those who typically listen to Top 40 music and don't go around searching for new artists they like, because they assume the big artists are rich and the record companies don't need the money, so they feel no moral imperative to spend money on a product they can get for free.

Unfortunately, there is some indirect suffering spread among the music community because of the money being lost by the major labels. Look into something called the Music Performance Fund. From their website (http://www.musicpf.org/AboutUs.html): "A small royalty on the sale of some recorded music is contributed to the Fund by signatory recording companies, and these funds are supplemented by co-sponsoring organizations." This funding has declined significantly due to the large decrease in music sales.
Here's an article from the LA Times last year about what's happening to the MPF: http://articles.latimes.com/2008/jun/14/entertainment/et-freemusic14
I know someone who played in a community band in Connecticut whose concerts were sponsored by the MPF, and that band is no more because of this. It's sad, but that's how the music industry is evolving. Hopefully something else will come up and take its place, or the fund will be able to adapt. Interesting point from the article:

Quote
Recorded music had been steadily supplanting live gigs for the rank and file. After 1929, film soundtracks eliminated the job of silent-movie accompanist. Jukeboxes were knocking humans off barroom bandstands, and radio stations increasingly filled their music hours with cheap discs instead of costly live performers.


The more things change, the more they stay the same.
As for making money in the midst of declining album sales, licensing has become pretty huge, as a lot of TV series and movies have gone the way of putting existing songs in their soundtracks as opposed to hiring composers to fill all that time.

I think most people don't have a  problem paying for music, but people won't pay for music until it's easier to acquire purchased music than it is free music.  The Big 4 have been dragging their heels in making this possible because they would rather spend their time suing normal people for ridiculous amounts of money and blaming everyone but themselves for getting into this mess.

The popularity of the Internet coincided with an increase in broadband availability and digital storage technology, so not only is it easy to get music instantaneously, but anyone can keep all the music they could ever want without ever having to buy a CD.  The record companies should have adapted to this 10 years ago, but refuse to learn their lesson (they had the same problems with cassette tapes and recordable CDs when they came out, until they worked out a deal where they got part of the profits of blank media sales).  Now they are stuck because it seems like they feel that finding a solution that will make them happy is too daunting a task.  Instead, they increase media cost and decrease production value (10 songs on a crappy pop CD for $22?) in a shallow attempt to illustrate how much piracy is hurting them, which only makes consumers angrier and more likely to download stuff for free.  It's a really stupid cycle.

They FINALLY got the idea for pay-per song downloads.  This is great, but I think it needs to be more accessible before people will use it.  I refuse to pay for songs on Amazon because the quality is not audiophile grade, and I don't want to deal with DRM (I want to play my music wherever I want on whatever device I want).  DRM is damaging their pay-per-download model more than anything else (DRM is hurting the gaming industry in the same way).

The other problem is that since anyone can pay for a web server and payment processing, the artists can distribute their music on their own, and cut out the record labels entirely (large services like iTunes and Amazon are the only way the record industry is really going to make money for downloads).  It is lose-lose for the Big 4.  Bummer, but technology has evolved.

The movie industry is suffering in the same way.  Both the MPAA and the RIAA try to show you how terrible piracy is by pretending that the people at the bottom are the ones being hurt (while execs, producers, and big name stars make all the money).  They jack up prices to ridiculous levels that nobody can reasonably afford (it should not cost $80 for a family of 4 to go to the movies for a couple of hours) and then say that it's because nobody is buying their stuff anymore.
