Duska,
Thanks for your thoughtful post.  I'm not able to give it the response it deserves, because I'm at work.  But I'll try to do the short version, anyhow.

Not to get uncomfortably personal, and I know it has little to do with the discussion.  But I grew up on the South Shore of Long Island (not the North Shore, which tends to be where the money is at).  My parents divorced at a very early age, so my brother and I were raised by a single mom who always struggled to make ends meet.  She spent much of our younger childhood doing daycare in the home because that way she could earn some money, but still be there to raise us.  My grandparents on both sides grew up in Queens and then moved to Long Island.  My father's parents as original Levittowners.  So in a way, my living in Queens is a return to the land of my forebearers, if you want to be overly dramatic about it. :-)  Went to public school, worked my ass off, was in the top 10 of my high school class, and then went to Fordham.

At Fordham, as well as in high school, I was just about the only conservative I was knew of.  My conservatism probably developed in part as a reaction to my modest upbringing.  I subscribe (at least somewhat) to the argument that great society entitlement programs have made things worse for the working poor.  (For a brilliant account of this argument, see "Losing Ground" by Charles Murray).  I work a modest think tank job, and I don't make the big bucks (yet.  Fingers crossed).

I know there is much to say about your post, and I feel bad for not being able to do give you a proper response right now.  I will say that while the poor are taxed as a higher percentage of their income, the rich actually provide the lions share of tax revenue in this country.  The rich are also, obviously, responsible for more of the employment and economic activity that we all benefit from.  I'm not sure that we'll ever be able to separate government and corruption.

Have you ever read Atlas Shrugged?  This conversation is touching upon many of the themes...
