You don't need a special antenna for digital (DTV) reception, so pretty much any set of rabbit ears would do (since you can't install a rooftop antenna).  Just make sure that you are getting a VHF-UHF antenna since many of the digital signals are in the UHF band, even if the HDTV set and the converter boxes "map" them back to their familiar locations (2, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, etc.).

HOWEVER, keep in mind that most over the air broadcasts are NOT in true HD, since many stations split their bandwidth by broadcasting additional stations on the same frequency.  So if you are looking to buy an antenna with the hope that you will get HD picture quality on all the channels, you will be out of luck.  There is a difference between plain digital and HDTV.  Odds are for many of the stations, your picture quality will not be different from what Dish Network provides (and they do carry almost all of the local stations, including fringe stations that would be difficult to receive, from what I understand, though they don't carry the subchannels you would get over-the-air).

Neo did a pretty good job of explaining things but I'm going to expand on his post a bit.

This link will tell you which channels you might be able to pick up as well as their actual frequency. Right now everything is UHF is find but WABC and a couple of others going to switch to VHF in June.

http://www.antennaweb.org/aw/welcome.aspx

The guide as to what you can pick up may be a bit off due to the fact that there are a lot of buildings in Astoria so you may have multipath issues. On an analog antenna they may look like "ghosting". On digital it may cause you to be unable to lock on to the signal if it is severe enough. For the NYC stations, this is most likely to occur if you don't have a good line of sight with the Empire State Building, which is where most of the NYC stations are broadcasting from. A directional antenna could help you out a bit with this if you have problems, which you might since you're facing another building. In this case, you may be able to pick up a signal on the bounce. Since we're only about 3-4 miles from the transmitter, you probably don't want an amplified antenna.

As far as whether broadcast is HD or not, it mostly depends on whether the original source material is in HD. Some network shows are still not in HD. Also, pretty much all reruns are not unless they originally aired in the last 2 years or so. However, the broadcast stations tend to be less compressed than cable and satellite. This is because they have less motive to cram as many signals into the same bandwidth. They have about 20Mbit/sec to use across all the streams. Right now, most stations have a main channel that gets most of the bandwidth and a subchannel or 2 that gets much less. Out of the big 4 networks, CBS is the only one that broadcasts a single channel from its NYC station. Quality for programs that were filmed in HD is usually pretty good. The cable operators on the other hand, are generally trying to put as many channels into the wire as they can, which means they will compress as much as they can. This makes it more likely you will notice compression artifacts, such as blocking or patches where color is a bit off.
