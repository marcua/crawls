Post by: ssewell on November 11, 2007, 05:57:47 PM
My daughter is in 1st grade as PS 122, she started last year for Kindergarten and was in the Champions program.  I volunteer at the school as a Learning Leader, as that is the only way to participate in the school day outside of the occasional PTA programs.

The school is very rigorous academically, and the teachers are extremely motivated to make sure everyone is up to the state standards.  That means there is a lot of support for students who are struggling as well as for the gifted students in the academy. They offer in-school tuturing (that is where I volunteer), and Saturday school to help those who need extra attention to get up to state standards.  The curriculum is challenging, and the school seems to get quite a bit of teaching support from Teacher's college. There is a debate among the parents about "teaching to the test", but I have to say that my daughter has done very well and loved both her teachers and is reading at the 3rd grade level--and yes, I am bragging.

That being said, there is not a focus on physical activity, and I think it needs to be addressed, esp. as the children are pushed pretty hard and have no place to let off steam.  This is mostly due to logistics, there are 1500 students in the school, with one lunch room and 2 gyms.  The children have one recess for about 20 minutes after lunch.  They have three lunch periods to accomodate all the children. Moving 500 students per lunch period up and down the stairs is a lesson in logistics, and so the response has been to keep them in one area as much as possible. 

When my daughter was in Kindergarten, they took their recess on the small playground.  Now that she is in 1st grade, the children play in the enclosed area in the center of the U shape of the school.  They are constricted in their play to Duck, Duck, Goose most days, which means even if they are getting outside that day, they are spending most of the time sitting on the ground unless they are the occasional Goose.  If the weather is unpleasant--rain, cold or excessive heat--the children spend their recess in the auditorium watching a movie.   I had asked about the gyms and having the students move there instead of the auditorium, but again, moving 500 children up the stairs would take up almost all of their recess time and they would have very little time to play.

Each class has gym once a week, which means that the children do not get any real physical activity in 6 hours.
They can't make the day any longer in order to comply with the union rules, and I don't want my daughter to miss out on Spanish, computer, art and music, which are also offered once per week to make more time for gym.

Like you, I enrolled my daughter in Champions hoping that she would at least have a chance to run around, but last year the Kindergarten program spent most of the year sitting at tables doing crafts, rather than playing in the gym during their scheduled gym time.  The reason given was that they didn't have enough staff to watch the children in the gym.  I did not enroll my daughter in Champions this year, as I work part-time and could pick her up, as it seemed like the main goal was crowd control for the younger grades, as opposed to teaching any of the sports and arts mentioned in the material.  I went to all the parent events and picked up my daughter at different times to observe the program, and I do think it is a good choice for the older children, like 2nd grade on up. And, of course, many parents do not have a choice and an afterschool program is a real benefit.

I was hoping to bring up the lack of physical activity at the next PTA meeting, but I didn't want to just focus on a problem without offering a solution--any ideas? 

RE: communicating with the school--I always get a response if I talk to Judy Thomas or Ms. Aprea.  They can be hard to reach, because they spend most of their time in the halls of the school (more so Ms. Aprea), but if I leave a message for either of them, they always call me back.

Hope this helps!

Champions is not funded by the DOE, it is part of the non-profit group the Sports and Arts Foundation.  Their funding comes directly from grants, and I thought they had been approved for a multi-year grant to fund all the PS 122 students for two years for a year-round program, covering school holidays as well as after school.  I haven't heard anything about this grant, did it not come through?  I think most parents would be willing to pay more, if it meant additional trained staff for homework help and active play for all students.

I agree that Champions fills an immediate need for after school programs and has some very good points, esp. for the older kids, like tennis and the shows.  But the dance show you mentioned does not include any of the smaller students, neither does the end of the year musical.  The Kindergarten and first grade children do not get tennis instruction.  Their only physical activity is the active play period in the gym or outside on the playground, so if they don't get that, they are adding 3 more hours of sitting down.  The truth is that 300 of the 1500 PS 122 students are in Kindergarten and 1st grade, that is not reflected in the Champions programming.

I know that the staff works very hard, but one of the main problems I see is there is not active oversight above the onsite program director, Mrs. Vasquez, who created the program (and employs her son as one of the physical education directors) so I would guess feels a huge sense of ownership, justifiably--to create a program from nothing and have to go begging each year requires a considerable amount of dedication.  I don't think any parent wants to take away from the program, they only want a way to be heard.  Champions does not answer to the principal or any other supervisor at PS 122, they answer to their CEO offsite. There is not a system to document and evaluate the program from the parents' perspective and therefore have measurable outcomes, and frankly, I have seen a real resistance to putting a system like that in place.  The Champions response to any gentle criticism is always that Champions is better than nothing, which only plays on parents' fears. You don't have to throw the baby out with the bathwater. 

The truth is there are other organizations that offer free on-site afterschool programs, like the YMCAs, that are also funded by grants, so the choice is not Champions or nothing.  I think it's either PS 84 or 85 that offers a virtual Y afterschool program.  But I don't want to get rid of Champions, I just think that if Champions had a system in place to address parents' concerns or some kind of parent representation similar to the role the PTA plays in the school, that would only help Champions by having dedicated parents for fundraising and other needed roles.  And I my suggestion as a parent is that Champions has got to broaden their focus to the younger grades, esp. in homework help and physical activity.

I will be at the PTA meeting on Thursday, so I'll defintely add my voice to the discussion of physical activity for all the students.

Hi,

I don't know much about the academy, but I understand it is a rigorous program and prepares kids for the top city high schools like hunter and bronx science, etc. The academy is something that is applied to, so the students don't necessarily come from the nearby neighborhood. I haven't met any academy parents that I know of.

I have dealt mainly with the parent coordinator at the school, who is friendly and very helpful.

That said if you don't meet her, it is not a particularly welcoming place. No warm fuzzies. If you don't get involved through field trips, pta or other volunteer work, you will see your kid's teacher probably four times in the year--two at parent teacher conferences, once on curriculum day and once at promotion ceremony. If you put in the effort, you can get inside and your experience will depend on the teachers your kids get, as is true at ANY school.

I have heard some comments from other parents that the school does seem a little more welcoming thus far this year. Principal Mary Kojes retired at the end of last year (somewhat suddenly it seemed). The city hired one of the assistant principals to be principal (over what were probably a lot of very good candidates, given the school's rep). Among the parents I know, she is well respected.

So that might be part of the change in atmosphere.
