I didn't want to chime in, but I feel I have to.

A professor of mine - a smart guy and a great mentor - begged me not to go to law school.  I was like "how else am I suppose to market myself and stand out from the crowd?".  Law School was a means to an end.  I looked at it as a ticket to a better career, and by default, a better lifestyle.  Law schools are always touting how much the average salary thier student make the first year out of school.  Let me tell you what actually happened.

Law school was extraordinarily difficult.  Life was spent studying, studying, and more studying.  This was not college anymore where I was the lone smart kid in the class.  Everyone at law school is the same smart kid in class.  I couldn't just read a few pages, cram the night before and still do well.  I had to prepare by reading every night till my eyes bled.  Challenging...Yes.  Fun...absolutely not. 

Not to mention your competing against your friends for coveted spots, like law review, student aid positions and summer internships.  You start to learn quickly how real world "politics" actually work.

So then you graduate, and it's off to take the bar.  Again, a summer completely gone from your life (assuming that you pass it the first time).  And right after, it's off to find a job.

Only here's the part the law school brochures don't tell you...top firms only look for three things:
1) Where you went to law school
2) What rank you graduated in
3) Whether you were on law review

Most of the top firms won't even LOOK at your resume unless you come from a top school, graduate in the top 10% of your class, and you were on law review.  If you don't have that on your resume, they won't even consider you.

So if you don't have this criteria, and you don't have a good hookup with a law firm right after graduation (which is why they say your 2L summer internship is so critical), you're now back to competing against all those other people who just graduated all the other law schools in your same year.  And there are a ton of them...all wanting the same jobs your looking for.

Now, if you become part of this group of lost souls, you then realize that you're not going to be making the big bucks, and firms are NOT knocking down your door.  And the salaries being offered at this point for the jobs that are left for recent grads are a lot closer to what many of your friends (who, incidently, worked the last three years and didn't go to grad school) are making.  Remember in the first paragraph when I refered to those salary numbers that the schools often mention?  Well, you then learn after you graduate that those numbers only reflect the salaries of those grads who voluntarily provide thier salary information.  And guess who those people are?  Yeah, those are the one's at the top of the class who made law review (or who already had a job through a family member upon graduation).  If the entire class were to put up thier first year salary numbers, the true number would be far lower.

Oh, and as was already mentioned, you'll probably have a boatload of debt that'll start to come due right out of law school.  And with the strict standards on loans nowadays, banks are not so willing to consolidate those loans into one low interest loan.  So your loan payments are going to be large...very large.

And then there's the issue of job satisfaction.  I've only met one lawyer...one...in my entire post-graduate career who was truly happy as a lawyer.  As soon as I got a gig on wall street, you'd be surprised how many of my friends begged me to send in thier resume to my firms. 

-----------------

I mention all of this, because as was mentioned earlier, it feels as though there's an ethical obligation to spare others of what the "reality" of getting a law degree is.  But hey, if you can get into Harvard or Columbia law, then by all means go for it.  And even if you don't do that, maybe you'll graduate at the top of St.John's or Cardoza law, in which case, you're still ok.  And even if that's not the case either, maybe you have no wife or kids and are sitting on a nice trust fund.  So you'll get your degree without any financial incumberance.

But if you don't fall into any of these scenarios, as I did, be VERY wary.  The road after law school will be much, much, MUCH harder than you think. 

My 2-cents...for what it's worth.

NYCGoalie, I agree with pretty much everything you said. I graduated from Cardozo Law (it's Cardozo, not Cardoza)... a fairly young school (by law school standards) usually ranked around 50 nationally, but they just threw a boatload of money into improving the place, so I'd expect it to continue rising in the rankings over the next few years. That said, it took many of my classmates ages to get jobs after school (and that was before the dotcom bust that had record numbers applying to law school -- I imagine it's worse now). A lot of my classmates ended up doing temp contract work (and nearly a decade out, some of them still are). Unless you are independently wealthy and will get out of law school without student loan debt, or your committment to practicing law is so great, then I would seriously consider how much debt you are willing to take on for this education -- odds are it won't pay off for decades.

And for what it's worth, I LOVE being a lawyer... it was worth every bit of the $120K of debt I'm in (providing I win the lottery and can someday actually pay it off).
