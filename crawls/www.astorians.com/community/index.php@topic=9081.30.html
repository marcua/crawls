They tell us that the typical graffiti artist is a middle class kid. Many are above average in education and intelligence. Who cares? All we know is that graffiti makes a community look like its going to the dogs. If you see it on a store, you think its dirty inside with out of date food. If you see it on a building, the word S–L–U–M comes to mind.

Why is Astoria looking worse every year, and other parts of the city (that formerly looked bad) are now getting better? We leave this to you, dear reader, to speculate.

But perhaps part of the problem is that we are not taking the steps needed to really clean it up.

For example:
Quote He suggests the city crack down on property-owners to clean up the graffiti left on their buildings by vandals as a way to discourage the problem. Don't you think this perspective is skewed just a bit? I'd prefer the city crack down on the vandals instead. Unquote

Why? What logic backs this up? Why, if we have not been successful, we stubbornly stay with this solution?

First, this is an expensive and possibly unconstitutional solution. You need cameras. Yes, lots of cameras, a lot more cameras than they currently have (has anyone noticed or even commented on them along Broadway etc?). Perhaps even the level of cameras like they have in Britain. Are we willing to accept this staggering cost (paid out of tax dollars)? Are we willing to accept this level of police monitoring of our lives?

Secondly, the police are not very responsive. How many times have we seen them cruise by a car whose radio is blaring (violating some noise code), or look the other way as someone is acting oddly on the sidewalk? Besides, even if they respond, the kids are long gone. Yes I know that the 114th has the highest number of collars. It also has the highest growth of graffiti.

So what it the solution? Do everything possible to clean it immediately! This we found from experience.  When a building is tagged, go out and clean it that day. If they do it again, clean it again. They do it again, clean it again.

Guess what? They stop and don’t come back.  Kids do not want to waste their time if their work is erased before their buddies can see it the next day after school.

Why is this plague is spreading in Astoria? Perhaps we have too many absentee landlords or property owners with no vested interest in how the community looks. In the not too distant past, the owner, more often than not, almost always lived in the building. People talk about how they used to scrub the stoops in the morning, for heavens sake! Tags were gone within a few hours.

Now the landlord often lives elsewhere and only shows up to milk the cow.

Now it is getting worse. This recent speculative building frenzy that has gripped this community has caused the problem to jump to a new level. Buildings are covered with this garbage even before people move in:

http://queenscrap.blogspot.com/2007/11/astoria-eyesore.html

I understand the building/real estate industry has a big influence in this city. But a growing number of people that we talk to agree that the property owners must step up to the plate, either voluntarily, or by legislation. The building industry, on so many levels, just has a feeling that they are above the law, perhaps from the sense that generous campaign donations skews the system in their favor.

Rents are skyrocketing. Landlords are making out like bandits. The least our elected officials can do is force them to keep our community clean.

Building owners should be compelled to promptly clean their buildings of graffiti.

Just an opinion. Looking foward to hearing other perspectives and particularly, stories on how people discouraged tags.

It seems we are in agreement that property owners should clean their buildings and that action works best to combat the graffiti problem. 

I didn’t mean police intervention should be the first or only response or that it would solve the graffiti problem -- but am I to believe that law enforcement is to play no part?

Using a similar analogy, police should not arrest drunk drivers and instead focus their efforts on a crack-down on bar owners who provide people the space to drink. (I would argue both may be necessary.)

My point is that the blame for grafitti should be placed squarely on people who tag.

My family used to own a property that was frequently hit. We did our best to promptly remove grafitti but I have to tell you it wasn’t easy and the graffiti wasn’t always removed within a day or two. We did feel a civic duty to be part of the solution but I refuse to accept that we were the cause of the problem.

What about elderly owners/residents -- who may not the physical ability or the financial means to keep up? Should we as a community heap scorn on them? Should the city fine them or should we all as a community help them?

Not every owner is a rich, absentee landlord intent on neglecting their property.

Astoria and LIC had problems before the current building boom – including to a certain extent, grafitti – and steps were taken in cooperation between private citizens, building owners, community groups, police, police task forces, and politicians and the problem was managed – as it only can be.

Looks like the community, working together, needs to step up their efforts again.

I think the problem is that it can't be just a few or even just most property owners that clean it up immediately. That because tagging is a way to gain notoriety, the only way to eliminate the allure of it is to remove the fame that comes from putting a tag up. To remove the name recognition, you have to remove the tag quickly. If a neighborhood has a reputation of not allowing tags to remain for long, the tags will, eventually, stop coming.

Maybe the solution is to have government assistance in cleaning up, provide alternate space for children to make constructive public works (like under the bridge on 23rd Ave and... 35th St? Not sure the exact cross street), and to have police attempt to catch those tagging. I think the issue is that addressing one part of the problem won't solve it, we have to do all three to be able to actually stop the tagging from happening.

OK, go after the kids. Get the cops on the case. But leave no stone unturned.

First of all, talk to Woodside on the Move, 114th Civ-Op, and SUNN, all successful groups. How have they removed graffiti? They go out repeatedly and remove it ASAP.

Their communities are looking better. Indeed, walk around NY - yes graffiti is common, but in community after community you do not see the same level as 20 or 30 years ago. There is some factor, though, about Astoria that has caused this to explode in our area.

Astoria is increasingly viewed as not a place to put down permanent roots, but a stepping stone to something else, as a business deal to be viewed with flinty eye. You recently have had a big jump in speculative property ownership and a mirror decline in owner occupied  housing.

Graffiti is just part of the overall deterioration we see in those buildings as a consequence of this change. Paved over front years, a lack of former cleaning and maintenance, scruffy lawns, piles of garbage where none existed etc etc etc.

And the new speculative buildings just seem to be magnets. Just today I saw where a lovely old building was destroyed for Crap on 20th Road and Steinway Street - with a big ugly scrawl on its side.  Lets see how long that stays before its removed.

As to seniors, they still have an obligation to clean their sidewalks of snow and litter. This should be extended to graffiti.

We call upon city council to enact legislation (with teeth that is enforced) to compel property owners to keep their property clean and neat and attractive enough that one would (gasp) live in it yourself. Perhaps city council should look into a body of legislation to encourage owner occupied housing (and also discourage real estate speculation).

Graffiti on property drives down property values. It helps create a sense that this is a community plagued with social ills that only can be remedied by a generous infusion of taxes. These are the hidden costs imposed on you and me by these absentee landlords.

The speculative real estate types may make money, but it should not come from our pockets.
