Title: Re: Driving in the Bay Area
Post by: kerrigankrewe on August 06, 2008, 10:23:51 AM
SF and Sonoma are very nice to drive in. I'd say the biggest difference is the steepness of some roads. Driving in the valleys north of SF are fun and fantastic. We drive out from the city to the beach each time and it's the simplest drive. SF is mostly grid and is very small. Driving gets easier and less frustrating the farther away you are from the center of it all - Market & Mission.

We always take the metro to the city from SFO and then rent a car from there. Hertz is widely available there and some cars have GPS. I'm a GPS addict so I always use it to get around.

Parking is difficult as there is minimal street parking. Most everything is garaged. It helps if you get a hotel with a small parking lot. Be prepared to pay about $25 a day.

Believe it or not, I'd say having a car is a necessity for getting around and living in SF. The metro doesn't cater to every neighborhood and walking is so not an option unless you carry Olympian calf muscles - one hill will take you out.

I lived in the Bay Area for 9 years and drove the whole time there.  It was pretty easy (driving stick shift makes it harder).

I agree that if you've driving in NYC, driving in Berkeley/Oakland is a piece of cake.  Even driving in SF is ok, except downtown near the financial district.  You have to deal with crazy drivers like you do in NYC, coupled with the hills.  Not pleasant. 

White liners, yes - motorcycles often drive in between the lanes.  I never had a problem with them. 

One thing CA does well is the length of off-ramps and on-ramps.  They are usually nice and long and give you plenty of time to speed up to traffic, or slow down to make merging into town traffic easier.  Enjoy.  I swear, here in NY the traffic engineers must have gotten their degrees at K-Mart.  On-rams and off-ramps SUCK here, and are dangerous.  Yuck.

I think you have nothing to worry about, actually.  Be sure to check out the parking meters in Berkeley - Columbus Day there (which is exempt from feeding the meter) is called Indigenous People's Day. 

Sidebar:  I find it interesting in CA that in southern California you say "the 101" and in northern CA you say just "101".

As an aside, the reason is that the on-ramps and off-ramps suck around here is that the roads were built mostly in the 1930s (thank you Robert Moses), when speeds were much slower -- more like 35 mph.  Many of the parkways were designed as leisure roads -- e.g. for Sunday drives at slow speeds -- rather than for today's commuters.  It wasn't poor planning, but rather planning for a style of driving that was extinct by the mid-20th century.  When they are rebuilt, you will notice that they are often straighter and longer than they are out West, but still not ideal.  That's due to space constraints.

Title: Re: Driving in the Bay Area
Post by: Astoria Luv on August 18, 2008, 01:53:44 PM
So, I'm back from the Bay Area and I have to say that it was one of the best vacations/trips I have ever had.  Despite my own crazy anxiety about driving, in particular driving in an area that I was unfamiliar with, I didn't get into any accidents or get a moving violation.  It wasn't easy, though.  I got lost a couple of times, well a lot, and at one point, I had to back-up into oncoming traffic on the Bay Bridge because I had missed my exit and was about to drive into San Francisco.  Totally unrelated to actual driving in the area, I came out of my hotel to leave for Oakland Airport and could not start the car.  The battery died.  I called Hertz and they got a third party vendor to give me a boost within 30 minutes.  I was then off to the airport and, of course, I got lost again.  Fortunately, I left about three hours early based on my NYC experience with JetBlue (see below) and I got to the airport way ahead of time.

I absolutely love San Francisco.  I spent the entire day on Friday just walking, taking trolleys (for $11 you can ride any trolley or San Francisco bus for the day) and riding the BART.  It was about 70 degrees in the city with a light breeze and plenty of sunshine.  I was wearing a t-shirt, but I had brought a jacket because of what people told us about the weather in San Fran.

Oh, and as an added bonus, I didn't even need to drive the before-mentioned car, except to get to the rehearsal dinner and the wedding.  I was staying in Emeryville, a small town across the bay from San Francisco and bordering Berkeley.  The town of Emeryville has a lot of big box stores that people from outside use.  There was a bus stop right outside of our hotel.  It was free and it was quick 15 minute drive or less to the MacArthur BART station in Oakland.  Unfortunately, I couldn't figure out the ticket machine at the station and ended up wasting a couple of dollars. 

Thanks again for all of your suggestions. 

PS: A word of advice to anyone who is flying JetBlue out of JFK: Maybe it was just a particularly busy day for them, but it took almost 2 hours to get to the gate!   The sheer number of passengers, along with the layout of the terminal building led to confusion and really long lines.  There were several employees of JetBlue who were "yelling" directions like prison guards. 

Having said all of that, I would recommend flying JetBlue.  They have a in-flight DirectTV and XM satellite radio installed. You will have to bring your own headset or buy one for $1.00.  This entertainment was really great, considering the baby who was screaming in front of me on my flight from Oakland.
