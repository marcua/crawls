Seeing as it is my vacation week, I am of course obsessing about work.  Cannot.  Pry.   Brain.  Away.

I have a question: When it comes to teaching at high school and at college levels, there is this hot issue of Retention, i.e., "How To Make Them Graduate, Even When Dropping Out Is So Much Easier."  I believe that there are different levels of expectation for where our responsibility, as teachers, is in preventing dropping out.  It seems that high school teachers have to take students more in hand and maybe almost shove them through the work, even monitoring in some cases that students are doing their basic responsibilities to learn enough to pass, or to learn that study skills DOES make a different.  In college, I think a teacher's job is less to MAKE students pass, and more to hold out as an offer what they need to do, with suggestions as to how they do it, in order to learn what is needed for any given course.  I think this, but I probably have a professor's bias.  I'd like to hear more between these 2 groups about this question, and I have noticed that there are representatives from both kinds of teachers in this thread.  This could get contentious, but that is not always a bad thing.  I wanna hear differing opinions.

So...where is the line between doing too much to Retain a student, and doing not enough?  I have been struggling with the question of how aggressive I ought to be in pushing students to get enough done to pass a course, rather than dropping it or failing.  I can identify students in trouble easily, but how much do I push/remind/email these students to do what they need?  If a student never takes any notes in lecture, then struggles to get through tests, do I point out to him that he might try writing down what I say in lecture?  If a student's priorities are clearly ranked such that schoolwork falls so low that she is unlikely to get by, do I tell her that?  Do I try to scare a student with failing if he does not reorganize priorities?  Or do I merely let her know, without the scare-tactics, that she is not going to make it if she skips another lecture, or fails another quiz, and then let the proof of that big fat F shock her into deciding to re-organize priorities?  Maybe this is an issue best settled by an individual teacher's personality and pedagogical outlook, but....well, I'm looking for more viewpoints.

So, thoughts?  Or stories to illustrate successes/failures in this regard?
