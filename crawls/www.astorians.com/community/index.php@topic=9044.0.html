This post points out one of the biggest problems with all of the development in our community.  I may be inclined to admit this house is out of context with its neighbors, but how it got that way is the real issue.  The DOB is understaffed and incompetent to enforce the law of this city against illegal development.  The wink and the nod going on between the administration and developers has to be called out.  Our elected representatives have to do a better job on representing those who actually live here!

If someone wants to build, they should follow the letter of the law.  PERIOD.  Apply for the permits, go through the process. Actually allow the community to have a say on the character of its surroundings.  Our DOB should be funded adequately to deal with all of the development going on.  Right now, too many calls go unanswered and the fines are not levied with enough force.  Where are our electeds when illegal development go up?  Are their cellphones broken?  Do they lack the ability to call DOB or organize the community when they spot a violation?  Are they even aware when something goes up that doesn't look right?

We need to hold our electeds and the community board accountable to the promises they make about protecting the integrity of the law and the character of our community.  I urge anyone who is part of this board and angry about the lack of a real plan for development to come to the LICA meeting on January 8 at the Quinn Building, join a political club or organization or show up/help plan the next community forum.  Only an organized voice can help build a sustainable plan that will protect the integrity of our neighborhood and have our infrastructure keep up with the development.

I’m bracing myself for the negative nods but hear me out:

Pictured on its own is what appears to be a quaint, lovely, wood-frame, one story, one-family house that inspires “aw shucks” gushes of nostalgia but beyond that can someone point out anything of significant architectural value that merits this house being preserved for posterity?

Look to both sides, there appears to be 1- to 3-family homes constructed of brick and if you click on the link on the Queenscrap site that shows what’s being built on the opposite side of the street, there again appears to be mainly brick 1- to 3-family houses to the side. One could make the argument that it is this little yellow house that is out of scale with the rest of the block.

The problem for me is not that it will be torn down, but what replaces will essentially have no design and will most likely be shoddily constructed using cheap materials – as evidenced in what’s being built across the street.

I’m always sad to see these types of houses go but they are often falling apart on the inside and need someone with deep pockets and a whole lotta love to restore and maintain.

What I’m arguing is that here we have what appears to be a site suitable for development, a larger house perhaps, which would not be out of scale with its neighbors. If only a developer had a sense of vision, taste, and pride to build something that would be considered a great addition to our area. That type of developer is non-existent, which means that unfortunately, this house will be torn down and Astoria will be poorer for it.

Is it really worth the effort to focus on a house such as this when in my opinion, the real threats to the character of the neighborhood are out-of-scale development, the mutilation, super-sizing and destruction of row houses, for example Norwood Gardens, and the demolition of the historic houses in Old Astoria and elsewhere.

I believe most of the small-ish buildings are investment properties where the apartments are offered as rentals only a small portion are actually sold off as individual condo units.

If only more were sold -- at least we'd get longer-term residents moving in. It seems to me as rentals all landlords care about now is extracting the most income from properties, which means high rents and a more transient population.

A comparsion if I may...

On 47 street at the corner of 31st avenue, there used to be a parking lot for the now defunct Boulevard Hospital. When the hospital was closed the lot was sold off and a short row of two- or three-family houses were built (sometime in the mid 80s). Architecturally they are typical brick row houses -- nothing special and as I remember them, I thought they actually looked drab and ugly.

But they were sold to people/familes who bought them to live in -- not as investments -- and it shows. They have since much improved aesthetically mainly because the owners took pride in their homes, upgraded them and compared to what is being built today these houses can now be considered beauties. Having people buy and move in made all the difference.

These days nothing seems to be built for anyone to buy and live in. It's all rentals and I wonder how this will affect the neighborhood.
