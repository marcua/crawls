I find this article to be particularly specious in its argument about the acceptability of breastfeeding throughout history. You cannot compare art to real life. For one thing, people have the choice of when and whether to consume such art. Just because artists painted women breastfeeding does not automatically mean it fit with the zeitgeist of the times. Second, the time period that they cite as supportive of breastfeeding (1200s - 1800s) include times in history when society was anti-breastfeeding. During that period, it was at times considered unhealthy (either for the mother or the child), inappropriate, or inconvenient to breastfeed. The inconvenience is, in fact, one reason why wet nurses became so popular during that time period - so women of means would not have their social lives hampered. Then came the Victorian era right afterwards when it was absolutely unacceptable to breastfeed in public because breast milk was seen as unclean and it was unacceptable to expose one's breasts in public. I just have a huge problem pointing to art in an era to support your argument when the actual practice of society at points during that time was so unforgiving of the same practice. It undermines the validity of the whole argument.
