Don't do what I did the first time a made a turkey! Don't forget to remove the gizzards and neck from the inside of the turkey, where they're often stored in plastic bags.  :ugly:

Oh, and don't do what I did the second time I made a turkey either! If you're making gravy, and it's not thickening, when you add a bit of cornstarch, make sure it's cornstarch! If it's accidentally baking soda from a similar yellow box, you'll get yourself a baking soda + acidic gravy volcano ala 3rd grade science! :ugly:

You might also check out what Meg H. says in her roundup of turkey advice. I'm particularly intrigued by Cook's Illustrated's suggestion of icing the breast of the turkey before baking to keep it cooler and thus less likely to dry out during roasting...

Good luck!  My biggest piece of advice would be don't overcook it.  It's a delicate balance, but it's really done before you think it is.  Every year, my parents take a huge (20 lb +) frozen turkey (generic brand), thaw it for three days, remove the gizzards, etc., stuff it, and stick it in the oven on a rack.  They never brine it, ice it, chill it, cover it, put it breast-side down or do any of the 1001 tricks published every year that are supposed to make your turkey taste better.  It is lovingly and often basted - that's about it.  We use the leg test to determine when it's done -- when the leg moves freely in the socket when you grab it and wiggle it around, it's done.  Every year, it's perfect.  Easily as good as any free-range, fresh turkey I have ever had (which ordinarily would be my preference).  The breast meat is moist, tender and has flavour, and the dark meat is as well.  The skin is golden and crispy for those who like it.

This is personal preference, but I much prefer flour to cornstarch as a gravy thickener.  You need to make a slurry first (that is, mix flour and water together in a separate bowl) and then pour it into the liquid to be thickened.  The best kind of gravy is mushroom giblet gravy (of course, once again, personal preference).  Ok, I've never made it all by myself from start to finish (may well do, under supervision, this year), but the basic idea is this:  cook all the giblets and the neck from inside the turkey in a pot of water with onion and garlic.  Remove the giblits, cut them into pieces, and reserve the resulting broth.  In another pan, saute sliced mushrooms until cooked through.  After the turkey has come out of the oven and has been set aside to rest, put the roasting pan on the stovetop with all the drippings and turn on enough burners to heat the roasting pan.  Add the broth, and scrape all the bits up well.  Add the giblets and mushrooms, thicken with flour, and add salt/pepper to taste.  This makes a vatload of gravy, literally quarts, which I find to be a very good thing.   8-)

For the crock pot hopefuls I hope the crock pot is ENORMOUS, once you get a turkey under 20 lbs. you have more of a chance for there to be less Breast meats, which usually is the meat everyone enjoys. 

As for the brining, I would never brine a "fresh killed" turkey or even a fresh (flash frozen) turkey either. I agree with the simple approach. Place your turkey on a nice sturdy pan that is deep enough to catch the drippings.  Be sure you watch your turkey throughout the cooking process, basting it with premade turkey stock (turkey parts, soup greens) or warm water (never cold).  If you're looking to thicken your gravy I also use flour/warm water mixture prepared on the side then SLOWLY ADDED IN, this aids you to not have flour clumps in the gravy.

Finally test your turkey with the leg. If it wiggles and falls off, your turkey is done!!!
