COBRA insurance
« Reply #1 on: September 20, 2005, 12:35:12 PM »
COBRA is a continuation of your insurance provided by an employer after you're let go. AFAIK, it's not something anyone can just sign up for.

The plus side was that it continued my coverage when I was out of work. The down side was that it was exhorbitantly expense (no surprise) so that I'm in good health, so I eventually dropped it. I simply didn't have that kind of money and couldn't find good catastrophic/hospitalization coverage.

In my particular case what made matter worse was that my original plan, Oxford, was administered by Aetna under COBRA.

I had a doctor's appointment (scheduled before I was let go) that I kept. I thought it would have been covered under COBRA. It should have. But the right hand didn't know what the left hand was doing, ie Oxford and Aetna -- AND I had the misfortune of going to a local doctor in Astoria.* The short of it was that I paid the full whatever for the doctor's visit, and never got a dime reimbursed. Insurance co. says take it up with Doc. Doc says take it up with Insurance. Frikken runaround.

Unless you have a lot of money, avoid COBRA. If you're self-employed, look into Healthy NY or whatever may be offered through the Freelancers Union.

« Reply #9 on: September 21, 2005, 01:14:31 PM »
When I did COBRA, my monthly was $365, but 280's also a big chunk o' change.

If you go the no-insurance route, there are several free or sliding scale clinics in Manhattan that are decent.  A friend of mine has been without insurance for 3 years, and that's where she goes.  I can get info from her if you want.  For dental, you can go to the NYU dental clinic.  It's either free or very low cost, and I know several people who have used them.

I'd say there are two reasons to maintain your insurance in the short term:  1.  If you have a pre-existing condition, whether or not it's currently being treated.  A number of health plans exclude pre-existing conditions if you have a gap in your health coverage.  2.  If you are currently being treated for a condition that needs regular prescriptions or doctor visits.
