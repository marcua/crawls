Snow (sort of) this morning!

I stepped out of my apartment building this morning to a most unexpected sight - there was actual, literal snow falling!  I stood in the doorway for a minute or so with my mouth hanging open because I simply could not believe it.  I'm from Southern California - no snow there unless you go the mountains.  I've never actually seen it fall.  It was beautiful.  I was very happy I had my new earmuffs on because it was freezing cold, however I frolicked down 35th Street to the V train like a little kid.  I suppose snow would be a strong word ... it was more a flurry ... well, I guess it was closer to what a flurry would do if it were feeling particularly unambitious.  Sadly, it had stopped by the time I got to the train.  Regardless, I was ecstatic and cannot wait till it snows for real. 

Did anybody else see it this morning?

Quote from: enigmacat on January 10, 2007, 02:44:08 PM
I think there is a temperature range for snow. It has to be warm enough. And the cloud cover also makes it a bit warmer.

I think this is right... When they had those huge blizzards in Denver over the last couple of weeks, someone on the radio said that part of the reason is that it's because it's warmer than usual. It can snow when it's very cold, but it tends to snow a lot only when it's not so cold.

The cloud cover is a good point too. Clouds keep the ground heat from radiating off into space. The coldest nights are clear skies. So if it's the middle of winter, and it has been bitter cold, you might get a warm front that would result in steady snow, warmer temperatures, and cloudy skies...

On the other hand, snow on the ground reflects a lot more sunlight/heat than does bare ground, so it'll be a little colder after the snow than it would otherwise be. That's why everyone's so worried about the ice melting in the arctic. Not only is it a symptom of global warming, it's a cause in a big feedback loop. Open water absorbs most of the sunlight/heat, increasing water temperatures even more, while ice reflects most of it back out into space.

(I didn't take the hard physics and chem classes in college... But I did take Weather and Climate with all the poets and historians!)

Congratulations on the snow viewing, JD! And welcome to the North! :) I'll be interested in your reactions the first time there's some accumulation. I once knew a guy from Miami who always felt like slow on the ground looked like sand, even though he knew it wasn't...
