The split makes sense to me.  Think about it - posters on this board go to 36 Ave, Broadway, 30 Ave, Astoria Blvd and Ditmars to catch the N/W, but anyone who takes the R is either at the Steinway or 46th St stations.  Less to choose from, and if you've seen how packed Broadway and 30th can get in the morning, well - I'll buy that more people who consider themselves "Astorians" are riding the N/W train.

I've done both and lived halfway (really!) between the two lines.  I can still take the R in a pinch, and sometimes I'll hop on the R, especially if I want to stop by Bravo or the Broadway library.  If the weather is nice I might even take it home just to enjoy the walk, but if I'm leaving from home I take the N/W.

I do like the flexibility.  With the way the MTA has been the past few years, it can't hurt to have backup options.

I'm three blocks from the N/W.   Yeah, as a kid I lived for about 4 years under the rusty glare of the J train at Cleveland Street, where you could just look up from the second floor true railroad apt., and see the wheels and the sparks fly far closer to the Brooklyn digs than they do here.  As a 10-year old kid I don't ever recall being roused from sleep by the train;  sure, it was always there but you sort of put it out of your mind.  The other day while walking below the south-bound N/W track I was speaking to someone.  I could no longer hear what the person was saying when it was directly overhead.

I don't know what it is about elevated trains that make people abhor them so much.  It may be the rustyness of the metal, which actually would mingle with the rainfall and stain cars parked underneath the Brooklyn J.  Here in NYC there's no great mystique like with the Chicago el.

I like the elevated trains, but I wouldn't want to live right next to them or park a car under them, and I don't like walking under them either. But I like them because there's stuff to look at out the window, and in most weather conditions it's much nicer to wait for the train outside than in the underground stations. Only time it sucks is when it's pouring rain, when it's really windy, and when snow is piling up on the platform. Unfortunately in NYC the elevated lines seem to be associated with poor neighborhoods, which might be why they carry a stigma.
