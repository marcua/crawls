Why settle with him?  Warren Buffet ftw!
http://www.investopedia.com/articles/01/071801.asp
It's a simple theory: buy when the company is undervalued; sell when it reaches or over-reaches its value.  Of course in practice actually determining a company's value is tricky, and you have to rely on the market to raise demand for the stock by identifying that value gap after you have.  But nothing good comes easy.

Some more nuggest of advice:
Do your homework and due diligence.
Don't listen to Jim Cramer.
Leave emotions out of your decisions.
For a novice investor, don't let any position slide more than 10% (some texts say as little as 7%) before getting out.
If something goes wrong, understand why it went wrong.
If you don't think you can beat the market, take all your money and put it in an ETF (i.e. SPY - S&P 500) you'll save yourself the headache of watching stocks, doing research, and paying for trades. 
Find yourself some solid gold insider information and abuse the heck out of it!  The SEC isn't out to get you.

UPDATE 3-U.S. charges 13 in insider trading, bribery cases

By Paritosh Bansal

NEW YORK, March 1 (Reuters) - The U.S. government on Thursday charged 13 people, including employees at major Wall Street banks, with securities fraud, wire fraud, bribery and other charges in what authorities are calling one of the most pervasive insider trading rings in years.

Michael Garcia, U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of New York, said the insider trading schemes involved a UBS Securities () executive who is accused of selling information about upcoming analyst upgrades and downgrades, and a former Morgan Stanley () attorney accused of giving out information about mergers and acquisitions.

Prosecutors said that two brokers at Assent LLC found out about the scheme involving the UBS executive and blackmailed some of the people involved.

In addition, prosecutors charged that a Banc of America Securities () representative allocated shares of initial public offerings and secondary offerings to a hedge fund, Q Capital, for cash kickbacks.

Prosecutors said that all 13 people had been arrested and four had pleaded guilty.

Also on Thursday, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission charged 11 people and three companies in a civil suit related to the insider trading schemes.

It was "one of the most pervasive Wall Street insider trading rings since the days of Ivan Boesky and Dennis Levine," said Linda Thomsen, director of enforcement with the SEC, at a joint news conference with the U.S. Attorney and the FBI on Thursday. She was referring to major insider trading scandals of the 1980s.

The SEC said those involved in the schemes used clandestine meetings, disposable cell phones, secret codes and cash kickbacks to conceal their conduct, making profits of up to $15 million over five years.

OPEN BUFFETT
By JANET WHITMAN


March 2, 2007 -- Attention young investment wizards: Warren Buffett wants you.
The Oracle of Omaha said he's looking to hire candidates who could inherit his job as chief investment officer at his multi-billion dollar holding company, Berkshire Hathaway.

Buffett, 76, said his octogenarian right-hand man Charlie Munger and Lou Simpson, who's 70 and runs the investment portfolio of Berkshire's Geico insurance company, are both too old for the post.

Simpson "is only six years younger than I," Buffett wrote in his annual letter to investors yesterday. "If I were to die soon, he would fill in magnificently for a short period. For the long term, though, we need a different answer."

Buffett said he intends to hire a younger man or woman who could manage a very large portfolio at Berkshire and succeed him "when the need for someone to do that arises."

The Omaha, Neb., company, whose stock investments include large stakes Coca-Cola and the Washington Post, may end up taking on several candidates, he added.

"Picking the right person(s) will not be an easy task," Buffett wrote.

"Over time, markets will do extraordinary, even bizarre, things. A single, big mistake could wipe out a long string of successes," he said. "We therefore need someone genetically programmed to recognize and avoid serious risks, including those never before encountered."

Buffett acknowledged Berkshire also faces a challenge in finding a new hire that won't end up leaving to take home a much bigger paycheck elsewhere.

With Buffett more than 10 years past retirement age, investors have long fretted about who might be in line to take over as Berkshire's investment pro.

He told investors last year that the company has three strong candidates to replace him as CEO to oversee the company's operating businesses, which include companies like Geico, Dairy Queen and underwear-maker Fruit of the Loom.

"The board knows exactly who should take over if I should die tonight," he wrote in the letter yesterday.

But he acknowledged that it's "not as well prepared on the investment side" of the business.

"The good news: At 76, I feel terrific and, according to all measurable indicators, am in excellent health," Buffett wrote. "It's amazing what Cherry Coke and hamburgers will do for a fellow."

His investing savvy helped Berkshire report a 29 percent jump in net profit to $11.02 billion or $7,142. per share in 2006.

Berkshire's Class A shares rose $410 to $106,600 a share.
