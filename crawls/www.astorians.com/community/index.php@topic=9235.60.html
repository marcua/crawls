I do think that's an important distinction to make, of course: people with dirty habits, vs. people who are somehow inherently "dirty". And insight from my social psych course (too long ago to remember who studied it) might be useful here - it talked about the habit of littering, and how greatly the environment, not the people in it, influences it. From what I remember, 3 different "litterscapes" were allowed to develop at different times on the same patch of ground: 1) a pristine, not-a-scrap of litter to be found scenario; 2) an almost-pristine with one or two styrofoam cups spoiling the beauty scenario; and 3) an absolutely filthy ground littered with trash scenario. The "spychologists" then observed people's behavior, and found that in the middle scenario, people acted more conscientious - as if the few bits of litter spoiling the landscape jumped out at them and made them think twice about being so conspicuous themselves. Oddly, the first immaculate litterscape had more littering behavior - the conclusion being that people assume someone is maintaing the space and will clean up after them. And of course pig sty setting invites pig sty behavior. That's not to say that some people aren't always conscientious whatever situation they're in, and that other's are always litterbugs no matter how conspicuous their litter appears. It's just to add another layer, which I think is important in this Steinway discussion. Why are the surrounding streets clean, even a block away from Steinway, if the same trash-bearing people have to go through them to reach it? Why are some parts of Steinway dirtier than others? These are the kinds of questions that a BID might need to look into.

That being said, I also agree that Steinway between 30th Ave (my stop) and Broadway is just hideous. No helpful suggestions tho ;) just a spychological observation!

The “avenue” shopping strips are doing relatively well by concentrating on what they do best: offering food, drink, and services, i.e., types of businesses that rely on foot traffic and that benefit from a steady stream of commuters on their way to and from the subway stations.

Steinway on the other hand is a different story. It was always touted as a  “destination” shopping strip, concentrating more on the clothing, gifts, household, etc. though I don't know how much traffic actually comes from far outside the neighborhood

If longer-time residents are honest, it’s supposedly been in decline since forever. The “what should we do about Steinway” is one of the longest running saga in Astoria. My mother complains about the crappy shops now as she has been for the past 30 years, so I’m often left to wonder when exactly was Steinway’s heyday.

In my opinion, the whole concept of Steinway as a shopping street needs to be rethought. It may actually be better to scale back retail, not at street level but redevelop the upper floors over the shops and turn them back into places for people to live in.

More people living on Steinway would, I hope, mean a safer, cleaner street, better services, and perhaps a turnaround in some of the types of shops, which would allow the street to be re-integrated into the neighborhood. Just a thought
