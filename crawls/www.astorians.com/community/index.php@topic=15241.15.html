Hello again. I hope everyone had a nice weekend.

Here are a couple of more sites for historic photos. The first is one you may already be familiar with: http://www.forgotten-ny.com/ lots of interesting facts. Another is the extensive NY Public Library site that has a huge collection of photos of all types, if you do a search for Queens NY there are many of Astoria. A library card is not required.
http://digitalgallery.nypl.org/nypldigital/index.cfm .

I did another Street View visit to the Ditmars area and found some things had not changed. The funeral home near Immaculate Conception church is still there as is LaGuli the pastry shop. The flower shop next to the church used to be a well known ice cream parlor named Jacobi's. It was a busy family place with more adults than kids as customers. The N.E. corner of Ditmars and 31st St was the home of Woolworth's with a McClellans 5 and 10 just East across 32nd St. Across the street on the South side of Ditmars was a pizza joint with the best pie in town, thin crust, oily, and loaded with cheese. You flipped the pointy end back to keep it from dripping in your lap.

Over on 23rd Ave. and 31st St. there was Marrotta's pharmacy on the N.E. corner, the Marrottas lived in the apt. house on 32nd St. near 23rd Rd., me and their son Arty went to P.S. 85 together. On the S.E. corner where there is now a restaurant was Beebe's Radio shop. Mr. Beebe put a 10" TV in his window and piped a speaker outside so the neighborhood could enjoy the new wonder of television, which he also started to sell in addition to fixing radios. Adults brought a chair and us kids sat on the ground and watched the few movies and programs that were on at night. An averaqe night before it got cold would see about 10-15 people out there.

Just East in the middle of the same block was Joe Pospisil's bicycle repair and rental shop. Joe was probably responsible for 98% of the local kids being able to ride a two wheeler. He rented Schwinns and Columbias for ten cents per hour since very few kids had their own bike. If you didn't have a dime Joe would let you make some money sweeping out the store or taking out the trash, a great member of the neighborhood who was like every kids uncle, he even sawed off the handles on worn out brooms to make us stickball bats.

The single house across on the North side of the street was the Marino family who owned a fish store next to the house. I went to school with their son Andy. East of Marino's where there is now a tax preparer was Sam's candy store. Sam Abramson and his wife and two kids lived in back of the store where they sold candy, cigarettes, razor blades, shoelaces, yo-yos, newspapers, magazines comic books and anything else Sam could hang up or squeeze into his little shop along with the 6 stools for someone who wanted coffee or a Coke, ice cream soda, egg cream, tuna on rye etc. You were lucky to get a seat though because at least two stools were always used to store the daily paper. Sunday morning my folks would give me a quarter to get the NY Daily News,(5cents) the NY Daily Mirror(5cents) and the Journal American (10cents) Sunday editions. I made a nickel on the deal. I went to school with Bernie Abramson who was the only Jewish kid in the neighborhood but never got any crap about it.

See ya later for stickball under the railroad trestle on 33rd St. or a game of marbles in the empty lot.  :-)

That great ice cream parlor next to the Immaculate Conception Church called Jacobi's was later called Herman's.  Herman Wullert was the guy behind the counter making all the sandwiches.  He also made all the ice cream by hand, all the chocolate bunnies for Easter, and all the soups and salads.  Never had a hamburger since that came close to the ones I used to get there - along with a chocolate milk shake.  Mmmmm!  Herman was my dad.

Thanks for the kind words Wooly.

Bohemian Hall was a big part of neighborhood entertainment and events back in my time of the 40's and early 50's. The large Bohemian population supported the activities and I think everyone of any nationality enjoyed the celebrations out in the beer garden on Memorial Day, July 4th and Labor Day. There were many dances and dinners and parties for many easily found reasons by a group who liked to eat, drink and dance the polka until the wee hours of the morning. I was only five or six and being carried home by my Irish born father who was singing along with my Czech mother and grandparents is a nice memory, him singing in Czech with a brogue still brings a smile to my face. He fit in well with these fun loving Bohemians.

Bohemian Hall also hosted a Sokol organization. A carry over from the old country that promoted exercise programs and other activities for the boys one and all regardless of national background. This was a very serious group and to the leaders and instructors exercise and health were not to be taken lightly. We learned and practiced rope climbing, broad jumps, high jumping, the parallel bars and other competitive skills along with running, push ups, pull ups etc. Once per year we went to a meet in West Jersey to compete with other Sokol groups. One year of that was enough for most kids.

I was in Astoria about twenty years ago and stopped in to Bohemian Hall for a beer. I had not been there in at least 30 years and everything was the same. The bartender let me wander around outside and downstairs and it brought back a lot of memories. There had been a two lane bowling alley downstairs that had been removed, a major renovation.

I'm glad someone is enjoying the Astoria history lesson, I have many items of interest to me that others may enjoy if you are a history buff as I am. I will gladly post items as time goes by and I'm more than happy to keep Astoria's past alive.

I am opening a separate topic for a longer newspaper item from 1922 regarding the Chinese farms on Chinky Hill so as not to clutter up this thread with something not everyone may be interested in.
