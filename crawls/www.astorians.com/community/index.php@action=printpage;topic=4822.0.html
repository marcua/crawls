OT? I wanted to respond to the teaching fellows questions and since there is no new topic yet....

Basically, the Teaching Fellows program provides an opportunity to become a teacher with no formal training. You are assigned to a classroom (yes usually in a less than optimal area) and as you begin teaching, you begin your master's coursework. You do not need to have an education background in order to join this program,

Why the bait and switch criticism? Once you enroll int he program, you are told that you have additional education classes to take. While you thought you didn't need to have an education background, afterwards you have several pre-requisite classes you need to take in order to complete the program. This frustrates a lot of peole.

I have heard positive and negative things, most vearing toward the negative, but I think that isn't so much because it is a negative program, but rather the expectations are not clearly laid out. It is made out to be easier than it is, probably to recruit. I don't think it should be easy, but I think that the program should be more honest in how it presents itself.

BTW I'm a teacher and professor

I teach too!  I'm a professor, and I enjoy eavesdropping on my HS teacher friends for time-saving tips and classroom management strategies.  First, they are busier than I am.  Second, they more likely  have a pedagogical background, unlike me.  So thanks, everyone, for this interesting dicussion...we are in the best line of work, ever.

Re. the Requested Class Notes controversy:  My take is that they pay to come to lectures, and it is up to them to be there, or not.  I have a lot of frosh students, and I see my job to train them in attending lecture, IF they want good grades.  In lecture I will do almost anything to entertain, re-phrase, get input, and explain things 6 different ways, with drama, wit, whatever.  But lecture only happens once, they are making sacrifices to be in college, and it is up to them to start deciding how to respond to opportunities.  I won't email class notes, and I won't boil down a 90-minute lecture into a 30 second answer to "Hey, professor, did I miss anything in class on Tuesday?"  I tell students to get notes from another student, and then bring them to me o go over with them in person.  Nobody ever takes me up on this offer.  In fact, I think they rarely bother to get the notes from other students.  I also believe that text-versions of a 90-minute lecture will not do them any good, and might actually mislead them on certain point.  I think my take on this is harsh, but reasonable (given 4 classes with an average of 160+ students a semester).

No no no!  No throwing out the window of the note-taking skill-formation!!  Not out the window.  Still inside the building.  Don't tell them their future college-peers won't all know how to take notes!!  Shhhhhh big secret!  lol  They do need it--to do well in college, but more importantly because it is the only way for a person to come to learn how to teach him/herself something.

I think powerpoint can be helpful for some things, I think those of us who use them in classes (me included) don't mostly use them to their best advantage, and I think that students who already know how to take notes are not harmed by them.  So, I feel ok about it.  But they don't replace notes.  And I say this.  Over and over.  "If I handed out an outline that would take 30 seconds to read, and yet lecture went for 90 minutes, it seems likely that there is MORE to know than what the outline covers.  Riiiight?"  But some students don't believe me.  But these are the same students who figure that attending 6 or 7 out of 10 classes is probably enough effort to earn a B+, and in my experience these students will only learn the nature of their misconception when they find out about their not-B+.  And then they will decide what to do with that realization, and that is up to them.

Powerpoint helps me, probably more than them.  (1) When I hand things out, students feel (correctly or not) more secure about their sense of where we are in the course.  This makes them more willing to trust me, and I need that in order to get them to lower their defenses.  (2)  When I know they already have the outlines for the next week, I am forced to stick to lecture schedule, come hell, high water, flu, or big class fight.  This is how I keep from falling behind on lecture material.

The bottom line is that students *are* getting images to study from on the course website, not to mention that many of the most important images are in their textbook anyway.  I've only had this request from one student, who has been very demanding in other ways - what I don't want to encourage with this particular student is her midguided sense that her success in this class depends on *me* rather than on her own effort.  There is a difference between students asking for tools so that they can do the work for themselves, and students who want teachers to take responsibility for them.  Yes, it takes time to develop a visual memory, but that's a skill they'll learn to develop in the class.  And the point is not to have them memorize what I say, but for them to learn how to look at objects.

I teach in Astoria and work with the alternate assessment population, most of whom have paras.  Many of the paras I have work with are excellent and love to be involved in the classroom.  If they are willing or able, include them in your lessons by assigning them tasks like handing out worksheets or working with a particular group.  You can also encourage them to ask questions in class, as many students are often to afraid to.  While the majority of paras are wonderful, don't hesitate to remind those who are not fulfilling their obligations what is expected of them.  I had paras that read the newspaper in class (and of course I got in trouble in the observation) and I've heard of many other problems like those who leave their kids in the classroom when they are supposed to be with them at all times, and those who are inappropriate and make loud, rude and vulgar comments about students in front of other students (such as, "she smells like peepee").   Incidents like this should always be reported to your A.P. (C.Y.A., you know?) So, observe how they act in the classroom and if they seem like they would be open to becoming involved in the lessons approach them about what they might like to do.

Post by: kaitlin on October 25, 2007, 10:53:02 PM
I am a special ed teacher... there are ratios to describe the type of class you are teaching. The first number is the number of students, the second number the number of teachers, and when applicable the third number is the number of paraprofessionals. There are 12:1, 12:1:4, 8:1:1, and 6:1:1. I teach 6 students with autism in a classroom with one teacher and one paraprofessional but I also have a one-to-one para in my room which means that we have two paras and one teacher for 6 students.  The other ratios generally describe different types of disabilities: for example 12:1:4 is usually multiple disabilities, 8:1:1 i think is ED (emotional disability/disturbed), 12:1 or 12:1:1 LD (learning disabled) or a mixed bunch and 6:1:1 as far as I know is almost always children with autism.

hope that helps clear it up :)
