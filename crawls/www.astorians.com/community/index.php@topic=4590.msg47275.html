Please do your reseach before you think of buying in this dump. Read this story from the NY times awhile back. I live here and lived through this nightmare. Please email me for more details if you have questions.Astoria Acropolis Gardens ForeclosedResolving Defaults in Converted Co-ops
By THOMAS J. LUECK
Published: June 16, 1991
EIGHTEEN months after the first reports that the sponsors of co-op and condominium conversions were defaulting on their bank loans and maintenance payments, the problem has spread steadily and now involves more than 400 buildings and thousands of apartment owners in New York City and its suburbs.
Experts say the problems are being aggravated by the region's prolonged real estate slump and tight credit. New York area lenders, reeling under steep losses from their loans to commercial developers, home builders and others, are refusing to finance apartment purchases in buildings that hold the slightest risk of sponsor defaults.
"I don't think we can be sure the worst has passed," said Frederick K. Mehlman, the deputy New York State attorney general in charge of the state's Real Estate Finance Bureau. At a June 5 public hearing at City Hall, he criticized banks and thrift institutions in harsh terms, saying they were "redlining a certain category of co-ops and condominiums."
Despite the alarm over the growing number of troubled buildings, most co-ops and condominiums -- even among those converted during the real estate boom of the 80's -- remain untouched by the sponsor defaults and are on firm financial ground. Mr. Mehlman said the defaults that have surfaced so far represent slightly over 10 percent of the 4,000 conversions of rental properties authorized by his bureau in the last decade.
Nor are all the defaults proving intractable problems. They have spawned an enterprising new field of legal arm-wrestling in which lawyers have found ways to avert large-scale bankruptcies and foreclosures while transferring the assets of the ailing sponsors to their worried fellow shareholders.
Along the way, some co-ops and condominiums may be emerging stronger than ever.
"We are going to have one huge celebration," said Lillian Rodriguez, treasurer of the board at Hyde Park Gardens, a complex of 747 garden apartments in Kew Gardens Hills whose sponsors, holding 500 rent-regulated apartments, went into default last year. The complex was one of New York City's most severely troubled co-ops.
Now the problems appear to have been resolved through a sequence in which banks foreclosed on the co-op's sponsors and took back their unsold, rent-regulated apartments. The banks, with little desire to become landlords, then offered the apartments at steep discounts to their tenants or other shareholders.
As a result, the defaults at Hyde Park Gardens have been paid in full, more renters have invested in home ownership and many of the landlords of the remaining rental apartments are themselves Hyde Park Gardens residents.
Despite such happy endings, public officials are expressing alarm over the extent of the defaults. One reflection of their concern was the sponsorship of the City Hall hearing by all five borough presidents, who called it a forum on the city's "co-op and condominium financial crisis." It attracted a standing-room-only crowd of more than 300 to the public hearing room, formerly the Board of Estimate chamber.
"There is a real possibility of people losing their homes," said Claire Shulman, the Queens Borough President, whose staff has counted 20,000 co-op and condominium units whose owners are threatened by sponsor defaults.
In one case, at the Acropolis, a 617-unit co-op complex in Astoria, the apartment owners have received notice of foreclosure from the Bank of Tokyo, which holds a $25 million underlying mortgage on the complex.
"The good news is that every problem has a solution," said Stuart Saft, a Manhattan lawyer who represents the Hyde Park Gardens board and more than a dozen others. He estimated that 75 percent of the cases of sponsor defaults that have emerged since the end of 1989 have now been cleared up or are near resolution.
But others talk in bleaker terms. Mr. Mehlman said he did not believe more than half the cases of default reported to his staff were resolved or even close to resolution. And in some, solutions are proving elusive.
At the Acropolis, 388 of the 617 apartments are occupied by rent-stabilized tenants who elected not to buy when the complex was converted into a co-op in 1988 and whose rents are less than the monthly maintenance. Some renters of two-bedroom apartments pay under $400 a month, even though the maintenance on their units is over $700.
The sponsor of the conversion, Martin Tepper, defaulted beginning in 1989. In an effort to resolve the problem, he auctioned 353 of his rent-regulated apartments in September. But because of the gap between the rental income on the apartments and what they are charged in maintenance, they sold for only $1 apiece to a New York investor, Daniel Weinberger.
Now, according to Don Novitt, the attorney for the Acropolis shareholders, Mr. Weinberger is himself in default, and the co-op is 12 months behind in its mortgage payments to the Bank of Tokyo. Others involved in the negotiations say it has taken pressure from Peter F. Vallone, the City Council majority leader, and Ms. Shulman, who has met repeatedly with officers of the bank, to forestall foreclosure.
Foreclosure would make the shares in the co-op worthless while leaving the shareholders in debt to other banks that extended them individual mortgage loans.
In some buildings, layers of legal complexity have added to the frustration of shareholders trying to sort through their sponsors' financial problems, sometimes with little access to financial records.
One unresolved question is the the role of the Resolution Trust Corporation, the Federal Agency charged with disposing of the assets of insolvent thrift institutions. In taking over more than a dozen New York-area lenders, Resolution Trust said has gained ownership of 1,000 occupied rent-regulated apartments in converted co-ops and condominiums. The agency expects to take over 1,000 more apartments through foreclosures on investors and sponsors.
In an intricate legal battle, the Federal agency has threatened to evict its rent-regulated tenants, saying that vacant co-ops and condominiums are worth much more than occupied rentals, and claiming that its mandate authorizes it to bypass New York State rules protecting renters.
The state, through Mr. Mehlman's office, has responded by suing Resolution Trust, and experts familiar with the case say it will take months and perhaps years to work its way through the courts. Resolution Trust, meanwhile, said it has stopped trying to sell its occupied apartments, even to investors who might buy them on the condition that they abide by rent-regulation rules.
A more immediate problem, according to co-op and condominium board members and state officials, are the lending policies of New York area banks and thrifts, which they say have tightened mortgage lending just when co-ops and condominiums need it most.
The lenders "are contributing to a sense of panic," said Theresa Racht, attorney for a 24-unit co-op on West 83d Street that was was converted from rentals in 1984. Its underlying mortgage was due to be refinanced on Dec. 31, but a lender willing to approve the loan has not yet been found.
Ms. Racht said 18 units in the building are owner occupied, it has no defaults or other financial problems and its reserve fund is ample.
The problem, she said, is the six unsold units, which have been retained by the co-op's sponsor, Francis Greenburger, president of Time Equities, a major converter that, in December 1989, became the first to disclose serious financial problems.
Even though the financial condition of the building is "very good," she said, "we've been turned down all over town."
Experts say tight credit policies have come at a critical time because many co-ops, like the one on West 83d Street, must now seek refinancing. The reason is that most buildings were financed with short-term "balloon" mortgages -- most commonly requiring full payment in five years. Since 1986 was the peak of the conversion boom -- state records show that 457 New York City buildings with 41,324 apartments were converted in that year alone -- lenders are now receiving a surge of applications for refinancing from co-op boards.
More widespread, according to Mr. Mehlman, is the problem faced by individual buyers and sellers of co-op and condominium apartments. In a real estate slump that has already reduced the value of most apartments, many would-be buyers are finding it hard to get mortgage loans if the apartment they have chosen is in a building where a sponsor still owns many -- or even just a few -- apartments.
The lending policies were what Mr. Mehlman referred to as redlining, a term that gained widespread currency in the 60's and 70's to describe the illegal practice of arbitrarily denying mortgage loans in designated neighborhoods, usually populated by minorities.
Mr. Mehlman said he had found no legal grounds to challenge the co-op and condo lending policies, but added they resembled the illegal racially drawn guidelines in that "a certain category of property is arbitrarily denied" financing. This category, he said, includes buildings where sponsors hold more than a predesignated proportion of the apartments, as well as small buildings regarded as a greater credit risk than large ones.
The lending policies, although subject to change on a daily or weekly basis, were documented in a recent report by HSH Associates, a mortgage information publisher in Butler, N.J. It surveyed 24 of the largest lenders in New York on May 16, and found that almost all had adopted strict rules for co-ops and condominiums.
In most cases, the survey found, lenders that were willing to finance sales in buildings where sponsors retained a large interest limited themselves to large buildings. Those that required the smallest sponsor interest were willing to deal with a bigger variety of buildings, including brownstones, rowhouses and others buildings with fewer than 10 apartments.
In one example, Bank of New York said it would consider financing sales in co-ops where sponsors retained up to 49 percent of the shares, but would consider only buildings with a minimum of 20 apartments. In contrast, Greater New York Savings said it would finance sales in small building -- with a minimum of two apartments -- but would reject any application from a building where a sponsor held more than 30 percent of the shares.
Only three of the lenders in the survey -- East River Savings Bank, Lincoln Savings Bank and the Williamsburgh Savings Bank -- said they would finance apartment sales in buildings where sponsors retained more than 50 percent of the co-op shares.
