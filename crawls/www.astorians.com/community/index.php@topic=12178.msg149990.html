I'm pretty sure that KK is incorrect on this one. The analog/digital transition in 2009 refers to over-the-air signals only. The analog signal you're getting from your wall for free is almost certainly the basic cable service from Time-Warner. Do you get NY1? That and local access are on cable, not over the air. The number of channels that you can get will continue to decrease, as TWC transitions more and more to digital signals. (RCN is already all-digital.) There are two types of converter box here. The ones at Best Buy that are linked to will convert the over-the-air digital signals to a signal you can plug into your TV, for free. That will do nothing at all for the cable coming from your wall (assuming it's a TWC cable.) You can also get a digital cable box or a Tivo with a Cable Card slot, which will allow you to purchase cable TV from TWC without using one of their converter boxes. But you have to rent a card from TWC, which costs $40/month or whatever.

Does this make any sense...? It's pretty complicated...!

Re: converter boxes

To clarify on all points:

1. The switchover that is taking place in February 2009 affects over-the-air signals ONLY.  Cable (and satellite, and Verizon FIOS) will be completely unaffected, regardless of whether you have analog or digital cable and regardless of whether your television set is DTV or HDTV-ready or not.  The switchover will only affect those that have over-the-air reception.

2. The converters that are being sold are *only* if you currently receive your signals with an antenna, over-the-air, and *only* if you do not have a television set that is DTV or HDTV-ready (an older model, most likely).

3. Analog cable will eventually disappear, I'm certain, and when that happens, the white cable entering your apartment will not work anymore in the way you are using it now (connecting it directly to your TV and getting the channels that are not scrambled by the cable company).  But whenever that does happen, it's completely unrelated to the over-the-air analog to digital switchover that is taking place, and the converter boxes being sold currently for over-the-air purposes will not help you get those digital cable signals.  At most, your cable company will install a new digital cable box for you.  Connecting the cable line to the analog-to-digital converter box *will do nothing*, because it is not over-the-air reception and it is thus unaffected by the February 2009 switchover.

4. The converter boxes have an input in the back where your antenna (rabbit ears or rooftop antenna) can be connected.

5. The converter boxes can be connected to your TV either through regular coaxial cable (the ones that have a thin, needle-like point at the end, most likely like the cable wire coming into your apartment), or through the newer AV cables (the ones that have a thicker point at the end and are color-coded red, white and yellow).  Some older TV's only have the coaxial cable inputs, some TV's have both, and I've seen some newer TV's that only have AV inputs, but the converter box will work with all of the above.

6. If you don't have rabbit ears or a "hole" in the TV to plug in the rabbit ears, you can simply buy rabbit ears (or a rooftop antenna, if you are able to put one up in your location) and connect those to the converter box, which you can then connect to the TV with AV cables (supplied when you purchase the converter box).  Without rabbit ears or a rooftop antenna, your converter box *may* work *if* you are in a location that receives a really strong signal...some possibilities would be on the upper floors of a relatively tall apartment building, or perhaps if your apartment/house has a clear shot of the Empire State Building and the Conde Nast Building (where the DTV signals originate).  Since digital reception is an all-or-nothing proposition (you will either get a clear picture, even if your signal strength as measured by the converter box is fairly low, or you will get no signal at all if the signal strength is very low or nonexistent),  if there is a strong signal where you are, you may be able to get, at the very least, the major NYC signals without an antenna of any kind, or even by improvising with a paper clip or wire hanger.  Otherwise, you can purchase a simple pair of rabbit ears from RadioShack or BestBuy or even Eckerd or CVS for around $10 and that will do the job.

One final thing:

A surefire way to determine whether or not the cable entering your house/apartment is for cable TV or just connected to a rooftop antenna would be to scan the stations to see if any non-broadcast stations appear...NY1, the QPTV public access stations and a few others would likely appear on random channels.  The rest of the channels likely will not have static or "snow," but a scrambled picture, if it is a cable line.  If it is an antenna, you will get static/snow on the rest of the stations.  Some rooftop antennas will only give you a good picture for channels 2-13 (VHF), while others will get you channels 14-69 (UHF) as well.

The reason for this is because many older buildings have VHF-only master antennas, whose elements are not tuned to receive the higher frequency signals of UHF.  They were probably installed back in the days where there was nothing at all on UHF.  Most rooftop antennas, however, have VHF and UHF elements and will get both kinds of signals.  Why is this important?  Because many DTV over-the-air signals will be broadcasting on the UHF dial.  For instance, Channel 2 actually broadcasts on Channel 56, Channel 4 actually broadcasts on Channel 28, etc.  However, the DTV converter box (and DTV or HDTV-ready TV's) will "map" those channels to their familiar locations...meaning your box will tune to those channels when you tune to 2, 4, 5, etc.  However, if your house/building has a rooftop antenna that is VHF-only, you may have problems with many of the DTV signals, since most of them will be on UHF.  (Rabbit ears that are sold will receive both VHF and UHF, so no worries there).
