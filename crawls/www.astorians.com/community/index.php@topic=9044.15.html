the problem in astoria is the area is full of apartment buildings and private homes that are old and are not keep up because the owners no longer live in the dwellings and only care for the rents that they get ever month ... thoses building are over 90 years old  you should keep them in good condition.

Well and a further element is when the buildings are that old they are likely rent-stabilized as well (mine is). So for landlords I imagine there is a shrinking incentive to put money into the buildings (costs/taxes rising faster than rental income).

While my building is in decent shape, I can't see them doing any vast improvements when they can't then raise rents to pay for it. Of course the easy answer to that from non-property owners is "they make plenty, they can afford it", but that seems a naive answer to me (a non-owner). Even if they were/are all making mountains of money, very few people will willingly accept losses that they can't recover on. "You should keep them in good condition" is a sentimental argument (that I agree with), but not a business one when such upkeep has no significant financial upside.

If there was a simple solution to the situation(s), I imagine this wouldn't already be an issue all over the city. I'd also guess (total guess!) a majority of owners in the city these days don't live in their own apartment buildings.

This issue is not limited to Astoria/LIC. In Whitestone, North Flushing and Bayside, traditional, single-family, Colonial-, Tudor-type, etc. houses are being replaced with un-traditional (at least as of yet), single-family "Soprano" style (sorry I couldn't think of another adjective) mini-mansions. I took a ride around a few weeks ago and I really thought I could be in Florida, not Queens.

Now these houses are bigger -- and that may be the sticking point for some -- but essentially, a single-family detached house was replaced with a single-family detached house (note this is not always the case).

The difference being to me, and I know this is extremely judgemental on my part, is that what was probably a lovely, pefectly functional, older house -- which appeals to my taste was destroyed and replaced by an ugly monster. I have to say context matter though. If some of the same houses were moved to Florida I wouldn't have the same reaction/feeling.

The same can be said for the new buildings going up in Astoria. But I don't know what can be done that will really make a difference.
