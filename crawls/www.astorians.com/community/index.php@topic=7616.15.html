Quote from: purplecurlygirl on August 22, 2007, 02:38:44 PM
At one point in time that may have been the case, but I think the internet has pretty much ruined any company or industry's chance at creating a nanny state. The unrestricted use of the internet in America allows so much information to be shared in so many different ways that I think it would be very difficult for bookstores ever to get a monopoly on where people get information. Of course, China is a good example of a country which polices the internet so tightly that this COULD happen, so I wouldn't say it's not within the realm of possibility. Still, I think there are enough watchdogs that even if it were to start slowly drifting that direction here, it would be stopped before it became a serious threat.


I completely concur, but I think the stipulation that it needs to be a good and not a service should be added. Particularly a non-perishable, transportable good. Which a great deal of our goods are.

This goes along with Frappe's train and trolley point. A company, with internet sellers and the like, cannot create a strict monopoly on goods that can be shipped without accompaning federal or state regulation. Companies can, however, create monopolies on services because those often are location dependent. Certain services are not, but many are.

I would also like to point out that it really isn't de facto censorship. For it to be that, censorship would have to be "in practice" but given that one can order from numerous other booksellers and given that OJ's publishers can go to other outlets for advertisement, it really seems like a straw man to label this as de facto.

Quote from: Frappe on August 22, 2007, 02:26:22 PM
So we find acceptable the de facto censorship which occured during the 1950's red scare because it was applied by private interests that claimed to have its strict bottom line as its guiding interest and that it would be possible, albeit more difficult, for the consumer to have access to blacklisted artists?

There was nothing 'de facto' about the Red Scare of the late 1940s. It was censorship through intimidation and worse. People were threatened with arrest and their careers were ruined solely because of their political beliefs -- not necessarily for anything they wrote, filmed or said.

And this was all courtesy of the U.S. government, not private interests, through the House Committee on Un-American Activities and later in the 1950s through Joe McCarthy and his nonsense. 

This is where I draw a distinction. Censorship is most real and dangerous when it’s perpetrated by the government and  most appalling because technically they do it in the name of the people.



Quote from: paratactical on August 22, 2007, 12:38:35 PM

This is a very good point. All publishers, in fact all information providers whether they be print or broadcast, exercise some sort of editorial judgment (or at least I hope they do),  which is influenced by many different factors.  If a writer’s book is dropped is s/he being censored or is the publisher exercising their editorial discretion? Who knows?

The Internet now allows readers and more importantly, writers, instant access to electronically publish their views, rants, secrets, masterpieces, or rubbish for all the world to see.  Printing technology has also advanced to the point where it’s now affordable to many people to self-publish their own bound book -- without the need for a traditional publisher.
