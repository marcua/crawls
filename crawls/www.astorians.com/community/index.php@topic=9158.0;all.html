When politicians park illegally
« on: January 14, 2008, 09:59:03 AM »
As many of us know, the situation with double-parked and illegally-parked cars in the vicinity of Ditmars Blvd. and 31st Street is pretty horrendous.  So what do I see this past Friday evening, at exactly 6 pm, as I am walking down 31st Street, outside of the Cingular/AT&T store and Subway?  A Cadillac pulling up to the curb, in a spot designated "no parking" or "no standing" (didn't catch which of the two it was, but parking was not allowed in that spot, either way), with the license plates 1 NYC 1, the engine being turned off, and none other than Peter Vallone, Sr. coming out of the car and crossing the street to go into some stores.  No one else was in the car, and, of course, he did not get a ticket either.

I don't know about everyone else, but things like this infuriate me, because of the obvious poor example it sets, the arrogance of some politicians thinking that they are above the law, and the fact that if it was probably just anyone else parked there illegally, they surely will have gotten a ticket, what with the meter maids out in full force and with their quotas, or, rather, "performance standards" (  :roll: ) to fulfill.

I'm sorry, but I don't think anyone has "earned" that right.  I thought we were (in theory, at least), all equal in the eyes of the law.  I don't see how the same kind of parking is legal for some people, based on their profession and career, and not legal for others.  Perhaps if elected officials like Vallone had to search for a legal parking spot, like us "common people," they might have a better impression of what the daily lives and concerns of their constituents are really like.

Plus, as another example of this sort of abuse, I often see the NYC Traffic Enforcement vehicles double-parked, or parked illegally in bus stops and other spaces where you are otherwise not supposed to park.  That just creates more traffic chaos, and I don't see any excuse at all, not even some sort of "perk," excusing parking in, say, a bus stop or fire hydrant.

Finally, and this may be a bit of a stretch, but I'll say it anyway...we currently find ourselves, as a city and region, trying (in theory) to combat congestion and pollution, through looking at various congestion pricing schemes, etc.  While I am opposed to those proposals as currently construed (one reason being that they don't do enough to combat congestion in the outer boroughs or address certain realities of our road/public transport networks presently), it's important to note that the actions of all of us have an effect on congestion and pollution overall and for everyone's quality of life.  Seeing certain people park illegally, double park their cars (and cause traffic), leave their engines idling, pass red lights, etc., just because it's a "perk" of their job as a politician, judge, etc. while many of these same people preach to the public about the importance of using mass transport, driving less, obeying traffic laws, etc., just reeks of hypocrisy.

Any Special Parking Identification Permit (SPIP) allows for on street parking at most City curbsides including: "No Parking" zones (except for taxi stands), "No Parking" or "No Standing" spaces which are authorized for doctors, diplomats, the press and government agencies, and at parking meters without depositing a coin.
