When I was in college I waited tables, diapered baby butts, cleaned toilets, and worked in an un-airconditioned bakery (during the summer). It was a huge relief when I got a job at the campus library. And even better when I finally scored a summer office job (temping) that paid more than minimum wage.

Even after graduating, I found myself sometimes doing crappy work just because it was the work that I could get when the economy tanked and I didn't have the experience on my resume to get a better job. Telemarketing, data entry ... a really hideous marketing job for Disney that involved hyping AIDA wares to theater-goers. Happily I'm now in a very good job, and very happy. But until I had the degree and the experience, I had to do whatever I had to do to bring in the cash.

Jobs have nothing to do with how good anyone is.

People are telling you where to look, but nobody is really telling you how to get the job.  Sometimes it's hard to get a meaningless part-time job while you're in college--I'm sure most of us have been there!  Just last summer, I graduated with a master's degree and needed something before my full-time job started and had trouble finding it.

So, here is an idea:  Go to Queens Center Mall.  Now is the time that they should be hiring holiday help.  Most places are probably accepting applications, and employees are colecting them, but you need to take the extra step.  Ask to speak to the manager and hand it personally to them and ask interested-sounding questions (do not ask about things like time off until you get the job!)  If they don't set up an interview on the spot, or there is not a manager available at the time, ask when the manager will be there and go back in a few days to follow up on the status of your application.  When you go in there every time, dress nicely.  I'm not saying to run around the mall in suit, but maybe wear a nice pair of black pants, nice shoes and a nice sweater.  Oh, and be sure to come there prepared.  You know that there will be questions on the applications about references, former jobs, etc., so be sure to have those references, addresses and phone numbers with you when you come.  If you have that resume, it couldn't hurt to bring it.

And as said above, don't give out your first paycheck!  You need the money.  And don't waste your time commuting out to Jersey for a job, because if you're not making that much, it is not worth the time and expense to get there.

Good Luck!

Noone told you the most important thing: lie. Not on your experience or skills, etc., but on the fact that you don't plan on working at said gas station/restaurant/pharmacy/department store/etc. for the rest of your life. They're more likely to hire you if they believe (regardless of the truth) that you're going to stick around for awhile. And don't worry, they won't cry when you tell them you're leaving. Trust me. Just make sure you give them the appropriate two weeks notice.

Other people on here might try to tell you that if you're honest about not planning on staying at said crappy job for the long-term the manager or person doing the hiring will appreciate the honesty and still give you the job. While this is true in rare situations (when the manager is desperate), the vast majority of the time the manager will have several other candidates they believe will stick around longer and will give them the job as opposed to you.

I understand the point here but as a recruiter for my company I have to advise against this.  Firstly, 99% of the time we figure it out.  For those of us who interview people for a living, this sort of thing becomes really obvious.  Secondly, lying like this incurs "bad karma" a.k.a. you're "burning bridges".  I can tell you that screwing over an employer by leaving them in a lurch - which is their perspective if you told them you'd be with them longer than you honestly intended - is a bad mistake.  Your employer might not cry but you'll probably piss them off - especially if you're any good at what you do.  You can forget about putting that employer down as a reference, and I'd be wary of putting them on a resume altogether.  And so early in your career you want as hearty a resume as you can muster.  I can also say that I typically throw out resumes that have too many jobs on them - I can't help but think, "why can't this person stay in one place?  what's wrong with them?". 

The problem with college students is that they do leave unexpectedly, they're unpredictable, unreliable, flaky, and not focused on your business because they're focused on school (which is how it should be).  This is, of course, a broad generalization.  BUT, after 17 years of hiring it's VERY uncommon for me to consider a college student.  We have a few right now and they're spectacular but this is a RARITY. 

The problem - and understand that I have been in your shoes - is that you really just need a job, you're not going to devote yourself to the success and growth of the business you will work for.  You'll show up and do whatever you need to do to get your money.  This is really unattractive to most employers - especially in this economy.  That being said, there are still plenty of jobs out there - they're just not high paying.

Also, since I interview ALL the time let me give you some advice:  1)Make sure your resume is PERFECT, and not too many jobs on it, 2)show up 15 minutes early to your interview, and under no circumstance - barring death - call to reschedule, 3)regardless of the position, dress nicely and be well groomed, 4)DON'T CHEW GUM, 5)Ask questions - but not about compensation or benefits - this only applies on the 1st interview, you can ask about these things on subsequent interviews [asking about pay on the first interview is considered bad taste], 6)don't AKS anything - ASK - I don't know what's with the epidemic of "AKS" as opposed to "ASK" but I can tell you right now that it's pervasive and I never hire anyone who speaks that way, and 7)follow up with an email or hand written note - this still wins points.

I know these are basic but I cannot tell you how infrequently all of these items are satisfied - they're very important.

We're not hiring at the moment but if you want to PM me your resume I can ask around.
