I am going to have to really temper my response, because I can see that I am outgunned in this thread on my very pro law enforcement views.  I'm such a square.  :-) 

You guys don't seem to be very big on the "Broken Windows" theory of policing.  I'm a proponent of this theory myself, so it's particularly upsetting to me that the cops out by your neck of the woods don't seem to be actively doing patrols.  One of the most important lessons of Broken Windows is that cops riding around in cars (or doing less than that) don't work.  You need cops on the ground, engaged in the community.  And obviously, they should not laugh at people who try to engage them, as apparently happened in the case of Alexa.  My perception is different, though, because I live in the area of Broadway, and I see tons of cops every day, both on foot and in cars.  Also, I worked for a very short time in the Police Commissioner's office in a civilian capacity when I did a fellowship with the city government.  And in my current job, we work a lot with law enforcement agencies.  In fact I work directly with George Kelling, one of the people who devised Broken Windows back in 1982.  So I have seen firsthand what works and does not work in policing, and I guess to a certain extent it would be fair to say that I have consumed some of the Kool Aid. It was the Ecto-Cooler flavor, which they no longer make.     However, it is an indisputable fact that this city is safer than it has been in recent years, and safer than at any other time.  The numbers don't lie.

In regards to the altruism/non altruism discussion, it's not for me to say what motivates people to become cops.  But the fact is that, whether they become cops because they want to help the community or because they want to retire after twenty years, they are all aware of the fact that they are out there to protect us.  And they know all too well that this could lead to their serious bodily injury or death.  Just look at those poor cops who got shot at close range by those repeat offender car thieves.

In regards to what barrister said, we have to keep in mind that the aggressive policing of so-called minor offenses such as graffiti, smoking pot, etc. is part of why crime rates are so low.  I don't mean to suggest that I am in favor of police brutality, and the cases you describe seem way out of line.  But, statistics bear out that the policing of such minor offenses precipitates a huge drop in major crimes.  It's not that all people who commit minor offenses commit major ones.  But the vast majority of people who commit major offenses also commit minor ones.  Case in point, back in the early 90s, the transit police, then a separate department headed by Bill Bratton, started to go after turnstyle jumpers.  Violent crimes and other serious offenses plummeted.  The proof is in the pudding.  The most frustrating thing about negative encounters with the cops is that this perpetuates negative feelings towards law enforcement.  Also, it perpetuates the sense of defiance towards authority and air of entitlement which likely led barrister's clients to commit their crimes.  That hurts all of us.

You guys don't seem to be very big on the "Broken Windows" theory of policing.

First off, the "Broken Windows" malarky has been disproven countless times. Crime rates dropped in the 90s in cities with and without policy changes or increased police presence. There's suggestions citing the Rockefeller drug laws, demographic employment shifts and, most famously, Levitt's suggestion that the legalization of abortion had a lot to do with it. But it's pretty well regarding that the windows thing is full of it.

David Thatcher, from the urban planning group at UofM had this to say:

"...social science has not been kind to the broken windows theory. A number of scholars reanalyzed the initial studies that appeared to support it ... Others pressed forward with new, more sophisticated studies of the relationship between disorder and crime. The most prominent among them concluded that the relationship between disorder and serious crime is modest, and even that relationship is largely an artifact of more fundamental social forces."



Quote from: Billz1981 on August 23, 2007, 09:33:04 AM
But the vast majority of people who commit major offenses also commit minor ones. 

Just because people who commit major offenses have minor ones on their record, does not mean that people who commit minor ones will go on to commit major ones. This is a logical fallacy that is too often used to justify unnecessary force and preemptive strikes on problems that could be handled in non-violent progressive methods. In fact, the suggestion is out there that the harsh brutality of the police is more likely to create major criminals than the pot being confiscated from them.

It is not universally accepted the Broken Windows has been debunked, though many of the elites seem to stand against it.  I don't have the energy to research and mount a full defense of the theory, which will fall upon deaf ears.  But suffice it to say that while crime did drop in general in the 1990s, by far the greatest drops were seen in Broken Windows cities.  Note for example that Mayor Dinkins enjoyed a 3 percent or so drop in crime his final year in office. suddenly, Giuliani takes over, and the crime rates fall in the double digits.  In the documentary "Giuliani Time," which is pretty much a mouthpiece for Wayne Barrett of the Village Voice to slam Rudy, Dinkins makes the rather dubious assertion that crime rates would have gone down just the same had his administration continued.  I highly doubt that we'd see the same reduction in crime over the years in a non Broken Windows environment.  It's no coincidence that the minute Bratton instituted Broken Windows in the subways, crime started to drop.

If you reread what I wrote about people who commit major offenses, I was very careful to qualify what I said.  I am not suggesting that brutality be inflicted upon anyone, be they the perpetrators of minor crimes that barrister deals with, or major criminals.  Nor did I suggest that minor crimes on a record mean that someone will go on to commit major crimes.  What I said, and still maintain, is that the vast majority of major criminals commit minor crimes as well.  And when you police minor crimes, you actually wind up netting major criminals as well.  Interestingly, this is also proving true in the case of terrorism.  Many would-be terrorists are busted doing things like selling counterfeit DVDs or untaxed cigarettes.  I am not saying that I am in favor of unnecessarily violent tactics, but I feel like some of the so called progressive tactics have had their day and failed.

Whoops, you reread my post as I was about to ask you to reread it.  Thank you!  Look, I'm not closed to the possibility of working with minor criminals so that they don't become violent offenders.  But the unfortunate reality is that the gateway is often contact with the system, which can mean being arrested for your criminal offense.  And again, I am not for anyone being brutalized.  There is a gray area where violence should probably be employed, such as those who resist arrest in a bodily way.  But I don't think that anyone should be preemptively beaten by cops.  On the other hand, I am all for people being charged with criminal offenses.  If this can lead to some sort of program that will empower them to steer clear of crime, then great.  If that program is community based, rather than a government program, all the better.  Punishment for crimes does do something for us as a society.  It helps maintain the rule of law which holds us together as a cohesive society.  I'm not a cultural conservative or anything, but it's pretty clear that I am as conservative as heck on this board.  :-o
