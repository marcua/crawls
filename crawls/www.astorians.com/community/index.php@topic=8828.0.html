Hey JavaJay,

I just went through a similar situation of starting up a company.  I needed a bit of capital because of inventory purchases, but I hope the following can be useful.  Please forgive me if there is anything here that you already know.

First, you need to start your own company.  Sole Proprietorship is free and easy.  Limited Liability Corporations are more expensive to start up (about $200 if you do your own paperwork) but they protect your present assets.  You can always start out as an SP and then move over to an LLC if things start picking up.   You can do it over the net.  You can also get your federal EIN number and state tax ID number at the same time.  Those cost nothing to get and are pretty easy.  Both an LLC and SP allow you to file your earnings as a part of your own entity, which makes things easier from a books perspective in the beginning. 

Second, create a seperate bank account.  From the same bank you have is fine, but do it right away.  It won't cost you anything, but when it comes time to start paying for things with checks, you do not want to be rushing around, offering up starter checks.

Third, do your market homework.  Determine how much it will cost you to produce your product, then figure in your prices which you can sell them for which will make it worth your while.  If you can then take that number and double it, then you will be able to sell to retailers at a wholesale price and allow them to make at least a 100% markup.  In determining your expenses, include the costs of renting a commercial kitchen, packaging and a website.  If they can't make that 100% markup (minimum), then you will be limited to selling directly to the public. 

Fourth, You will need a food handling license, which is a course which the city offers.  I think it might be online now.  Its a bit of a pain if it not, for it is five weekday classes and the wait to get in on them is a bit, unless you enroll in the Spanish classes, were the wait is not so long.  You will probably need other licenses, but you can get all of that at the city's website.

Fifth, get your internet site going.  Even if its a boilerplate special, the ability to put Jay@JavaJayBakery.com on your card and packaging is miles ahead of JavaJayBakery@yahoo.com.  This will also let people find you once they have tried your cookies and order from you directly, or atleast find the retailers who carry your product.  It'll also add to your standing in the eyes of retailers.  I had my website done on the cheap, but its getting remodeled as we speak.  My web designer is honest, efficient and reasonable.  She's also from Astoria, I can give her name and contact info if you message me for it.

Sixth, you'll need to come up with attractive packaging.  This might seem superflourous, but good packaging is the difference between someone thinking that you are a fly by night operator, or a food producer that has standing and will not be providing food which is tainted.  Food is so much more touchy of a thing to buy than clothing or dvd's.  Even cellophane wrap with a sticker which you printed is good enough, just make it look professional. 

Seventh, you'll need to either rent out a commericial kitchen.  This is a fudge factor here, as if you come across as an established operator, you might be able to get away from doing this.  You can rent them out for the day and they have all the equipment you need and are licensed to produce foods for commercial sale.  I actually saw one for rent in the Astoria area on craigslist.  For retailers that will push the legal aspect of it all, it is illegal to produce foods in home kitchens, though I'm sure it happens all the time. 

Eighth, you'll need to decide if you want to sell directly to the consumer or to retailers.  Street fairs are a great way to go for the former, as the investment is about $150 and a $10 a month street fair license.  Again, packaging is absolutely key here, as people do not purchase products, they purchase the experience of purchasing a product.  Very few folks ever say Starbucks is the best coffee, but their packaging and marketing is so fantastic that they can charge a premium for their coffee and desserts.  If you are going to sell to retailers, be prepared to offer up your products on cosignment.  Let them take it and try and sell it.  They are cautious with anything new and putting it on cosignment removes their risk.  If it is a good seller, they'll be wanting more and then you can move off of the cosignment basis.

Ninth, start saving every reciept.  Even for things which you purchased before you became a business.  The IRS allows run up expenses.  I purchased my Mac Book before I started my thing and now I use it constantly.  Thats a nice write off right there.

Its too late for this year, but I can think of atleast two vendors in the Bryant Park Holiday Market that would love to get their hands on well priced baked goods from a baker which will respond to their needs (i.e. delivery to the park on a regular basis, altering your product to quickly respond to the market).  This holiday market is nothing to sneeze at, as they both have sales over $100,000 for the season, they are great forms of advertising and their position in midtown Manhattan permits them to command premium prices.
