Hi caroslime –
For a bit of perspective, when the stock market crashed in 1987, property prices continued to rise, peaking in 1988, and then declined for about 5 or 6 years until they bottomed out in 1993-94.

In New York, prices declined on average by 30% and it took nearly 10 years for prices to reach their peak levels again.

So if prices peaked in 07, and the market declines over 5 years like it did the last time, it could be as late as 2012 until it bottoms out. I’m not saying this will happen this time around, there are different circumstances with this recession, but it is a possibility.


As for the pros and cons, well the biggest pro in owning is that you don’t have to answer to a landlord, which is also the biggest con, because you don’t have a landlord to turn to when things go wrong.

In additional to the usual bills you’ll have extra bills like property taxes and water/sewer to pay, which you probably expect, but be prepared for huge increases in energy costs. You may be surprised at how much it costs to provide heat and hot water to a 2-family house – it ain’t cheap.

Yes, you’ll have a rental income to help offset it, but with property prices the way they are most of that income may be needed to offset the mortgage.

With that said, I still would buy if I could but even if that 750K houses drops to 525K it’s not going to happen for me.
Hope you have more luck.

Investigate with your accountant how the tax write off for the mortgage interest will impact your returns.  as you probably know, the majority of what you'll pay in the early years of homeownership is interest, not principle, and there's a write-off associated with that.  What we discovered was that given the magnitude of the write off, we were able to change our allocations for a significant increase in take home pay which was a 'hidden bonus' that allowed more cash to cover potential increase in expenses of ownership.

Houses are still 40%-50% overpriced in Astoria. It never is. And with the job losses in NY outpacing the rest of the country, the crash here will be significant.

You answer your own question. "Rents went up as well". But houses went up double what rents did. As I said, the metric is for comprable houses in the same location, not houses here to rents in "Armpit Idaho."
If you buy now, your house will go down in value. The rent to own ratio works because it is the best indicator of what people can afford for a place to live on a month to month basis given their income. When the metric gets out of whack, well, we see the results now with the housing collapse.
Prof. Robert Shiller of Yale, the countries leading expert on Real Estate costs, has shown that  home prices , adjusted for inflation did not change in the U.S. from 1890 to 1999. In the next 5 years they doubled. They will now fall back and return to the mean, as they say in Economics.
The "going prices" in Florida 4 years ago were 50% to 80% above what they are now. Was that a good idea to buy then, overpriced or not.
