The article, and those who are quoted within it, attribute the current situation on Steinway Street to the bad economy.  While that may be true to an extent, I feel that Steinway's problems have existed since long before the latest economic downturn.  Vacant storefronts and for rent signs have been commonplace on Steinway for almost a decade, and a lot of those storefronts took quite a while to be rented again.  Case in point: the former KB Toys, which must have been vacant for at least two years before finally being rented to a discount store about a year ago, though the second floor remains vacant.  I think the bottom line is that the rents are too high, the landlords aren't budging, and the spaces are empty as a result.  And while, logically, this is happening because the landlords are biding time until someone comes along that will pay their asking price, I still can't understand that frame of thought: certainly going 1-2 years without any rental income at all must be worse than lowering rents slightly, getting that income, and injecting some life back into Steinway Street in the meantime, which will, in the not-too-long term, raise Steinway's profile as a retail destination again.  That's not going to happen if 20+ stores are vacant on Astoria's "premier" shopping strip.  And as far as the national retailers, they certainly don't seem to be coming to Steinway in droves either...not even before the economic downturn.

It's a slightly more complex problem then the DN made it seem, although that doesn't shock me much. A big factor involved with "problems" like this is the way commercial leases are structured. 10 to 15 year terms are pretty common, and the increases from 1 year to the next is often spelled out in advance at the beginning of the lease. A lot of retail in places like the Village have had to cope with their rents doubling or tripling when the lease turned over (and they often end up moving out as a result). Someone new moves in, and they often have higher prices to deal with the higher rent they are being charged. While it may seem silly to you and I it certainly is possible that a landlord might be better off.

As far as national tenants go, what landlord wouldn't want them? The have better creditworthiness and often research an area before they look at expanding, they do their homework. The risk is mitigated if you are a landlord.

I think part of the problem might be Steinway street itself. The retail doesn't seem to be as "nice" as what you find on Ditmars or even parts of 30th Ave. I wonder if the shopping demographic is different then other parts of Astoria?

All good points.  However, as someone pointed out, national chains do their homework before moving in: market studies, measuring foot/automobile traffic, area demographics, etc., before deciding whether to open a franchise in a particular location.  The increased amount of vacancies on Steinway Street (and the lower quality of establishments that do exist) have driven much of the foot/car traffic away.  That, in turn, makes the area *less* appealing for national chains to move in.  It's a catch-22 situation.  The landlords are holding out for the national chains, and in part they are justified in doing so, however, by keeping so many storefronts vacant for so long, it also drives away the shoppers that once made Steinway into a bustling commercial strip, and in turn, that makes Steinway Street a less attractive option for national retailers.

I walked Steinway Street earlier this year and did a survey of the stores.

http://www.astorians.com/community/index.php?topic=9483.0

There were about 10 vacant stores on the prime blocks (Broadway to 30th Avenue) at that time so the number of empty storefronts has basically doubled in alittle over 6 months.

The discussion around what to do about Steinway is often framed around the danger of national chains replacing mom and pop shops but I’ve always maintained that what is more of a danger is empty storefronts – which seems to be happening now, so a 100% increase in vacancies in such a short time is troubling.

This is not the first time Steinway has faced trouble like AJ said, there were problems in the late 70s early 80s when I was a kid but like everything nowadays it seems worse, especially given the current gloomy economic news.

There could be a real opportunity here though if the landlords and brokers made a real effort to work together and radically rethink the street.

Mixed-use developments that combine living, shopping and working have been gaining favor over standalone shopping centers and I’ve been thinking that it’s like reinventing the wheel because that’s what Steinway essentially is, or rather used to be.

There were more people living on the street in the past and more people seemed to make Steinway  more dynamic (though my memory could be filtered through rose-tinted glasses)

I wonder if the spaces above stores were re-converted back into apartments and more people moved in, if that would make the street come alive again. Right now everything shuts down in the evenings when the stores close. If Steinway were made to look more like the avenues the street could be transformed.
