Awesome.  Hahaha.  I'm on the quitting train, too.  I'm working on getting on with the FD and smoking-related massive heart attack deaths at a very young age run in my family.  My grandfather was 39, my dad was 49; so it was REAL dumb for me to smoke. 

But, like you, I had been smoking pretty regularly since I was 13 or so.  Oddly enough, I never felt PHYSICALLY addicted during any of that time, although mentally is another story.  I have a very addictive personality that, once I start something, I speed up my intake.  Once I have that first cigarette or drink, it's pedal to the metal from there.  I could go until 7PM without having my first smoke, but I could power through almost an entire pack by the time I went to sleep at 2 or 3AM if I was out drinking.  Then, every morning, I hated smoking and swore I was done; only to get bored/start drinking and cave by the next night. 

Anyway, I've been off and on quitting for years.  Like you, my longest was 6 months.  Certain triggers (mostly certain people/situations that I had always been a heavy smoker with/in in the past) would set me back off.  But, I've been quitting since the first of the year.  I've had a few stumbles here and there, but definitely a far, far cry from the pack of day I previously smoked.  I always wake up in the morning, even if I just had one, and want to kick myself for having it. 

Kinda along the quit.net thing, I also set up a reward for not smoking.  I stopped buying packs, but I set aside the money I would have spent to buy a digi cam that I've wanted for awhile.  So, I finally saved up enough and bought the digi, which is about the size of a pack of cigarettes, and I now keep it in my front pocket where I used to have my smokes.  So, I get to enjoy the money for those random pictures instead of feeling like a bag of smashed up a-holes in the morning.

Anyway, +1 on the exercising and avoiding the drinking situations/people that will trigger your smoking urges.  Like a relationship gone south, you'll think less and less about them over time and, eventually, you'll realize you never needed 'em. 

Hope it's still going well.

I've a serious nicotine addiction. I tried Zyban- it helped b/c if I had a smoke it made me nauseous, but after a few months I went back. I did cold turkey and lasted about a year. Et cetera.
Then I called nyc 311- they sent a generic version of the patch, free! (They also did an intake over the phone that made me realize I have been smoking twenty years!)
The great part about getting the patch was realizing that I was not demonic from withdrawal, however, the adhesive is terrible (it's free and generic), so, I started spending the money I used to spend on smokes on a name brand patch.
The adhesive was much better- very important to me as I am a swimmer, jogger, and the generic patch would either fall off during the after-exercise shower, or while I was on treadmill from  sweat.
I still messed up many times. The day I go by is the first day of last summer, by then I was much less tempted. I had absolutely zero alcohol for three months, because the people around me who helped noted I was most vulnerable when under the influence of alcohol.
I also outlawed smoking in my kitchen; even the second hand smoke was enough to reawaken my addiction.
It has been about nine months now. I've had a few setbacks, always while under the influence of alcohol.
The other night I went out and overdid it to the point where I don't recall walking home and I woke up panicked wondering if I had smoked. My clothes didn't smell like smoke, so I concluded I hadn't. (yeah, I know that's not the worst thing about not remembering ten minutes of one's life, but, whatever)
Some of my closest friends smoke, so I try and go places where smoking isn't allowed. I haven't been to the cafes in a while- too tempting.
Carrying a pen and paper is my substitute. If i feel nervous (one of my triggers) I just start writing- I write about how i want to smoke but i will not.
That's they key, i think; know what your triggers are and have a strategy for substitute behavior (like pounding madly away on a keyboard). And don't kick your ass when you falter-it's too easy to go on a binge.
Besides, why give those dirtbags your hard earned money- since I stopped the patch I have set aside the money I used to spend. I spend it on concert tix these days :-)
