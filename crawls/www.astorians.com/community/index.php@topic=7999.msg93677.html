Alright, I couldn;t waste any more time waiting for the punch line, What is it  -- if that wasn't a joke it was just plain stupid.  It's like when you were in elementary school and people kept coming to class to tell everyone how "special" they were. yuck.

Stylistically, that video isn't for everyone. I guess that some people might consider it simplistic, even condescending, though I found nothing in it to be inherently so. In fact, I thought it was a lucid presentation, intellectually consistent, and, I would think, eye-opening to people who aren't used to thinking about individual liberty in more depth than usual.

It clearly didn't appeal to lobber, but lobber's response has nothing to do with the core ideas it presents. In that way, lobber's rant... "waste of my time, a joke or stupid"... is typical of most of the responses I encounter when attempting to discuss public policy in terms of principles, ideas, and economics. 

In my experience, most leftists want to talk about public policy because of an emotional reaction to an issue which they then immediately translate into a public policy stand without much application of logic, critical thought, reference to empirical findings, and reference to history. Somebody is suffering = the government should do something about it, they assume, and if a person challenges that equation, they label the person "uncaring, inhumane, stupid, out of touch, extreme, a little crazy." Or a "right-winger", which is a convenient, damning catch-all description of anyone who doesn't believe in the benevolence of government.

That video does a decent job of emphasizing that the basic unit in any society is an individual human being. Individualism and voluntarism are, in fact, what all of us count on every day of our lives in virtually all of our dealings with everybody we have personal and business relationships with. It is only in the realm of public policy that people seem to forget about this and engage in all kinds of group-think and coercion advocacy.
