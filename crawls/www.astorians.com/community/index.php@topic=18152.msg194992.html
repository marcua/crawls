Nothing is cut and dry with the energy situation.  Drilling for more oil is not a panacea, nor is "green" technology.  Here is a worthwhile article on several reasons why green energy is not the miracle cure that it seems to be: http://www.manhattan-institute.org/html/miarticle.htm?id=6160  Chief among those reasons is the inefficiency of so called green energy.

One key piece of the article: "solar and wind technologies require huge amounts of land to deliver relatively small amounts of energy, disrupting natural habitats. Even an aging natural gas well producing 60,000 cubic feet per day generates more than 20 times the watts per square meter of a wind turbine. A nuclear power plant cranks out about 56 watts per square meter, eight times as much as is derived from solar photovoltaic installations."

I personally believe that we need to investigate all potential energy solutions, especially nuclear.

« Reply #14 on: May 08, 2010, 12:49:37 PM »
Quote from: mangopaco on May 05, 2010, 11:28:38 AM
There's a lot going on in the world: volcanoes, earthquakes, floods, and terrorism; but, although this oil leak has been in the news almost every day, I feel like there's no sense of urgency coming through.  Maybe I'm misinterpreting the "urgency."  Anyone have thoughts on it?

I don't know what news you've been watching, but the stuff I've been reading has sounded pretty urgent, and I just look at what's on Google News. Back when this started, people didn't realize how bad it really was. It's taken a while for it to sink in. It's not so much an "oil spill" as it is an oil flow. The perceived lack of urgency you're seeing might be because no one's really sure what to do about it. It is kind of overwhelming to think about; the depth of the leak makes this very difficult to deal with. The Ixtoc blowout in 1979 was only 50 meters under water, and this one is almost a mile down.

There've been a couple stories taking the angle that people are angry at Obama for not acting faster, but what's he supposed to do aside from making sure those who can handle it are dealing with it as best they can? The only people qualified to handle this are those in the oil drilling industry. It's not like Katrina, where the primary concern at first was getting thousands of people out of a flooded area, and the more resources that were immediately dealing with it the better the rescue effort would be. This is about stopping millions of gallons of oil from coming out of an extremely deep underwater hole. There aren't too many people qualified in dealing with this sort of thing, not much stuff exists for dealing with this sort of thing, and even those who are qualified in dealing with it don't seem to know the best course of action for 1) stopping the flow and 2) cleaning up all the oil. And cleaning up the oil is of course very important, but you don't want to do that in a way that destroys even more of the environment and wildlife than this already will.

Clearly we're going to need oil for a long time, though hopefully a time comes soon where we don't need to rely on oil nearly as much because we have a variety of other energy resources as well as much more efficient energy-using devices. But as for the criticism that green energy is simply not as efficient as energy from fossil fuels, while I'm no expert on the matter, I have to think that is at least partially because the technology hasn't been developed yet to its fullest potential. We've been working with oil and natural gas a hell of a lot longer than we've been working with solar panels and wind turbines, and more energy-efficient devices are being developed all the time.
