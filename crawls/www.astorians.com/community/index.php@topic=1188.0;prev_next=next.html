I too am among those who would prefer that Astoria remain under the radar.

I grew up there and lived there until I was in my mid-20's and I've always loved the fact that it was so close to Manhattan yet so different at the same time - a little slower pace with more affordable living across the board.  And even though many apartments were rent-stabilized it almost didn't matter because the market itself kept the rents affordable.

I then moved into Manhattan where I met my now-wife who was living on the UWS in a super-cheap rent-stabilized apt (and who coincidentally is from Long Island).  Living on the UWS you realize that even though the rent for people moving in now can be exorbitant, there is a huge income disparity among residents on the UWS.  There is a lot of public housing as well as large elderly, hispanic and lower-middle class Jewish populations.

I was also amazed at how many middle-class families are still living there (assuming they had purchased their apts before the boom or had rent-stabilized deals).  And all of these groups of course mix with famous and well-to-do who occupy another stratosphere yet are still only 2 buildings away.

Anyway, we now have 2 children and a few years back we left Manhattan for Hoboken, NJ (I lobbied for Astoria but people from Long Island just don't get Queens) and now I'm constantly amazed at the continued gentrification of Hoboken.  It's crazy that the prices just keep going up and and up and up.  When we moved here, $1,000,000 for a house would have been laughable but now just 3 years later I know of at least 1 that went for over $2,000,000 and a number that sold for well over $1,500,000.

Since it has always been my long-term plan to return to Astoria I can only hope that the same thing doesn't happen there.

I fear I am about to add some fuel to the fires, but here goes...

One of the reasons that Astoria has always been a nice neighborhood is that many of the smaller buildings/ houses have historically been owned by small property owners, people who own one or two buildings, usually lived in one of them, and take good care of the property. With increasing development this is changing.

There is also the ethnic aspect- Astoria is traditionally a Greek and Italian neighborhood, and I hate to say it, but the old residents did a very good job of keeping the people they didn't want out. The first time I looked for an apt in astoria, I was turned down immediately because I wasn't Greek- end of story. I finally did get and apartment because both my roomate and I said we wanted our parents to see it before we would sign the lease. But as a Greek friend of mine once said, "the first thing a Greek does when he gets money is move out of Astoria" (he's now living in Forest Hills).  So that is changing too.

Which brings us to where we are... which is an odd mix of gentrification and popularization. The people who are moving into the area are not all hipsters and yuppies. There are also a many immigrant families from South Asia, the Middle East, south America, and Central Europe... all people who would traditionally have been kept out of the neighborhood.

So the issues are not simple. Certainly rising prices make it difficult to keep Astoria available to those of us that moved here years ago and have given the neighborhood a sort of rebirth. but it's also nice to finally be able to get people to come out to queens for a night out.

I've only been an Astorian for a month and a half, but I really like the low hipster ratio right now.  Every time I go to Williamsburg, I am filled with relief that I don't live in a neighbourhood full of trendy clones who pay way too much for "vintage" (i.e., second hand) bikes.  

Also, those kids don't know how much groceries are suppose to cost, and consequently the grocery stores in Williamsburg are both expensive, and subpar in terms of quality of produce.  I don't know why that gets under my skin so much, but it does.  

I've also lived in Greenpoint and at the time, the hipster ratio was low.  However, the predominantly Polish neighbourhood didn't feel very inviting.  In fact, at best it felt like we were invisible.  I was pleasantly surprised by how friendly and inclusive Astoria feels to a newcomer such as myself.
