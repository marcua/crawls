1) Belief in the social, political, and economic
    equality of the sexes.

2) The movement organized around this belief.

3) The advocacy of women's rights on the grounds
    of political, social, and economic equality to
    men.

I'm interested not so much in feminism as in gender overall and how it's constructed at an individual and societal level.  The reason I prioritize gender above feminism is because assumptions about one gender are often accompanied by assumptions about the other gender; in other words, notions of femininitiy and masculinity are interdependent.  For example, one might think that boys are better than girls at science, or that girls should play with dolls but not boys; the end result being the assigning of specific social roles as being more suitable for one gender than the other.

Having said that, I have to add that I've never experienced as much reinforcement of traditional gender roles from the outside world as after I got married, and that moreover, most of it has come from women.  It's always been women who have addressed mail to me using my first name and my husband's last name; one male friend, on the other hand, addressed a piece of joint mail to my husband and me using my last name!

I feel that real feminism is empowering to both male and female because real feminism has nothing to do with "power" or the "elevation" of one sex in relation to another at all. Real feminism as I understand it is centered around human and gender authenticity. For me it's not about "equality" so far as "power" over another sex goes, but is more about creating a society in which all human beings can live authentically by balancing their individual roles with that of society. In this way I call myself a feminist.

That being said, I also feel like a good number of people who call themselves feminists misrepresent the feminist movement as something that is about the "power" of one of the sexes, about one sex "standing up" to the oppression of the other sex (as in the "victimization" of one sex, and the "abuse" of another sex), and about the "liberation" of one sex from the other. This language is counter-productive and represents an over-simplification of the problem for me. Gender is a more complex issue in my mind, so I believe that some of the "feminist" talk I hear undermines the very philosophy that real feminism is built upon. It also means that sometimes I wouldn't want to be labeled a feminist.

As an example, I have a similar feeling about Christianity. For me real Christianity is built upon a similar philosophy of authenticity, but that most of the systems that have been put in place on top of the spiritual "founder" of the faith (Jesus) actually entirely misrepresent the core philosophy. In the sense of human authenticity, and questioning of power, acknowledging the poor, and being a pacifist I could call myself "Christian". But because of the current status-quo representation of "Christianity" in the mainstream, and based on the behavior of many religious organizations who call themselves "Christian" I don't want to be labeled as one.

I think the same could be said of many philosophical groups though. Real masculinity, real Americanism etc. But none of these things seem to be manifest in their core form these days. Which is why I can completely understand why someone wouldn't feel comfortable all the time with the "label" of "feminism" or anything else.

And as I understand real feminism, "labels" are out of style anyway.

Ah, yes. Two of the better known women of the first-wave of feminism. They laid the foundation, the framework for the second-wave which followed in the 1960s. This wave was very basic in its concept. ". . . to take action to bring women into full participation in society—sharing equal rights, responsibilities and opportunities with men, while living free from discrimination." And as it was taking a foothold, it became corrupted. The reasons are moot, at this point in history. The more important aspect of its corruption was, that even at *that* time, it laid the foundation for the third-wave. I believe that it is now starting to take shape.

mirki2 also mentioned the glass ceiling. I wonder how many of today's young women have even heard the term. I believe it was coined in the early 1980s. And it is still alive and flourishing around the world.

There has been change. Not all of it for the better. For example, I was among the group of women who were vilified in our protest marches against the NYFD when they changed the requirements to allow women to pass their test and become members of the department. That was *never* the intent of the movement. We just wanted women to have the same rights to apply (test) for the job. There used to be a test that required an applicant to carry a dummy weighing a certain weight down a ladder from a third story building. That weight was reduced so that a woman (READ: smaller statue) could handle the dummy. That should never have been done. What women wanted was for them to be able to do the *same* job as a man and get paid the same for doing it. Once that restriction was lowered it became easier for the courts to lower others. After an historical (landmark) case brought by a group of Hispanics, the height restriction for the job was lowered to allow people shorter than the average person to pass the basic requirements of the job.

Frankly, I am not comforted when I see 2 uniform cops walking the streets of my neighborhood, carrying guns, and I realize that they are both women and both 5 feet 2 inches tall. There used to be a regulation that no-one under 5 feet 8 inches could even apply to the department. I am not encouraged by these kinds of changes.

I still find it difficult to understand the ramifications of the defeat of the Equal Rights Amendment. It would have opened the door to all the other issues that are currently plaguing our Country.

1. Equality of rights under the law shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of sex.

2. The Congress shall have the power to enforce, by appropriate legislation, the provisions of this article.

3. This amendment shall take effect two years after the date of ratification.

By adding words after the word sex, in section one, it could have covered all the ills we are experiencing. Sexual preference. Race. Religion. National Origin. And on, and on.
