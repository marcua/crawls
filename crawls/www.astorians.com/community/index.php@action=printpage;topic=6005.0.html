Quote from: Shelby on November 25, 2007, 11:20:38 AM
My problem is that I am burning through light bulbs incredibly fast in my kitchen overhead fixture.  [...]
Is there something wrong with the fixture?
Here are some guesses:

Could it be running too hot? Something like a recessed fixture with thermal insulation behind it?  Or using a higher wattage bulb than the fixture is designed for? If this is the case, maybe a compact fluorescent bulb would give the light you crave without the problem.

Poor contact between the socket and the bulb? If the thing flickers or dims for no reason, or makes little sounds when it's operating, there could be a poor connection socket-to-bulb, or in the wiring, or a faulty switch.

Abnormally low voltage or use of a dimmer can shorten the life of halogen type bulbs - the process that makes them "long-life" depends on maintaining a particular range of operating temperature.

Abnormally high voltage. A symptom might be that the light gets brighter when some appliance like the refrigerator switches on. This would be unusual, and indicate a serious flaw in the wiring that had better be corrected.

Actually, any of these problems need to be corrected.

As the Handyman said, most fixtures are designed for 60 watt bulbs.

If the socket body is made of plastic and has shiny pvc insulated wiring, or it's metal with a paper insulator like a typical table lamp socket, it's probably designed for 60 watt bulbs. Sockets for higher wattage bulbs are typically made of porcelain ceramic material, and have wires with high temperature insulation.

High wattage fixtures are designed to allow air circulation around the fixture. If the fixture is enclosed and essentially flat against the ceiling, its probably intended for 60 W bulbs.

Fixtures of recent manufacture will have a sticker in there too, though maybe that is all burned up by now. :)


A normal run-of-the-mill light bulb has an expected life of 750 hours average. If it's on 24 hours a day, that's about 31 days.


The 5 year bulb is a mystery.  Some specialty bulbs are designed for a particular orientation - the box or bulb may something like "Base down to horizontal", meaning it shouldn't be used in a fixture where the socket base is above the body of the bulb.

Dimmers and certain automatic-timer switches do not play well with ordinary compact fluorescent bulbs.


The suggestion to find (someone with) a voltmeter is a good one. It is possible, after all the stuff con-ed had to replace last year, that they have made a mistake and the voltage to your house is just plain high.
