I feel like there should be some sort of moral cost versus moral benefit calculus, which I can't put my finger on how to define.  Wantonly hurting people is out of the question, but we do need to get information from terror suspects because it saves lives.

The problem of using waterboarding or other methods of torture is that it gets people to offer false information in order to get the torture to stop, thereby undermining the reason for using it in the first place. People's bodies can only withstand so much, and even less one's mind under that kind of extreme stress and torture. There is an actual reason why the Supreme Court banned the use of third degree interrogation tactics (those involving physical "persuasion") - it makes it more likely that innocent people will confess to things they did not do as well as negatively impacting the reliability of actual perpetrators' memory. It just doesn't make sense to use these kinds of tactics when they reduce the diagnosticity of whether the information we receive is good or bad.

Title: Re: Straight Dope writer waterboards
Post by: jenriet on January 14, 2008, 07:59:34 PM
Quote from: Billz1981 on January 14, 2008, 03:55:11 PM
That is fascinating.  Was the intent to learn how the Nazi mind worked, deconstruct their reasoning skills, etc.?  Or was the intent to sort of bond with the Nazis so that they would be more prone to helping their American captors out?  Do you think that there are cultural barriers that would prevent that tactic from working here?  We probably had more in common with Germans in the late 40s and 50s than we do with our detainees now.  Or do you just mean, in general, that kindness is a sort of universal language which greases the skids and would get them to share information?

Actually, a leading tactic used by police today is along these same lines of making "friends" with the suspect. The idea is minimize the seriousness of the crime and to get the suspect to think you are on his/her side, which makes it psychologically easier to confess. Unfortunately, research has also shown that this gets people to provide false confessions too. The problem with interrogation is that the combination of tactics can lead vulnerable people to make false confessions. Then when you add in situational factors like sleep deprivation, food deprivation, etc. you really run the risk of getting false information from people. Though there are some promising leads in psychological research as far as developing better interrogation tactics, it is still really in its infancy in figuring out better ways of doing things rather than showing what's wrong with the currently utilized tactics. The other problem, as you mentioned, is that this research doesn't look at cultural differences in developing interrogation techniques, which limits it utility for training terrorist suspects of foreign origins. Then again, the leading trainers of interrogators in this country train people all over the world (including the Middle East), which shows that some techniques work on people of all cultures.

Truth serum is very ineffective. It actually makes people more likely to again report false memories or distort true memories to the tune of what is being suggested by the interviewer.
