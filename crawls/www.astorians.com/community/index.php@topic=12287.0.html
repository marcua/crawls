I don't know, Oliver.  Your implied "love it or leave it" take on this topic implies the same sort of redneck mentality a lot of people from "Bird Turd" thought they had left behind when they moved to a city.  Don't like it here, boy?  Leave!  We don't like your kind around these parts!

I came to NYC from rural America about 20 years ago, spent 10 years in a ragged Bronx neighborhood, and now 10 years here. (And I moved here because it was affordable - when you hear me whine here, more often than not, that will be the impetus.) Not sure how I'm supposed to pigeonhole myself when it comes to the right to complain?  I'm not from here, not a native New Yorker (and proud of it), but have surely lived here long enough, and in another urban environment far more "urban" than what Astoria has going to grasp city life and how it works ... but I really have no problem listening to people complain about various seemingly minor lifestyle issues (that probably aren't so minor when you're caught up in them).  To me, this website is often like asking someone, "How was your day, dear?"  Sometimes it's all roses, other times you get this sort of stuff.

Astoria provides a nice little cushion for you to feel secure in avoiding things that don't agree with you, although as you noted, you consciously choose to avoid these things rather than deal with them.  The things that grate on me about Astoria now (namely gentrification and rising costs of living) aren't so easily avoidable.  Another thread notes the empty storefronts on Steinway, and part of the reasoning being once an old lease a long-time tenant has ends, the new one is prohibitively high, and the tentant must leave, often leaving behind another vestige of the "old neighborhood."  I suspect this will be happening a lot more in the future ... and I don't like it.  You can't avoid your rent tripling -- and there is no complaining in such a case, you just leave!

I surely hear what you're saying and, in fact, am inclined to give you a quite "hell yeah" on a lot of points.  I can't pigeonhole the native New Yorkers I've known.  Some are so unassuming and down to earth, you'd never know they're native New Yorkers.  Others are like neurotic stereotypes straight out of a Woody Allen movie. But I do know the redneck mentality -- because I have it -- and pointing out you've lived in one place for 30+ years is one of those things akin to sleeveless Nascar t-shirt in my mind.  "Adaptability" isn't a word that comes to mind.  Native New Yorkers fled New York by the hundreds of thousands in the 70s, when the crime and sense of the city losing control got to be too much for even them to deal with.  Some things, no one should ever "adapt" to.

On Sunday, I was coming down the subway stairs at Ditmars, when I saw two young women just coming into the station, in their 20s, obviously new to the neighborhood, very well dressed, not going to church, just going to Manhattan, and one of them kept blurting out, "Ew, it smells like piss in here!  Oh my gawd, what are we doing here?  Ew, this place is so grody."

And all I could think was, "if only you were just visiting and not one of the dupes who just bought an overpriced apartment here."  But something tells me they're not!  But, surely, after scores of recent interludes like that, Oliver, I know exactly where you're coming from on this topic.  But just thought I'd pass along some insights from a fellow redneck who knows damn well the value of laying down roots in these here parts.

I agree with Oliver, there are lots of things that are different between urban and rural environments and nothing is perfect, you have to take the good with the bad.

However, I think it is more a personality issue than where people are coming from.  I know people from Midwest, USA that don't make a fuss about excessive noise/graffiti/any other inherently urban issue and I know native New Yorkers that get up in arms (funny enough, now that I think about it, most of those have moved to the suburbs now).

What I find disturbing is the lack of personal responsibility a lot of these rants exhibit. It seems to me that people want somebody else to fix whatever it that is causing their inconvenience. There is graffiti, where is the police? (how about rolling your sleeves and cleaning it? how about looking out for who's doing it, snapping a pic and giving the police a hand?) There's noise, the church should be more considerate (like the church has had a pristine history of consideration anywhere in the world?!  how about getting plugs for the noise? how about getting a noise reduction machine? how about looking for a different apartment?)  There is street violence, those teens should go to jail (how about volunteering with kids, so violence patterns are broken?!)  The streets are dirty, let's write to politicians (how about joining New York Cares and cleaning parks and streets?! How about forming a street-cleaning group like Jackson Heights have?) Somebody even went as far as suggesting that the moderators of this board take the responsibility of following up on community issues.  Give me a break.

Yes, we all rant. However, the only person that can fix your situation is yourself.  If the expectation is that institutions (government, churches, etc.) change to accommodate the "needs" of a few, very unorganized citizens, well, there will be disappointment.

I've lived in the Bronx, Brooklyn and Astoria (and if all go well, soon Jackson Heights).  No place has been perfect, but in comparison, the quality of life in Astoria is a bit higher (less crime, relatively cleaner/calmer), so truthfully, it's hard for me to see what all the fuss is about.

AJTNYC - I wasn't here in the 70's and 80's, so the only information that I have is what I read, which seems to indicate that the "clean up" of New York City was not a citizen-inspired movement, but part of a country-wide economic trend that resulted in decreased crime rates after the crack-cocaine days.  If you (or anyone) have any good articles about what happened in those days and how the city cleaned up its act, please send them my way!
