urrisgirl, I used various combinations of castile soap, cider vinegar (white is better but cider vinegar was all I had!) and baking soda.  The castile soap is particularly awesome.  Dr. Bronner's makes something called "Sal Suds" that has fir and pine oil in it, and has been formulated to cut grease better.  Like their other castile soaps, a little goes a loooong way.  Dr. Bronner's is also fair trade and they use recycled plastic.  I now use this soap to wash dishes, and dilute it to clean the counter and sink.  I also use it in conjunction with baking soda to clean the bathroom sink and the shower/tub (baking soda for a bit of grit).  Also use a little castile soap in the toilet, but I understand that vinegar will also clean it just fine. 

I sprinkled baking soda on the stovetop and then sprinkled vinegar on it (it bubbled!).  It cleaned the stovetop like a charm.  I used plain vinegar to clean the bathroom mirror.  It was perfect.

I used diluted castile soap in hot water to clean the kitchen and bathroom floors.

I want to look into borax (hence my other post) and Bon-Ami, too.  I plan to buy a big-ass container of white vinegar this week, and some spray bottles.  I'd like to get some of the lavendar castile soap, too (their regular soaps use organic oils), for a few other uses.

Oh, and I use the sponges with the scrubbers on one side.  I'd like to pick up some of these super-drying cloths from Trader Joe's.

I love the fact that my cat will not be harmed by jumping in the tub right after I clean (she loves the tub faucet and the water in the tub near the faucet!), as everything is so safe.  When I used chlorine-based stuff or things with ingredients I couldn't pronounce, I had to keep the door closed before she could jump in the tub, and open the window specifically to get rid of the toxic fumes.

I've been inspired to make these changes by the No Impact Man:
http://noimpactman.typepad.com/

I have other plans to de-tox my environment, too.

I had a similar reaction as Urrisgirl so did some searching online and came up with this great solution (that really works!)

"Most people can hardly believe that all I use for cleaning the oven is baking soda and water, and that the project requires no scrubbing. I suppose we have all been so brainwashed by household products companies that we mistakenly believe we require powerful chemicals for cleaning. But most people don't know that simple household minerals such as baking soda can clean like magic. The key is using enough of the mineral.

To clean my oven, I sprinkle baking soda all over the bottom until it is covered completely with about 1/4 of an inch of baking soda. Then, using a clean spray bottle, I spray the baking soda with water until the baking soda is thoroughly damp but not flooded. After that, this lazy cleaner goes off and does other things. When I think of it, I dampen the baking soda again if it is drying out. Before I go to bed, I do that again. When I wake up in the morning, the baking soda can effortlessly be scooped out of the oven with a sponge, bringing all the grime with it. That's it! The only downside is that you need to rinse out the white residue left by the baking soda, but it sure beats Turkey à La Oven cleaner because there are no fumes!"

Tip from apartmenttherapy:

http://www.apartmenttherapy.com/ny/how-to/how-to-unclog-your-drain-without-toxins-020259

Quote
1. Get as much water out of the area as possible
2. Pour 1/2 cup of baking soda down the drain
3. Follow with 1 cup of HOT white vinegar
4. Wait 10 minutes or until it stops bubbling and wash with boiling water
5. Use a plunger if necessary

Slightly larger proportions than I've used before.
