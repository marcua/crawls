There are some rules about building a Sukkah. as I recall, gee, it has been such a long time for me, the minimum height of the walls should be approximately three feet; the Sukkah must be at least twenty-six inches long and twenty-six inches wide; the walls may not be higher than thirty feet. The material used for building also have some rules: the walls may be made of almost any material, but there must be a roof—a S'khakh or Skhakh—not sure of the spelling. It must be made of organic materials—something that grows from the ground— like branches or I remember a lot of evenly spaced bamboo poles.

The thing I remember most vividly are the taffy, candy or caramel apples on a stick. Now, I would probably need permission from my dentist to bite into one!  :roll:

You know, we could still celebrate the holiday without all the trappings. After all none of us are traditionalists. It would still be fun just to be together to share memories and the joy of being with each other.

This is awesome. 

I think the suggestion to get together and build a sukkah is a fantastic idea, and a great way to spend an afternoon (pizza + beer + construction project = fun), provided that Miri is able to get permission.  As far as the actual building of the thing goes, I have some experience with this, although in the past I have either built sukkahs from pre-fab kits or from pvc pipes (the thing doesn't need to be made of wood, just covered with vegetation -- skhakh, as a pervious post rightly said).  I am happy to look into the options for this, but the thing that would be essential is a car (a trip would need to be made to -I'm guessing- Long Island or Brooklyn for a kit, or to Home Depot for do-it-yourself building materials), which I don't have.

Now that I think about it, though, the timing of building it would be difficult, since Yom Kippur is on a Monday and Sukkot starts the following Friday night -- that doesn't leave a convenient day on which to build it.  It would be possible to build on a weeknight that week, but only if the lighting situation was such that we could see what what we are doing (and of course, not make too much noise). 

It may be that this is just too ambitious for now, and it will be best to just try to use a sukkah at the Astoria Center or one of the synagogues in Long Island City, or else just forgo the sukkah entirely and have a dinner at someone's apartment (in which case I would offer to host such an event).
