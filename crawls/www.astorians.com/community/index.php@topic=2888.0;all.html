My friend's mother lives in a rent-controlled (not rent-stabilised) apartment in Clinton Hill, and has been there since 1968.  Her rent is $150/month.

According to the NYC Rent Guidelines Board, the maximum change per every two years for a rent-controlled apartment is still 7.5%, according to the Fact Sheet #1: Rent Control and Rent Stabilization; however, that 7.5% raise is only up to the Maximum Base Rent limit.  The Maximum Base Rent Program that is used to calculate rent-controlled rents is explained in Fact Sheet #22.

In general, that website has a lot of good information, although it can be a bit tricky to navigate.  There's even a stabilised building list by zip code, although the site warns it is not complete.

Re: Yuppies in Astoria?
« Reply #17 on: August 10, 2006, 09:37:12 AM »
Jane, what you may be seenig is the roots of gentrification, because it surely is happening here.  I wouldn't use a silly term like "yuppies" -- but it surely isn't working-class folk moving in here when studio apartments are going for upwards of $800.

I got a bad jolt the other day by using one of those real-estate appraisal websites, which shows housing prices for any given street address.  I typed in my address, and on my block, which is all "two story with a basement" rowhouses, the average price of a house is $750,000.  Back in 1995, a friend of mine bought a house just north of Astoria Boulevard for $150,000.  In the past decade housing and rental costs have gone through the roof here.  Again, back in mid-90s, I had friends here renting two-bedroom apartments for $800 -- this was the going rate for a long time.  My first apartment, in 1997, was a one bedroom near 23rd and Ditmars for $600/month.

You're going to continue seeing plenty of working-class folks for awhile, as many of them are lucky enough to own the houses they live in.  But as time goes on, you're going to notice more and more younger professional white folks moving in -- I've noticed it a lot in the past five years, and a radical change in the type of stores/restaurants/bars that are moving in.  Places like 718, or even a Starbucks, wouldn't have existed here 10 years ago.

I remember a few years back, when that worker got shot in the Trade Fair supermarket, there was a hue and cry in the neighborhood that this happened "because all of those people from Manhattan are moving in here."  I had a few neighbors express that sentiment and read it in a few of the news stories at that time.  (Turns out one of his ex-coworkers was doing a simple robbery, and he was in the wrong place at the wrong time.)

It's instructive to look at places like Park Slope and Williamsburg.  A friend grew up in Park Slope in the early 70s -- it was basically a gritty, crime-ridden neighborhood.  Fast forward to the mid-80s, when that neighborhood really caught on, and he couldn't afford to live there anymore.  I remember Williamsburg in the late 80s and early 90s -- it was a ragged little Polish neighborhood, rough-edged to say the least.  Now?  I couldn't afford a closet there.

Gentrification rarely happens overnight -- it generally takes a decade or so to truly kick in.  Real estate prices skyrocket, and only people willing to pay very high rents start moving in.  Businesses spring up to cater to these new crowds.  As real estate goes up, people with homes, especially older people, consider selling them -- which is a no-brainer when you buy a house in 1968 for $35K and someone offers to buy it in 2006 for $700K.  You'll have a core working-class neighborhood for awhile, but by simple attrition, this will whittle down over the course of years.  Next time you're in Park Slope, walk around and realize that in 1975, that area was a hard inner-city neighborhood.

I think when you hear people saying stuff like "all the yuppies are moving here," they're telling you that the people they know who are moving here do, indeed, have money to burn and are quoting rents that may appear good to (insane) Manhattanites, but are far above the neighborhood standard -- much more than the people already here are paying.  And a depressing glance at any bulletin board in a real estate agency window verifies that.

Will the oncoming national real-estate slow down affect this?  Christ, I hope so.  But I would be surprised if it wiped out the boom completely.  From what I've seen, gentrification works over the course of years in any given neighborhood, it's not an overnight transformation.  And it's surely happening here, for better or worse!
