I'd offer one additional thought here.

There are some doctors that will refuse treatment because they believe that it is more harmful to the patient. For example, there are risks associated with the morning after pill, or they may be personally convinced through their own research that it poses and abnormal risk to the patient. I'm not saying this as an excuse for doctors who won't treat because their pastor told them not to.

In this case it seems equally wrong that the law should force a doctor to engage in a treatment that she deems is unhealthy or dangerous. Medicine should be something that is based on relationship, trust, and individual patient care, not law. So I'd equally be concerned about a "response" to "religious medicine" that instead replaces it by "legal medicine" which I think is the exact same beast just in secular clothing. In other words I think it could set a dangerous precedence if we were to start forcing doctors to administer certian drugs just at the request of the pateint. Since the drug companies have huge influence in Washington I see this as a slippery slope.

The bottom line is that the thing we are complaining about isn't religion, and isn't law. It's doctors who put some external arbitrary "rule system" above the health of their patient. Either forcing a doctor to administer a drug, or forcing a doctor not to are equally bad in my opinion, and are the same beast just in different clothing. Either one prevents doctors from "practicing" medicine, so this is a sticky issue for me.

I'd prefer that doctors have choice, and that patients have choice (more than one doctor to choose from), to the alternative of doctors not having choice (or patients having no choice). I agree with you that if indeed there's only one doctor and one pharmacy within driving distance then that's a problem, but I don't think the majority of the U.S. is this way, though I could be wrong. I don't want my doctor to be a robot for extremist religion, nor do I want the state practicing forced medicine.

And, just because the FDA says a drug is "safe and effective" doesn't mean anything. Seems to me that good doctors are the ones that might hesitate to prescribe some new medications; and guess what, those doctors that don't just follow the recommendations of the FDA sometimes save their patients life, or permanant health damage.

I'm not meaning to sound like I'm excusing extremist dogmatic medicine, but I'm just trying to state that this is a difficult issue for me because the opposite extreme is "legal medicine" i.e. medicine dictated by lawyers and politicians - who I also do not trust with my health.

My point is that just because it's a legal drug doesn't make its prescription compulsory. We may disagree with the doctor in this case, but the alternative is equally as scary to me. Like putting one's "faith" in blind and arbitrary dogma, putting one's faith in the FDA is equally as dangerous and irresponsible IMHO.

All doctors are forcing their beliefs on patients, this is the nature of a free society. Go to any doctor and ask them for a prescription of Codeine or Morphine and you'll see what I mean. Both are legal drugs. Some will prescribe it, some won't.

What solutions would you recommend that are more balanced, or that make this issue less muddy? I can't think of any legal solution that preserves the doctor's right to practice medicine on an individual basis, and yet also preserves the patients right to good healthcare.

I wouldn't disagree that I think women should have access to the morning after pill, however, I have this nagging and conflicting belief that doctors should be able to practice medicine as freely as possible. I don't trust the FDA, lawyers, and politicians to know what is best for my health; I prefer a one on one trust based relationship with a doctor who knows my individual health needs. I want to be sure that doctors have a lot of room to practice.

Also, I don't think this is really a problem with beliefs. Everyone has beliefs no matter what their background. I don't think anyone is against beliefs here, because I'd be comfortable going to a doctor that believes all the evidence out there that high blood pressure is bad for me. A doctor that practices medicine with no beliefs doesn't exist. The problem we have is with doctors that practice with blind arbitrary beliefs - i.e. they believe everything their pastor tells them, or they believe everything the FDA approves is healthy. These kinds of doctors are dangerous and are not looking out for the best interest of their patients.

The question for me is: how do you weed out such "bad" doctors without imposing on the freedom of the GOOD doctors to practice medicine in a creative and individualized way?

In this particular case it's easy for me to say, well give them the drugs! However, the way that law works in the United States is that when you create a law you also set a precedence for other cases that follow. This question involves more than just a raped woman's rights, but also the rights of the doctor to practice medicine in a way she feels is most beneficial to her patients. And also the patient's right to know that his doctor isn't practicing law, but good medicine that is tailored to his needs.

My issues with this debate are philosophical and long-range. I personally think the morning after pill should be available over the counter. But I also "believe" that the right of a patient to have input on their treatment balances with the right of the doctor to prescribe said treatment.

Beliefs don't get in the way of good medicine, it's arbitrary, blind belief that does (because "God" said so, because the "President" says so, because the FDA says so, because a lawyer says so etc) - and those kinds of unfounded beliefs are independent of religion, culture, or gender.

Quote from: Cary119 on July 27, 2006, 12:11:31 PM
What if a patient has a standing DNR order and a doctor ignores that for his so-called religious beliefs?

You make an excellent point. In this case I'd side with the right of the patient to not be resuscitated.

But in the example you bring up we are talking about the patient's rights to not be treated against their will. To compare apples to apples this would be like a doctor forcing a young pregnant girl to take the morning after pill even though the girl wants to keep the baby. Nobody would disagree. This is a different argument however than what we're talking about when it comes to forcing a doctor to perform a treatment or prescribe a drug he feels is not in the best interest of the patient. In other words, just because it may be your right to not get forced treatment from your doctor, this doesn't automatically mean the reverse; that if you want some treatment that you may force the doctor to provide it.

For example, what if a patient without cancer wants a prescription for chemo therapy and the doctor won't prescribe that? Is that wrong? Or what if a patient wants oxycotton (think heroin), should the doctor do whatever the patient says?

I don't think anyone would disagree that the patient is the most important person in the room, but that isn't without limits. I think medicine should be a partnership between doctor and patient, and in the above case I hope the doctor's beliefs would prevent him from giving into the patient's desire to harm herself.

This isn't only a patient's rights issue, it has other implications that come with it. Where exactly do you draw the line? Just with abortion, and just with that one drug, but then with any other drugs it's okay for the doctors interest to override the patient's interest?

This argument assumes that all drugs are safe, and that all "religious" doctors only are acting on unfounded, irrational religious belief by not prescribing the drug. If I accept that all drugs are safe, and that all religious doctors are irrational then I can agree with you and I'd feel safer by making some law that forces them to be more rational, but since I don't accept that all drugs are safe, or that all religious doctors are irrational I must balance my patient rights with the right of the doctor to not prescribe a drug to me in my best interest.

I only say this to point out that I think this is a very difficult issue, and by forcing doctors to prescribe a medication simply for the asking you introduce a host of other problems into the equation. There are tons of drugs that are labeled as "safe" by the FDA that kill people. Just because it's legal and I want it doesn't mean my doctor should have to prescribe something to me against his better judgement.

The right to not get forced treatment is not the same as the doctor's right to not give it. I'm cautious to call for a legal precedence that says that doctors have to do whatever patients want them to.

Here is the hippocratic oath, by the way. Is it still meaningful to doctors now?


Hippocratic OathModern Version

I swear to fulfill, to the best of my ability and judgment, this covenant:

I will respect the hard-won scientific gains of those physicians in whose steps I walk, and gladly share such knowledge as is mine with those who are to follow.

I will apply, for the benefit of the sick, all measures [that] are required, avoiding those twin traps of overtreatment and therapeutic nihilism.

I will remember that there is art to medicine as well as science, and that warmth, sympathy, and understanding may outweigh the surgeon's knife or the chemist's drug.

I will not be ashamed to say "I know not," nor will I fail to call in my colleagues when the skills of another are needed for a patient's recovery.

I will respect the privacy of my patients, for their problems are not disclosed to me that the world may know. Most especially must I tread with care in matters of life and death. If it is given me to save a life, all thanks. But it may also be within my power to take a life; this awesome responsibility must be faced with great humbleness and awareness of my own frailty. Above all, I must not play at God.

I will remember that I do not treat a fever chart, a cancerous growth, but a sick human being, whose illness may affect the person's family and economic stability. My responsibility includes these related problems, if I am to care adequately for the sick.

I will prevent disease whenever I can, for prevention is preferable to cure.

I will remember that I remain a member of society, with special obligations to all my fellow human beings, those sound of mind and body as well as the infirm.

If I do not violate this oath, may I enjoy life and art, respected while I live and remembered with affection thereafter. May I always act so as to preserve the finest traditions of my calling and may I long experience the joy of healing those who seek my help.


Written in 1964 by Louis Lasagna, Academic Dean of the School of Medicine at Tufts University, and used in many medical schools today.
