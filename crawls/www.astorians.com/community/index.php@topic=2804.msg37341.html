Hi,

I happened to be browsing at Bluestockings yesterday, and I came across this book: Rebel Bookseller.
http://www.rebelbookseller.com/

Sounds like just the book you need to read!  It was written by Andy Laties, who currently runs Vox Pop in Brooklyn.  He self-published the book, so I don't know if it's being sold in big mega-stores like Barnes & Noble, but you can definitely find it at Bluestockings.

The website also links to his blog.  I only skimmed through some of these posts here and the blog, but here's the gist of what I understand:

~You can't start up a "middle-of-the-road" typical-looking indie bookstore.  It has to be specialized in some way for people to want to keep coming. 

~Laties has opened other bookstores in the past, and made alliances with powerful non-profit organizations/institutions, like museums.  He would essentially be the museum's affiliate gift shop, so the museum's patrons would always flock to him.  He also sold non-book, high-markup goods as well in his bookstores.

You might even be able to contact Mr. Laties, since he's operating a bookstore in Brooklyn now. 

Hope this helps.

To all who have been part of this thread:
     It is good to see the interest in having a bookstore come to our neighborhood.  It's been an intellectual fantasy of mine for a few years, and I'm beginning to realize that it won't happen unless we all do something more than just talk about it in forums.

To that end, I want to move this forward into reality...I've been sending out surveys to friends and colleagues to glean their reading habits, an am designing a postcard to pass out to commuters at the subway stops to gauge the community interest and support (outside of ourselves ) for a bookstore.

At first glance, it seems to be a daunting task - the logistics, the money, the staff, etc.  But if enough of us can get involved with each of our experiences/strengths/contacts to put together a proposal (I would be asking for a time commitment, not a money commitment) this ideal bookstore that we have in our heads can become a reality.

So the question I have for each of you are: what are you committed to doing to make this happen?  Let me know, as the more people we have behind this, the better.  Let me know your thoughts and your interest in being part of this.

I currently work as a publicist at a major publishing house and it is tough.  I have thought about opening a bookstore in this area, but the start-up costs are astounding and I work in publishing.  But what makes an indepenedent bookstore successful, here are some things I've learned from various booksellers across the country:

Know your customer, really know them.  Know their names, their preferences and most important their email address.  Which leads me to the newsletter.  You want an extensive email database to send out information. 

You're not going to get famous authors, there are too many established indies and the chains in the city for you to even think of getting a big name.  Don't rely on this.  You can do authorless events.  There is a great childrens book called Fancy Nancy.  Hold a best Fancy outfit contest for girls aged 4-12 and they win a book.  You have to be creative with your events.  If down the road you do get a big name, sell tickets that include the price of the book.  Publisher's love this because we can quantify how successful an event is.

And I totally recommend alcohol.  It loosens people up and they end up buying more.  Pair it up with a wine book or a travel through Tuscany book.

You have to be the go to place for people to talk about what is going on in the community.

BOOK GROUPS!!!!  I know one indie that has over 200 book groups in their store.  You will need one staff person to organize this, or someone from the community.  You can offer suggestions, a place to meet and the possibility of getting in touch with the author if it is a mid-list or debut author.  And even better, you know what they like b/c of their book group preference and can suggest other reads.

You are lucky enough to live 20 minutes away from all of the major publishing houses.  Make friends.  Call their customer service numbers and see if there is someone that will talk to you about starting a new bookstore.  Call other indies from around the country - Schwartz (the midwest), Tattered Cover (THE indie bookstore, located in Denver), BookPeople (austin), etc.

Search for and join NAIBA - it's a regional organization for independent booksellers in the North Atlantic.

Don't be a literature snob.  There is a reason why Mary Higgins Clark, Janet Evanovich and Nora Roberts are on the bestseller list - THEY SELL.   Get a subscription to Publishers Weekly to see what is doing well and what trends the industry is facing.  To that point mass market romance readers are rabid readers - up to 6 to 8 paperbacks a month, we like them.

Publicity - this is a great story... pitch it.  And annouce every event or sale you have.  Make friends with local reporters, create a media list.  Publicist will ask for this when they call your store to book an author, if you don't have it, it means more work for us and we don't like that.

I'm getting wordy, so here a couple more and then I'm out:

It would be lovely to have an actual book store (e.g. one that sells books one might like to read) in Astoria.  I would be willing to curate a reading series and/or the poetry section.

Info. about me is here: http://www.marklamoureux.com

Based on friends I've had who have run independent bookstores and kept them going, it is more or less essential that they have an online component, since most of your business will come by way to that avenue.  At this point (sadly) an indy bookstore storefront is pretty much icing on the cake of other distro. components.

As an indy, it is important to develop a core audience, and a sense of community--it is essential to develop specialties and a relationship with local readers.  With all due respect to danielle1028, trying to run an indy by kowtowing to mass-markets is a fool's errand--these titles can be obtained at bus stations, airports, and places like Amazon.com at deep discounts that an Indy cannot compete with.
