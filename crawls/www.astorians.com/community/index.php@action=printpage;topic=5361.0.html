Title: Young whippersnappers more narcissistic?
Post by: Victorine on February 27, 2007, 11:42:40 AM
http://news.yahoo.com/s/ap/20070227/ap_on_re_us/self_centered_students (http://news.yahoo.com/s/ap/20070227/ap_on_re_us/self_centered_students)

According to the article above, the very fabric of our society is being undermined by young people and their self-centered ways.

What do people think about this?  On the one hand, as we discussed a little bit before in the American Idol thread, certain aspects of popular culture do seem to encourage (or do they reflect?) greater levels of self-centredness, valuing of fame, etc.

On the other hand, it's a well-documented phenomenon that older generations have a tendency to think that younger generations are morally bankrupt and vastly inferior.

On the third hand, the study does seem to use a set of semi-objective measures (however much self-evaluation can be objective, which I don't think it can 100%) to measure these things.  But could the measures being used be calibrated according to an older generation's set of values that sees different things as markers of different qualities?  I.e., not that the younger generation is objectively more narcissistic, but that it engages in more behaviors that the people who designed the study--and who presumably have a different (not better or worse) worldview, and a different age-based social code for signifying certain qualities about themselves and reading them in others--view as signifying narcissism.

And you could also, for example, chalk up increasing belief in being "special" over the past century to a changing economy--e.g. fewer of us are among throngs laboring in factories or fields, and increased professionalisation of the work force,  hence more people feel more different from one another, and more autonomy in daily activities. 

I dunno.  I'm interested in people's thoughts.

Post by: Harlan on February 27, 2007, 02:38:31 PM
Quote from: Alexa on February 27, 2007, 02:24:35 PM
These measures are incredibly biased by both the measure creators and the respondents...validity of the measure is highly questionable.

Interesting. The tests are supposed to be measures of narcissism. As you say, they're doubtless biased in such a way that they're not perfectly measuring narcissism. (I assume you do agree that narcissism is at least a reasonable approximation to a personality attribute that we might want to be interested in.) Whatever they're measuring is presumably at least somewhat like that personality trait, however. So I'm not sure that the measure creators can necessarily be faulted too much for trying to get at least some useful data. A more interesting question to me is whether the respondents react to the questions in the survey the same way. Maybe society has changed our understanding of concepts like self-esteem such that a participant who lived in the cultural context of 1975 might respond one way to a question about the way they feel about themselves, while the same person, with the same internal state, might respond differently to the same question now? That is, perhaps the language -- the meaning of the terms used in the test -- has changed in such a way that it's not functionally the same test any more?

In short, because it has hard data on how the generations really differ. Most books on generations throw around ideas about social trends and pop culture, but don't have much data on the actual characteristics of people from different generations. This book has the data -- it summarizes thirteen years of my research on the responses of 1.3 million young people. I found research reports on children and college students who completed questionnaires measuring everything from anxiety to sexual behavior. Many of these questionnaires have been used since the 1950s or 1960s, allowing me to see how today's young people differ from Baby Boomers and previous generations.
