iPods are wonderful.  With podcasting and audiobooks you can turn that subway commute into a highly productive trip by keeping up on your news or other programs that you might miss on the train.  Not all of us iPod people are just listening to music although it is an amazingly freeing thing to have thousands of songs you enjoy at your fingertips.  Really, wouldn't you have that pod person quietly enjoying their whatever than blasting a boom box or talking loudly on a mobile phone?

Yay for mobile phones.  A great way to keep in touch with people.  Just have the willpower to turn it off.  Strategy tip.  If you don't want idle chit chat, let it go to voice mail and immediately check the voice mail.  If it's an emergency, you are notified.  Otherwise, call 'em back.  There are always rude and unaware people who will find a way to bother others with electronic pestering or inappropriate behavior, but let's not throw the baby out with the bathwater.

Go technology!  Quiet lovers, throw your support behind the portable cone of silence like in Get Smart.

I have to agree with Telesphoros.  I find nothing bothersome or noisy about people listening to their ipods and even derive some vicarious satisfaction when I see someones face enraptured with whatever they're listening to.  Simple pleasure seems to be in short supply these days, especially during that long and trying subway/train/bus commute.  Personally I have no desire to "experience the moment" or breathe deep the air while being packed into a tin can like a bunch of sardines on the way to yet another grind of a day. And as far as initiating conversation with the other passengers (for example you see someone reading an obscure book you've once read) is most of the time not only intellectually unstimulating but extremely problematic, as it seems the most you get for your efforts is a short (albeit polite) answer and a look as if you've grown a second head. :-)(-:

  Internet radio (a thing that has been around for quite some time now) has grown in popularity by leaps and bounds since the advent of the iPod.  And when I'm looking for other sources of news from all around the globe in order to get a more balanced perspective of what's going on, this is where I go.  I like the spoken word; not only is my eyesight shot, but I genuinely like listening to other people's voices.

 OTOH, artgirl does have a point about the safety issues involved in not hearing what's going on around oneself especially in light of the recent trend of people getting robbed (which even resulted in the death for one teen in Brooklyn recently) over this coveted and portable technology.

  Cell phones are great and have the potential of offering people freedom from the incumbent service provider.  But there is a time and a place for everything and nothing annoys me more than when someone that I'm trying to have a heart-to-heart with answers the phone, starts chatting away and totally ignores me!
  Some people also don't seem to realize that when they get bad reception on their phone that it doesn't help at all (and more probably makes it worse) to raise their voice in response.  If lowering the voice or moving around doesn't help, a simple "I can't hear you now, I'll call you later" would.  Problem solved.

  Mobile phones, televison, radio, printed material, etc. can all be used to greatly expand our awareness of the world around us and the people who inhabit it as long as we are the ones that are in control of how we interact with this technology and don't allow ourselves to become slaves to it or to the "bad" people who use it for their own questionable ends.  Simple rules of etiquette are in order.

@Telesphoros: LOL at the "Cone of Silence" reference.  Shoe-Fones 4eva!
