I also found this as well.  I think this might address your particular situation better:

http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/faq/repairs.html#long

"How long should we be without our refrigerator until the landlord replaces it?

There is no specific regulation on the amount of time a stabilized tenant may be without his or her refrigerator as long as the owner makes a good faith effort to replace that service. You should be aware of the following:

The landlord must repair a broken or faulty appliance that was provided in the lease when you rented the unit or replace it with a unit of the same type or quality if it cannot be repaired for no extra rent charge. If you allow him to put in a new unit, you may be charged a "1/40th," where 1/40th the cost, or 2.5%, of the improved unit is added permanently to your monthly rent.

So, you may wish to ask for a replacement unit of the same quality or for the current fridge to be repaired if you don't want to pay additional rent. The owner must give you a unit that is the same quality as the one provided for in the lease, not a lesser unit.


The lack of a refrigerator may constitute a violation of your warranty of habitability and/or a reduction of services, so you may want to file a reduction in services complaint with the NYS Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR), the agency which administers the rent laws.
For additional information on how to deal with getting repairs accomplished, visit our Apartment Guide section on Maintenance advice."

THE ANSWER TO ALL YOUR QUESTIONS IS YES AND NO....................

If your apartment is NOT rent stabilized the landlord cannot change the terms of the lease (increase the rent) until the lease expires. A lease works BOTH WAYS.

"Rent controlled" means monthly rent remains the same, no increase at all as long as you live there.

WRONG!

Rent controlled apartments get from 7.5% to 13% increase each and every year since 1972. They also have to pay a per room share of the cost of the fuel to heat the entire building. These apartments are lived in by people who lived in them when the stabilization laws were passed. (READ: elderly) Once the controlled tenant moves out (or dies) the apartment must be registered as a stabilized apartment, and then may follow the stabilization laws. If the landlord does not register the apartment properly with DHCR, the rent can revert to the one from the previous tenant until the apartment is registered properly.

[OT]
There is an apartment in my building that was not registered properly with DHCR. The current tenant filed overcharge papers with DHCR and was awarded over $30,000. She will not have to pay any rent for the next 3+ years until the award monies are used in their entirety for her rent. As a result many of the newly stabilized tenants in my building are doing the same. DHCR will go back 3 years to adjust any fraud in your current rent. If you live in a building with more than 6 units, be sure your apartment is registered. If it isn't, file overcharge papers ASAP.
[/OT]

Rent stabilized apartments get from 2.5% to 3.5% increase per year or 3.5% to 5% increase for a new 2-year lease.

Check this out from the NYC Rent Guidelines Board site:

http://www.housingnyc.com/html/guidelines/orders/order38.html

"ADJUSTMENT FOR RENEWAL LEASES  (APARTMENTS)

Together with such further adjustments as may be authorized by law, the annual adjustment for renewal leases for apartments shall be:

Where heat is provided or required to be provided to a dwelling unit by an owner from a central or individual system at no charge to the tenant, the adjustments are as follows:

For a one-year renewal lease commencing on or after October 1, 2006 and on or before September 30, 2007:                 4.25%

For a two-year renewal lease commencing on or after October 1, 2006 and on or before September 30, 2007:                 7.25%

Where heat is neither provided nor required to be provided to a dwelling unit by an owner from a central or individual system, the adjustments are as follows:

For a one-year renewal lease commencing on or after October 1, 2006 and on or before September 30, 2007:                  3.75%

For a two-year renewal lease commencing on or after October 1, 2006 and on or before September 30, 2007:                 6.75%

These adjustments shall also apply to dwelling units in a structure subject to the partial tax exemption program under Section 421a of the Real Property Tax Law, or in a structure subject to Section 423 of the Real Property Tax Law as a Redevelopment Project."
