Quote from: kungfoofool on January 23, 2007, 03:37:07 PM
the study was funded by the Joyce Foundation, an organization that is obviously supportive of gun control.

It makes perfect sense to me that people who are interested in increasing controls on guns would fund such research. But that doesn't make the study misleading automatically. Just because clean air studies for example are funded by people who care about air quality doesn't make it misleading.

Quote from: TRX on January 23, 2007, 04:07:34 PM
I expect there is a lot of rain, where there are a lot of umbrellas.
But I would not expect umbrella ownership control to reduce chances of rain.

But reducing roads might reduce car accidents.

You argument here isn't making a fair comparison. In your analogy:
Rain = Violence
Umbrellas = Guns

Which is how you arrive at the faulty conclusion that reducing umbrellas will not reduce the chances of rain, I agree. You then conclude that reducing guns will not reduce violence. The assumption is that violence is a constant (like rain) that cannot be controlled, and that guns are an independent variable that is unrelated. I don't think guns are entirely an independent variable here.

Guns are equipment (tools) that can be used for a lot of other purposes other than killing people, this is clear. However, when people misuse them to kill people, it makes sense for everyone to limit the access to that equipment in order to reduce the number of people killed as a result of those people who misuse them. I don't think this is a logical fallacy. If the goal is to reduce violence, then clearly guns are not the only tool used so only going after guns doesn't make sense. If the goal is to reduce gun violence, then by reducing access to guns, it seems reasonable to believe you could also reduce the use of those tools.

The classic counter-argument to gun control goes something like this: "Guns don't kill people, people kill people, so don't take away guns from responsible people just because a few people misuse them." Then you have a few people that go a step further and say "if everyone owned a gun, there would be less violence." That first argument I can deal with, the second is propaganda I've never been convinced on, yet I've heard it put forward as completely rational more than once.

I'll put forward another analogy that I believe is more fair to both sides of this issue.

Cars are equipment. If everyone were to be given free license to drive a car I would reason that more people would be killed. I admit this is no fault of the cars, and the cars are not the problem (as guns are not the problem, people are the problem). However, I believe it can be stated within reason that by limiting access to cars, you also reduce the number of people that would be killed otherwise. By reducing access to driving through testing, age requirements, vision requirements, etc - the number of deaths by cars is reduced. If reducing deaths by car accidents is a goal, then limiting access to cars is a reasonable way to reduce those deaths. It would be absurd to argue that by giving everyone a car and license to drive that accidents would go down.

Cars are not an independent variable. People use cars to kill people. While it's true that some people might argue to get rid of cars altogether I'm not making the claim that guns should be gotten rid of altogether. But in the same way I'm in support of car-control, I'm also in support of gun-control (not gun abolition) - and for the exact same reasons.
