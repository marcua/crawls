Cosmic - I understand what you're saying, but I have to respectfully disagree.  This is only my own personal experience, My daughter reads and does math far, far above grade level.   When she was in a GenEd class she was bored and acted out to get attention because she wasn't being challenged.
 
Now that she is in a G&T class with an accellerated curriculum and peers who are at the same academic level as she is, she is thriving.
 
The word "gifted" is perhaps the problem.   My daughter is no "better" or "more blessed" than anyone else - it's just that she is advanced academically.  As such, she is best / most appropriately educated among classmates who also learn quickly and by a teacher who understands the best way to get children like her interested and movitivated.
 
I liken it to sports.  You have some children who can run very fast.  You have others who may be great dancers.  There are still others who have great hand-eye coordination.   Each will excel at different things.  Forcing them all to play exactly the same sport in exactly the same way is doing a disservice to them all.  Some will be bored, others frustrated.  If each is given appropriate training, designed to foster their strengths and improve on their weaknesses, they will all excel. 
 
That's what I want for all children in our district - the appropriate education that will allow them ALL to excel.   For some that means G&T classes, for some that means CTT and additional support.  In the end, they should all succeed.

I feel as a lifelong Astoria resident, an alum of PS 122 and an educator who has studied gifted education, I must make a few points.
Let me preface by saying that ALL students deserve quality education.  ALL students deserve good schools and highly trained teachers who work together to meet every student's individual needs.  By advocating for the gifted, we are not ignoring or diminishing the needs of every other student.
It is unfortunate to read some of these comments that suggest that people feel that gifted students think they are entitled to "more."  No one is arguing that they are entitled to more, but that they are entitled to a proper placement in a classroom that meets their needs.  Gifted students have many emotional needs and they benefit from being in a program with teachers who understand their specific needs.  It is not about boredom or accelerating the curriculum.  When we are stressing the importance of every child making progress and growing, how can we cut gifted programs that allow for gifted students to grow and make progress that they would not be able to achieve in the regular classroom.  The idea that if there is not enough room for all then it should be phased out is not a solution.  Make room!  It is the city's responsibility to provide appropriate education and meet the needs of all its students.  You wouldn't cut services for special needs students because not everyone received this help.  We would fight to make sure that every student with a special need received the resources they needed. 
I also want to clarify something about 122.  My family has been in Astoria for about fifty years.  My parents attended 122 in the 1960's.  It has always serviced elementary students.  It is a k-5 school!  In the late 1980's the district designated 122 as the home for the gifted middle school population.  Thus was born the Academy for the Intellectually Gifted at PS 122.  Students never stayed beyond elementary school.  They graduated and went to IS 141 (their zoned middle school) or other middle school programs.  Not because the gifted program took up space in 122, but because 122 was never a middle school.  Over the years the gifted program has expanded to include all grades and now there is a K-8 gifted population at the school.  But the school itself has never been a k-8 school.  The reason why it is the only middle school in district 30 that is solely for the gifted population is because it is a Middle School that was created for the district's gifted students.
Furthermore, I don't know why anyone would be in favor of destroying a successful program.  It is one of the top middle schools in NY State and out performs other gifted programs throughout the city. 
By changing the school to a k-8 school, the population would increase greatly - leading to overcrowding.  As a parent of a young child, I don't know if I would want to send my preschooler or kindergartener to a school with 500 middle school students. 
PS 122 is a great elementary school.  The Academy for the Intellectually Gifted at 122 is a successful program.  Let's preserve both the elementary school and the most successful gifted middle school program this city has.
