The problem with arguing against shipping jobs overseas is that you're also then increasing prices of everything.  If we didn't have immigrant labor (legal or illegal), cheap manufacturing in other countries, or cheaper workforce options, everything would COST MORE.  Americans want things cheaply and to maintain low prices means to move jobs and services to where things are cheap. 

It's the principle free markets and, unfortunately, it seems that most Americans don't want to accept that.  It is all interconnected.  We expect/demand lower prices, yet higher wages.  Companies can't let their margins get cut by doing either (or both) of the above, so they have to find other ways to provide that; with an obvious bias to lower prices.

Even these "cheap goods" bring jobs.  Wal-Mart (while being a horrible employer) has provided many people with jobs.  The shipping companies that bring products into the country and to the stores have brought more jobs, the amount of people needed to market those products increase, etc.  While it might be frustrating to call an airline and have to talk with someone in India that is tough to understand; but that "inconvenience" to you and cost savings to the airline has allowed them to buy more planes, have more routes, and keep fares down.  If they kept those jobs in the US, they would likely not be able to do those things.

I grew up in a town where just about everyone's parents worked for GM/GE/Delphi/etc., made good money, and had great bennies.  They were able to specialize in one thing and never had to worry about learning new trades, getting degrees, or constantly wondering if their jobs were secure.  But, those days are over.  Complacency hurt the American auto industry and everything with it.  The people who never had or cared to learn other trades were left hung out to dry.

It's the same now.  Thinking that we can keep jobs from going overseas, yet still get high salaries and low prices, is futile.  Globalization is inevitable as information and technology expand.  The only way to combat it is to diversify your talents by learning other trades, furthering your education, constantly keeping an eye out for developing trends, and keeping expenses low and a solid savings buffer in case of downturns/lost employment.

It is a vacuum.  As jobs go overseas, new ones are created here.  However, they require more knowledge and, perhaps, different skills.  But, as long as the worker always keeps their "tools" sharp, there will always be a need for them.  We've become a much more "educated" and "skilled" society because of this pressure to continually evolve and the lack of complacency in employment.  Personally, I don't think it's a bad thing.

Para - I agree that what I say and do is somewhat contradictory.  However, it's very personal in more ways that one.  First, I've worked, kept a roof over my head, and a cold beer in my hand (food mighta been in there somewhere) waiting tables and know the difference a good tip can make and, really, the amount of $hit you have to put up with from customers who get a kick out of treating someone like crap that they know can't do anything about it. 

I also know that, personally, it's worth more than $1 or $2 to not have to get on my shoes, put on my coat, and walk the 5-10 blocks one way (or more) to go pick up the food.  Since I wouldn't (obviously, it's why I asked for delivery) go that distance for $1 or $2, I shouldn't expect someone else to without just compensation.

The Democrat in me says, "they need the extra $1 or $2 in tip more than I do.  If I needed it that bad, I'd walk my happy a$$ down there and pick it up myself or just not eat out in the first place."  The Republican in me says, "I worked hard for my pretty crappy pay and shouldn't feel obligated to tip someone else more money because they work a 'crappy' job.  They should get out there and learn new skills or better themselves somehow if they want more money or a better job."

The reality is somewhere in the middle.  Not everyone has the chances I, or most of us, I'm guessing, had growing up to better ourselves, so some extra help is needed and appreciated.  However, it's not our job to keep another afloat if they aren't willing to help themselves.

But, I say that as a, I'd like to think, reasonably intelligent guy (I stopped eating paste, finally) that has a college degree yet works a job that probably pays less than most of these delivery guys, has absolutely no benefits, and requires even less thought.  Then again, what I do with my time while at work (mess with my personal stock portfolio) and the fact that I'm just waiting for another job to come through makes me a little more comfortable with my current situation.  Plus, working for The Man (who might just ship my job overseas) is something I don't enjoy.

I actually experienced this when I was a bartender (not a very good one, but I did just fine where I was) for awhile last year.  The owner up and sold the bar on 3 days notice and, next thing I knew, I was unemployed.  Awesome.  But, it was another skill learned and something else to fall back on, in case of emergency.  I had to pick myself up and go look for another job.  After looking for awhile, I still hadn't found anything so, at that time, I took what I could get.  Sure, I'd prefer to be doing something else, but this job has hidden perks like the ability to mess with my stock portfolio, or the fact I can take vacation whenever I please.  I'm trading off a big paycheck and a "career" job for the ability to teach myself a skill I can always use, no matter what, and the ability to travel as I please.

Too many people think they're owed something; a guaranteed job they love, a cheap place to live in a great neighborhood, praise for all the wonderful things that they do, etc., and it's just not how things really work.  Life is full of struggles, disappointments, and rough patches, but that's what makes it amazing and makes the positives that much sweeter.  If you are constantly trying to better yourself, setting new goals, and never giving up, everything always works out.
