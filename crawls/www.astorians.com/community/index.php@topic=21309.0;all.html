The wheels won't come off the tracks. Derailments usually occur for other reasons.

There are safety mechanisms in place that prevent the trains from exceeding certain speeds. If you look at the tracks you will notice a steel lever powered by a hydraulic mechanism.  Look at them the next time you miss the train, you will see the lever rise up. Then as a train approaches, or if you are looking at a train across the tracks, look at the front part of any car, just in front of the wheels. You will notice a steel trigger that is just at the level of the lever that rises up on the tracks. If the track lever is up, it will trigger the braking mechanism.

Also, train suspension and chassis are engineered so that the train leans during turns, specifcally to prevent it from flipping over. If you have ever taken an Amtrak Acela train, you will notice that at high speeds the train leans. Another factor is that on turns, the tracks themselves are position to NOT be level and to allow for a gradient.
Think of a NASCAR race as the car approaches the turn on the oval, the road tilts. I can go into the physics of it all, but if you search centripetal and centrifugal acceleration with tracks + roads you should find good details.
