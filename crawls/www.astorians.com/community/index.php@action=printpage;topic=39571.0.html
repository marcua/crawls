Rent Stabilization laws dictates your landlord must register the rent with the city. This is your "legal regulated rent". He/She can only raise your rent by the prescribes amount. Anything outside of that is really easy to dispute. If you take him to Housing Court, he will be fined and your rent will be regulated at the legal amount. You do not need a lawyer for this.

Title: Re: Rent stabilized building issues
Post by: yippee1999 on November 15, 2016, 02:48:22 PM
Knowledge is power as they say, and it's a good idea for all tenants in Rent-Stabil apartments to know the rental history of their particular unit, and the term length (expiration date) of the 421(a) tax abatement status afforded to the building/landlord.

Much to my surprise, I found the NYC RGB or DHCR (can't recall exactly which office I contacted....) very helpful in answering questions over the phone, and providing me with hard copy documentation showing when rent-stabilization for my building as a whole would end (and all apartments within then become 'market rate').  I also got a document that listed all former tenants of my particular apartment, the term lengths of their leases, and how much the rent increased each year, and how much the rent increased when one tenant vacated and a new tenant moved in (i.e., 'vacancy lease').  This in turn gave me insight into the Landlord's M.O.  ;-)    I saw that prior to say 2010, the rental increases were very low to none, from one year to the next.  Once the LL realized that Astoria was becoming 'hot', suddenly the rental increases were higher.  And of course, for vacancy leases, the rent was a few hundred more than for the tenants who had vacated.

As others here have said, the law is very clear and you shouldn't need a lawyer for this.  Your LL is probably just trying to 'intimidate' you into submission, but most people who own property are not dumb, and they know they law.  If you show indication that you too know the law, the LL  would then be smart to back down.  So long as your unit truly falls under Rent-Stabil guidelines, then he cannot raise your rent as much as he wants.  He can only raise it as high as the 'Legal Rent' amount determined by the city, for that unit.  However, some landlords charge below the Legal Rent ceiling, at what is referred to as 'Preferential Rent'.  In such an instance, it doesn't matter if the Preferential Rent charged is even Hundreds of Dollars below the maximum Legal Rent amount.  If at the next lease renewal, the LL then decides that he is going to up your rent by Hundreds of Dollars, to bring it up to that maximum Legal Rent amount, then that is within his rights.

So.... find out what is the legal Maximum Rent for your unit.  Find out when the 421(a) Tax Abatement status for your building comes to an end.  And get a record of the rental history for your particular unit.  Good luck!
