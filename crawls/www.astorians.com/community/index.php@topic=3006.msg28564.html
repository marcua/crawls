About as big in total space as the Time Warner Center at Columbus Circle, Silvercup West is one of several huge development projects now planned across the five boroughs.

Developers Alan and Stuart Suna are seeking to build three towers with 2.7 million square feet of studio, office, and residential space on the East River waterfront, just south of the Queensboro Bridge

Following community concerns that the development would be a rich, gated-community, the city's Planning Commission and the local City Council member, Eric Gioia, brokered a deal earlier this summer with the developers to add 150 units of affordable housing, to be located within a half-mile of the site.

A senior fellow at the Manhattan Institute who specializes in development, Julia Vitullo-Martin, said part of Long Island City has been "like a cemetery of broken dreams." "This is one of those cursed sites that looks very good and doesn't quite work. This is a huge development to put on a site that has a history of problems," Ms. Martin said. She said the project's office and residential space could come on the market at a time when demand for real estate had dropped off, and added that she worried what would happen to important low-rise neighborhoods in Western Queens if the waterfront became a wall of glass towers.

------------------------------

GOODBYE, WATERFRONT

Dear Editor,

I am writing to you to tell you that I wish we had a civic association and magazine like yours here in Long Island City.  If we did, perhaps we wouldn't be bombarded right now with high-rises that will crowd us - and price us - out of the neighborhood many of our families have called home for generations.  Over time, we had formed a quiet, tight-knit community of people who knew each other on a first name basis.  We enjoyed our little blue-collar enclave just a stone's throw from the waterfront, and liked the fact that we were always a mere subway stop away from the excitement of the "city." 

Now our lifestyle is threatened by looming towers and big money being forced upon us by the powers that be.  First, it was the monstrous and out-of-character Citicorp Tower, followed a decade later by the beginnings of Queens West.  The current wave of development is threatening to further destroy the area.  We aren't chic, but we currently own the "view."  While other areas of Queens are being downzoned, we are being upzoned.  After the first skyscrapers are built, others will be have to be built taller in order to cash in on the view.  Doesn't the middle class deserve to enjoy the view, too?

Unfortunately, we will always lose out to those with the big bucks, especially when we are governed by a mayor who is a billionaire developer from Boston, and a city council representative, Eric Gioia, who made his family's working class Woodside roots his central campaign theme ("from the neighborhood, for the neighborhood"), yet after being elected, quickly high-tailed it to a luxury waterfront condo at CitiLights in order to better position himself for higher office.  Both of them had electrical power in August.  We didn't.  But that's ok, the Mayor sent us "community assistance" in the form of one Matthew Gorton, who couldn't seem to answer any of our questions or find a way to help us out, and basically just hung around and watched while we stood on line for ice.  Then, a melodramatic Gioia made a public display of anger at Con Ed in order to deflect attention away from his promotion of SilverCup West, which will drain even more power from the unstable grid, but give millionaires nice looks at midtown.  What a way to go!  The legacies of these men are shaping up nicely. 

JPCA, please keep fighting for your communities.  It looks like the working people of LIC may soon end up there.

Mary [mod]

Long Island City
