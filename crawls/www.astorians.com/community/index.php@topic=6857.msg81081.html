Actually, it had a charming character, but like most of the rest of the City, it's being emptied of its uniqueness by a number of factors, most of all by mindless money-grubbing.
First, I want to point out that I love college-educated people; in fact, I taught college for many years. It's great that so many college-educated young folks who are being priced out of Manhattan are living here now, but there is a difference between "crashing" somewhere and making a home. Turning the place into a dorm environment benefits no one, except the greedy landlords.

I've been living in the area for over ten years now, and I'm still wondering what the local politicians actually do. If they cared about the communities they supposedly represent,  they would be proposing legislation to clamp down on illegal rentals and organizing candlelight vigils at City Hall every time the lights went out. Astoria is now the province of the powerless and the just passing through. Who knows, though, the kind of folks who made the neighborhood what it was may yet come back, when they get sick of the suburbs and long for the camaraderie and the richness of life in what was once a real global village.

Lived in Astoria from 1973 to 1993 and this is what I remember:

Filth, trash, garbage worse than it is now.
Culturally dead, nothing going on.
Would never go into Astoria Park.
Entire busses coming into the parking lot of the Park and thousands of people using the pool and leaving trash behind.
No sophistication at all (I could not speak to anyone about art and literature).  Some of you complain about not having a bookstore.  Folks, I remember having a hard time finding the New York Times!!!, and not because they were sold out.
The only place people went to for vacations were the Poconos and Mattituck.  I used to call Mattituck 'Astoria on vacation'.

I am sorry if I sound like a snob but I am here now because I witnessed a positive change.  Yes, it is getting far more crowded and I can't park anywhere, but I would never trade this Astoria for the old one.  This Astoria makes a middle aged woman feel like she is living in a vibrant community where things are happening!  I have met so many interesting people (you have to make the effort) and we chat about theater, books, music, art etc.  In years past, I spent most of my time in Manhattan.  Working there is enough, I don't have to go there for food or entertainment.  I love it here!

Quite frankly, I'm growing tired of the naysayers who keep trying to convince us in vague and doomsday terms that Astoria is going downhill.  And toward what end?  For those who believe that Astoria is facing urban decay, it will always be a self-fulfilling prophecy.  If you have a specific issue that you wish to discuss, such as littering or graffiti, then fine.  Otherwise, what is the point??

As for myself, I love Astoria and my neighborhood in particular because of its diversity - we have all sorts of people in my neighborhood, singles and families, young people and retirees, people from all racial and ethnic backgrounds.  So I would have to disagree that a neighborhood is defined only by "intact families."  We all have a choice and a stake in creating our neighorhood, and my neighbors and I *do* know and care about one another.

As a graduate student, I know that my stay in Astoria will be temporary, as dictated by the academic job market, but it doesn't mean that I'm contributing to the "decay" of Astoria.  It's unrealistic to demand that people should live their entire lives in the place where they were born and equally unrealistic to believe that people who live in a place for a temporary period of time don't care about building a community.

My original letter was published in the print media; that's where it should have stayed. I've enjoyed the give and take, but, because it's so easy to "rant, "  what began as a serious debate has devolved into a sniping match among the computer literate. The loss of a neighborhood I loved and still live in is too serious a matter for the kind of silliness that denies reality in favor of "looking on the bright side" and other assorted cliche's and stock phrases. Nonetheless, just so that no one accuses me of being a  whiner or a "naysayer" I want to relate a conversation that took place this past week between me and a staff member from a local politician's office regarding a quality of life complaint I filed.

Staff person: "I'm calling from .......Office regarding the graffiti situation."

Me: That was a while ago. You're just getting back to me now?"

Staff person: "Just where is this graffiti you mentioned in your email?"

Me. "It's all up and down 23rd avenue. I called the local police precinct and they sent a squad car to my apartment, which was embarrassing.

Staff person: "When you say 'up and down' 23rd Avenue, can you be more specific?"

Me. How specific do you want me to be? Why doesn't he [the politician in question] take a ride up and down the block and see it for himself. It's bad enough he couldn't do anything to get the lights back on last year. It seems like the least he can do."

Staff person: "Well, it's not like we can just go and clean up graffiti, if it's on private property."

So much for civic involvement.
