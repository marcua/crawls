I had a very nice dinner on Friday night at L'Artiste, a small French bistro on the corner of 31st Avenue, 43rd Street, and Newtown Road (next to Cavo).  I started with a salmon tartare on top of avocado and a roasted beet disk--both the salmon and avocado were creamy, and it was a substantial portion.  My entree was sliced duck breast on top of squash puree and vegetables in cream sauce.  The duck was cooked medium rare as ordered, and was generally tender except for a few tough bits of skin.  For dessert, I had the pear tart in a caramel sauce.  Though the pastry part could have been crisper, I liked the flavors and it was easily large enough for share.  The red sangria is interesting--it has cinnamon but no pieces of fruit. 

The owner, who is originally from Morocco, and his staff are very friendly.  Prices are average--appetizers in the low teens, entrees in the high teens to low twenties.  It's nice to have another French restaurant in the neighborhood.

I FINALLY went last night.  I had purchased a Groupon for $30 late last year and for whatever reason just haven't been able to get there.


Anyway, I insisted we go last night.  We walked in and were greeted in a very friendly manner and escorted to a table.  They were about 1/2 full.  We ordered escargot, I ordered the lamb, he ordered the NY Strip.  We also had a very nice bottle of Cote du Rhone and the chocolat fondant for dessert. 


This place is sublime.  Or maybe I should say WAS sublime.  Everything was absolutely perfect until the very end when they brought the check over.  They automatically put an 18% gratuity on the bill.  When I asked them what it was they said they do it for all Groupons. 


What a way to ruin an evening!  I own 2 businesses that have done the Groupon thing.  I understand how it works:  it's great for the consumer and it positively SUCKS for the business owner.  You're eviscerated on what you sold because of Groupon's cut.  In the beginning, and because of the economy, it seemed like such a good idea:  get more people in the door!  Introduce your product/service to new people!  Grow your tribe!  The reality is that a new culture developed:  the grouponer.  It's a group of people who ONLY go to places that have deals.  You'll never capture them and so the promotion is a waste of money.  Let me add this:  there are some people (like me) who buy these things who are not about going to the cheapest place possible all the time.  I'm a total candidate for becoming a "raving fan" of a place (read my numerous sycofantic SugarFreak posts).  All L'Artiste did was piss me off with their auto-gratuity scam.  Let me also say that gratuities are OPTIONAL.  I'm not even sure it's legal for them to add it on - at least without telling you ahead of time.  I understand they did it because maybe they noticed that Grouponers - who aren't interested in a relationship - don't care if they tip appropriately, and by that I mean tipping on the full value.  That's just the way it goes, though. 


The kicker - I would have left way more than 18%!  Not only did they piss me off to a level that I'm going to bitch about them to everyone but they ultimately shortchanged themselves.


What a shame!

With all due respect - I know what gratuities are for - I work in the service industry.  It pains me to see how cheap people can be sometimes.  However, a gratuity is an OPTION - not a requirement.  On the groupon printout they do mention to remember to tip on the full amount - which is a great idea as some people might forget (and others might need a gentle reminder that it's expected).  It is RUDE to demand tips from your customers, it is RUDE to even discuss it.  It is at the customer's discretion - this isn't France, it is still our choice.   


I recognize that tips are ingrained in our culture and to tip improperly in and of itself is rude.  However, it is the best (and sometimes only) way for a client to demonstrate his or her satisfaction or lack thereof.


The restaurant clearly made it a policy so therefore, they are responsible.  I don't begrudge them wanting to protect their servers.  The hard truth though, is that short of tacking it onto the bill - which is RUDE - there is nothing you can do about tips.  Sometimes they're good, sometimes they're not.  I know some restaurants print a little chart at the bottom of the receipt demonstrating what 15, 18, and 20% of the bill would be.  This is a good idea and they could have easily have done this but made the calculation based on the full amount.  That would have been fine - some people hate math and that makes it easy.  But to put it directly into the bill is nothing other than poor taste.
