When you sit on the outside of the forward-facing two seater, you are effectively blocking the inside seat. It's a pain in the ass to move past the person sitting in the outer seat to get to the inner seat, so the polite thing to do, if you are sitting in the outer seat, is to slide over to the inner seat. It's equally easy, if you are sitting in a 2-seater, to swing your knees toward the aisle to let the newcomer in to sit by the window.

When I was recovering from gallbladder surgery a couple of years ago, I was sitting in the outside seat of the two-seater when a short woman came along and asked me to move over.  I pointed out that being nearly an Amazon, I can't really fit in there (most of my height is in my legs), happily offered to do the 90 degree thing so she could get in.  She said that no, she had to have that seat because she had bad knees.  At this point I retorted that I also have bad knees (two years of teaching special ed preschool with emergency lifting of tantrumming children on staircases can do that to you) and was also recovering from recent surgery, but she proceeded to escalate the argument until all I could do was stand weakly.  I don't like arguing.

Someone else who had overheard offered me a seat, and I was very grateful for it.

I really wish I'd pointed out to her that there are in fact seats reserved for people with disabilities, and since I had mine, she should have taken one of those, but I didn't.  I was fairly shaken by the whole encounter, honestly.

Whoever said you can't always see disability is right - I've had days where I don't mind standing, and also days when I need my seat.  That was one of them, and that woman was foul.

Oh, and she works in my industry.  At the time I didn't know who she was, but now I know exactly where she works.  You never know when that kind of thing can come back around to you.

On a better note, I was very relieved about the courtesy of N train riders when I sprained my ankle last winter.  While I was on crutches and visibly hurt, people were quick to offer seats.  Once I was over the crutches I was still having balance issues, and was lucky enough to get a seat most days, but one day the train was packed and since I 'looked' normal I decided to tough it out and hang on for dear life on the way home.  I just didn't feel right asking people to give up their seats when I was walking fairly normally and appeared perfectly healthy.

At Fifth Ave the train did the sudden brake thing and I wasn't able to hold my weight on the injured ankle, which ended with me tumbling into a couple of people (yes, it was humiliating and yes, I apologized quickly).  Someone overheard me laughing about it and explaining the state of my physical therapy to the coworker I was with, and offered me a seat.  Whoever it was, thank you! 

It wasn't one of the people I'd fallen on, either.
