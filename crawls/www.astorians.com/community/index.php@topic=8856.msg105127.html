Quote from: 28Grand on December 13, 2007, 08:02:18 AM

One question remains for me though: If the opposition was so poorly organized why has it taken so long to get landmark status in Sunnyside?


Well, from what I understand, Landmarking usually does take a while.  The area and its buildings have to be reviewed to see if they meet the requirements.  The LPC held a couple of community meetings, but didn't get around to putting the matter on its calendar until early in 2007.  Once that happened, things moved fairly quickly.

I don't think that the delay in getting SG landmarked was due to opposition. More likely it was just slow-moving bureaucracy. There was no real opposition until right before calendaring. Oh, sure, there were a few people who grumbled, but they were largely dismissed. The opposition became noisy only after the last community meeting; they claimed that they were not notified of the move to landmark until the last minute.  That's total hooey, since people were collecting signatures in the area for years, and meetings were held. 

SG is very much an activist community.  If there was much opposition to landmarking before calendaring, we would have known about it. Fliers would have been distributed and meetings would have been held. 

I don't think that the opposition folded because we won them over to our way of thinking.  Rather, I believe that 3 factors were at work:

1.  The core organizers dropped out of the battle, leaving a demoralize, real rag-tag bunch to continue opposition.

2.  They just were not very good at organizing or convincing people.

3.  The LPC hearing drew far more supporters than opponents.  In addition, statements were made from some politicians and lots of preservation organizations. Once they saw how strong the support for landmarking was, they realized that they were doomed to loose.

As for what parts of Astoria to landmark, or declare a Historic District, that's a matter for discussion. The one area that comes to mind is Old Astoria Village, where many beautiful, historic homes have been destroyed.

Landmarking is a slow process for a number of reasons.  First, the Landmarks Preservation Commission has a small staff and it's a big city.  The staff has to do the historic research on the individual buildings in the area under consideration, prepare a report and also determine the community support and finalize the boundaries of the proposed district - it often changes as the proposal moves forward.  The process includes community board review and hearings at the Landmarks Preservation Commission.  The proposed NoHo district got divided into three pieces and the final part is only now getting hearings, about a decade after the initial work began.  Sunnyside Gardens seemed to move fast compared to some of the other possible districts in the city.

Well, when a building or area is declared a city landmark, any real exterior changes must be approved by the Landmarks Preservation Commission. The LPC "grandfathers in" the buildings as they are when the district is designated, so people don't have to go out and restore their buildings to the way they looked originally. However, any changes made to the exteriors have to resemble what made the building special. For example, the owner cannot build an extension, cover a building in pink siding, or, in the case of SG, rip up the garden and put in a nice ugly carport.

The majority of applications for building work are approved on an administrative level by the LPC, usually fairly quickly. The LPC has very knowledgeable staff who try to work with homeowners to come up with practical solutions. A few really major projects require approval of the Commission via a hearing process. 

Here are answers to a few of the common pieces of misinformation put out there by landmarking opponents, who like to resort to scare-tactics and race-baiting:

Landmarking does not hurt property values.  In fact, one study shows that it helps raise them!

Landmarking does not create a housing shortage. They LPC does not regulate the interiors of homes, so the number of units in a building can remain the same.

The LPC does not regulate what you plant in your garden.

The LPC could care less what you do to the interior of your home.

The LPC will consider alternative materials if they resemble the real thing. For example, slate roofs can sometimes be replaced with quality substitutes.

There is financial assistance to low-income homeowners who need to make repairs.

Landmarking advocates are NOT Archie Bunker-types who want to drive minorities or immigrants away.  In fact, the people testifying on behalf of SG's designation resembled the United Nations.

Most minor repairs get approved quickly and painlessly.

Landmarking protects the homeowner's investment. Living in a historic district means that my property values won't be hurt because some clod tears down his home next door and puts up what resembles a stack of shoe boxes.

Landmarking does not interfere with rent control or rent stabilization.

As for buildings of under 6 units, landlords in these are already allowed to charge market rates, so when a house is landmarked it has no impact upon how much rent tenants pay, or how much a landlord may charge.

I hope this clarifies a few things.  As for what parts of Astoria should be landmarked, that is up for discussion. I for one would love to see the beautiful homes and churches in Old Astoria Village preserved.
