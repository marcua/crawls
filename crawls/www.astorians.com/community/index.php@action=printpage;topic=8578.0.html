If you like to read about financial issues, I recommend picking up a book called "Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds" by Charles Mackay.  It's an excellent book written back in 1841 that talks about previous stock/real estate/speculative bubbles in the past.  Some of these were: The South-Sea Bubble (England around 1720), The Mississippi Scheme (France around 1719-1720), and Tulipomania (The Dutch in the early-mid 1600s).  Anyway, this book describes "mania" where greed took over and reason was lost.  Credit rates were overextended, central banks became involved in companies' fate, and the governments took their base valuing off gold and coin and put it on money; which they printed more and more of.  This caused mass-hysteria and ensuing pandemonium as these runs made the commoner vastly wealthy, only to have it all come crashing down and taking those very same people, and more, into poverty.

Now, this might seem a bit extreme right now, but it's really not too far off from what's happening in the U.S., and the world, right now.  Skyrocketing real estate values had brought riches and higher wealth to many people.  Speculation and easy credit drove values up to astronomical values and allowed people to take loans out on these higher values and spend more money.  But, it started to falter in 2005 and, now, has really only begun to get bad.   

According to Deutsche Bank and JPMorgan, there are almost 30 BILLION dollars in subprime and adjustable rate loans resetting to higher values EVERY MONTH.  I'm sure many of you have read about it in the news.  Well, it's ONLY STARTED to get bad.  The first large reset month was last month and it is going to continue through the end of 2008.  The New York market isn't immune, either.  The only reason things have not been as bad here is because, on average, it takes 488 days for a home to go into foreclosure in New York; opposed to 21 days in Texas and a few months in NV, CA, FL, etc. (the states you're already reading about).  So, there's a lag in what is going to happen here.

On top of our money being worth less, inflation is getting higher.  Fuel costs much more; food costs much more; the things we use on a daily basis cost much more.  Oil is almost $100 a barrel.  Homes aren't selling, values aren't increasing, and people are getting stuck with higher payments or foreclosure.

Back before Nixon and 1971, a dollar was called a "promissory note."  You were able to, if you wanted to, take a walk down to a U.S. Treasury vault and trade in your dollars for a specific amount of gold (1/35th of an ounce per $1).  This is part of the reason why things were so much cheaper when you were a kid.  $1 had much more value, inflation was in better check, and the government was unable to just "print more money" (which floods the market and causes inflation.  Read about Germany before WWII, when their government did this and you had to take wheelbarrows of money to buy a loaf of bread).
