I remember back in '01 when I had to look for apartments.   I saw one apartment that was as nice as what I had and it was more expensive and the broker wanted me to sign a lease that said I was paying a rent a lot higher than what I would actually be charged.   That turned me off.

And the brokers would drive you nuts.   I was specific in my needs -- a large one-bedroom near the subway.   They would take way away from subway access or show me apartments that were much smaller than what I would ask for.

And I saw a couple of howlers.   There was one new building on 31st Street on the 2nd or 3rd floor right near the subway platform.   There was no way I was going to live on 31st Street, especially in an apartment that faced the elevated trains.   The train would go by and it felt like it was going through the living room.   Maybe you get used to that, but I took a pass.

And another apartment I was showed was nowhere near the subway, but the funny thing about it was this closed.   I swear to God it was the smallest closet I've ever seen.   It was so small that you wonder why they even bothered.   You opened the door and there was about 3 inches of space between the door and the back wall.  You wouldn't be able to fit a shirt on a hanger in there.   I couldn't believe it.

Ya, I'm a little slow getting into this conversation, but here it goes anyways...

I currently pay 1300 for a 1 bedroom on Crescent between 21st Ave and Ditmars. Moved in August of '07. On a real lease (for the first time in NY) and rather than having to pay a brokers fee, I went directly through the management company and paid a much smaller application fee.

Hey Dullnboring,

If you have a lease, the landlord is required, by law, to offer you a new lease under these guidelines.  Below is the summary from the NYC Rent Guideline Board website, which is a good resource and has been cited on this board repeatedly.  http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/dhcr/dhcr4.html
Hope this helps!

The owner must give written notice of renewal by mail or personal delivery not more than 150 days and not less than 90 days before the existing lease expires. The offer to renew the lease for New York City tenants must be on a Renewal Lease Form [DHCR form RTP-8]. For tenants outside of New York City, the renewal notice must be sent by certified mail not more than 120 days and not less than 90 days before the existing lease expires. To obtain an approved form outside NYC, contact your local Rent Office.
