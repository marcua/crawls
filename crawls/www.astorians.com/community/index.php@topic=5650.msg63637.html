I think you can draw a line in the "everyone's entitled to their own opinion" sand at the point where an opinion is connected with behavior that clearly harms another. Even then, people are entitled to their own opinions. We can't legislate thought, we can only legislate behavior. If someone thinks racism is not wrong, but does not own slaves or engage in discrimination - it's just an opinion. An ugly opinion, imo, but no one is being harmed other than the opinionator. Sure, you can argue - and I won't say you shouldn't - but it's hard to change people's minds on these issues by arguing.

If someone thinks racism is not wrong and owns slaves, that is something that can and should be acted on.

Life is complicated. People I think are sick and twisted probably think I am sick and twisted. Some of us would probably have burned each other at the stake many times over if we could get away with it. As long as we can all remember our own and each other's humanity - the possibility that we are mistaken, the possibility that intelligent people can disagree with us for good reasons, the possibility that their intentions are good even if their logic is bad - we have some hope.

To take this back to the original topic: I think there's a valid argument that it's harmful to not inform teens about sex, though I don't think it ranks at the same level of harm as child abuse. And frankly, most of our parents do harm us in one way or another, and it's just something to live with. I mean, why do we rant about this instead of not being breast-fed, being potty-trained before we were ready, or that god-awful over-cooked liver. :P I don't know where we get the idea that we all have a basic human right to never be hurt or misguided. Ultimately we're responsible for our own actions and our own happiness.

That said, I still think the father-daughter ball is just plain weird.

Quote from: enigmacat on March 22, 2007, 07:30:06 PM
Good point...

Good point? Really?

If a person's ignorance leads him to disapprove of another person's rational perspective/behavior, the proper response of the rational person is not to chalk it up to diversity and celebrate it. One proper response would be to say, "You're entitled to your opinion, but not to facts. Here are the facts and evidence you haven't clearly considered." Or, "You're entitled to your opinion, but not to impose it upon anyone else."

The range of human behavior that involvles fraud and/or coercion should be excluded from the list of diverse experiences worth celebrating. Dishonesty and aggression are essentially bad. (Lying to a Nazi about a Jew in the basement is less about being dishonest and more about refusing to enable aggression. One can posit a logical rule, that when honesty leads directly to coercion("The Jew is in the basement, Mr. SS man."), a person has a moral sanction to be dishonest. But aggression, I contend, is unjustifiable. Some utilitarians like to say, "What if a man develops the cure for cancer in his house and refuses to share it. Do we have a moral right to force it from him?" My answer is "no." I would also point out that the man who cures cancer, realistically, isn't likely to behave the way utilitarians NEED him to behave in this kind of absurd attempt at moral argumentation.)

Regarding this thread, it's great if only because it got me to hum the AC/DC song, but also, seriously, because it brings up a point that makes reasonable people very uncomfortable.

We're all on the same boat (if not in the same room on that boat) about beating children constituting bad parental behavior. But when a parent commits fraud against his/her children, say by imposing the strictures of Christian/Islamic/Judaic mythologies on them as if there are real post-mortem consequences when there simply aren't, our legal system doesn't make it possible for anyone to emancipate them, including the kids themselves. It's legally permissible to tell a kid that he'll spend eternity in hell if he takes penicillin, or if she has premarital sex, or if he or she masturbates, for all kinds of reasons that are utterly fraudulent. In the workplace, we don't tolerate the adult boss telling the adult secretary to blow him or lose her job because of the Power he holds over her in his position as employer. That power pales beside that of a parent filling her child's mind with fairy tales about sin and hell that make her resent her/his body, her/his sexuality, her/his curiosity, her/his intellectuality.

I'm not saying there should be a law against parents "teaching" their children to be servile, anti-sexual, anti-intellectual automatons. There shouldn't be laws against any ideas per se. But maybe there should be a legal framework within which children can more easily emancipate themselves from their parents on the grounds of seeking to avoid religious indoctrination.
