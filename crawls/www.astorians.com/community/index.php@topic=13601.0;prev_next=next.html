The dangers of automatic payments
« on: January 08, 2009, 01:57:00 PM »
So, I had a gym membership last year. I signed the little form to let them make an automatic debit every month to make my life a little easier.

This year, I decided it was time to tighten the belt, do pushups in my living room, and cancel the extra expense. I talked to my gym, no problem, made the request in writing, etc etc.

Because I had a friend who had a horrible time trying to cancel a gym membership a couple of years ago - they kept billing his checking acct despite his very clear desire to no longer be their customer - I wondered if I could protect myself a little further by blocking further charges from the gym. I emailed my bank to ask them about this.

They told me I would have to cancel my account and open a new one. We went back and forth on that a couple of times, but that was all they could offer me. There's absolutely no way, once you authorize the ACH, to revoke it. You are completely reliant on the merchant's 1) good organization and 2) ethical business practices.

I find this really problematic. I told them I thought it would very nice if they offered their consumers the extra protection from fraud. I really don't expect my gym to try anything, if I truly thought there could be a risk I'd go ahead and close the account.

Has anyone else had similar experiences? Or do you have a different bank with different practices? (I'm with Chase/WaMu) Does anyone know if there's some reason that it's so hard to prevent a non-legitimate transaction at the request of the consumer?

Oh, I have no intention of ever using that service again.

But I do think it is really weird that there are so many clear-cut fraud cases where companies abused this service, and banks still haven't done anything to either educate their customers or put some time frames around the ability to access the acct. This is really poor risk management.

Ideally, when a customer signed an automatic payment agreement, the end-date of the agreement would be entered into the system and when that date rolled around the customer would have to sign a new agreement before the merchant could withdraw any further funds.

I've had automatic renewals that I've forgotten about - my only problem has been that names get so abreviated as to be meaningless, or the billing name isn't the same as the service rendered so I go nuts looking at my statement, going W - T - F is ________????

For automatic renewals and other once a year payments, I divide the presumed renewal amount by the number of paychecks I will get that that year and budget a rounded-up rounded off amount to deposit in the bank each time I get paid.  On my budget I include the division number, so I know at, say paycheck # 20, whether or not I'm using that service enough to continue using it.

When you call a company you're usually dealing with a low-level employee with a script of road blocks, so the moment you don't get what you want, ask to speak with a supervisor, and if you still don't get what you want ask to speak with that person's supervisor and that lets them know you are not going to be quitting in your puruit of getting what you want.  Verifying the location number of a plant so you can "mention it when you speak to corporate" helps at this level because they would rather you be a statistic on a sheet of paper than a voice mentioning them by name.
