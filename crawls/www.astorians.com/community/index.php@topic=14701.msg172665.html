I wonder if it's a bit more complicated than just a single number. New York is a weird market, since it includes a huge high-end sector in Manhattan, but also a much larger proportion of condos and (often owner-occupied) multi-family dwellings compared to other markets, which have more single-family homes. My vague uninformed understanding (please correct me if I'm wrong!) is that prices in Manhattan are down drastically, but this is much less true for middle-of-the-range stuff around here. I suspect you're right overall, but I'm not sure that it's true for both high-end and low-end real estate. Anyone have P/I charts for NYC at various price/borough brackets?

(It's also true that despite the Wall Street collapse, NYC's economy is doing relatively less-bad, compared with areas with economies based on manufacturing and construction. So there may be less of an income drop, supporting less of a RE price drop here than elsewhere...)

From the Miller Samuel report.
"Although the number of properties available for sale declined 7% to 10,421 from 11,206 listings in the same period last year, inventory expanded 6.1% from the prior quarter total of 9,822. The rise in inventory levels from the prior quarter was the result of the sharp decline in the number of sales over the same period. There were 1,801 sales in the first quarter, down 52.2% from 3,771 sales in the same period last year and down 34.2% from 2,737 sales in the prior quarter. The number of sales tends to decline from the fourth quarter to the first quarter of any given year, however, the decline during this period of the year was more than twice the five year average of 12.6% and outside of the five year range of -9.3% to -16.4%. The weakening regional economy and credit crunch as restricted the level of sales activity in the Queens housing market. Sellers continue to price property "behind" the market. As a result, the absorption rate - the number of months it would take to sell off existing inventory at the current pace of sales - has more than doubled over the past year. The absorption rate was 17.4 months in the first quarter, up from 8.9 months in the same period last year, both well above the 6.8 monthly average of the past five years... "

That is an indication of a very sick market. From what I've seen median prices are still around $450K for a house which is about 10x median income in Queens. ($44K)
And while NY has not had as bad a downturn yet, past downturns have had a bigger impact on NY than other regions, it's still a long way to go before thing stop falling and start coming back.

Quote from: webmamma on May 04, 2009, 06:42:13 AM
I remember reading somewhere that the value of real estate in New York has historically doubled every five years, even during the great depression. I remember seeing a chart somewhere...

That is historically untrue and mathematically falsifiable.
First, Robert Shiller has shown that housing in this country (including NY) has not increased in value beyond inflation from 1890 to 1999. It then grew into the biggest bubble in world history.
Second, if a house cost $5,000 50 years ago and doubled every 5 years, it would now be worth over $5,000,000. (5,000 x2, x2, x2.... you can do the math)
And we can add periods in the 70s and 90s when prices fell up to 50%.

Do you think the small, wood-sided, 80 year old single family homes that people in Astoria are trying to sell for $600,000 will be worth $1,200,000 in 5 years?

All good points by posters on this thread.

While I recognize the points made in the Times article, I'm not so sure one can apply the same potential impact to certain NYC areas - Queens included. In Manhattan, many renters are seeing their rents renegotiated to lower prices upon renewal. A childhood friend of mine recently had his monthly rate lowered from 4k to $3,250. And no - his parking is separate and remains the same lol.

When home and condo/co-op sales drop in most areas, don't rents normally respond inversely? That really doesn't happen in NYC. When sales prices fall, we're seeing rents become cheaper. This also happened in the City in the early 1990s from what I understand. When recessions occur, many professional types without work living in NYC return to their home states until the job market stabilizes.

In my unprofessional opinion, people love to buy on momentum. It makes them feel like they're on the winning team. Buying low doesn't provide much company.

Anyway - I'm not tossing any chips into the pot unless I have an inheritance I don't know about.
