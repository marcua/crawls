I was personally against the bailout package, because as a free market capitalist, I believe that the risks and rewards should be borne by the market.  Wall Street was happy to make big profits on their risks, and now they should take their lumps.  I am also very uncomfortable with what is essentially the nationalization, a la Hugo Chavez, of the bulk of our mortgage industry and many of our banks.  I will note, though, that there was not monolithic Republican support for the bailout.  Many Republicans (and Democrats for that matter) did the right thing and stood against the plan.  I think that the market crash is of such dire importance that most people in congress recognized that something had to be done and done quickly.  The divisions were not along the traditional ideological lines.  And at the end of the day, many Republicans, as well as Democrats, screwed up.

Jindal of Louisiana, who is doing great things there, and is also a young, energetic guy.

The Republican party at this point has been hit with a perfect storm.  McCain is a less exciting candidate than the charismatic Obama, who is also fortunate to have very little legislative record to have to defend. We've hit tough economic times, which I attribute more to the subprime loans encouraged by liberal activists than anything, but McCain has been tied into that. And the current lackluster presidency has been used effectively as a weapon against him. 

But if the Democratic party has a more cohesive message, what is it?  They have simply managed their candidate better.  They have wonderful soundbites, and offer up what people really want in troubled times.  Paternalistic reassurance.  Their brand is "we're not the other guy," and that is a good brand to use for this election cycle.  The rhetoric is short on specifics, and those that are offered are terrible.  Obama wants to give tax cuts to the 40 percent of Americans who don't earn enough to pay taxes in the first place.  So it's not really a tax cut, per se, but an income redistribution. Foul idea.  And to consider raising taxes on anyone else is just nuts at a time like this.  The way out of the economic crisis is to encourage growth and entrepreneurship, not to try and tax our way out of it.

I don't see either party coming out with any amazing prescriptive ideas to fix all of our problems.  So I will vote for the best worst choice, so to speak.  I just hope that the Democrats do not score a super majority in congress.  I think most of you will concede that Pelosi and the rest of the leadership are more towards the left of the party.  There will be effectively nothing to moderate Obama's policy wish list.
