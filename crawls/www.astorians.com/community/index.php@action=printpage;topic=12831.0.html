Recipe (serves six)

1 99 cent store package of six frozen breaded chicken patties divided into individual portions
1 pkg 99 cent store imitation mozzarella cheese product divided into individual portions
1 jar 99 cent store spaghetti sauce
1 box 99 cent store spaghetti

Boil the spaghetti
Cover the chicken patties with the imitation mozzarella cheese product and nuke until the faux cheese is melted
Plate the spaghetti, chicken patties, and sauce
Re-nuke until everything bubbles and smells delicious
Serve and enjoy!

Total cost: $3.96  Price per portion: $0.66

You can serve a great dish for six for the price of McDonald's "Value Meal"!  :lol:

 Tomorrow's lunch, made this evening--Roasted Cherry Peppers stuffed with double smoked bacon, goat cheese feta, and onion and garlic.

  Yesterday, I bought eight largish cherry peppers from Berry Farms and the rest of the ingredients came from  either C Town and EuroMarket.

Recipe:
Roasted Cherry Peppers stuffed with double smoked bacon, goat cheese feta, and onion and garlic.

Cut off the top and stem of the cherry peppers (or any medium sized pepper of your choice)
Deseed and devein the pepper creating a hollow cavity
Line the inner wall of the pepper with Euromarket's double smoked bacon--one slice goes a long way
Insert goat cheese feta (also available at EuroMarket) into the cavity of the pepper
Press minced garlic and onion into the feta cheese
Place stuffed peppers on a cookie sheet and brush them with olive oil
Pop then into a pre-heated oven at 425F
Roast until the peppers are fork tender
Serve with Ajvar to taste and enjoy

  While, I'm sure that this isn't an original recipe--it is a recipe that I developed by using ingredients that are locally available in Astoria.

PERUVIAN GREEN SAUCE (approx recipe)
2 cups iceberg lettuce
1/2 bunch cilantro
2 jalapenos. Seeded and deribbed
1/4 cup white vinegar
 2 tablespoon mayonnaise
1/2 cup or less oil
1/2 medium onion
2 clove garlic

A quick and simple soup

1-14 1/2 oz. can of stewed or diced tomatoes(juice included)
1-15 1/2 oz. can of red kidney or cannellini beans,drained and rinsed well
1/2 of an 8 oz. can of tomato sauce
3 cups of water
1 cup of frozen mixed vegetables
1 cup of uncooked elbow or other small pasta
Salt,pepper,and basil to taste
Grated cheese(I prefer locatelli) for sprinkling on soup

Put tomatoes(if they're too big,you can cut them smaller),beans,tomato sauce,and water in a 3 quart pot and bring to a boil.Season with salt,pepper,and basil.Lower heat to medium and stir in frozen vegetables.cook about 10 to 15 minutes.Lower to a simmer and stir in pasta.Soup is done when the pasta is done.Top with grated cheese if desired..

If you have leftovers,the pasta and beans will most likely soak up the liquid.Add the remaining tomato sauce and enough water to cover.Heat up.

Mojo Criollo is a Cuban marinade.  Here's a blurb from Goya's website:

Mojo Criollo
Zesty Mojo Criollo with garlic, onion, and citrus for tender, tasty meat and poultry.

Ingredients

    * 1/4 cup lime juice, fresh squeezed
    * 1/2 cup orange juice, fresh squeezed
    * 1/2 cup olive oil
    * 1 teaspoon dried oregano
    * 6 garlic cloves, pressed
    * salt, to taste
    * black pepper, fresh ground, to taste

  The actual recipe calls for sour oranges--(Citrus aurantium, L.), also known as bitter, bigarade, or Seville orange, which are hard to find here in Queens.  I pick them up at the Mercado Mi Bandera in Union City, NJ (518 32nd St, Union City, NJ 07087), a great Caribbean/Central and South American grocery store.  The combination of oranges and limes come pretty close to the flavor of sour oranges.
  Goya's product is decent and readily available in most local groceries in the "Spanish" food section.   I prefer buying imported brand--that cost about the same--in UC or Washington Heights or making my own when time allows.

  As for Mi Bandera, I would suggest a quick trip through the Lincoln Tunnel for anyone interested in Cuban and Central/South American food to check it out as well as the rest of Bergenline Avenue between UC and West New York, NJ.  There are a lot of great restaurants and markets along the B Line.  Yikes!  Queens is "bad enough", but New Jersey! :evil:
  UC/WNY is, actually, pretty accessible even if you don't have a car.  Simply, catch an NJT 156 bus at Port Authority and get off at the 48th Street and Bergenline stop ($2.80 each way) about three stops past the tunnel.  Total travel time from the PA Bus Terminal: fifteen minutes + tunnel access time.  Walk south for Union City or north for West New York, you'll be surprised at what you might find.
  When it's time to come home, simply hop on a bus or jitney back to NYCPA along Bergenline Avenue.

My husband had requested meatloaf and instead of going out and buying stuff for my mother's rather complicated (but yummy) meatloaf, I searched for something simpler to make the most of what I had in my kitchen. I found this basic easy meatloaf recipe that was credited to Katie Lee Joel and made it tonight.....

And it actually was very very good!!! So I am passing it on to the meateaters out there. I halved everything and it turned out fine.

INGREDIENTS

    * 1 Tbsp. olive oil
    * 1/2 medium yellow onion, diced (about 3/4 cup)
    * 1 garlic clove, minced
    * 1 medium red pepper, finely diced (about 1 cup)
    * 1 bay leaf
    * 2 Tbsp. chopped fresh flat-leaf parsley
    * 2 tsp. chopped fresh thyme
    * 2 pounds lean ground beef
    * 2 large eggs, lightly beaten
    * 3/4 cup dry bread crumbs
    * 1 cup ketchup
    * 1 Tbsp. Worcestershire sauce
    * 2 tsp. kosher salt
    * 1 tsp. freshly ground black pepper

 Preheat the oven to 350¬∞. Line a baking sheet with parchment paper. Spray lightly with oil. Heat the olive oil in a medium skillet over medium heat. Saut√© the onions, garlic and bay leaf until the onions are tender, about 3 minutes. Add the red pepper and cook until the red pepper is tender, about 5 minutes. Stir in the parsley and thyme and cook for another 2 minutes. Remove pan from the heat and let the onion mixture cool. Discard the bay leaf.
In a large bowl, combine the beef, eggs, bread crumbs, 1/2 cup of ketchup, the Worcestershire sauce, salt, pepper and the cooled vegetables. Use your hands to mix everything together.

Transfer the mixture to the center of the baking sheet and form into a loaf. Coat the meat loaf with the remaining 1/2 cup ketchup.

Bake for 1 to 1 1/2 hours (depending on the shape of your loaf), until the meat loaf is firm. Let set for about 5 minutes before slicing.

We caught some blackfish (http://www.yankee3.com/Fish/blackfish.html) over the weekend.  It's a traditional chowder fish, but we didn't have enough for it to be the only main ingredient.  So we bought half a dozen large sea scallops (bay scallops would be excellent but they're more expensive) and a few chowder clams to bulk it up.

I used Jasper White's fish chowder recipe (http://www.epicurious.com/recipes/food/views/New-England-Fish-Chowder-105200) as a starting point, and came up with:

4 slices bacon, diced
2 T unsalted butter
1 leek, cleaned and slivered
1 medium onion, chopped
2 bay leaves
1 1/4 tsp Old Bay seasoning
salt/pepper
1 quart chicken broth (or good fish stock)
3 smallish red potatoes, cut into small dice (~ 1 cm cubes)
3 chowder clams, rough chopped, with their liquid
2 fillets blackfish, cut into ~ 1" chunks
6 sea scallops, quartered
1 1/2 - 1 3/4 cups half and half

I browned the diced bacon and removed the pieces, leaving the rendered fat in the pan.  Added butter, leeks, onion, bay leaves, and seasoning.  Lowered the heat to medium-low and cooked until onions were soft and translucent but not browned, 5-10 minutes.  Added chicken broth, clam liquor and potato cubes, raised heat, and cooked until potatoes were soft, about 10 minutes.  Lowered heat to low.  Added the clam pieces, simmered for 3-4 minutes, and added fish and scallop pieces.  Simmered for 5 minutes, turned heat off and let sit for 10 minutes, covered.  Added half and half and adjusted seasoning (it took a surprising amount of salt for my taste, but I'm a salt fiend). 

All of the seafood was *just* cooked - the fish was tender and plump, just barely flaking in big chunks, and the scallops were just past translucent.  The proper chowder flavour was there, but it's not a strong-flavoured recipe.  It was more subtle than I expected, but it was still very satisfying.  Heavy cream would definitely add richness, but it's not necessary.

This works best with fresh fish.  One of the fillets we used was frozen from a fishing trip a couple of weeks ago, and the texture difference was obvious.  The pieces that were frozen were much drier and flakier, although they didn't taste more done.

Greek style red snapper stew

Good olive oil
1 medium onion fine chop
2 lg zucchini cubed
2 cloves garlic sliced
1/2 cup pitted Kalamata olives
1 lemon. Half for squeezing, half for wedges.
2 cups puttanesca sauce OR 2 cups crushed tomato, 4 anchovies, 1 bay leaf, red pepper flakes.
2 lbs red snapper fillets cut into large cubes. Can use almost any fish or seafood for this dish.
1 tablespoon fresh dill plus more for garnish.
1/2 cup feta cheese

Use a heavy bottom 18" pan or similar. A large wide pan will let you cook faster as the stew is spread out over more surface area.
Over high heat saute onion in plenty of olive oil till translucent.
Add garlic and zucchini. Season with touch of ground black pepper. Saute till softened.
Add olives and puttanesca sauce. 5 minutes on high heat covered.
Stir up and push fish down into the mixture.
Crumble on feta, dill and squeeze of half lemon.
Cover and simmer 10 minutes.
Gently stir then plate in bowls with more dill as garnish and a lemon wedge.
One grind of black pepper and a drizzle of olive oil.

Serve with bread for sopping up the extra sauce.

Pork ragu casserole with Tuscan bean crust
-Basically a left overs dish.  Be creative, it's really all about the dark rich meat in sauce and the creamy yet crusty topping.

2 qts prepared pork shoulder ragu in tomato sauce (or similar recipe)
2bs dried white beans. Cooked with bay leaf, garlic and olive oil till softened then drained.
- Save liquid and remaining beans for soup.

Mix beans with chopped garlic, olive oil, bread crumbs, chopped parsley, chopped sage or rosemary, lemon zest, salt and pepper. You want a moistened mix but no mushy broken beans.

In casserole dish coated with olive oil, lay in the pork ragu and smooth down.  Layer over the bean mix. Pat down and sprinkle over more bread crumbs for crust.  Drizzle over olive oil.

Into a preheated 350F oven for 30 minutes or until crust is browned and bubbling.

I promise you will love this one.  Hearty, rich, delicious.

NOTES:
Good one pot dish with little clean up.
Left over beans and liquid can be pureed and used as base for soup with chopped chard and chicken stock.

French onion soup stuffing:
-I love hearty dishes and wanted the great taste of this soup but with more in it to make it a side dish.

6 lg onions sliced thin
2 tbs butter plus more for dish
1 tbs sugar
2 qts liquid (1qt chix stock and 1 1qt water. Or all chix stock)
1/2 cup cheap brandy or similar
Peasant bread (crusty with hearty interior) cut into inch cubes. enough to fill standard casserole pan.
Gruyere or swiss, sliced. Enough to cover top of casserole with at least 1 layer. More if you like.
Salt and pepper to taste.

In heavy bottom pan on high heat melt 2 tbs butter then add onions, stir in sugar and some salt. 
Keep stirring onions over high heat till some water releases. Lower heat to medium high and occasionally stir onions.
You are looking to wilt down and then lightly caramelize the onions.  This will take 20 minutes plus. Do not let them burn. 
When light browned and softened turn up the heat and add brandy. Stir up and cook till dry.
Add 2qts liquid, bring to boil, stir up and lower to simmer. Cover and simmer 30 - 60 minutes or until it smells great.
Adjust for seasonings.

Butter the casserole dish. Push in cubes and mound them like an apple pie.
Ladle over soup to fill, put lid on to squish down cubes of bread. Let sit 5 minutes to soak in.
Repeat process with soup till bread is soaked but not broken down.
Bake in 350F oven uncovered for 30 minutes. This step will puff up the bread and make it light and fluffy.
Bake in oven till cheese gets bubbly and browned on edges.

This is a great, cheap and filling side dish. I served it with breaded chicken breast topped with butter tomato sauce.

Ingredients:
2 tbsp olive oil

6 soft tortillas, cut into 1-inch pieces

Post by: riteus on February 05, 2009, 02:27:35 PM
Sure, it's pretty simple.  The foundation of this steak recipe comes from the Korean BBQ short rib called "Kalbi."

Skirt or flank steaks work just as well for this recipe if only because they have similar meat texture and marble but without the bone.  Please note that I don't have any set recipes and that everything is done by look, feel, smell, and taste, so the exact measurements could be off.

In a large, flat container, add honey (or sugar), light soy sauce (as opposed to dark soy sauce, which is used primarily for stewing in Chinese cuisine, not to be confused with low sodium soy sauce), lots of fresh garlic, and a slight drizzle of sesame oil.  The smell should be a pungent sweet, salty, garlicky aroma with an obvious douse of sesame.  Variations of this recipe often use fresh squeezed orange juice, zest, green onion, ginger, but all of it is unnecessary since most of these flavors are overwhelmed by the garlic and sesame oil anyway.

Take some healthy slabs of steak, remove all silver skin, and place in marinade.  Marinate for 30 minutes to an hour, occasionally switching sides to make sure both sides are flavored (no more or else the meat will get too salty from the soy sauce.  It also depends on how thick your meat is, it's up to your discretion).  The marinade acts as a flavor enhancer but also brines the meat to ensure tenderness.  If the steaks are cold and just coming out of the refrigerator, it's best to marinate them at room temperature so that the meat can come to room temperature while soaking up the flavors, thereby killing two birds with one stone.  It's not a good idea to cook cold steak at high heat anyway because the center always turns out to be undercooked.

Heat a hot skillet as scorching hot as it can get (usually 3~5 minutes on high flame).  Open a window because the searing will cause smoke and smell up the place.  Remove meat from marinade, pat as dry as possible with paper towel.  Place meat on skillet presentation side down (that side will get the best sear).  Sear for 3~5 minutes.  For the first couple of minutes, place the meat on the pan and don't touch or check on them.  After a couple of minutes, the meat will naturally loosen from the pan, meaning it is well-seared.  Check to make sure it's the color you like (usually a dark dark brown with some bits of black char both from heat on the meat and the caramelization of the sugar in the marinade).  Turn meat over and sear other side.  Usually a couple of minutes as well.  Liquid will juice out of the meat, don't worry.  Just let the meat cook till it forms a nice, dark brown crust.  At this point the meat will be a nice medium rare-ish.  Prod the meat and feel for texture (soft as a pillow = rare, some firmness = medium rare, hard as a rock = well done).

Remove meat from skillet, place foil over and let rest for 5~10 minutes. 

Remove fat from the pan.  Reduce flame to medium/low and place marinade liquid into skillet.  There will be a lot of sizzling.  Reduce liquid till a thin syrup consistency.  Turn off heat.  Add unsalted butter to finish.

As for okra, it's even simpler.

Wash and cut okra into small, cross-section slices (the smaller you make them the faster it will cook).  Julien a healthy amount of fresh ginger.  Split green or red chili in half (leave seeds for extra spicy, take out seeds for light spicy).

In a pan, add oil, then okra and chilis.  Season with salt.  When the okra starts to soften, add ginger.  If the okra starts eating up all of the oil, add a little bit more oil.  If the okra you have is older and less tender, add a splash of water, just enough to have some liquid in the bottom of the pan and let simmer while covered for a couple of minutes.  If the okra is nice and tender, you can omit this last step.  Serve immediately.

Also, any kind of stainless steel pan will work as well.  It doesn't have to be cast iron.

And a tip for cleaning pans used for searing:

When the pan is still hot, add room temperature/lukewarm water.  It's very important that you do NOT add cold water or else it could ruin and deform the pan.  As the water sizzles, use a spoon to scrape along the bottom of the pan.  If you were using wine or vinegar, this process would be considered deglazing.  Once you collect all the bits along the bottom into your water concoction, dispose (usually in the sink).  Your pan should be pretty darn clean only requiring a little soap and a light scrub.

Broccoli-Potato-Cheddar Soup

3 medium roundish potatoes, diced
1 big bunch o'broccoli, chopped
vegetable broth
cheddar cheese, shredded
half & half
olive oil
1 red onion, chopped
spices

Sautee most of the onion in a couple of tablespoons of olive oil in a big pot (about 5 min).

Add vegetable broth and some water, filling the pot up about halfway. Bring to a boil.

Add potatoes, spices to taste (salt/pepper at minimum), and some half & half. Simmer for 20 min.

Turn off heat and let cool (add a bit more half & half to cool it a little faster.) Reserve about 1/4 of the soup and blend the rest.

Pour blended soup back into pot, add broccoli and shredded cheddar, and heat up again. Simmer another 5 min or so while stirring.

Serve with lightly toasted slices of Italian bread, wasabi peas, or regular crackers.

Of course, this is the "hard" way; you can buy a box of potato soup and make this a LOT easier. But having the potato chunks is nice.

Onion
Dashi Powder (Japanese kelp and fish based flavoring)
Kabocha (Japanese/Asian) pumpkin
Garlic (1 or 2 cloves max)

So Rick Bayless has been impacting my cooking lately with his Frontera recipes and travels to Mexico in his show "One Plate..."

I feel like I've got a pretty solid grip on authentic Mexican now, including being able to differentiate most of the peppers along with their flavor profiles.  Tonight, I'm making a Yucatecan specialty: cochinita pibil with pickled onions...except I'm making it my way, not Bayless's.

First off, I used dried pasilla peppers reconstituted with stems and seeds removed.  I toasted cumin seeds, peppercorn, Mexican oregano, and nutmeg.  I ground the spices in my spice grinder and popped all of it into a food processor along with fresh garlic and ginger.  This created a dark, mole-colored paste.  I then marinated a pork shoulder roast for two days in this paste.  I saved the pasilla pepper water.

Fast forward to today: I lined my pot with banana leaves, added the marinated pork into the pot along with ripe yellow plantains and filled the pot with the pasilla pepper water mixed with two generous tablespoons of brown sugar.  I also added a generous portion of salt since my paste had no salt in it.  Lastly, I doused off my creation with some rum and topped it off with more banana leaves.

The meat will slow simmer for 4 hours until it is shreddable at which point I will mix the shredded meat into its own juices and serve alongside with crema fresca, steamed corn tortillas, and pickled red onions mixed with lime juice and serrano chillis.

My version of a Bayless recipe.  A little different, a little richer, but hopefully just as satisfying.

Mott Street Chinese Mushroom Soup

Ingredients (in proportion)
5 dried Shiitake Mushrooms (buy the more expensive variety they have more flavor)
5 cups Water
1 bunch green onions (finely minced)
2 cloves of garlic (finely minced)
1 bouillon cube (I use Bao Long Vi Bo Kho cubes from Viet Nam)
1 tsp black pepper
1 tsp sea (or kosher) salt
1 Tbsp Soy Sauce
1 Tbsp Sesame Oil (I prefer Sesame Chile Oil)
2 Tbsp Black Bean/Garlic Sauce
1 Tbsp of coarse ground Chile Powder (I use either Korean of Vietnamese--piment miett or Korean--Gochutgaru)
20 Raw peanuts (goober peas), shelled no need to discard the husks
2 oz Tofu of choice (I tend to use flavored pressed tofu such as su ji or spiced pressed tofu with soy sauce, but any tofu will be a welcome addition) cubed into small pieces
2 Handfulls of Bean Sprouts
1 handfull of Bamboo shoots, sliced into short threads

Directions
(add water as needed to maintain a soup)

1. Add green onions, garlic, black pepper, and salt to 5 cups water. Bring to a partial boil. Allow to cool to the

temperature of warm tap water

2. Add 5 Shiitake Mushroom to warm water and allow to soak for 30 minutes (or until rehydrated) to create a basic broth

3. Remove Mushrooms, stem as needed, and return to basic broth

4. Add Soy Sauce, Sesame Oil, Chile Powder, Black Bean Sauce, and peanuts to broth and simmer for 30 minutes

5. Add Tofu to the broth, continue simmerring

6. Add Bean Sprouts and bamboo shoots, continue simmering until the peanuts have the texture of a frozen pea.

Cypriot Sour Trahanas Soup

Bought a package of sour trahanas from Mediterranean Foods out of pure curiosity & did some experimenting - good results if you like a tangy, yogurt-like sour-ish flavor to a very hearty soup. 

Dump bag of trahanas into bowl & covered with tap water & set aside.  In large stock pot, boil a dozen chicken legs in salted water with shallots, celery & carrots for about 1 1/2 hours until broth is rich & meat was falling off the bones (skim as needed).  Remove chix legs from stock and add in trahanas (which by now should have absorbed water & become like an oatmeal consistency).  Stir to combine & heat on low stirring often to prevent sticking to bottom of pot.  Shred meat & add back into soup.  Add salt/pepper to taste & ladle into bowls.  I like to top the bowl with a small pat of butter & sprinkle with dried mint - really wakes up the flavor.

I run several meet up groups and we hold our meetings at Bella Napoli on Madison in Midtown--an excellent and relatively cheap Italian Restaurant that serves up great Italian fare at a very low price. 
I'm a huge fan of their Straiatella alla Romana soup and thought that I would try to recreate on the cheap (it's the economy you know...) this evening with a little help from the W3. 
I can't wait for lunch tomorrow, because I think that I nailed it.

So, Here goes:

Stratiatella Soup for one or two:

Ingredients
  • 2 cups chicken broth (you can also use 2 cups of water and a Maggi bouillon cube)
  • Half a box of frozen chopped spinach
  • 1 egg--preferably not laid in an Astoria apartment
  • A few generous pinches of grated peccorino romano cheese (I substituted Asagio)
Spices
  • A couple of pinches of custom Italian seasoning blend (basil, thyme, oregano, marjoram, parsley)
  • A shake of of garlic powder
  • A bit of crushed dried red pepper to taste
  • Salt and Black Pepper to taste
Method

In a sauce pan:
  • Raise the chicken stock and spices to rapid boil
  • Reduce heat to a medium boil and add the chopped frozen spinach
  • Once the Spinach is thawed out and cooked reduce the heat to a medium simmer
  • Add a cracked egg and stir vigorously to create boiled egg threads in the broth
  • Add the cheese and thicken the broth
  • Serve or reserve
Time to prepare:
About 10 minutes
