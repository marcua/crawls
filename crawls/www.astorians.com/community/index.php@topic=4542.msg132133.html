I would also like to point out that the condition I have is called a food aversion, but if you say you have a food aversion people think either you have a fear of the food or that you simply don't like it.
I really didn't want to belabor this any longer in thread, but you seem to have a need for us to understand. So, let me tell you what I know about this "allergy" you call food aversion.

Food aversion is psychological avoidance and psychological intolerance caused by emotions associated with food rather than the food itself. Unlike the medical conditions known as food allergies or food intolerances, symptoms of food aversion do not appear when the food is given in an unrecognizable form. The reaction is purely psychological and it can be difficult to impossible to convince a person with a food aversion that they're not allergic to a particular food. A person diagnosed with food aversion displays a psychological repugnance to (certain) (some) (all) foods that are purely provoked by emotions associated with the food rather than by any chemical properties either within the food or any chemical responses from within the body of the person so afflicted/diagnosed. Anorexia nervosa is regarded by many medical specialists as an extreme or exacerbated form of food aversion.

Knowing this saved my life when I was an undergrad. I developed food aversion and became quite ill with Anorexia. The Psychiatrist who treated me respected my inane intelligence and gave me facts to support why I was dealing with food the way I was. He told me that I was the only one who could save me from myself. The first thing he did was to send me to a Board Certified Allergist who specialized in eating disorders. I was tested for things that I didn't even know existed. At the end of the testing period I was given a fact sheet of those items to which I had actual food allergies. These foods were to be avoided at all costs during my recovery period so as to not interfere with my progress. At the time, I was lucky to be surrounded by family and friends who were 100% supportive of me. Each 'light bulb' moment helped me to recover more quickly than had been anticipated. I still have things to which I am considered extremely allergic. I just don't feel a need to make any issues out of them. I simply avoid eating them whenever possible, and am always prepared to treat an allergic reaction when taken off guard.

I don't know where you were diagnosed, but I certainly would seek another diagnosis to help you fortify a healthier future.

If I had known, in the late 60s, that allergies were genetically inherited, I might have had second thoughts about having children. It is such a terrible thing to have to live with, but you don't have food allergies, you have a psychological disorder known as food aversion which, unfortunately, manifests itself as a quality-of-life physical problem.
