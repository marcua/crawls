I came here to post a ground beef thing I had for dinner last night and it is really kind of similar to the beef picadillo recipe that IrishJess posted. Anyway, here goes:

Yesterday I started craving ground beef around lunchtime. On my way home I got a cheap package of ground chuck and some mushrooms. I think this is what you would call "hash".

Brown ground beef and one diced onion. Drain off the grease (or rinse the meat in a strainer if you really want to get the fat off). Set aside.

Sautee diced green pepper, chopped scallions, garlic, diced potato, currants, and sliced mushrooms. I threw all of this stuff in together, I later wished I had cooked the mushrooms first, then added the other stuff. Toss it around a bit, add some spices (paprika, chili powder), cover and add a little water. When the potatoes are tender, add the beef, some chopped fresh parsley and dill, and a few canned tomatoes with juice. You don't want to make it into a tomato sauce, just add a little flavor. Maybe a quarter or half a cup. Salt, pepper, adjust seasonings.

this would be really good wrapped in a tortilla, stuffed in a pita, or over rice. I had it as-is, in a bowl with a glop of yogurt on top.

Great thread, and definitely one I could use as a 20-something!

Here's a few "frugal" things I do:
1) Save money. I have a Vanguard IRA Roth that I opened a few years ago and while it's depressing to look at right now, in a few years it will be up again, since the economy is always cyclical. I also have a 401(k) through work where I allot 7% of my paycheck and my job matches 3%. Lastly, I have a general savings account through ING Direct, though I've been touching it a bit more lately than I'd like (moving and whatnot).
2) Avoid credit cards. I paid off my debt and now I only use it for small purchases that I can pay back within a few weeks. I heard that it's good to always keep it active so that's what I'm trying to do minimally.
3) Pack lunch, eat in more. I've found that if I have no cash in my wallet, I'm less likely to splurge on snacks and food in the city. It kind of serves as a "hint hint, you didn't put cash in here for a reason" to my brain.
4) Go to the library for books, CDs, etc. Ends up saving me a lot of money. I also use Netflix and try to only go to the movies as matinees.

Here are my "least frugal" expenses:
1) The cost of food. I'm a vegetarian and I try to eat healthy -- but organic food is a lot of money. I save money from buying healthy and cooking vs going out regularly, but not as much as I would if I were content to live on mac & cheese and ramen. It's a tricky situation.
2) Girly stuff. I go to the salon every 6-8 weeks and I try and get a facial once every 6-8 weeks. Spas and salons are a lot of money so I try not to drastically overdo it, but what can I say, I love them.
3) Travel. I love NYC but if I don't get away from it fairly often, I start to feel bonkers. I just got back from a trip to Austin, where I had somewhere to stay, so that saved some money. I also try and take weekend trips when I can every few months. Staying put for long stretches of time is not an option for me, but I recognize there's ways to lower costs by visiting friends, sharing rooms, etc.
4) My cat. I adore the little hairball but pet expenses sure do add up. Wouldn't trade it for the world though.

So those are my good and my not-so-good lists. I pretty much spend one paycheck a month on rent/bills and the other on assorted things throughout the month, so I just about breakeven when all is said and done. I'm good at long-term saving plans and avoiding debt, but I find it very hard to save liquid cash. My paychecks pretty much go up in smoke minus the 401k allotments.

I did this the other day and cut down on my grocery bills this month. I tend to "shop" when I go to the grocery instead of only picking up what I need:

Look in your cupboards, fridge and freezer.
Take stock of everything you have.
Come up with as many dinner options as you can based on what is there with very little to buy, less than $20.
Write down a menu list for the week, or however long it takes you to eat what's there.
Check off each meal as you go.

I did this and I had over a week of meals!

Costco is my greatest grocery shopping friend. I get meat for the freezer there: a lamb roast gets cut in thirds and makes three meals for two of us - sometimes with a leftover serving for lunch the next day. A pork loin or large package of chops gets divided into freezer bags and makes multiple meals. Same for chicken, beef roasts, ground beef, etc. MUCH cheaper than buying one meal at a time and good quality.
I do the same for veggies: yesterday I got a huge bag of spinach, four cups for a soup I was making, another 3-4 cups went in the refrigerator for salads or a pesto later this week, the rest filled two freezer bags and went in the freezer. I can pull out a handfull whenever I want some hidden veggie in whatever I'm making. I also bought a bag of green beans; I'll make a couple of servings fresh and put the rest in baggies in the freezer.
Also their huge boxes of lettuce - even if you don't eat it all, its still MUCH cheaper than buying a bag or small box at a grocery store.
And the same thing for paper products, although they are a little harder to store. But the cost of a 12 pack of paper towels is about the same as 4 or 5 rolls at Duane Reade! Stick them under the bed if you have to.

And I agree with the person who said clean out your cupboards before you buy anything more... My mom can make a meal with what seems to me almost nothing - it's that Depression era mentality and we're going to have it too one of these days...

This is a recipe I love - it's super cheap, great for cold weather, and need very little attention thanks to the crockpot! It's originally from Slow Cooker Cooking by Lora Brody.

French Onion Soup

3 pounds Vidalia or other sweet onions - peeled and cut into 1/8-1/4" thick slices
1 stick butter
6 cups beef, chicken, or vegetable stock
Salt and pepper, to taste
Bread of choice (I prefer a crusty french baguette)
Cheese of choice (I do a mix of swiss and locatelli cheeses)

Put the onions and butter in the slow cooker for 12-14 hours on low. They should be a deep dark brown mahogany and very soft. Afterwards, drain the onions, reserving the liquid that has accumulated. (After you let the liquid cool, you can skim the congealed butter from the top of the onion broth and use it to flavor another dish). Then combine onions, cooking liquid, and stock in a pot, bring to a simmer over medium-high heat, and season with salt and pepper to taste. Then ladle soup into ovenproof bowls, float a slice of bread in it, top with cheese, and put in the broiler until the cheese melts and starts to bubble. It only takes 1-2 minutes in my broiler. Enjoy!

Since I make my own chicken stock and always have cheese and butter in stock, it usually only costs me around 7 dollars to make a huge potful of soup.

I shop nearly every night on my way home, since I like to buy fresh produce. Before it goes bad, freeze it- currently, I've got 3/4 of a baguette in the freezer, plus a Ziploc full of sliced mushrooms, broccoli, and other leftover vegetables. They thaw more quickly than frozen vegetables (does that make sense?!)

Had some chopped potatoes leftover from last night's soup, put those in the 'fridge- they'll make good home fries or something.

Always have wheat bread at home so I can make pb&j sandwiches for work. That saves a bunch. As would eating breakfast at home, but my morning coffee/donut or eggs on a roll........can't seem to break that habit!!

Speaking of which, instead of a diner brunch, I'm just fine with the $2 deli special on the weekends- coffee and eggs/roll (no meat.) It's delicious and satisfying, though the coffee isn't the best.

I've cut back severely on bottled drinks, but when I do splurge, I water them down (drink half/add water.) Sometimes, I take the empty bottle home and make my own herbal iced tea, not bad!

Also- mojito or margarita mix in a bottle. $3-4 a bottle gets you a couple weeks' worth of drinks (I hate plain water unless I'm working out in warm weather.) A small bit of the mix in water suits me just fine.

As for shopping- rarely, unless there's a sale or I really need stuff. I'm a "return-ista"; if I spend too much, I can't stand the guilt.

I have an $80 couch (not including delivery) from the antique/thrift place nearby; a gently-used Jennifer convertible that I paid $50 for somebody to help move; Ikea loft bed (twin, b/c I already had a small mattress) for $125, holding up great after 9 years; two storage ottomans for $110 each from QVC; bookshelves from the Ikea scratch-and-dent section (at least 50% off).......

OH, and cars. I inherit them before they go to the dump, and are surprisingly reliable despite the occasional high maintenance bill. Not much to look at, though! The car I have now was dented before; just got MORE dented again while parked. But there's a certain comfort to driving a car that you don't need to worry about.

I'm also not a gear-head, as it's crazy to spend $$$ on electronics that are quickly obselete. Has happened enough that I'm sick of it!!

What else, what else....student haircuts, henna instead of chemical/professional coloring; homemade facial scrubs; no maincures (they don't last) but the occasional pedicure is an occasional indulgence.
