I suppose my reasoning for posting the above article is related to an apex of financial events that I know I'm not alone in experiencing.  I've lived in Astoria for 7 years now.  I've had some career advances, and in some ways life has become more comfortable.  But at in the last couple of years, despite those career advances, everything is getting more expensive.  I'm getting my annual raise at work in Feb, but it is already all accounted for because of the things I list below.  So my standard of living is staying level at best.  My health insurance premium is going up significantly, my co-pay has just gone up to $25/visit so I can't afford to go to physical therapy, my lease renews in April, and despite what is still a very good deal on my apartment, it's still a $100 increase for a 2 year renewal.  Food is more expensive. Etc...  And let's not forget living the American dream of credit card debt (which, yes, is my own doing).

I guess I am frustrated because just about everyone I know is feeling like we're all getting squeezed despite working hard, and doing what we're "supposed" to be doing to improve our lives and better our standard of living.    But it's only getting harder every year.  If things keep going in this direction, I just don't see how staying in New York is feasible unless I were to live in somewhere like East New York, which I'm just not willing to do because of the commute.  I've lived in Astoria for 7 years, and feel like I've contributed something.  But I'm getting squeezed, and pretty much feel a good bit helpless in the situation.  I don't get the impression the Mayor or the city gov't really cares about making it easier for people to maintain a residence in a city they help support and pay taxes to.

Something as seemingly harmless as section 82 really can have a disastrous effect on people for the benefit of landlords.  But I haven't heard of anything being done to change that.

Ok, that's all for now.  That's my rant.  Thanks for listening.  But I'd like to hear other stories.  I find it interesting, and I take solice in knowing I'm not alone.

Does anyone believe their standard of living and well-being is tied to the bonuses of others?

I see a connection between high bonuses and higher demand for and willingness to pay higher rents, real estate costs, and other living expenses that make NYC one of the most expensive places to live in the country.  The majority of people in NYC do not receive large bonuses and yet have to cope with the rising costs of living that stem in part from the bonuses.

There is a fair amount of consensus that high bonuses for a few affect overall real estate values.

New York Magazine:  Wall Street Bonus Drop Presaging Real-Estate Meltdown?:

Quote
The annual sky-high Wall Street bonuses are falling — by an estimated 10 percent this year. . . . What does this mean for NYC real estate, a market now accustomed to massive infusions of bonus cash?

NY Sun:  Street Bonuses Support Hamptons Rush:

Quote
luxury real estate — $10 million or higher — which could soon make a splash in selected well-to-do areas thanks to an estimated $38 billion in year-end Wall Street bonuses. . . . recent surveys in these locations show housing prices up markedly.

Discussion of bonuses vs. Manhattan real estate values from a NY real estate blog:  True Gotham.

Another real estate blog with a somewhat detailed analysis:  Only Bonus Babies Really Know The Fine Print.  Note the chart listing Wall St. bonuses from 1985 through 2007, which is from the NY Comptroller's office and can be found on their website here. [PDF]

In this article on the Prudential Elliman site, one of their brokers is quoted as saying, "Thank God for bonus season."

Another blog from a real estate broker: 

Quote
What does Wall Street Bonuses do [for] New York real estate? If history repeats itself like it always does Wall Street bonuses fuel Manhattan real estate.

Finally, from the Wall Street Journal -  Wall Street Bonus Picture: Rich Instead of Very Rich:

Quote
That isn't just a downer for Wall Street professionals but might also be a drag on New York's hot real-estate market. The New York housing market is propped up by young Wall Street employees doling out millions of dollars for condos and luxury homes, thanks to their hefty bonuses.

Finally -- a chart, adjusted for inflation, comparing Manhattan prices per square foot with Wall St. bonuses.

Every single person living in NYC has been affected by the effect of large bonuses on real estate prices, including the majority who do not get those large bonuses.  We all end up paying more for our real estate costs, whether we rent or own, which lessens the amount of disposable income we have, which affects our standard of living.

Quote from: Sweeper on January 23, 2008, 01:48:13 PM
Correlation is not causation.

I'm well aware of that.  My input to this thread is not meant to be an exhaustive analysis of the issues.  However, the chart is consistent with the other statements and observations I came across in my brief research.

Quote
I find it hard to believe that I am competing with hedge fund managers for living space in Astoria. Like everyone I worry about my, and my families, standard of living. But bonuses on Wall Street have nothing to do with my standard of living.

You are missing my point.  I have no idea how many hedge fund managers live in Astoria; it's immaterial whether there are zero, one, or hundreds.  In the broader picture, though, we are all competing for real estate space in New York City.  Increase in real estate prices in Astoria reflects an overall increase in the market that is at least in part driven by these huge bonuses.  That inflates the top and, and to turn a phrase, real estate prices abhor a vacuum - it's a general trend upward and Astoria is not exempt from that.

Quote from: Hrmph on January 23, 2008, 02:03:28 PM
And many of the non-wall street people in the city have realized an appreciation in their real estate as a result of this as well, so not everyone is hurt by it.

I think that is true - if you are a real estate owner.  However, a majority of New Yorkers are renters - home ownership in NYC is 33% city-wide, and only 18.1% in Astoria (statistics are found in the links below from the Furman Center).  And we all know from this board and from our own experiences and that of our friends', that rents have also gone up sharply in Astoria.

And of course there are cycles and no, I'm not trying to blame it all on Wall Street.  But large corporate/financial sector bonuses are certainly a large source of the fuel.

The NYU Furman Center for Real Estate and Urban Policy contains a great deal of information on the State of New York City's Housing and Neighborhoods 2006.

Quote
Unlike the national income distribution of homeowners, which rises close to the median and falls off at higher levels of income, New York City homeowners are heavily concentrated in the upper income brackets. One third of the City’s homeowners earn more than $100,000 per year, compared to only 22 percent at the national level. This does not reflect the income distribution for all New York households, since only 17 percent of all households earn more than $100,000 a year. The data suggest that homeownership in New York City is less of a middle-class reality than in the rest of the country.

Quote
Housing Price Appreciation and Income Trends
Home prices in New York City have escalated rapidly in recent years, with the average real annual appreciation rate between 2000 and 2005 exceeding 10 percent, more than three times the City’s annual average of 3 percent in the 25 years prior to 2000. Today, home prices for all housing types in New York City have reached record highs, with a median sale price of $480,000 citywide in 2005, up from $285,805 in 2000; after adjusting for inflation, this represents a 68 percent increase in only five years.

So how does this fit with income and bonuses?  This is really going beyond what I have the time to look into, but I don't think it's out of line to draw a connection between the huge bonuses and soaring real estate prices in NYC.

The Economic Mobility Project, a "nonpartisan collaboration of The Pew Charitable Trusts and respected thinkers from four leading policy institutes — The American Enterprise Institute, The Brookings Institution, The Heritage Foundation and The Urban Institute" has extensive information on income and economic mobility.  Median family (numbers taken from people in their 30's) income grew by 9% between 1974 and 2004, only because women's wages increased, although they still make less than men.  Men's wages fell 12% over the same period.

That is across the board.  But according to the Congressional Budget office, the after-tax income of the top 1 percent rose 228 percent from 1979 through 2005 (see this Bob Herbert column).  After-tax income of the top 3% was $369,800, and the top 1% was $1,071,500 in 2005 (from this CBO report [PDF].  According to the New York State Comptroller's Office [PDF], "The average salary on Wall Street surged by 17 percent in 2006 to a record $339,910. The average salary in the rest of the City’s economy grew by only 5 percent to $59,530."

I've also found indications of some tantalizing indirect links that focus not on the direct impact of such bonuses, but rather on the bonuses as a symptom of the risky investing and lending structures that led the large financial institutions to post record-breaking profits that were spurred in part by the hot housing market and what has become the subprime lending debacle.  See this recent article from the Wall Street Journal.  I'm finding this very interesting, but I just don't have the time right now to investigate further.
