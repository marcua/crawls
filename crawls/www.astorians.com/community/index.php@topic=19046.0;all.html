That's probably part of it. Marino's seemed to me to put quality ahead of selection, and way ahead of customer service. Their hours probably dramatically limited their business (as other long, contested threads can attest to!). I also think that NYCMacUser's point about the increasing cost of fish is insightful. As overfishing has caused supplies of many fish to dwindle, and prices to increase over the decades, and as crop subsidies have slashed the cost of terrestrial proteins, it becomes more difficult for high-quality fishmongers to compete. Remember that lobsters used to be thought of as nearly inedible! Prisons used to be prevented from serving lobster to inmates too often, as it was thought of as cruel and unusual punishment! That's not the case anymore, and I suspect the fish industry, from sea to retailer, will see lots more stress in coming decades...

NYCmac, That particular topic did come up numerous times over the years but the did not want to do it.  I do know for a fact that a few factors really hurt them:
Recession over past 5 years.
Wholesale clients going out of business, needing to go cheap or just going to bigger operations like restaurant depot for one stop shopping.
The old neighborhood that died off or moved away had been their mainstay for decades.

Also Marino's ownership may have just been too old to change their ways.  Too bad.  They are all looking for other work on Long island where they live. 

As for catering to the younger crowd, it was more than just the hours.  I know for a fact that a number of old school shops feel that the only way to get the younger crowd in would be to switch to single serve items raw or cooked.  Single theme shops like a butcher or bakery or fish store just can't compete with whole foods for marketing, packaging, mass offerings and gourmet prepared selections.

Of course in areas of Brooklyn many young foodies ARE flocking to single themed shops for top notch artisanal products such as bakeries, cheese and butchers so go figure.  Astoria is not there yet and then it will be too late.

International meats is one of the old school shops that jumped forward, made the renovations, packaging and service changes to attract more wholesale and retail clients.  Cudos to them. 

I am bringing my son over to see Benny this week and say goodbye.  They've asked about him ever day since he was born.  It's going to kill me.

I used to buy the shell-on deveined ones from Costco, which are great for New Orleans style barbecue shrimp, but the frozen raw (not cooked) Kirkland brand in 2 lb. bags are wonderful for tempura or scampi. No smell or off taste whatsoever-very fresh tasting.  I believe all shrimp from the fish mongers-no matter where you go- are previously frozen, then thawed, so might as well buy frozen to begin with. Best Yet's boneless chuck pot roast is great. I add some tawny port in the pot and we feast on red meat for three days...ugghh! Short ribs I've given up on because a few times I've had to trim off too much fat which is always on the underside of the rib, hidden from view until you open the package and flip 'em over. Their rib lamb chops are very good, but stay away from the manager's special! I feel sorry for the customer who has to resort to buying green meat to save a dollar.
