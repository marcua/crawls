I don't know about the prevalence of the Al-Queda/Taliban-type of Muslims in Astoria, but I do know that long before 9/11, there was and still remains a large, very vibrant Muslim Bengali community around 36th Avenue, in particular the area west of the N train.  Bengali's from all over the area and sometimes the region, as my friend who owned one of the establishments informed me, go there to socialize and eat. 

Before 9/11 and as long as I can remember the area, there has been two mosques on both sides of the 36th Avenue N stop and they serve a very diverse group of Muslims in the area.

From my own experience, which includes hanging out in the homes of pretty conservative Yemen and Bengali Muslims (hope nobody reports me), I find many of the even conservative Muslims, at least before the latest Iraq war, to be fairly reasonable and willing to adapt to American society, as long as it does not compromise their faith.  In other words, they are not going to have beers with the guys after work.  It doesn't mean that they are going to commit violence against the bars. 

And the Muslims that I knew didn't want to start a jihad in America.  In fact, you would be surprised how much they have in common with fundamentalist Christians.  Again, with the point about broad generalizations being fresh in my mind and on this board, I would emphasize that this does not apply to all people of Muslim faith.  I'm simply relaying my own experience and I'm sure other people have had their own negative experiences.

Regarding the overall picture of the Middle Eastern and South Asian population in America, which again is hard to generalize.  I believe that the latest studies, which I don't have available right now, but I will try to find, show that they are solidly middle class and educated, unlike many of the European Muslim South Asian, North African and Middle Eastern population.  I think a lot of it has to do with racial and ethnic conflicts there, although I could be totally off.  While the US is far from being perfect, I don't think we have the same entrenched social issues and many of the Muslim faith who live here, no matter where they are from, are not radicalized through class, racial, religious and/or ethnic divides. 

Pardon the long post.
