Here's my best description, which will probably need help.

Basically, the coins are made of precious metals (silver, nickel, copper, etc.) and are worth a base amount because, in theory, you could melt them down and have that metal.  This price will vary, in general, as the price of the goes up or down, but it is highly likely it is going hold it's value.

Back before Nixon and 1971, a dollar was called a "promissory note."  You were able to, if you wanted to, take a walk down to a U.S. Treasury vault and trade in your dollars for a specific amount of gold (1/35th of an ounce per $1).  This is part of the reason why things were so much cheaper when you were a kid.  $1 had much more value, inflation was in better check, and the government was unable to just "print more money" (which floods the market and causes inflation.  Read about Germany before WWII, when their government did this and you had to take wheelbarrows of money to buy a loaf of bread).

Now, in 1971, Nixon took us off the gold standard so that he could pay back debts for the Vietnam war (somewhere in the neighborhood of $500 billion dollars).  He made the US Dollar based off of, basically, credit.  It is now based off of of the potential of the US economy (it's goods, it's holdings, etc.).  Something that is nowhere near as tangible.  Also, it allows the government to print more as they please (and as they are doing now) because, basically, the money isn't worth ANYTHING.  There is nothing to back it.

Here is a very interesting article about it that explains the money system in a little more detail and might open your eyes about the problems with things being the way they are.  It also talks about, perhaps, why we are in Iraq NOW and why we're getting aggressive with Iran.  I don't know the author or his views, so I'm not claiming it is 100% truth or not exaggerated.  However, it does pose some VERY interesting points and is DEFINITELY worth reading.  Even if you aren't a supporter or don't believe it when you're done, it's something to take a look at.

http://www.mondovista.com/money.html

It also is another good reason, in my opinion, to take a look at Ron Paul.  He sees what this "flat currency" is doing to society and intends to do everything he can to stop it, including putting us back on the gold standard.

I'll try.... :-D

Basically the dollar bills in your wallet are a promise from the Federal Government that they will honor the value printed on them. They back that promise with hard currency reserves like the gold locked away in Fort Knox. However -- and I might be mistaken -- I believe that even though your bill says $10, the government is not completely beholden to honor that promise to give you 'ten dollars' should you demand it.

In other words (though this is highly unlikely here, but it has happened in other countries), the government can say that they don't have enough gold to back up that 10-spot, along with the gajillion others in people's pockets, and make it of less real value. Like $8.50, or $3.12 or what have you. That's when people start dumping (er, exchanging) US currency for either another one (Yen, Deutschmarks, Pounds Sterling, etc.) or for literally cold hard cash -- ie gold and silver.

But still, your average American keeps the faith with the government that the dollar is worth something. Otherwise, folks would be ditching it for fear of it collapsing entirely. YOu'd see rampant devaluation and the dollar would become something like the Russian Ruble in the 1990s or the Brazilian Real in the 1980s. IMO, faith in the dollar not near breaking point, but we're approaching it and I fear most of us will be shocked when it does happen.  :|

The value of what that guy was minting wasn't based on a promise of value, but an actual, measurable market value at a given time for the metals contained. One could argue that that isn't necessarily predictable since market prices go up and down, but then again, how much more could you buy from a tenner 20, 30 years ago than you could now? And with the dollar losing value vis-a-vis other currencies, that 10 bucks is less promising elsewhere in the world.

And SeminoleB makes some excellent posts -- and that article is an interesting read too. Thanks for the link.....

I don;'t know enough about the gold standard, and on the surface, it may seem like a good idea. However, I believe that the risk is currency instability since gold can be hoarded and that there's a finite supply of it. Accordingly, gold is an inadequate measure the growth of wealth.
