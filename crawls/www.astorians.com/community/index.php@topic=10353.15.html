Quote from: essen on May 07, 2008, 09:53:19 PM
You know, I like Freezepeach. The place has a nice feel to it and their drinks are delicious. But this all seems a bit strange - how is it more of a "community service" than any other friendly local business? If they want to ask for donations or even have a Freezepeach fund raiser that's fine with me, but I don't get how it's a community service. You have to pay for the drinks and the wifi, right?

I think the FP is one of the most community oriented business I've ever been to--either in Astoria, or even back in Michigan.  Several local groups meet there currently, including Astoria Crafty Knitty, Astorians for Peace, and Astorian Vinyl Enthusiasts.  In the past several other groups, like the Astorian Sandwich Society, and Hellgate CSA have also used the Freezepeach as a contact point. From my conversations with Meesh, I know the focus has always been on making the FP a part of the greater community here in Astoria, and that why so many local groups have found a happy home there.

As I said before, this is obviously totally voluntary.  If you don't want to donate, you certainly don't have to.  For those of us who feel that FP is a great community or a great resource for our community, this is an opportunity to support it.  Frankly, patronage is even more desirable to the FP.  This just for those of us who feel like they want to do a bit more right now.

Hi,

For 10 years I ran a non-profit and it was a community service but we still had to pay the bills.   I find that Freeze Peach is a community service because it's a gathering place in Astoria.   I've met many interesting people there, feel like it's a home away from home.   It's livingroom atmosphere says a lot to me.  It's comfortable. 

No one, and I do mean no one, has Chais like Freeze Peach or teas like them as well.   They open the place to community meetings - like the Astoria Crafty Knitty's and other groups as well.  What other restaurant or business so willingly allows groups to hang out there.   It's not just a coffe bar or tea bar like Starbucks.   It's not just a place to to go for the WiFi.   It's a lot more.  It's a community.

That's very special to me.  Otherwise I'd have to hang at Starbucks - yuccchhh! (make that super yuccchh!)- or stay home all the time and be a hermit.

Owning or running a business is not an easy thing.   You have the general public to deal with and that is a thing in itself.

More later,

Hi,

This is about Freeze Peach, the little, lovely community feel cafe on 29th Street near Ditmars Boulevard.

I hear what you are saying about the paypal donate thing.  Whatever you can do, if you want to stop by and have tea all the better.   They need any supoort they can get right now.

I am here right now and the Ch'i has definately changed . . . if you believe in that stuff . . . .   It's amazing!  Fiona, one of the great baristas here is making a real effort to brighten up the place, there are Christmas lights on a pole in the back and they got rid of the big round table in the middle.  There are no more magazines all over the place.  It's quiet but it's nice. 

I think we should end this here.   Just come by and get one of the best Chai's I've ever had.  Did you know that Chai doesn't mean sugar?  Starbucks certainly doesn't.   You can get it with honey, sugar, agave here.   You can also choose your milk.   Soy, Heavy Cream, Half & Half, Whole Milk, Lowfat, Skim.   How many cafes offer Agave nectar?  O.k. maybe I don't know my cafes.

I hope to see you here.   I'm the one in the armchair in the back drinking Chai.

Just adding my two cents to comments from some of the earlier posters about Freezepeach being a very community-oriented establishment.  I'm sure most of the other longtime "Peachies" will agree that so many times the cafe felt like a second home, with a warm, friendly vibe and a group of people who felt like "family".  As a matter of fact, once I had been away for a while because I was sick with a bad cold.  I was reassured (I think by e-mail--ahh these senior moments) that I was part of the "Freezepeach family" and could contact the cafe if I needed someone to pick up medicine or anything else.  How's that for "community"?  Places like this are rare anywhere today and needed to be supported by our patronage and yes, our donations.
