1. Don't overlook a portable fan or two to keep the air moving around.  Especially if you don't have a lot in the way of westward-facing windows (that's where the prevailing winds are here)

The AC makes a huge difference in cost. In the cooler weather, my electric bill for my 1BR apartment was in the $60s every month. I had my 8000BTU air conditioner put in during the hottest period of the summer, and even though I was only turning it on when I got home from work, leaving it on overnight, and then turning it off when I went to work, my bill jumped to $140. Granted, mine is an old AC unit and a newer one would probably be more efficient, but your AC usage is probably what's doing it.

After seeing that jump in cost, I stopped using my AC unless I have guests over or it's over 90 degrees outside, or over 75 at night because I can't sleep when it's that hot. I rarely had to use it in August, and my most recent bill was closer to $110- still raised because I used fans a lot and I had to turn off the energy saver mode on my fridge because it was getting condensation on the outside, but better than it was before.

If you become more sparing in your use of the AC, you will see your bill drop significantly. It really helps to wear very light clothes around the house and to keep a box fan running if it's stuffy in the apartment. They sell cheap ones at the big drug stores.

I bought an meter that reads how much wattage is used by anything that is run by electricity.  You plug the meter between the thing you want to check and the wall outlet and it will tell you how many kW the unit is (or has) used over however long you leave it plugged in.  It's called a Kill-O-Watt.  Bought mine from Amazon, but I've see them at Home Depot, Lowe's, etc.  The toaster oven draws the most electric, then the toaster.  Of course you don't leave these running all the time, so something like the fridge or AC unit will cost you the most to run, since they are on a good deal of the time.
