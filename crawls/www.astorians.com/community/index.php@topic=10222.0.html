Depends what you want to do, there are three basic styles for adults (Barring specialty bikes, like BMX or Trials):
Road, Mtn and Hybrid.
Road bikes have a more hunched over position and are more suited for efficiency and long distance riding.
Mountain bikes are generally for trails, although a lot of people use them in thc city because they are generally more sturdy and easier to handle.
Hybrid bikes are a more upright position, built for comfort and more suited for street and recreational riding.

Head down to your local shop, I'm sure they'll help talk you through the details and find the bike that's right for you.

Quote from: paratactical on April 21, 2008, 10:15:42 AM
Don't buy a fixed gear bike. They're the ones that to brake, you push backwards--like the bikes you rode as a kid. While they're pretty popular, it's a lot safer for an inexperienced rider to have handle bar brakes and a few gears for the hills.

Actually, they're not like the ones you had when you were a kid. The ones you had when you were a kid had a coasting function. Meaning if the bike was going forward, your pedals could stay still. On a fixed gear bike, your pedaling is locked into the motion of the wheel- very dangerous for an inexperienced rider. If the bike is moving forward on a fixed gear, so are your pedals. To slow down, you don't simply pedal backwards, you have to slow the pedals down gradually or lock the pedals up to cause a skid. Good call on the no fixed gear thing, though- it's terrible for inexperienced riders.

You can tell it's a fixed gear bike if teh back wheel spins backward when you push the pedal backwards.  Most bikes have what is called a freewheel, which allows you to coast and pedal backwards without affecting the forward motion of the back tire. 
If you're going to a big box store you won't find a fixed gear bike.  However, I highly recommend going to a bike store, for a few reasons.  First the bike will be put together by someone who regularly assembles bikes, not an $8 an hour stock person.  Secondly, most bike shops offer a 2 or 3 year warranty, so if you need a tune up, or your wheels trued, they'll do it for free.  Third, is that a bike shop is more apt to fit a bike to your body correctly.  If a bike is too big or too small, riding it can be very uncomfortable.    Last, there are still deals to be found at a bike shops.  My first road bike in NYC I picked up for under $300 because it was last year's model.  Sportsman Cycles in LIC typically does a HUGE sale on bikes that they buy, build and rent for the Bike NY 5 Boro Tour that are great bikes.  Last year my better half picked up a really nice Fuji for $250, and the tour is only a few weeks away.  You can also check out Bikes By George in the East Village, for some good used bikes.

Quote from: jackinthebox on April 21, 2008, 06:13:19 PM
I own a mountain bike and both my front and back brakes are squeaking when I use them.  Any recommendations?

It's probably one of two things- they are either out of alignment, which is an easy fix, or the pads are worn. The latter is actually dangerous.
Out of alignment means the pads press flush against the rim, which is not the most ideal situation because you don't get as much gradual braking. Pads should be "toed in" which means they grab the rim at a slight angle.
