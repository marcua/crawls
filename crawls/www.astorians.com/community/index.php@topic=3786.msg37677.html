I love Acme. I make the schlep about twice a year. Most appetizing freezes beautifully. Their pickled lox in cream sauce is to die for. When I get it home, I add a large, sliced onion and let it sit in the ice box for a few days to marinate. I also like getting my lox unsliced. I have this flexible, very thin fish knife and I prefer doing the slicing the way I like it!

When I was a little girl, growing up in Astoria, we had an appetizing store on 30th Avenue between 35th and 36th Streets. I can vividly recall the Acme's horse-drawn wagon parked out front making deliveries. (BTW, there used to be a Glatt Kosher deli on the corner of 36th Street and 30th Avenue, where Frank's Bakery is now. They were the first business in the neighborhood to deliver.)

Acme used to be the best kept secret in Jewish New York. Now they are on the internet! Wow!

I also go to Flaum's on Lee Avenue in Williamsburg for some appetizing when I can't make a Friday morning at Acme.

As for Russ and Daughters, I love them too. But you *need* to bring your stocks and bonds to shop there. I used to make them a regular stop when I would go to the LES to Guss's Pickles and Kossar (who make the very best bialys in the whole world).

Should I post when I am making my next Acme schlep and take some of you with me?

Quote from: Cary119 on October 13, 2006, 06:50:12 PM
how can these things be appetizing?  I'll admit I've never tried whitefish or herring, so maybe someone can introduce me to these so-called wonders?

I think they're called "appetizing" (n.b.: it's a noun, not an adjective) because they were originally served as pre-meal snacks on special occasions in the Old Country (I'm thinking the English term is analogous to the Yiddish/German "forshpeis").  I know few people outside of the Eastern European Jewish matrix who dig this stuff, but if you're open-minded, then I encourage you to give it a try.  I think lox (smoked salmon) is probably the safest place to start.  The hard thing is that there is a HUGE world of difference between mediocre lox and really good lox.  I would advise getting a bagel-creamcheese-and-lox sandwich at Zabar's Cafe (80th and B'way, I think)- they have a breakfast deal where you get one of those sandwiches, a coffee, and an orange juice for five bucks (great deal).  Or, as Jonanthan suggested, Russ and Daughters at 1st and 1st (nexus of the universe) will also make you such a sandwich.  If you like that, you can move on to-- whitefish salad!  And then whitefish!  and then chubbs.  and then sabe, herring....  ahhhhh

Quote from: Jonathan on October 14, 2006, 11:25:46 AM
NYCmacuser, I didn't realize you grew up in pre auto NY. You look great!
It wasn't really pre auto. It was still kind of a mix of the pre and post. We still got our ice delivered by horse drawn wagon. We still had an ice box. The man who sharpened knives and scissors used to come around once a month by horse drawn wagon and the fruit and vegetable guy also came around every day with his really old nag. My Dad used to give me an extra penny to buy an apple to feed to her.

[OT]
My parents lived in Astoria. My Dad owned the Whelan Drug Store across the street from Radio City Music Hall. When my Mom went into labor with me, she called my Dad at the store and told him to meet her at the hospital, dropped off my older sister at a neighbor's, and grabbed a cab to get to New York Hospital on E 68th Street. As the story goes, there were so many horse drawn wagons on the 59th Street Bridge that traffic was moving at only 3 mph. My Mom couldn't wait, so the cab driver helped her deliver me right on the bridge. Every year, until he passed away, I got a birthday card and a little gift from Seymour, the cab driver.
[/OT]
