Title: Meditations on American Idol
Post by: Jtrane on January 16, 2007, 09:14:27 PM
So, American Idol is back as of tonight and got me to thinking.  Why do I watch this blaring example of the wasteland that is "American culture"?  I'm a professional musician with a degree in music performance.  What is it about this show that keeps bringing me back year after year to watch this mind-numbing epitome of mediocrity?  The only thing I can conclude is that it's like watching a train wreck.  Utterly disturbed but can't avert my eyes due out of morbid curiosity.  Watching the hordes of misguided wannabes who never had the benefit of someone telling them they have little or no talent.  And the worst part of this show is what happens to good singers.  Seeing singers that truly have potential being molded into consumer products by having any ounce of individual identity sucked dry.  The songs they choose and are forced to sing reduce them to just another sound-alike crappy pop singer.  In the long run, it seams that it would be beneficial to NOT win the show so you aren't pigeon holed into singing the worst example of bubble gum, over the top, ultra overly dramatic crap these assembly-line song factories can come up with.

And to touch on the absolute no talent ego cases...
How do these people get through their entire lives without a single person telling them they can't sing?  And how can they be so tone deaf that they don't know they suck?  And how can they get upset when someone is finally willing to tell them the truth?  Do we now live in a society that is so overly protective of people's feelings that we can't tell them they aren't good at something?  It's good to have goals.  But some goals are just not realistic.  Sometimes it's best to not patronize someone and give them false hope because the fall can be hard for those that are unprepared to hear the truth.

Hahaha.  A girl just sang, "If I were king of the forest" and thinks she should get a shot because she's "unique."  Poor stupid child.

What ever happened to hard work?  As other musicians on this board can attest to, talent isn't a "gift" in most cases.  It takes hard work and dedication.  This is a point lost on most of these singers.  Practice is what makes a musician great.  Not by being "unique."  Uniqueness comes naturally and isn't something that is conciously achieved.

OK.  Enough for now.  Happy new season of American Idol!!! :evil:

But seriously, in terms of wanting to be famous, I find the trend that American Idol is contributing to disturbing.  There's an interesting article in this week's Newsweek (link to article (http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/16600109/site/newsweek/)) about American Idol tryout junkies who have tried out close to a dozen times, using all their money to travel all around the country to do so.  The article quotes a recent (unidentified) poll as finding that "31 percent of teenagers believe they will be famous when they grow up."  I don't have time to find hard data that backs up my belief that this number is higher than it used to be, but I found a couple of articles that indicate it might be true.

Here (http://www.usatoday.com/news/nation/2007-01-09-gen-y-cover_x.htm) is an article from USA today that cites a Pew telephone poll finding that 51% of American Gen Y-ers want to be famous, and 81% wanted to be rich.

That does contrast with polls from previous decades:

Quote
In an annual survey of college freshmen by the Higher Education Research Institute at the University of California-Los Angeles, 2005 data show that money is much more on their minds than in the past. The percentage who say it is "essential" or "very important" to be "very well off financially" grew from 41.9% in 1967 to 74.5% in 2005; "developing a meaningful philosophy of life" dropped in importance from 85.8% in 1967 to 45% in 2005.

In other words, being rich (or well-off financially) and developing a meaningful philosophy of life have essentially traded places.  I find this disturbing, and I think American Idol is one piece of the culture that is encouraging it.

This thread has actually gone in the direction I had anticipated.  I'm not so interested in the actual show as I am the underlying subjects we've been discussing.  And I'm also trying to relate some of my own personal experience as a musician to it.  For example, I have no false hope of fame or wealth, or even the notion of making a living playing music.  I do it because I love it.  I don't feel as though I'm entitled to anything aside from what I've worked for.  And I'll fight tooth and nail to get gigs and perform, and meet new musicians, and network, etc...  But I don't expect, or anticipate anyone will magically give me all these things.  It's my responsibility.  And what I see with this show, is a general sense of entitlement from most, but certainly not all, of the contestants.  For example, there was a girl on last night with a really nice, big voice.  She was a crack baby, and had a tough upbringing.  She was just glad to be given the opportunity, and didn't expect to necessarily go through.  I'll be rooting for her.  But I find she is the exception to the rule with the contestants, and with a lot of the youth I see around NYC.  I agree that Myspace and other social networking sites are a symptom, and not the cause.

My overall point in this particular post, is that a sense of entitlement is BS.  And most of the kids that have a sense of entitlement need to be slapped silly and given a solid dose of reality.  Life ain't easy, and you don't always get what you think you want or deserve.  Instead of treating kids with kid gloves and trying too hard to protect their feelings, perhaps we should go back to teaching that life isn't always easy.  Sometimes it sucks.  And maybe, just maybe, you aren't going to be famous.  So what are you going to do now that your fame is nonexistent?  And while opportunities are given from other people, it's our own work that determines whether those people give us an opportunity.

I've watched American Idol every season since it came out, and I find no shame in that. I'm a singer and was a voice major in college, along with being well educated in various facets of music, but I find the show entertaining and enjoy watching the competition as it progresses. I like hearing the same singers sing different genres every week.. that's actually my favorite part of the show, the variety as it goes along. Some very talented singers have gotten their names out there because of the show, and I think that's a good thing in a country where you can only hope to be heard by the masses if you're signed to a major label. I don't like the crap music the winners have to sing either, but Kelly Clarkson's come a long way since she won and has actually come out with some very listenable stuff, albeit strictly pop/rock.

Most people seem to like these early auditions more than the rest of the show, but I don't like the early stuff. Mainly I don't like that they focus on so many awful singers who are clearly just trying to get their 15 minutes of fame by being the worst singer/personality imaginable. But I also don't like this new thing that they seem to be doing more and more: they play a sob story about the person who's auditioning, like that it's a someone from a really poor family who's saved up just to go to this audition and it would mean so much for them to make it through, or someone who was teased throughout their childhood and is trying to prove everyone wrong.. etc.. and so you're rooting for this person and expect them to sound good, and then they sing and they're just horribly, horribly bad.

Why does the show choose to shame people like this on national television? They're not arrogant, or nasty, or otherwise annoying and therefore deserving of national mockery. They're nice people who've had some serious problems and are genuinely trying to do something to improve their lives a little. I'm not saying that auditioning for American Idol is a great way to improve your life, but it would be nice if the producers of the show would grow a conscience and pick their losers a little more thoughtfully. I mean, at the very least, they could just show the auditions without playing the life story. I have no problem believing that someone who's tone deaf doesn't know how bad a singer they really are. I'm just confused as to why none of their friends or family would warn them not to audition if they didn't want to make fools of themselves. It's one thing if they're auditioning for the show secretly, but for the ones with parental support I just don't get it.

That kind of brings me to my next point. A lot of people have been talking about a sense of entitlement. The convenience of our society has lead a lot of people to think that things should just be handed to them without their having to work for them, and the seemingly ever increasing focus on children's self-esteem creates people that think they're great and special regardless of how good at anything they actually are, realistically. Society doesn't preach humility and respect; it preaches "you" and "me" and how we can do things to benefit ourselves. And being a rock star seems, to the general public, to be a great way to benefit one's self.

But people need to learn and accept when they're just not good at things. Parents are afraid to discourage their children by telling them they're bad at something. Some will even lash out at teachers when their child receives a bad grade or doesn't make it into an honors course. I have a friend who's a middle school band director, and she's actually gotten phone calls from angry parents demanding to know things like why their daughter isn't first flute. So of course they'll be supportive when their little one wants to audition for a nationally televised show to fulfill their dream of being a pop singer. You'd think the bad auditions would make anyone really cautious about auditioning, but it seems to have the opposite effect as horrible people who truly think they can sing just keep coming through.

Then again, I can be kind of extreme in that I basically think society only develops to destroy itself. We become smarter only to become dumber as a whole, all our great inventions only serving to make us lazy and complacent. I'm also aware that this particular sentiment has been around for longer than I have. Oh well. I'll watch American Idol again tomorrow night or whenever the next one airs.

The Fox Family Made for TV Movie proudly presents:  George's Lesson, staring Jason Schwimmer, Sally Fields and Dan Castellana.

George yearned to be a singer.  His voice had a deep resonance which exuded an erotic masculinity.  During childhood, while he battled the ravages of mental retardation, his parents, encouraged his singing, hoping it would give him a foundation for recovery.  The tears which slipped out of his grandmother's eyes while he sang "happy birthday" to her on her last year on earth would become a permanent piece of heart wrenching family lore.

As George grew, he could not socially keep up with the other kids his age.  He did mainstream, but he was teased constantly in school, especially after being caught singing "I Feel Pretty" in the school bathroom.  Though his talent was obvious to anyone, he began to feel the weight of an unflinching world that demanded perfection.  He was rebuffed in his attempts to join the choir, music teachers would ignore him, and even his dad gently told him to "forget his singing and get a job at the A&P"

But George kept at it.  He went to music producer after music producer.  Some were harsher than others, but their messages were the same:  "You're good kid, but you'll be laughed at."  No matter how well he sung, no one wanted to listen.  The next few years would sow a nadir only the aisles of the A&P would ever fully understand.  Only his mop would hear his mellifluous chimes.

Fate would find him on a long line one September morning with his always supportive mother.  She couldn’t bear to see her son suffering and she waited all night with him so he could audition on American Idol.  Though barely able to stand up straight and hampered by the steady stream of mucus which slipped down to his mouth and was thereupon spit out onto all but the most dexterous of passer byers, his version of “Girls Just Want to Have Fun” flabbergasted the producers.  The stone cold silence which followed his last notes was broken a moment later by the thunderous ovation by those who were trying to escape his stead only a few minutes before.  But the world doesn’t always operate on merit and after a few minutes of conferring, the producers shook their heads and turned him down.

His mother called up the very next day and asked why he wasn’t given a chance.  She was hung up on several times.  She finally got through to talking with one junior producer, who told her that due to a legal decision which the show’s lawyers made due to a strident posting in Astorians.com, people of his handicap were not permitted on the show.  He should try Jerry Springer instead.

But George had one thing on his side.  Laura, a big wig at the network, was in the bathroom reading the articles of Playgirl when she heard George’s voice through the ventilation ducts.  She connected the smooth butter like voice with the strapping square jawed men in the magazine and fell in love.  The memo she wrote to the producers was curt and precise: “Either he’s in, or you’re out”.

The rest is history.  George’s winning of the American Idol contest, his marriage to Laura and his eventual ascendancy to the Presidency of the United States of America.  America had learned a lesson she would hopefully never forget.
