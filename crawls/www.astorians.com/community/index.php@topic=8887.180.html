Frappe, Sweeper, here is a resource that evaluates nations on the basis of the freedoms their respective citizens enjoy. Note that while comprehensive within its own objective methodology, the project certainly circumscribes the results. That said, I find it a very useful tool for evaluating the health of civil liberties, giving me (and I apply my own personal value judgment here) an indication of which countries are "better" or "worse".

http://www.freedomhouse.org/template.cfm?page=15

From their methodology statement:
Quote
Freedom House does not maintain a culture-bound view of freedom. The methodology of the survey established basic standards that are derived in large measure from the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. These standards apply to all countries and territories, irrespective of geographical location, ethnic or religious composition, or level of economic development.

To put it somewhat tritely, while the number of IKEA outlets might not satisfactorily determine the level of freedom in a country, evidence of say, enforced, non-voluntary and gender-discriminate genital mutilation certainly does.

[snip]

But the point I'm trying to make is that not everyone has the benefit of access to information like you claim, especially in those countries that lack even a modest infrastructure.

Jtrane, I understand what you're saying, but some sort of "informatic determinism", whereby countries with greater access to information have an advantage in establishing rule of law/civil liberties fails to take into account quite advanced nations that not too long ago were ruled by totalitarian governments.

In other words, what excuse was there for Stalinist Russia or Nazi Germany? The latter certainly had all the "benefits" of being an advanced society -- industry, economy, culture, etc. Yet that didn't stop them from falling off into the (thank God short-lived) abyss....

Clearly the level of "advancement" in a society, while it might put the brakes on oppression, is not enough of a guarantee. And I believe it's been argued that precisely because (for example) Nazi Germany was so modernized, the govt could implement a horrifically engineered and institutionalized oppression (replete with legal, economic and physical infrastructure to support it).

So, if lack of/access to information doesn't adequately explain why oppression exists (and contiunes to perpetuate) in some countries, what does?
