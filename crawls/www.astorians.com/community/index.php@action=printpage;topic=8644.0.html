Title: Old Astoria Village
Post by: Gleason on November 16, 2007, 09:08:38 AM
Old Astoria Village (called ‘Two Coves’ by Goodwill Industries of obvious reasons) is the oldest and largest community without a shred of protection in the city. They tell us this is so because the community doesn't want it.

Some of its streets are mid-seventeenth century – it has homes and buildings dating back to the eighteenth century. Every year we loose a few 150 year old homes. Even eroded, it still has dozens of buildings worth saving. Most of its structure remains intact. It is a unique ante-bellum Long Island community just across the river from Gracie Mansion.

The point here, my dear friends, is that as long as Astoria treats its past like this, it will remain a laughing stock among some quarters of the city.

Getting a district landmarked is a lot of work; please believe me.  There are doors to knock on, petitions to draft, meetings to attend and arrogant opponents to deal with. Folks from outside of the community can help a great deal, and some will. Still, the primary stakeholders for landmarking will always be the residents of the community itself. Title: Re: Old Astoria Village
Post by: Squirrel on November 24, 2007, 12:29:17 PM
The problem is likely a combination of apathy, ignorance of the facts and a "my home is my castle, so I can do whatever I want" mentality. Here are some things for locals to consider:

1.  Landmarking helps protect your investment by keeping the area attractive.
2.  Help is available for homeowners who need to make expensive repairs in landmarked areas.
3.  Landmarking actually increases property values. Look at places like Cobble Hill and Brooklyn Heights.

Sunnyside Gardens residents wanted the advantages of landmarking, and that is why most were in favor.  Folks from outside the community who wanted to preserve this special area gave valuable support, but in the final analysis this was a fight won because so many locals put many hours of hard work into the process.  They collected signatures.  They did research. They sent hundreds of letters and e-mails.  All of this was more than idealism; they knew that landmarking works.
