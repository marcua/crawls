What is a CPU?
« on: August 22, 2005, 10:05:53 AM »
Hello,

I'm posting this to spread general knowledge of what constitutes various parts of a computer. It's quite confusing because some people call a computer a "hard drive" some a "computer", some a "motherboard" and some people call a computer a "monitor."

Here are the general terms (described loosely) in case someone is interested:

CPU - The Central Processing Unit - This is a chip, about the size of a quarter (a $0.25 piece) that processes the data. The CPU is only a single chip, ans is inside the computer. CPU is to Computer, as Engine is to Car.

Motherboard - This is the circut board that is inside the computer's box. The CPU is one of the chips that mounts to the motherboard, and other chips, such as a modem, network card, memory, all are attached to the motherboard. The motherboard is INSIDE the computer case and is the thing that brings all the mess of chips together. Motherboard is to Computer, as Frame is to Car.

Hard Drive - This is a small box, about the size of a VHS Video Tape, that is INSIDE the computer. It too attaches to the motherboard, but through a cable instead of being mounted directly to the motherboard like the chips are. The hard drive is a kind of permanant storage where you can store documents, programs, or other information. It will stay preserved until you delete it. A power failure will not erase information off a hard drive. A "hard drive" or "hard disk" is NOT the flat, square things that you insert into computers or store things on (see "floppy disk") Hard Drive is to Computer, as Gas Tank is to Car.

Floppy Disk - A floppy disk is so named because of the material the disk is made of. A floppy is the square plastic thing you insert into a computer to store small amounts of data on. If you open up the plastic cover, you'll see that there is a little plastic "floppy" disk inside the casing. Even though the outside is "hard" plastic, this is still called a floppy disk because it is floppy INSIDE, under the metal protector thing that's on the disk. The floppy drive is the slot you put the disks into in the computer.

Memory/RAM - The memory (in the form of a SIMM or DIMM [sim or dim]) is mounted directly to the motherboard. These are about the length and size of a bookmark for book, but a little fatter. They plug into the motherboard and determine the number of programs you can have running at the same time. Memory has nothing to do with the amount of "storage" on your computer, but rather the capacity of how many things you can do at once (listen to music + work on word processing + be compiling special effects for a movie + be hacking launch codes + printing pictures at the same time.) Roughly speaking: Memory is to Computer as Seating Capacity is to Car. How many things you can carry at once during one trip.

Power Supply - This is a metal box inside the computer, roughly the size of a box of Kleenx tissues. This is the component that converts the electricity to a form that's usable by the chips. Power Supply is to Computer, as Carberator is to Car

Monitor - This is the thing that sits on the top of your desk that displays the output of the entire computer. It is connected to the back of the computer through a cable. Monitor is to Computer as Speedometer/Dash is to Car

Case - The computer case is the "body" of the computer. It is the thing that encapsulates your entire computer and all its components. Case is to Computer as Body is to Car.

Computer - This is the entire contraption, including all chips, memory, hard drives etc. The whole thing. This is also referred to in technical circles as a "box." Computer is to Computer, as Car is to Car

I hope this is helpful.

Great post Merm,

I'll attempt to add a definition of Operating System.

Operating System -  the interface between the user and the computer

Now what exactly does that mean? To begin thinking about this definition, here are some examples of operating systems that you may or may not have heard of:

Windows XP
Windows ME
Windows 2000
Windows 98
Windows 95
Mac OS X
Linux
OS/2
FreeBSD

So what exactly is an operating system (OS)?  An OS is piece of software which provides ways for humans and devices to access the resources of a computer.   When you turn your computer on, the OS is the first program to start, and it stays running until you turn your computer off.  Here are some basic things an OS can do:

1. The OS knows how to get keystrokes from your keyboard, and display them on your monitor.

2. It can execute programs, like Doom 3, Quickbooks, Microsoft Word, Internet Explorer, Firefox, and Vi.

3. It knows how to talk to that nifty USB pen drive you just bought at Best Buy.

4. More advanced OS's keeps track of whos files are who's - e.g. you can't accidentally delete your little brother's term paper.

The OS is also responsible for a whole host of things that a normal user should never have to worry about (and which really don't apply to this post), like managing the computer's main memory, mounting/un-mounting filesystems, scheduling processes, interprocess communication, and lots of other things I'm forgetting right now.
 
A car analogy:  Consider all of the different controls in your car - the gas pedal, the steering wheel, the switch to turn the headlights on, the gear shifter, the automatic window controller ... All of those controls are interfaces to the cars main functions.  If you did not have those controls, you would not be able to operate the car!  The same goes for a computer - if you did not have the operating system, you would not be able to operate the computer - like a car without a gas pedal.    

So Operating System is to Computer, as "Controls" is to Car

Furthermore:

Those of you that have tried to drive an American and a Japanese car know that there are subtle differences.  You are still driving a car, but some of the controls are in different locations, depending on whether you are driving a Ford or a Toyota.  This can be compared to the differences between Windows, Linux, and MacOSX.  Each has their advantages and disadvantages, but in the end, all three operating systems do essentially the same thing:  they provide an interface to a computer.

This is a wordy post - if anyone has some reasonable clarifications, please do post them.
