« Reply #19 on: October 31, 2008, 07:29:48 PM »
And yet, in most nations where election day is a national holiday, voter turnout rates are much higher than they are here.  Yes, in some of those countries, voting is "required" by law...but these are usually laws that are barely enforced, if at all (and by barely, I mean that there's a tiny fine...in Australia it's $20).  No country has 100% turnout, law or not, but many countries, including those where democracy has much more of a dubious history, have much higher turnout rates than ours, and most of those countries have a national holiday on Election Day.

Then again, in most countries, whoever wins the election takes office immediately, not after two or three months.  Delays like this give the outgoing president/party time to do some "last minute housekeeping" while remaining relatively under the radar as a lame-duck administration in its last days.  Check this out, for instance: http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2008/10/30/AR2008103004749.html

"The White House is working to enact a wide array of federal regulations, many of which would weaken government rules aimed at protecting consumers and the environment, before President Bush leaves office in January.

The new rules would be among the most controversial deregulatory steps of the Bush era and could be difficult for his successor to undo. Some would ease or lift constraints on private industry, including power plants, mines and farms.

Those and other regulations would help clear obstacles to some commercial ocean-fishing activities, ease controls on emissions of pollutants that contribute to global warming, relax drinking-water standards and lift a key restriction on mountaintop coal mining. "
