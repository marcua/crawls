I think overall there's a general feeling among brokers that they are working for the landlords. The "good" brokers realize that they are working for YOU, because you are the one paying them. If you get a broker who isn't on your side, then fire that broker and go and get one that will work for you.

In our experience with brokers we found that nearly all of them work for the landlord (on their side), but of course want you to pay them. I've never found a broker I was happy with, though it's been a few years since we used one. We did end up renting our first apartment through a broker, but once you live here I think it's much more worth it to spend some time canvasing the streets and talking to locals to see what's available. How do you think brokers find the empty houses? Just become your own broker and save the money.

A friend of mine found an interesting way to put the broker to work for him. After he found out the price of the apartment he wanted he asked the broker to get a lower price, which of course the broker (who is working for the landlord) said that they couldn't go lower (besides, it's not in the interest of the broker to negotiate a lower rent for you, the person who is paying them, because they get a bigger commission based on a bigger rent for you [so twisted]). So he said he'd give a 200% bonus commission on every dollar the broker negotiated off the monthly lease. In other words, if the broker lowered the rent $50, then he gets an extra $100 in commissions. Surprise, surprise, the rent was lowered $200/month. Broker got an extra $400, but that paid for itself in no time.

The way this city works is really screwed up sometimes. Only in NYC do renters have to pay brokers who work for the interests of the landlord. What a joke.

Unlike Grace realty. Grace reality set off alarm bells. I went there to look at an apartment for which I was already in touch with the existing tenant.  I think I was going to sublet it or something. The first thing Grace, I guess it was Grace, said was that I had to pay for the credit report. I balked, saying I want to look at the apartment first. Then she raises her voice telling me, "that's how it's done here." I said, "OK," got up and left. Sorry, there are too many brokers around her to put up with that.

Though I don't exactly like having to pay a broker's fee because the moving outlay is large enough as it is, I understand the reasoning behind it, at least from my experience with Halvatzis. They're the ones vetting potential renters, doing the references and credit checks, which is why landlords rely on them. They arrange for apartment viewings, the paper work and so on, stuff that most landlords probably don't want to be bothered with. If I was a landlord, I'd go through a broker. It doesn't cost them anything.

I also found that it is a business relationship. If the broker believes you are going to be a good customer, I think most will try to get you as close as possible to what you want. If you go into a broker's office with a bit of a grudge that you have to "deal with these money-grubbers", then it'll probably not make the relationship with the broker terribly productive. Yes, some of them are money-grubbers and you can usually tell in the first few minutes of meeting them.

For people new to the neighborhood, it's worth remembering that to a broker, you're a potential revenue stream and that's it. They don't know anything about you and if you happen to become an awful tenant, a landlord may think twice about renting to people that broker finds for them. Take the time to get to know you're broker, and interview them as well. If they give you the creeps take your business elsewhere, as is your right. If you think they're worth trusting, let them know that you want to give them your business. They may go that extra yard to help you find what you're lookign for.

This is defninitley a good place to find out about brokers since most of us have had experiences with them at some point or another.

Good luck.

The only way you can get rent controlled is if a family member passes and existing rent controlled unit on to you.  There are roughly 50,000 such units in NYC.

Since that applies to, like, nobody, it is safe to assume that any apartment presented to you as such is actually rent stabilized, which means that 1) your rent can only increase by a certain percentage each time you renew and 2) you have the right to renew the lease if you want, except in very specific circumstances (like the landlord wants to put his family in the unit).  See the Rent Stabilized faq at http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/faq/rentstab.html (http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/faq/rentstab.html) or, more generally, the entire site at http://www.housingnyc.com/ (http://www.housingnyc.com/) is pretty f-ing awesome.

If you are unsure as to whether a broker is telling you the truth about a building, call 718.739.6400 or 212.961.8930 to find out.  Please note that some buildings are overall stabilized, but individual units within may not be.  So if a broker says "the building is stabilized," make sure to get clarification on whether the unit is.

The only way you can get rent controlled is if a family member passes and existing rent controlled unit on to you.  There are roughly 50,000 such units in NYC.
Not quite true. In order for you to become the legally recognized tenant of a rent controlled apartment, you must first prove that you are related to the legal (now deceased) renter and that you have lived there continuously for at least six months prior to the death of the original renter.

Trust me, you don't want a rent controlled apartment. These apartments have no leases, the tenants are responsible for defraying the cost of fuel to the whole building, their rents, by State law, increase 7.5% (minimum) every year.

Whereas a rent stabilized apartment has a lease, and only has to deal with 3%5% increases every two years. Many, many of those estimated 50,000 controlled apartments are now paying more than their rent stabilized next door neighbors who have new stoves and refrigerators and totally refurbished bathrooms.

Rent stabilized apartments MUST be registered with DHCR and they will protect your interests if it is not. If you live in a building of less than 6 apartments you are at the mercy of the building owner. Check your lease . . . if it says that your apartment is stabilized . . . verify that with DHCR. Post by: emilyR on September 02, 2008, 01:42:17 PM
I recently found my first apartment in Astoria. Here's what I learned from the experience:

1. Have all the documents you will need to rent a place prepared before you start searching. Including: W2s from past 2 years, two or three pay stubs, a letter of employment, bank statements, divers license (or other form of photo ID), references (phone numbers of friends, old landlords, etc.), and guarantor's info if necessary. You may also want to run your own credit report for free from one of the credit reporting websites to avoid this fee.

2. Take some time to familiarize yourself with the neighborhood on google maps, or by walking around. Go all the way up and down 31st street, from LIC to Ditmars.

3. It is possible to rent from owners, and anyone who has some time to search should begin this way. The by owner section of craigslist should do the trick, or look for open house signs in the neighborhood.

3. If you are short on time, or cannot find any by owner rentals, using a broker is a reasonable choice. The STANDARD RATE for brokers in Astoria is one month's rent (~8% of a year's rent). If anyone charges you more than that, you should neotiate them down before signing a contract with the broker.

4. We met with several brokers. I would recommend using multiple brokers, as they don't all have access to the same listings. Ask about specific details from their postings to make sure they aren't bs-ing. Be sure you don't sign any statements saying you cannot use other brokers in the area when you initially meet with brokers.

- Angelica, whose office is on 36th and broadway, is a hot mess who consistently forgets important details (eg. how much we were willing to pay for an apt.) She posts exact locations, but her apartments are usually far away from where she says they are.
- Buy Sell Realty had excellent customer service, and was clean and professional. I wish we could have rented an apartment through them, but unfortunately they didn't have what we were looking for.
- We ended up using R&R Realty (Richard and Rashida). They were very good, but tried to charge 11% for a broker's fee. We negotiated it down.

5. See as many apartments as you can, even if they don't sound like exactly what you're looking for. You'll have a better idea of what is overpriced and what isn't if you just look at many apartments.

In general astoria is safe, beautiful and a really nice place to live. I'm happy with our decision to live here, and I hope these tips ease the stressful process of finding an apartment.

Hi, jr4266,

Based on my recent experience studying the rental housing market in Astoria, the approach a person uses to procuring an apartment can be dictated most dramatically by how much time as well as money he or she has. For example, since we live in Pennsylvania and I work full-time, I searched the Internet late at night for leads on apartments and roommates that we could check out during a one-day blitz visit to NYC. I lined up six apartments for us to see through a broker for convenience. If we had chosen one of the apartments shown to us by the broker, we would have paid the equivalent of one month's rent to the broker and one month's rent as the security deposit immediately, and the first month's rent by August first. That is the price of convenience (!).

If I had had more time, I would have tried lining up properties for which there was a "middleman" (locator company) because I figured that the company would have a vested interest in dealing with reputable landlords. (I know that that assumption may be naive.) I've just gone through the process of renting a new apartment.  Here's my story:

I initially tried the craigslist route.  I intended to find a no-fee apartment by sifting through the nonsense and seeing places with the owners.  I've lived in Astoria for a number of years so I had a pretty solid list of "must haves" for a new place.  Even though some places I saw were OK, none really fit the bill.  And some were a complete waste of time (like places for 1750 with roach traps all over the place, filthy living space, awful building, just plain scary).

Eventually I had to break down and go with a broker.  I didn't have a lot of time to waste & I needed someone who could narrow down the choices for me based on my list of "must haves."  In talking to brokers, I made it clear that if they wasted my time showing me places that didn't fit my criteria, then I'd drop them instantly.  This generally isn't something most brokers want to hear, so it didn't surprise me that some didn't call back at all. 

One of the brokers that didn't call back was Tasos from Astoria Apartments.  In his case, I barely got a chance to tell him my criteria because he just kept talking right over me & wouldn't allow me a chance to speak.  He was throwing things at me left and right ("I got a great place for you; I've got the keys right now").  When I'd explain that I wasn't interested in, say, a fifth floor walk-up and why, he just kept on talking.  When he figured out that I wasn't going to have the patience for his nonsense, he said "I'll call you later" and I never heard from him again.  So, I wouldn't recommend this guy.

I saw an apartment with a broker named Richard (of R&R Realty, I believe - the one partnered with Rashida).  Richard was an extremely nice guy & wasn't pushy at all.  I never met Rashida (who I heard isn't so nice).  He showed me a place off 30th - great location, nice place, but it was missing a couple of vital things I needed.  Afterward, I didn't get the sense that he had anything I was looking for & he said he'd pass my info along to his associates.  Again, never heard from him again.  I figured I'm being picky, which I'm allowed to be, and my being picky was going to make things more difficult for me.  Alas, I kept trying.

I saw another nice apartment off 30th, but after seeing it, there were a few things that bothered me & I wanted to see it again.  I saw this with a broker named Jamie (from Rutenberg Realty - which has hundreds of brokers so it's hard to judge the whole company based on this one guy).  Jamie was very pushy and it was obvious that he was saying whatever he could to get me to take the place.  He made it very difficult and confusing, and pushed for me to put down a $500 deposit on the apartment before I was anywhere near sure.  I wanted to see the place a 2nd time to look at some of the things that bothered me & he tried everything in his power to keep me from doing so.  I finally put my foot down and said I'm walking away unless I can see it, so he begrudgingly made the arrangement.  After confirming that the little things that bothered me were deal-breakers, I told him that I was uninterested & stopped communicating with him as I knew he wasn't the type of person I wanted to deal with.  Sneaky, shrewd, pushy, dishonest - unfortunately common traits for many brokers, but this was to the nth degree.

I spoke with a few other brokers on the phone, but none were especially helpful.  They were either loud & pushy, or just didn't have anything in the area I wanted.

I spoke with one broker named Maria (from Aidonis Realty) who also didn't have anything in the area I wanted, but was very nice and helpful.  She basically coached me - at length - on how to get what I wanted and how to deal with brokers.  She absolutely didn't have to take the time to do this, but she did because she's nice.  We both knew that she wasn't going to be able to find a place I wanted, but she helped anyway.

The broker who finally got me a place was Peter Horowitz, also from Charles Rutenberg Realty.  Although I see he's posted on this thread, I didn't find him here.  He just happened to be representing a place I found on craigslist.  In his case, I liked the place so much that it wasn't necessary for me to see anything else.  It had everything I needed, and after looking at close to 30 places, I was sure that this one was a rare breed.  Peter was never pushy, and he didn't try to give me standard broker lines like "this is the best building in Astoria" or any other line of crap that most brokers say just to get you to sign.  He basically just let me look around & was there to answer questions.  He was friendly and helpful in mediating my "interview" with the landlords, and has helped me with things (like asking the super to put in some window screens that were missing) even after I signed the lease.  This surprised me because most brokers, once they have their fee, will be out of your life forever.  With Peter, I can't speak for how he'd be if I needed to see place after place with him b/c the first apartment I saw was a winner.

I hope this info helps.  I can't guarantee that your experience with all of the people I mentioned will go exactly the same as they did with me.  The best I can do is describe exactly how my story went & you can make your own judgement from there.

Good luck.
