Quote from: Gothamaldehyde on July 22, 2008, 12:25:15 PM
The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-time

I found that one enjoyable.

If you like science fiction, read The Diamond Age by Neal Stephenson.  It's not his best-known but it's his best.

If you really like science fiction, Kay Kenyon's Maximum Ice is cool (pun intended) and thought-provoking.

If you're not afraid of seeming un-hip, Anne Tyler is always a good read.  I loved Ladder Of Years but they're all more or less good.

As for non-fiction, H.L. Mencken had a great curmudgeonly style and The Vintage Mencken is the best and most concise collection.

If you like the Beatles and can find a copy of Paperback Writer by Mark Shipper, read it.

Mark Twain's The Innocents Abroad is great, albeit slow at times.

The Hitchhiker's Guide To The Galaxy is giggly hilarious but the sequels are progressively less funny.

If you like mysteries, the Peter Marklin series by Neville Steed is fun.  They're about an antique toy dealer in Devon, UK.

Sorry I don't have any hip-lit suggestions; I hate that crap.

Fiction

Love Jasper Fforde (Thursday Next series, Nursery Crime series).

Philippa Gregory (she does more historical novels focusing mostly on the female characters) - Wideacre Trilogy, etc.

A Spot of Bother by Mark Haddon (He wrote A Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time, which I totally loved)

The Pale Blue Eye by Louis Bayard (a detective story with Edgar Allen Poe as one of the main characters)

The Lovely Bones (one of my favorite books, but can't remember the author name right off hand)

I'll also echo His Dark Materials, although at this point I've only read The Golden Compass (which I loved).

Non-fiction

A Strange Piece of Paradise by Terri Jentz who was nearly murdered on a bike trip.

I am currently reading The Monsters by Dorothy and Thomas Hoobler. It's not the most riveting book, but it is interesting. It is a biography of several people, including Mary Shelley (Frankenstein), who were writers and who were intertwined in each others' lives. If you think you have problems...

Sala's Gift by Ann Kirschner - About Ann's mother, Sala, and her family during the Holocaust.

Conservatize Me by John Moe - Very funny read, esp. if you're a liberal.

If I am Missing or Dead by Janine Latus - Written by a murdered woman's sister. Fairly depressing.

A few that I have loved lately are:

Field Notes on a Catastrophe by Elizabeth Kolbert (an ex Times journalist who wrote a series for the New Yorker which became the foundation for this book on climate change)

Remainder by Tom McCarthy

Plague of Doves by Louise Edrich

The Gathering by Anne Enright (first Irish woman to ever win the Booker)

The Beautiful Things That Heaven Bears by Dinaw Mengestu

The Book of Disquiet by Fernando Pessoa (he is a great but until recently overlooked Portuguese poet who out of nowhere had a Times piece written about him not too long ago)

Lincoln's Melancholy by Joshua Wolf Shenk (a great more psychological look at Lincoln)

Hope that helps, and if a book club ever does materialize I would certainly be interested!
