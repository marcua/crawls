Quote from: 28Grand on December 12, 2007, 11:50:48 AM



Please name one politican, newspaper or expert who publically came out against creating the district.

RE: Sunnyside Gardens

State Senator George Onorato, for one, was opposed to landmarking. And Councilman Eric Gioia got on board the preservation bandwagon only after a prolonged period of sitting on the fence. As for the local freebie papers (worth every penny you pay for them!), I am not certain as to their official stances on landmarking, but they weren't exactly going out of their way to support it. And the NY TIMES seems to have given a lot of space to the small number of noisy home owners who were actively against designation.

Getting SG landmarked took some effort, agreed. But the press seems to have overblown the opposition and the alleged neighbor-verses-neighbor feuding. The opposition was a strange coalition of I'm-More-PC-Than-You types and homeowners who thought it was their divine right to do whatever they wanted to their homes, regardless of how it affected the community. They were in the minority, and started organizing literally years after the preservationist movement began. After making some noise at the LPC hearing the anti movement essentially disappeared. I recall no instances at all of real feuding like the press would have led readers to believe happened.

The opposition was not only poorly organized, but relied upon some pretty fuzzy arguments. Mostly they engaged in race-baiting by trying to portray the preservationists as being anti-everybody, and by trying to instill fear into homeowners by making outrageous claims. One of their organizers told me that it would cost $9,000 to replace my front door if my house was landmarked! Well, not only is the latter pure baloney, but the residents who testified in favor of landmarking at the LPC were a veritable United Nations. I know, since I was there.

Folks, landmarking a community IS possible. SG proved it.

One question remains for me though: If the opposition was so poorly organized why has it taken so long to get landmark status in Sunnyside?

To simply say politicians, newspapers (i.e., local freebie papers which rely on the real estate industry for revenue) and “experts” were against the idea doesn’t work for me.
Most politicians are also against raising the subway fare but it happens anyway because we all know it’s more complicated than that.

If the residents and homeowners of Sunnyside were so keen on historic preservation then the landmark designation should have sailed through the process.

As Squirrel states, it didn’t happen this way. Creating a historic district in Sunnyside wasn't exactly a new idea – it’s was kicked around for quite sometime before people started to take it seriously and then an equal amount of time was spent by those enlightened folk who wanted it to make it happen.

The fact that there was little opposition in the end means to me that people realized the organising committee made their argument and the case for landmarking was won.

There doesn’t have to be an official “Anti-Historic District Committee” with dues paying members in order for people to be against landmarking. There were plenty of people working on their own in opposition and general "public" anti-landmark sentiment, for years, even before the pro-landmark people organized – and these are the people who make politicians nervous. It’s easy to count up those in favor on issues such as this, just go to the meetings. Not so much easy to conduct a census of those on the other side and to be fair they vote too.

What’s missing here is perspective: In Sunnyside, the major issues in the past were privatization of shared gardens/space, paving front gardens for parking, window treatments and cladding – mainly cosmetic disputes (I'm sure someone will correct me if I haven't got that right.)

Somehow quibbling over the right type of windows pales in comparison to idea of your attached neighbor’s house being torn down and replaced by a horror double in size.

To me it’s all a case of pro preservation people (us) making a persuasive argument to residents (also us) and more importantly the owners of properties (again, also us) who maybe anti, sceptical, fearful, or may not be aware of the benefits that landmark designation is something that will benefit them – no easy task I know and it can takes years. Once that is accomplished the press and politicians (they) will follow.
