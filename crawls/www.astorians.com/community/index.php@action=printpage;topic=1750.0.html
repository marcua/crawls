Title: You Got a Problem With Queens?
Post by: Harlan on March 12, 2006, 11:44:57 AM
Funny essay (http://www.nytimes.com/2006/03/12/opinion/nyregionopinions/12CIcosentino.html) on the Metro Opinion page of the Times today, by a woman named Barbra Williams Consentino. In shocking violation of Fair Use, here's the text. If you disapprove, please buy a copy of the Times (and read the fascinating essay in the Magazine about the ethical and legal issue of "wrongful birth"...).

Quote
WHENEVER our friends the Mosses say, "Let's get together soon," my husband and I agree, mentally mapping out the best way to get to their apartment on the Upper West Side. Do we take the car and spend $30 for parking? There's also the F train to the D to the No. 1 uptown. Or, of course, we can always take the R train to 57th Street and walk from there.

Why do we do this? Because after 25 years of living in Queens as geographic undesirables, we know that "let's get together soon" really means "let's get together soon — in Manhattan." Every time the idea of seeing another couple comes up, we can't help but sigh, knowing that we will be the ones trekking into the city to visit our friends.

It's not that we're bad hosts. On those few occasions when intrepid adventurers have actually crossed the East River, we've served them excellent wine and good food. We've entertained them with challenging games of Trivial Pursuit or penny-ante poker. In fact, those brave enough to visit have claimed to have had a good time. So why then are we so often the visitors instead of the ones being visited?

When asked, our city pals offer these explanations:

• "Our trip to Queens is longer than your trip to Manhattan." Is that in miles or kilometers?

• "The street numbers don't make any sense. We're afraid we'll get lost." You have a degree in mathematics from M.I.T., and you can't figure out that 64th Road is in Rego Park and 64th Street is in Woodside?

• "Between Shea Stadium and the tennis center, there's too much traffic." But the theaters, museums and tons of restaurants in Manhattan attract lots of people, most of whom take taxis even though it can take 15 minutes to drive a city block in rush-hour traffic.

• "There's nothing to do in Queens." Then why is there so much traffic? Queens has some of the best ethnic restaurants in the city, historic sites like the Louis Armstrong House and the Queens County Farm Museum, bucolic parks and enough shoe stores and clothing boutiques to keep a shopaholic content for weeks.

• "We came to Queens already." Oh, yeah? Well, we paid taxes last year, and I suspect we will again this year.

• "I grew up in Queens and going back there reminds me of my tortured adolescence." So, you'd rather be reminded of your approaching senescence, sagging derrière and imminent addiction to prune juice?

• "I'm allergic to trees." And the trees in Central Park are hypoallergenic?

As tiresome as the trip into Manhattan gets, there are, however, some good things about leaving our neck of the woods to come into the big city. We have the opportunity to feast our Forest Hills eyes on some urban wonders — grimy skyscrapers, scurrying rats the size of Chihuahuas — and enjoy the cacophony of city sounds: screeching sirens, clanking garbage trucks, pounding jack hammers. We get to eat city food, prepared in restaurants or take-out joints since few Manhattan apartments have kitchens that are large enough to prepare a meal in.

And I guess there's some good things about the fact that people seldom visit us. It lets us keep the great things about Queens a secret. Why let others know that our public schools are stellar and that Queens has the best moussaka in the city?

On that note, I've got a great idea: Let's meet for brunch in Brooklyn.

Barbra Williams Cosentino, a clinical social worker, nurse and psychotherapist, is working on a memoir.

Astoria is exactly what is was planned to be.

Many, many years ago, when Astoria started to decline, the Steinway Street BID, the Broadway BID, the 30th Avenue BID and the Ditmars BID worked together in an effort to help control the area to retain the flavor that was originally intended.

Although gentrification had already begun—and YUPPIEs were moving in—had no-one stopped to understand what this community was becomming it would have ended up *just* a cultural stopover for the *better-off* young population. Astoria was a community of families. Not singles. Not even couples. Families. Schools, transportation, decent, affordable rentals, affordable shopping areas were the requisites.

Around 15-20 years ago, when Manhattan rents went sky high, people were relieved to find a small apartment in Astoria for half of what they would be paying in Manhattan. They had other options at that time. Greenpoint. Park Slope. Williamsburgh. They were ripping down some of those garish 6-story walkups, and all those beautiful brownstones were being gutted to accommodate 6-8 units instead of the old way which was a unit per floor.

Astoria used to be called the parking lot of Long Island. We used to be able to park our cars *without* driving around for hours or paying a month's rent for a garage! When the influx of the YUPPIE population showed their willingness to pay more for everything, the neighborhood started it's downfall.

What happened to the times when we knew we had to schlepp to Manhattan to buy books, or go to museums, or to a zoo, or buy ethinic foods? Why does *every* neighborhood.community have to accommodate all the needs of everyone? I really loved that Astoria was a bedroom community. That we had some fairly decent grocery stores; some that even delivered. That there was always somewhere to buy a birthday card, discounted, disposable diapers, a pair of everyday shoes, find a good doctor or dentist, walk to a few great playgrounds and a nice safe park.

I much prefer having a great place to live than having a place that gets crowded with people who don't respect *our* lifestyle.

Just my 2 cents worth on a crappy, depressing rainy Sunday.

February 2005
Astoria: The Last Cheap, Hip ‘Hood?
By Dorn Townsend

New Yorkers love a bargain, but news of a good deal usually means one has to act fast to find a cheap place to live.

Astoria, the traditionally Greek neighborhood in Queens, proves a happy exception to the rule. After a half-decade as a somewhat second-tier destination for new arrivals and refugees from high Manhattan rents, the increasingly hip ‘hood still has plenty of low rents, bargain sale prices and plenty of space in the brick-faced apartment buildings flanking its tree-lined boulevards.

"Once my boyfriend and I decided to move in together, we realized that neither of our apartments were really big enough for two, and we started looking all over Manhattan and in Brooklyn," said Astrid Conway, an advertising copywriter and recent transplant to Astoria. "What we could get with our budget was totally depressing, but when we visited Astoria we just couldn’t believe what we saw."

What they saw is a lively, multi-ethnic neighborhood a 15-minute subway ride from Midtown, but with comparative price differences that are a happy shock to prospective arrivals. While tiny one-bedroom rentals in many parts of Brooklyn and Manhattan average upwards of $1,800 a month, a similar space in Astoria rents for around $1,100.

The cost of buying is also a relative bargain. According to Jorge Alberto Perez, a sales associate for the Corcoran Group, the Manhattan-based brokerage which has begun diverting some of its clients looking for more affordable housing to parts of Queens, buyers can double their space for the same price. He said one client who had logged months of fruitless searching for a two-bedroom condo in Manhattan found a three-bedroom home with a backyard and balcony in Astoria for $570,000.

"There’s so many great values out there, it’s like the wild, wild west of New York City real estate," said Perez. "When I take my clients here, they just can’t believe it. The difference in what you get for the same amount of money is amazing."

Indeed, in the last several years as Manhattan and Brooklyn prices have rocketed upwards, a survey of a half-dozen Astoria brokers indicates that over the same period the cost of renting or buying in Astoria has not dramatically changed.

Housing options are also expanding as a handful of new buildings go up and former warehouses and factories get converted into apartments. Local brokers said nearly all these new apartments are slated to be rental properties. New buildings, like the condos being developed by Bridgeside Developments on Astoria Boulevard, are primarily concentrated on commercial avenues. Several factories and warehouses are also being converted for residential use. Particularly active is the Pistilli Realty Group, which is converting several industrial buildings into condominiums.

"After Sept. 11 rents rose about 10 percent, but since then they have stabilized," said Demetrius Partridge, an Astoria broker. "There’s a lot of interest here, and nothing stays on the market too long."

What is changing in Astoria is the demographic make-up of the community. For decades this enclave of blue-collar workers, Greek-Americans, and immigrants, particularly from South Asia and Brazil, was often perceived by professional New Yorkers as being situated on the wrong side of the tracks. At best, it was an interesting destination for an afternoon’s anthropological walkabout, but not a serious place to consider moving. Over the past five years or so, plenty of Astrid Conways have colonized swathes of the neighborhood, slowly injecting it with elements of the edgy panache of Park Slope or Williamsburg.

"I’d say that recently most of my clients have been young Americans," said Partridge.

Beyond the inexpensive rents and its proximity to Midtown Manhattan, what is attracting them are Astoria’s numerous cultural offerings, like the Museum of the Moving Image and the Socrates Sculpture Park. Commercial thoroughfares offer diners a virtual United Nations of restaurant options. In the last two years residents say the range of commercial choices has expanded as shops replace corner delis, while garages close and mutate into hip bars with black lights, live-music nights and visiting DJs.

"It’s amazing because when we get up on weekends we have just as many restaurant choices as we did where we lived in Manhattan," said Conway. "We don’t feel like we really have to leave our neighborhood to do something interesting."

While Astoria’s real estate prices have largely remained stable since 2001, many brokers predict an escalation as interest in the neighborhood grows.

Just as gentrification is altering Williamsburg, Greenpoint and Long Island City, brokers said Astoria, with its abundance of Tudor apartment buildings and convenient subway access to Manhattan, is poised for a boom. If New York wins the 2012 Olympics, Astoria will be thrust into the spotlight, as large stretches of its waterfront become venues for rowing and swimming.

"The value of property will go up, as more and more Manhattan money comes into the neighborhood," said Perez. "What’s happening in Astoria is still a bit of a secret, but that information will get shared."

I'm sorry that the area is losing its old character but at the same time, I'm happy for the businesses and property owners that have gained from the changes and will continue to benefit as the area continues to change.   

Things I love:  L'incontro, Starbucks, the jewelry store, Fatty's, the BG, Brick Cafe, Kyclades, Astoria bagel, and many more.

Most Starbucks I've been to use beans that have been roasted to death.  So the resulting coffee has a bit of a burned taste, at least with their drip coffee.  They haven't gained the snarky nickname "Charbucks" for nothing.  However, I have been to some Starbucks that make their coffee in a way where it doesn't taste burnt.  Not sure why it is so different among stores, since "consistency" is the brass ring.  And I've had coffee from Starbucks on both coasts and in airports. 

There are independent places that make terrible coffee, too.  I don't go there.  I happen to like getting my coffee where I like to get it, which isn't Starbucks.  And I have my reasons, a lot of it having to do with what I like coffee to taste like.  I totally understand why people go to Starbucks, and that's their thing.  And I have mine.  I try not to give my friends who drink SB coffee a hard time anymore.  When I lived on LI, I finally understood that little thing called "lack of choice" that my friends in less urban areas used to talk about.  When I was younger and less tolerant, I thought that people who went to SB were crazy - why didn't they just support a local coffeeshop?  Well, there wasn't one.  Just Starbucks.  During 4 years in Stony Brook, I drank my fair amount of Starbucks.  I found that I enjoyed the soy lattes there.  The soy milk diluted the burnt taste.  ;)

I like living Astoria because I have options that match my tastes and my worldview.  Others have theirs.  It is such a refreshing change. 

I also don't think all the architecture in Astoria/LIC is ugly.  Some of it is unappealing, but not all of it.  I'll post about that at some point.
