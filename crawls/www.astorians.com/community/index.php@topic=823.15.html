Though I think that merms argument is persuasive, I am not convinced that this law was not passed for the sole purpose of looking out for anyone's health. In agreement with Daveed, I think that the law was too sweeping, too general and didn't really allow for alternative solutions or compromise. At best I saw this law as a ploy to somehow garnish money, at worst as another in-your-face type policy that would show New Yorkers that Big Brother is in control and just looking out for his children's interests.

I simply cannot believe that the government cares for our health. Maybe this is false reasoning, but if this were the case wouldn't they pass laws against companies that pose health risks (to consumers and employees) far more hazardous than second-hand smoke? There is a vested commercial interest in cancer, and where government fits in this cycle is anyone's guess. I simply don't have enough information to have a convincing argument however... only a presentiment, and a feeling that I am being controlled.

In the days when smoking inside was legal, weren't there some restaurants that had no-smoking policies? If these policies ran counter to laws in place and people couldn't respect their hosts, wouldn't that be the place where the government should have intervened?

I don't think it is so much a campaign against something that is unhealthy, as it is a holy crusade against "vice" and those decadent people who still enjoy a little vice, masquerading under the facade of the medical community and our "best interests".

Perhaps I am wrong though,

I'm on the fence about the smoking ban.

On the one hand, I don't smoke. I hate the smell of it, and I hate how I feel after spending a few hours in a smoky bar. I also worked bars in the UK and I know how unpleasant it is for the staff (even the ones who smoke).

Plus, where I'm from, there's been a smoking ban since 2002, and so far the hospitality business has not suffered. (Some establishments have, but not the industry as a whole.)

On the other hand, it's people's private business whether they want to smoke or not; whether to allow others to smoke on their property; and whether they want to patronize a smoking establishment. I mean, everybody knows it's bad for you. You just can't force people to quit.

The grey area is always the staff who has no choice but to breathe in the smoke.

And of course the studies that show that people in non-smoking environments smoke less in total and are more likely to succeed in quitting.

Whew! That's a loooong rant. :-)

To reply to Zeno's thoughts:

I'm all for the freedom of an individual to do whatever they want with their body - the problem is where that freedom begins to invade the freedom of someone else and what they do with their body.

In cases such as enclosed subway cars it makes sense to me to ban smoking, just as it would make sense to make a law that says you cannot buy a can of raid bug bommer and set it off in a subway car. I think it would be a tough argument to make to say that we shouldn't "restrict" the right of teenagers to set off bug bombs in subway cars. There is a reasonable limit here.

On the other hand, smoking on a subway platform at Ditmars let's say seems perfectly fine to me. It's not an enclosed space, and if someone is smoking you can just move out of the way. I do think it's a little extreme to ban smoking in this regard, because in this case the smoker is not invading the space (or health) of anyone else.

I agree with the ban in any instance where non-smokers are forced to smoke because of the nature of the space (enclosed subway cars, or in closed public spaces,) I will probably disagree with the ban when a smoker can do their thing without forcing anyone else to breathe it.
