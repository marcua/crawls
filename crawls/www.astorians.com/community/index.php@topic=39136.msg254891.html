Gentrification basically means spiking increases in property values that ends up causing a displacement of residents and businesses of the lower and middle classes. This issue isn't just about the changes in the community, but the cost of living causes by the zoning changes is forcing out many long term residents and independent stores that can't afford the rents. Since 2000 the propert value of my home has gone up about 75%, but I am paying three times the property taxes, so gentrification is even affecting home owners.

Gentrification has to mean more than rising rents and property values, because under that broad definition, that would apply to every single neighborhood in the city, including the Upper Eastside. Does anyone feel bad that the merely rich are being displaced by the megarich?


Most people, including me, are upset that Astoria rents are becoming unaffordable, but going further, I'm also worry that there aren't many neighborhoods that are less expensive alternatives. It's happening everywhere. I accept this as a fact of life.


This isn't necessarily gentrification because if we're all honest -- who are these fat cats moving to Astoria pushing people out? I don't see one group of people replacing another group.  Everyone wants to know who are these "rich" people who can afford to rent (or buy) but what I see is middle class people moving in -- exactly like the people who already live here. The difference is that newcomers are forced to pay a much higher percentage of there income to live here and/or double and triple up to cover the costs. Just because a person can pay it, doesn't make it affordable.


In terms of general changes, I think the neighborhood is better in many respects, more lively. There are few empty stores and the streets are bustling. We've become a destination neighborhood in the sense that people who don't live here, come and spend money. That's why most of the bars and restaurants can survive. If they had to rely solely on locals they wouldn't I would guess. Of course there are costs to this. I have never seen the point of looking back and saying the past was better. It's dangerous.

I'm very concerned at the change,  particularly the PACE of it, which puts me in a bit of panic-mode.  I can't stand Williamsburg, and would just hate to see Astoria become that.

One of the biggest problems I see with gentrification (aside from higher rents, more crowded subways, etc.)...or should I say, what's often a typical 'bad side' of gentrification, is that the new people have zero interest in the old.  No interest in the long-standing populations that have lived here, no interest in the mom-and-pop shops or diners, no interest in getting to know people different than themselves, no interest in participating in all that that makes Astoria unique.  Instead, they prefer to come here for the proximity to Manhattan,  for the cheaper-than-wherever-they-were-living-before-in NYC rents, or because they heard it's a 'cool' area to live.  These people want to create their own little microcosm in Astoria of beards, tattoos, and artisanal this and bespoke that.

I remember about a year ago some smug little hipster barista in Manhattan (but who himself likely lived in BK), in conversation, learned I lived in Astoria.  He commented 'oh?...I've heard some really good things about Astoria ...I need to check it out one day'...(as if he was deciding he'd grace little Astoria, Queens with his presence one day).  I almost hurled

I've also noticed recently, more groups of young'uns who appear to be tourists, having a little 'field trip day' in Astoria (you know...sorta like what many tourists have been doing say to Williamsburg or Bushwick?)  That right there is a bad sign. 

In the end,  the root of the problem is greed pure and simple.  Disgusting developers,  ruthless brokers, landlords jacking up their rents,  not because they really 'need to', but just because the market forces now allow for it.

Sure, I too love some of the new places that have opened up.  But there's no control over it...it just feels like a runaway freight train that we are unable to stop.

An article about gentrification in New York City by Josh Barbanel in the Wall Street Journal online appeared this week:
http://www.wsj.com/articles/gentrification-effect-in-new-york-city-is-overblown-report-suggests-1462841235

The article references a study by the NYU Furman Center that can be found here:
http://furmancenter.org/thestoop/entry/new-report-analyzes-new-york-citys-gentrifying-neighborhoods-and-finds-dram

Specifically, Focus on Gentrification:
http://furmancenter.org/files/sotc/Part_1_Gentrification_SOCin2015_6MAY2016.pdf

The link to the study PDF is very interesting and its written in what I think is accessible language (i.e. non-academic speak). I'm still looking through it but think it's of interest.

In the study, 15 neighborhoods are classified as Gentrifying, one of which is Astoria. Some of the findings include:

Change in average rent in Astoria (2010-2014): +27%

Change in average income in Astoria (2010-2014):  +5%

Change in non-family households in Astoria (2010-2014): +49%

Change in Adult Population with College Degree in Astoria (2010-2014): +41%

As we all know, rents are rising in Astoria. According to the study, in Astoria by more than the city-wide average (21%), but less than the average of other neighborhoods classified as gentrifying in the report (34%). So we are not as bad off as other gentrifying areas, some of which are poorer on average than here.

Also from the report:
In short, households across New York City are experiencing significant rent pressures and shifts in household composition. Rent is going up everywhere in New York City, and at a rate faster than incomes. New Yorkers overall have become younger, more educated, and more likely to live in non-family households since 1990. These changes, however, are magnified in the citys gentrifying neighborhoods."

What do people think of the report?

What I take away is: rents are going up, incomes are not, more single, more educated (and mostly white) people are moving in and sharing.

Thats probably obvious to most people who live here, but I think its important to note that average incomes are not rising significantly, so how people are paying those rents, and filling the bars and restaurants is anyone's guess.

While it's true that incomes may not be rising at a fast pace, or high dollar amount for many people with years in the workforce, I think people may underestimate the spending power of the younger people moving to the area.  Regardless of potential family backing , starting salaries are high today for people with advanced degrees.  The job market is not stuck in the recession it was several years ago.  Skilled professionals with a proven record are jumping from job to job and earning higher pay each time.  Starting salaries are high and competitive in most professional fields.  It's not unusual for a young person with proven technology skills to land a job with a solid six figure salary.

My personal feeling is that when this topic comes up, there is generally a negative tone right off the bat.  You see it all over social media.  50 posts in 5 minutes from people saying how nothing is "affordable".  That may be true for many people.  And I can understand why many changes are not welcomed by long time residents.  And long time residents should not be dismissed or disrespected.  But lets face it, people can afford it and are moving into new buildings almost the second they're placed on the market.  And for homeowners in the area, the real estate market is a dream.  I think there is a lot to like about the changes I've seen over the past 10 years or so.  I'm out every weekend to our local bars and restaurants and am thrilled with nearly every new opening.  I like the improvements happening to our streets, parks, and other public spaces.  I like the fact that Astoria is a hot area where smart, young professionals want to live.  I also love our older, established ethnic businesses and restaurants.  My point is, if we can lose the knee jerk reaction to think negatively and really look at what's happening, I think the pros outweigh the cons.  I don't see anyone being pushed out in that awful way that some areas change abruptly.  I don't see new residents looking down upon long time residents.  Sure, there are idiots everywhere, but I don't see that in Astoria much at all.  Actually, I think people in Astoria are handing these changes extremely well - whether they admit it or not!
