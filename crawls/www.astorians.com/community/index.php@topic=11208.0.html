I find that home prices in Astoria (as in most of NYC) are ridiculously high. I think they will come down anywhere from 40% to 60%. Why? Simple economics, supply and demand. When houses and condos sit on the market for over a year. When the median price is 10x median income, instead of the norm, which is 3x median income, prices fall. They fall, not because those prices aren't fair, but because those who will buy need to be able to afford the price. Anything over 4x income and the mortgage will probably not be affordable. The old rules of Real Estate, like price being 120x monthly rent, work because they are economically viable. If you buy something and the rental income is below your monthly expenses, you've made a bad buy. And if you look at the numbers, rent is about half the price of owning. 

Look at these two houses for sale.
 

They are both listed at about $640,000! For that to work the buyer should make about $150,000 a year. Can you imagine anyone with that income living in these shacks?
Another thing I've noticed is that every Single Family House I have seen sold in the last year has been torn down and an Apt, Bldg. or Condo has been put up. That tells me that only developers with deep pockets and not regular folks can afford a house in Astoria.
As Wall Street tanks and jobs go south. As there are more and more empty places on the market, prices will correct. It's happening everywhere in the country, it will happen here.
The fact is we are coming off the biggest bubble in World History (Schiller/Yale) and EVERY bubble has busted. Every time someone says "It's different this time" (remember the tech bubble) or "It's different here" (check Florida, where they said it was different) they have been wrong, 100% of the time.
I don't know if it will take 2 years or 5, but prices will correct to the averages they had for the 50 years leading up to this bubble. And that means looking at the prices in the late 90s for measure.
One last thing. Just ask anyone you know that has owned a home for say, 8 years or more if they could afford to buy their home at today's prices. I haven't found anyone. Think about that. I don't know anyone here in New York who could afford their own home. That tells you something.
