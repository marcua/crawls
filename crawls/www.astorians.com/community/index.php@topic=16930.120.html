Everything has changed.  That's life.  We all long for things from the past.  We must also learn to appreciate things that we have now.  Although I long for some things from the past, I am also happy with what I have now.

Quote
I do have an issue with small houses being torn down for the sake of building multilevel apartment buildings -- and when they tore down the old Presbyterian Church on 33rd Street that was like a dagger to the heart! -- in that respect, we lose the flavor and character of the neighborhood.

Blaming it on newcomers from the Midwest, though, is just scapegoating and unnecessary. I'm sure there are much more worthy recipients of the OP's ire.

I live near the old Presbyterian Church, or it's former site. I think the construction sign says it will become a senior living center or some such... So that seems kind of cool. I wonder if measures will be taken to crackdown on the noise level in that area though. A lot of people like revving their motorcycles and cars because it's such a wide street.

I felt bad seeing the Church torn down. Especially for the pigeons that inhabited it's attic area. It seemed to be a home for a lot of pigeons and as the Church was slowly torn down, they never let go of it. They'd all wedge themselves on it's remaining sections in the freezing cold and soon had to seek refuge on the adjacent Church building until that was torn down as well.

With regards to this thread topic, I've voiced my opinion before in a similar thread and quickly got neg nodded. I'm a native Astorian, it's the only neighborhood I've ever lived in. When I lived with my parents we moved three times and we've always stayed here. When I moved out on my own I was lucky enough to remain here. But I've got to deal with the rising rent costs because of the gentrification. If my rent goes up next year I'll have to consider moving. Hopefully I can find something a little further away from the trains in Astoria, but if that doesn't happen I'll be forced to move away.

I think that's where some people might be coming from when they express some disdain for the changes. Sure, I like the new restaurants and bars but I'd still would have chosen to live in Astoria without all that. I just read someones comment about how many of the new people that come to Astoria are just passing through. And while my experiences don't define the situation I've seen and heard many people treat Astoria as just some trendy place. And sure that's nice, and I realize being trendy comes from having a lot interesting places to visit, hang, drink and eat at and all those places are a product of the citizens in a neighborhood and I'm sure many of those places could only have been created by newcomers but the downside comes when too many people, at the same time from out of the area start turning their heads to see what's going on here and especially when it's only because they get off on planting themselves in places that a magazine or something defines as cool and hip. That attitude is not something I think long time residents or people who plan on staying here for a while want to have.

I want to finish off this post with something positive and that'll be that we can't stop the changes. Many more people will come through Astoria and I've personally made some friends with out of towners or whatever you want to call them. They're genuinely good people. I just hope that Astoria doesn't become overpriced and only recognized as a place to hang out latenights and weekends. There are* people who want to live here because of some connection to their past. They can accept changes but you can't blame them for being angry that they're forced out by skyrocketing rents and a changing sense of community brought about by people who came here to hang out because some magazine said its hip.
