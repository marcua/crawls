my parents came to this country in 1974 with no American college degree and only a bare fundamental grasp of english.  however, they both worked 12-14 hours a day at menial jobs of manual labor and saved money for 8 years to ultimately buy a convenience store that failed.  then they purchased another convenience store, after saving money for another 4 years to have that fail also.  then they saved money again to buy a dry cleaners which turned out to be profitable.

i won't do you or your mother the disrespect of belittling her proprietorship of a small operation which has a business model that i don't understand.  but if it took 10 years to realize that she wasn't gaining traction and that she was making her real money at a part time job (am i understanding this correctly?), that overshadowed her business aspirations.  then to me, the logical conclusion for her would have been to realize that she was going to do better turning that part time job into a full time job.  also, i don't understand how you make money off people doing social work?  would you please explain this.  her clients were, i assume, poor to start off with (assuming because i associate social work with the poorer part of society) then what were the actual chances of really building a viable business?

are you asserting that because your mother was pursuing noble deeds that this merits "pooling resources" to ensure that she could continue her money losing venture and a lifestyle she "deserves"?  was it really the health insurance that broke the back?  i understand very well that health insurance is a big deal for small business owners, especially when the owner is at an advanced age where it's a necessity.  but from my personal experience with the many small business owners i know (and i know many, here and from seattle) it is not an insurmountable problem for them.

at various points in my parents life, they fully understood that sometimes it was better to quit a losing business and to approach running it from a different angle.  in this case, they took a dry cleaners, opened another dry cleaners, then learned the dry cleaning supply business.   then they sold all 3 businesses to finance a motel in nevada.  then they leveraged the equity in the motel after a few years to buy real estate in the form of strip malls and shopping centers.  now they have a large interest in a skyscraper in los angeles which allows them to stop working 14 hour days and to relax.  interestingly enough, their days are now filled with either the sheer pleasure and relief of not working 14 hour days and the astonishing and boring vacuum of a regular day where there isn't a business to run and maintain.  such that, on certain days, you can find my father sweeping the parking lot of the building he partly owns.

my parents came here with virtually nothing in the way of advantages when they came to this country, other than the absence of a sense of entitlement as to what they should be given.  this however, was enough to allow them to view clearly all the unbelievable opportunities available to anyone here who puts their head down, work, save and at the right moment, make the big decision to make their lives better.

so no, i reject the idea of a need to "pool resources" or that the american worker doesn't have every benefit they need to live a very good life.

My point is that in certain communities, there is no respect because of a myriad of factors: ignorance, of course but also because they have no incentive to participate in the Social Contract when they look around them and see no infrastructure.
There was a car on fire on my street one winter. Seriously. A burning carcass of a car. I thought it would explode. I called 311 and then the local police. That car continued burning for 24 more hours until it just burned out and then kids started playing in its ruins. Then addicts started living in it. The city did nothing for weeks. The sanitation department gave me the run around.

Now I ask you, if you lived in a place like that, why would you bother throwing your gum wrapper in an [over-stuffed, festering] wastebasket? You don't feel like you make a difference. The ridiculous potholes and broken streetlights tell you that the city has also forsaken you. Your main concern is probably to avoid getting mugged, not placing litter in the correct receptacle.

Here is where I lean toward JTrane's opinion. The Social Contract requires that both society and the individual uphold their ends of the agreement so that we can all co-exist in humane conditions. But if you're unluck enough to be born a black man in Bushwick, for example, you're accustomed to society not really holding up its end of the bargain: to provide every person with equal oppertunities for health and happiness. I do not see the free market mentality as helpful because capitalism pivots around profit. That is its purpose. There is no immediate profit in patching up the Social Contract. This all relates to Malcolm Gladwell's tipping point, Freakonomics, and of course the famed "broken window" theory.
