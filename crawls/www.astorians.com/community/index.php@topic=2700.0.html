I actually started looking into the idea about a year ago, thinking that it would be a great addition to the neighborhood and alternative to corporate publishing (the environment I'm in now), but the truth is that the outlook for an independent bookstore is DIRE. Even long-time retail people won't go near it. The profit margin is pretty low (though not as low as music retail), and NYC rent alone means you'd have to move a really large number of units just to keep the door open. Which influences the sort of stock you've got to carry, which immediately starts turning it into something other than the offbeat indie book store of one's dreams. I dearly, dearly loved the idea, but the chances of even paying back the initial investment are so slim. And I've already done the dirt-poor artist thing.

Anyway, I'm worried that this isn't the most coherent post in the world, so I'll stop now (under-caffeinated and sooooo tired on a Monday morning).

If the big concern is getting your hands on books w/o leaving Queens, go to the public library and get them through interlibrary loan for free.

Right now the Astoria libraries seem to be pretty small and with short hours, but if more people patronize them they will get more funding and probably longer hours.

I hear a lot of different wants here - people want the book they want when they want it (this means a huge stock, which means a huge space, which means high rent. it also means buying the top ten best-sellers instead of that little indie book of poetry). This almost HAS to = B&N. (Please feel free to point out any alternatives I have missed.)

And then there's the desire for something small and independent and funky and non-corporate. Chances are they won't have what you want when you want it, but they can order it for you. And I think Seaburns does that.

Frankly I like Seaburn's. If I want B&N, I go into the city. If I want the book I want and I want it NOW, I order it from amazon. If I want to hang out at a local bookstore and browse and explore, I go to Seaburn's.
