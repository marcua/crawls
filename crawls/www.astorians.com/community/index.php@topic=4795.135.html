What if the people who worshipped the big, furry bunny limited their interactions with non-bunny-believers to practicing kindness and promoting social justice, and didn't attempt to convince those people to accept the bunny.  (Hee.  :-) "Accept the Bunny.")

Being nice and just because an invisible bunny said so is pretty deranged. Would you really trust this person-- a person who is nice and just not because he/she empathizes with other people or recognizes each person's equal right not to be coerced, but because his/her god (however cute) has so directed? Keep in mind that the person who follows the dictates of a god is going to follow them irrespective of the consequences of following them. Because bunnygod said so is their moral modus operandi, as opposed to, because no person has the right to use other persons as a means to an end without their consent.

In other words, this person will be a fine companion up to the point where he/she has a "revelation" about the bunny changing its mind and preferring militant expansion.

Quote from: Victorine on February 09, 2007, 04:18:03 PM
Or what if all of the above, and they saw the big furry bunny as a metaphor, and/or an idea left over from historical time periods when people conceived of things more literally, but felt that the message of peace and justice attributed to the bunny was valuable?

Peace and justice are valuable to human beings personally and socially (even when they have a significant comparative advantage in brutality, initiating force will only get them so far). Peace and justice are terms that correspond to real human relationships and real social constructs. 

Introducing a god basis for peace and justice... well, historically oppressed people did invoke gods as a way of establishing that the oppressors were not somehow immune from accountability for their actions simply because they had power. Nowadays, we would and should talk about the rule of law as opposed to the rule of men (the idea that laws are instituted to preserve every individual's liberty and that no person is "above the law"). More often, religion and politics combine to oppress than to liberate.

I don't really disagree with this, but in regards to the personal and social value of some of these constructs, these are all subjective, defined differently by different peoples. I'm more interested in tracing this evolution of belief. As mentioned in previous posts, historically people have been battling over "my god is better than your god". Implicit in this statement was that people did believe in these other gods, or else wouldn't have felt then need to exhalt theirs over someone else's. But let's take it back further. Hundreds of years, and it all comes down to life and death. Archeologists identified grave sites from prehistoric man, indicating that an interest in the afterlife had developed. This is how it began. Fear and unsurity about death (anybody not struggle with this?) Entities were thought up and created to answer questions that we didn't have the answers to, and rather than spending time thinking about the answer, create an entity and problem is solved. The reasoning is circular, but if it saves cognitive space to think about something else, can't be too bad.

The origins of religion are another debate in itself, and I would love to partake in a discussion, but if the purpose was to explain the unexplainable, can we continue to make decisions and take political stances based on entities that evolved from a primitive responses to unanswerable questions?

The alternative, by the way, to a universe "ordered" by God is NOT one that is chaotic and random, at least not in our case. The question is, where does the order in our universe arise from? Is it a result of slow, adaptive changes occuring over eons or the output of a consciousness with a purpose? We have evidence for the former and none for the latter.

What relates this to politics is that societies have problems they would like solved. The frame of reference for a voting public and those they elect when they identify something as a problem and then proceed to develop solutions has a direct impact on their lives. Our lives. Do they take an evidence-based approach to public policy? Do they take a faith-based approach? Our lives are affected either way.
