I teach too!  I'm a professor, and I enjoy eavesdropping on my HS teacher friends for time-saving tips and classroom management strategies.  First, they are busier than I am.  Second, they more likely  have a pedagogical background, unlike me.  So thanks, everyone, for this interesting dicussion...we are in the best line of work, ever.

Re. the Requested Class Notes controversy:  My take is that they pay to come to lectures, and it is up to them to be there, or not.  I have a lot of frosh students, and I see my job to train them in attending lecture, IF they want good grades.  In lecture I will do almost anything to entertain, re-phrase, get input, and explain things 6 different ways, with drama, wit, whatever.  But lecture only happens once, they are making sacrifices to be in college, and it is up to them to start deciding how to respond to opportunities.  I won't email class notes, and I won't boil down a 90-minute lecture into a 30 second answer to "Hey, professor, did I miss anything in class on Tuesday?"  I tell students to get notes from another student, and then bring them to me o go over with them in person.  Nobody ever takes me up on this offer.  In fact, I think they rarely bother to get the notes from other students.  I also believe that text-versions of a 90-minute lecture will not do them any good, and might actually mislead them on certain point.  I think my take on this is harsh, but reasonable (given 4 classes with an average of 160+ students a semester). 

So...is it too harsh?  Any input.....?

Quote from: jennifer on January 29, 2007, 08:03:08 PM
So all the time i spend convincing students that they need to know how to take lecture notes because they're have to in college.... THAT"S out the window??


No no no!  No throwing out the window of the note-taking skill-formation!!  Not out the window.  Still inside the building.  Don't tell them their future college-peers won't all know how to take notes!!  Shhhhhh big secret!  lol  They do need it--to do well in college, but more importantly because it is the only way for a person to come to learn how to teach him/herself something.

I think powerpoint can be helpful for some things, I think those of us who use them in classes (me included) don't mostly use them to their best advantage, and I think that students who already know how to take notes are not harmed by them.  So, I feel ok about it.  But they don't replace notes.  And I say this.  Over and over.  "If I handed out an outline that would take 30 seconds to read, and yet lecture went for 90 minutes, it seems likely that there is MORE to know than what the outline covers.  Riiiight?"  But some students don't believe me.  But these are the same students who figure that attending 6 or 7 out of 10 classes is probably enough effort to earn a B+, and in my experience these students will only learn the nature of their misconception when they find out about their not-B+.  And then they will decide what to do with that realization, and that is up to them.

Powerpoint helps me, probably more than them.  (1) When I hand things out, students feel (correctly or not) more secure about their sense of where we are in the course.  This makes them more willing to trust me, and I need that in order to get them to lower their defenses.  (2)  When I know they already have the outlines for the next week, I am forced to stick to lecture schedule, come hell, high water, flu, or big class fight.  This is how I keep from falling behind on lecture material.

Anyway, that's what I figure.  Slides are a HUGE time-suck, though, when the course is a new preparation.
