I think that you may have accidentally posted the same link twice.
But to respond to the article I can see- it still does not make sense to me. The central argument in this article seems to be that a child needs a mother and father, as it is his/her "birthright". Somehow, gay marriage takes away this right?
I don't believe that to be a valid reason for the simple fact that :
a) We as a society allow (Voluntarily or involuntarily)single parents to raise a child by themselves. No one argues with those parents that they are somehow stripping away a child's right to have both parents. It is not illegal to be a single parent.
The author puts together a very flimsy response to the single parent issue, saying that children need to recognize that a "non-traditional" parental structure has to be the result of some tragedy, and that right is somehow being denied of the child. That makes absolutely no sense.
b) Male-female marriages exist where no children are involved, and are legally recognized. Should we disallow marriages that do not result in a "traditional" family unit?
c) What about gay couples that choose to not have children? Are they somehow depriving other children of anything? Parents in "traditional" households are incapable of explaining to children that sometimes a man and a man fall in love?

The whole Op Ed piece seems to me like it is using children as a pawn to try to trump up victims that don't exist in this case.
I don't think I "ignored" anything on this one, I think I just recognized an extremely flimsy argument from the author David Blankenhorn.

Quote from: casicua on May 07, 2009, 03:39:31 PM
There is a gaping hole in the argument of this second link: What about straight married couples that choose not to reproduce, or are physically infertile? Are we outlawing those too?

The premise of the essay really is that the primary reason that the state provides marriage to hetero couples is because of their ability to produce offspring and thus "grow the state". Additionally, hetero households are more 'stable' supposedly in the raising of children. I agree there are problems with this logic. I would argue we can see the true intentions of the state by looking at how the state allows even the most reckless marriage and remarriages, gives the same benefits to economically and psychologically unstable households, and allows single parents to adopt.

What the argument overlooks is the fact that marriage also brings certain responsibilities that benefit society and the economy. Ignoring the ridiculous 'reproduction' argument (which is hypocritical and disingenuous at best) the argument conveniently overlooks the fact that married couples are also more economically stable. When one spouse loses a job the other is less likely to need to rely on social services such as welfare or other programs. Additionally, when the couple or one partner takes on debt, the other partner is also responsible - ask the bank whether repayment is higher on single loans or those with cosigners.

This by no means exhaustive, but there are many economic benefits to allowing gays to marry as well that the essay misses.

One aspect of this debate that hasn’t been stressed enough is that when we talk about opening up marriage to gay people we are talking about civil marriage as granted by the state.

The state issuing marriage licenses to gay people in no way infringes upon or obligates religious orders to perform or recognise gay marriages/civil unions as a matter of church practice or theology.

In the end it’s about fair and equal treatment by the state of all its citizens and of all its families.
