She wants you to give her the broker's fee as a deposit.  They are two different things.  You can give a deposit for the apartment now to express your sincere intention to rent it.  The receipt should indicate it is a deposit for a particular apartment at a particular address.  I believe that the  broker's fee is not due until you sign the lease, since that is what the fee is for and not because some day in the future the owner of the apartment will hopefully sign a lease with you. 

I'd be very careful.  It's probably ok, but if the lease never gets signed...good luck getting the fee back.  If this is an agency, I'd call the actual broker directly (the owner, not the real estate agent) and tell them what is going on.  It could just be the agent wants to try to ensure their fee. I don't think a reputable broker/agency owner would support what the agent is doing.

Also, certain brokers in the area have a pretty poor reputation and others are pretty well recommended.  I would search the boards and see if the agency you are going through has a generally good or bad reputation on here. That should give you a good idea as to what the situations could become.

Cash to a broker?  Danger Will Robinson!  Danger!

Reputable brokers will work with checks.  Many times brokers charge fees to run credit reports and the sorts.  If the broker is that hard up for cash, walk away. I make it a habit to pay broker fees only after the deal is done. 

Just like the other postings/ replies, there is a difference between Security Deposit and Broker fees.  If you needed to make a check to the Management Company of the Apt. Bldg that is most likely a deposit, which would be reflected on the lease that is signed. 

Do not pay prior to signing a lease.  The lease is your legal document binding both you and the apartment owner.

The broker fee pays for finding you an aparment AND facilitating the execution of the lease.  Brokers do this service for both the leasee and the leasor.    Why pay before the job is done?
