I know the place quite well. It's one of the few shops left from back in the day when people would open stores with the intention of being at that location forever, not just two or three years and move on - as it seems is the case around here these days.

If you've gone in there, you will have seen they have a solid brass door and an inlaid floor at the entrance with a beautiful Art Deco "F" - so I would guess the original owner(s) had a last name beginning with "F."

Anyway, I was in there several months ago when they were putting that dreadful black and red paint job over the original pinstriped enamel front. I told them in no uncertain terms that they'd be NUTS to redo the rest of the front of that store.

So far, they haven't. Here's hoping they never do.

Around Astoria there's still little clues here and there from the former naming and numbering system.  On 20th Avenue, for example, around 41st or 42nd Street on the south side of the avenue, is a row of red brick houses.  Right on the walls of the corner buildings are old street signs, one of which reads "Winthrop Avenue" (can't remember what the other one says).

On 33rd Street, heading north between 34th Avenue and Broadway, there's some buildings on the right-hand side which still have old glass mosaics above their front doors with address numbers in the 500 range.  There's also that unusual house that's built on an angle compared to the rest of the houses on the block...apparently a road used to cross adjacent to it that connected with Newtown Road (still in existence)....that road has disappeared but the house has remained "crooked" ever since.

I remember as late as the late 80s or early 90s, there were still some streets in Long Island City that still had cobblestones.

The Archie Bunker's Place clip was awesome.  I think the shots of Astoria included a shot from the corner of 31st Avenue and Steinway, if I'm not mistaken, and a shot of fruit markets along 30th Avenue.  The one that I clearly recognized was the Steinway Coffee Shop, which was actually in existence until around 1999 or 2000 on the corner of Steinway and Broadway, where Lite Bites is today!  It was one of the last real old style "greasy spoon" coffee shops.
