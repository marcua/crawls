Title: Astoria Park Dog Run
Post by: sooperpaz on September 04, 2008, 05:01:00 PM
I don't know about other dog-owners out there, but I'm not very impressed with the size or location of the current dog park in (barely) Astoria Park. There are many areas around the park proper, specifically right under the bridge on the south side of the pool, that would be ideal for larger dog parks if fenced off.

I'm currently looking into the feasibility/funding possibilities for this. While the Department of Parks and Recreation has a large say in what goes on in city parks, dog runs are generally results of community groups and community care. With the right fencing, landscaping, and a few benches, I don't think it would take too much time or money to make a nicer, more practical, more appealing dog park for Astoria residents and their dogs.

If anyone's interested, has any ideas, any connections, or wants to help out, please join this group! Before I start bugging city and park officials, I want to make sure I have enough community support.

Thanks!

Excellent response.

One of the other issues that most people don't discuss is that this particular dog run is a germ infested dump. I have used the same vet for the past 35+ years and he hands all new clients a severe warning about the use of most dog runs. There are so many diseases spread in these places by careless and negligent pet owners who don't get their dogs vaccinated; who encourage their dog's aggressiveness; who find it cute that their dogs (male and female) will hump anything that is vertical (please remember that humping is not sexual with dogs—it is a very aggressive, and manipulative power move); that come with intact males or females in heat; that spread intestinal worms through their feces that owners don't clean up . . . dog runs are confined spaces that need to be clean, safe and healthy places for both dogs and dog owners. The only times I have seen this in New York City are the few that have a full time attendant or full time volunteers to help maintain both a clean and safe environment.

Dogs need to run freely to use up all their energy and, along with a proper diet, it also helps keep them in condition; as a result they tend to live longer and healthier lives and are happier pets. In my personal experience, people who will only take their dogs to a dog run are dog owners who have not succeeded at training a well-behaved dog. A well-trained dog is a well-behaved dog; a well-behaved dog can be allowed off-leash in a public space because they will follow their owners' commands to sit, stay come, etc. A well-behaved dog is not aggressive. A well-behaved dog is non-reactive. A well-behaved dog is a pleasure to be around. The only way to achieve having a well-behaved dog is through a healthy diet, reinforced obedience training, daily breed- and personality-specific exercise, consistent discipline, and lots and lots of affection.

Quote from: Valentin Mosnegutu on January 23, 2011, 04:04:27 PM
and is it true that the less you take your dogs for walks or just let it around your back yard that's its more likely for them to get arthritis ?
No. There are actually over 120 forms of Arthritis, and exercise has nothing to do with it. The Primary causes in dogs are: ligament, tendon, or muscle disease; fractures involving the joint; developmental disorders; congenital disorders;  dietary and hormonal disease; metabolic disorders; cancer; degenerative joint disease; inflammatory joint disease and degenerative spinal joint disease. In fact in many types of Arthritis any form of exercise is contraindicated.

Quote from: Valentin Mosnegutu on January 23, 2011, 04:04:27 PM
and how can you make a nonsocial dog into a social one ?
There are two separate and equal socializations that a dog must be taught: human and canine. Both are very important to the well-being of the dog. It is somewhat more difficult to teach an older dog, but rehabilitating an antisocial dog can be done by a patient and loving pet owner. The proper time to socialize a dog is during the brain imprinting months of puppyhood.

If you have an unsocialized or an antisocial dog the time to start is NOW.
