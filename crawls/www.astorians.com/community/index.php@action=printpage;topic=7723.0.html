ANIMATED COPS TO PATROL WEB IN CHINA.
The Chinese government announced today that it would use animated beat
 cops
to patrol 13 of China's top Web portals in an effort to steer users
 away
from illegal content, reports THE ASSOCIATED PRESS. Starting Sept. 1,
 2007,
the cartoon cops will walk, bike and drive across Web surfers' screens
every half hour, warning them to stay clear of material "the Communist
Party finds politically or morally threatening." By the end of the
 year,
the animated warning will appear on all websites registered with
 Beijing
servers.

Designed by Web firm Sohu, the male and female cartoon characters are
 meant
to startle users and remind them that the government is monitoring Web
usage. Additionally, if surfers need police assistance, they can click
 on
the characters, which link to the police authority's website.

In a statement, the Beijing Public Security Ministry said, "We will
continue to promote new images of the virtual police and update our
Internet security tips in an effort to make the image of the virtual
 police
more user-friendly and more in tune with how Web surfers use the
 Internet."

heff, I'm aware of the Tibet situation but I also think it is a lot more complex than what you presented.  There are radical Tibetan groups based in India, but the Dalai Lama won't meet or engage with them.  There are problems with the educational system in Tibet, especially the language of instruction, but the biggest problem is funding, the same as in China, and with children gaining access to education at all.  Traditional Tibetan culture will never be allowed to die out in the Tibetan Autonomous Region, because of its attraction to tourism, which is a huge cash cow.  Tourists cannot travel anywhere outside of Lhasa without obtaining a travel permit, which you can only get by hiring a driver and guide for each day of travel - very costly.

So the Tibet situation might qualify the Chinese government as fascist in your definition, but it is a big difference from a fascist society.  I am not condoning internet censorship, but I am saying that there is a lot more information and discourse available to individuals than the press on censorship in China would lead people to believe.  And if the government doesn't go through with "vulgar" censorship, it is because the people won't stand for it or will find a way to get around it, aided by new technology.  The biggest problem will be if the current urban generation, who have the potential to become something like the baby boomer generation in the U.S., cease to care.  The greatest worry I have regarding censorship, quite frankly, is censorship of textbooks and what young people are learning about China's history vis-a-vis their Asian neighbors, especially Japan.
