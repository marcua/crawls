Higher density within existing city limits is proving to be better for the planet overall.  I'm thrilled to see Queens Plaza shaping up in this way.  There are many subway lines to absorb the commuters. Not to mention that more and more people work from home, the road, and at various times of day.  Tall residential buildings are fire proof for the most part.  A sprinkling system is included to extinguish any fires that start within a particular apartment or part of the building.  Like any high rise office tower, most people can safely evacuate to floors above or below an incident until the problem is under control.  It's rare you would go to the street level ever.  I'd live there in a heartbeat if it were within my budget (we'll see come 2019).  The views, facing any direction, will be spectacular.

I've read several articles recently that due to all of the new construction in LIC and Astoria that supply is exceeding demand and this is causing rents n Northwestern Queenswweto decline for the first time in years. This is especially the case in regards to Astoria's older housing stock. A case in point: I've lived in the same prewar building for the past fifteen years and there's never been a vacant apartment. As soon as someone moved out, the next tenant moved in on the first of the month. The building currently has two "market rate" units that have been available for several weeks. The apartment next door sat empty for several months while the landlord found a "suitable" tenant--i.e. someone willing to pay "market rate" for a hundred year old apartment with no amenities and thirty year old appliances. Who wants to pay to pay 2K for a 3 room tenement apartment when for just a little more you can live in a modern apartment with decent appliances? Apparently, only 20 somethings with rich parents from Peoria who buy into the hype that Astoria is a destination.
I say keep building. The realtors keep saying that the addition of more "luxury" units will cause area rents to trend upwards to delight of greedy landlords. Economics and real world experience prove otherwise. My landlord is slowly figuring this out as more of his apartments are sitting empty for longer and longer periods of time and the average tenant moves out once his first lease is up to a better modern apartment down the street for the same "market rate".

The problem with all these luxury buildings is that it creates a sort of closed neighborhood within a larger more open neighborhood.  Take Battery Park City, or LIC by the waterfront, and now Qnsboro Plaza.  They all feel totally soul-less, with no real connection to the surrounding neighborhood or longer-term tenants.

Everyone lives in their little ivory towers with their glowing big screen TVs in their individual apartments, and their doormen, and their fitness centers, and their rooftops with gas grills.  I myself lived in such a building for two years, and sure, while it was nice to have a doorman for a while, and to have built-in neighbors you could hang with on the rooftop, honestly...I don't miss luxury apartment living at all.  Now I am in a simple Astoria small apt building where the LL himself lives with his family.  My apt may not be 'luxury', but it's nice enough and has more of a homey feel (versus a hotel feel, which is what the luxury apt I lived in had...on 21st Street near 30th Ave).

I am very concerned about the N/Q line, which is already to capacity during rush hour.  Bway and 30th Ave are getting overly-saturated with new shops and restaurants, and Ditmars is quickly becoming that way as well. 

Then you have all the lazy locals with their cars who can't even walk a few blocks, and instead get in their cars to go someplace right within the neighborhood.  Streets like Steinway and 30th Ave and Bway should ONLY be open to delivery trucks and MTA buses.  NO private traffic.  Or else, private cars should only be able to drive down the streets, but NOT park on these streets.  Because as soon as private cars PARK on these streets, the delivery trucks are then forced to DOUBLE PARK while they unload their trucks.  It's becoming a traffic nightmare, which clearly will only worsen unless something is done about private cars on these streets.
