That's just the thing. Nobody knows what it is like to be Jeffrey Dahmer or Osama Bin Laden. There are just some people we don't want to be, but if we understood them more, I think that we might understand better how to relate to them or to people like them. You wouldn't necessarily take on their persona, but everyone would think "You are Osama bin Laden. You are a terrorist" or "You're Jeffrey Dahmer. You are an incredibly sick individual." Or better yet, "You're Britney Spears and you have a seriously twisted life and don't deserve to be rich." Or "I wish I was you, Britney Spears. You're rich." And the list goes on... (Isn't Jeffrey Dahmer dead, though? Dead people would be excluded. It would probably suck to be dead for a day - not that I know what that is like, though.)

I don't think that if you switched bodies with someone it would necessarily mean that you would have their previous memories. If you have seen the old Freaky Friday (and I am sure the new one is similar) the people who trade bodies are still their original selves, just within someone else's body. It would be a chance for a white person to experience racism (which sucks by the way - I experienced that mildly in Europe a few years back) or to experience poverty, etc., etc.

I think merm's intent in starting this post was to get people to figure out how to change the real issues or to think of things we can't already accomplish. Sure it is one thing to wish that you had money or a dishwasher or got to sleep in later, but these are all things that people can already change themselves. I also believe my wish isn't perfect or even entirely positive, but every wish always has repurcussions. Even the good ones. I would hope that the positive would weigh out the negative in this wish, though. It most certainly would change the world for a day.

I'm enjoying reading the comments here.

I have a couple thoughts:

If someone is taking care of themselves so that other people don't have to take care of them that's not selfish. Selfish is taking care of yourself at the cost of others being able to take care of themselves.

Also, I wish that more people would subscribe to a kind of "think local" approach to change the world. However, thinking "global" and thinking "local" aren't mutually exclusive. You could argue actually that they are one in the same. A person who truely thinks global cannot exclude the local, and a person who thinks local must also consider the wider community (since no community is an island unto itself.)

It's a perversion of these views that ends up in a kind of twisted "think local/global" approach. Like when one starts thinking local in the sense that they are better than anyone else and who cares what happens to the rest of the world. Or when someone is only focused on feeding the starving African children, while they abuse and starve their own children.

As to my comments about the things that can be solved by Bill Gates. I suppose that what I was getting at was that I'd like to use my three wishes for something that cannot be solved simply with the use of more money. I see money as more of a part of the problem (to poverty, war, inequality, etc) than part of the solution. So I was wanting to make the genie's job a hard one.
