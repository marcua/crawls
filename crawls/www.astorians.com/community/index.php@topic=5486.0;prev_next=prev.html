It is amazing how much more crowded the N/W trains are.   I live between 30th Avenue and Astoria Blvd Stations and always get off/on at 30th Avenue.   However, in the a.m. I started to take the longer trek to Astoria Blvd. because a) I had a better chance of getting a seat; b) wouldn't be standing on a crowded 30th St. platform watching the W/N go by as the train ran "express."   

It is also true at night, particularly late at night, as you can't get a seat after 42nd Street when you are coming back to Astoria.   The trains are packed like rush hour due to the changing demographics of Astoria.

My bad subway experience occurred on the N/W line, mid day, as I was traveling back to Astoria from 57th Street.   This young, ahem, women was sitting next to the window in those two seats facing horizontally.   She had her feet up on vertical or three-seat group of seats and a bag and other stuff on the other seat next to her.   Of the three vertical sets, one person was sitting in the seat next to the door, farthest from the girl.   I went to sit in the seat where the girl had her feet and said "excuse me."   She kept her feet on the edge of the seat with her shoes touching my thigh pants.   I asked her to move her feet, she refused and it escalated into a shouting match.   The woman at one point jumped out and rummaging through her bag trying to get some sort of weapon.   Things eventually cooled down.   I realize now that this woman was no better than an animal and in retrospect I should have just stood up or tried to find another seat in another part of the car.

Adding more trains is a band-aid.

That will not help the power grid, traffic on Steinway Street, overwhelmed sewage plants (remember 'the smell'?), dirtier and noisier streets etc. etc. etc. - all topics discussed at one time or another on this board. All these threads come together and do make a pattern.

The response from most of NYC to this problem (and overcrowding seems to be a pretty universal complaint all around town) is downzoning and some local restraint to, and control over, unfettered development.

It is great to sympathize with someone else's housing crunch in Manhattan, but frankly, our first concern should be in the quality of life for those people already living here.

And shoehorning more people without a like increase in resources will only make things worse for both new residents and old timers.

To get back to the subject of fights on the subway, I have had the extreme pleasure of witnessing 3 subway fights on 3 separate subway lines. 

The first was on the 1 train traveling uptown at around 1am on my way home from my restaurant job.  A cracked out woman and a guy got into it and started throwing each other around the car (literally).  The train stopped at the next station and wouldn't leave until cops showed up (even though the fight spilled out onto the platform) so we got to view the whole thing.  It was pretty nasty.

The second was on the A train going uptown at about 8pm.  It happened during the express run from 59th St. to 125th St. so we were stuck with these stupid guys pile driving each other around the car.  That one was a little scary because it was impossible to get out of their way.

The third one occurred a couple of months ago on the N train.  Two guys in their late teens/early 20's got on at QB plaza, one sat down next to me.  The other came up to him, ask him what he was looking at and, next thing I know, they were throwing punches.  I managed to duck out of the way just in time to not get hit.

Either there are a lot more fights on the subway than people realize or I've just been in the wrong place at the wrong time!
