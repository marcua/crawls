Effective IDT/energy scammer tactic
« on: December 13, 2011, 10:19:14 AM »
Hi all,

I know what you're thinking - not another IDT/Green Mountain Energy etc thread.  But I thought this anecdote was worth sharing, as I think it provides a good blueprint for how to get these people to leave your apartment building.

I arrived home from work last night around 9:00 and spotted an IDT representative while walking up the stairs to my apartment.  Most of the apartments in my building are occupied by families, and I found the idea of this pest going door-to-door at nine at night in an attempt to bilk people out of $30 a month so detestable that I decided to do something.

I approached her, identified myself as a resident of the building, and asked her leave.  Naturally, she refused.  I offered to fetch the super or call the police, but IDT has no doubt trained its representatives to rebuff this threat.  She still refused to leave, went through the usual rigmarole of showing me her binder and IDT badge, and even invited me to call the police.  OK fine, I thought.  Have it your way.

I stood next to her as she knocked on another door.  When the tenant answered, she immediately launched into her spiel.  After a few seconds, I cut her off.  "Hello," I said cheerily to the tenant, a woman in her pajamas who looked a combination of confused and annoyed to find two strangers at her door at this hour.  "My name's Henry, I live on the 6th floor.  Please don't show this woman your power bill.  It's a scam, and you can search 'IDT' on consumerist.org if you'd like to know more about it."

The IDT rep attempted to retort, but the damage had already been done.  The tenant thanked me and closed her door.  "This is fun," I told the rep.  "Let's do another one."  She started to get in my face, saying perhaps I should get a job, seeing as how I had so much free time.  "I'm just home from work," I replied, "and I can't think of a better way to unwind than getting you out of my building.  Besides, it's only fair that your targets are presented with both sides of the story."

She knocked on another door, and the same thing happened.  She went into her pitch, and I cut her off.  The tenant thanked me and closed the door.  Now she was pissed.  She made a number of assumptions about my work habits, relationship, and use of personal time.  I smiled, asking if she'd like to visit another apartment.  She put on her coat and left the building.

Next time you find an energy scammer at your door, I invite you to do the same.  I know the current job market is pretty dire, and that New Yorkers need all the employment they can get.  But duping the elderly, non-English speakers, and the gullible into paying your crooked company a monthly stipend seems a lousy way to earn a living.
