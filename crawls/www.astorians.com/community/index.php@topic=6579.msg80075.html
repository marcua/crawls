There's nothing wrong with paying cash for a home but the majority of us don't have that kind of money.

So you have two options; rent or buy.

Rent...you are basically throwing your money away.  Not only are you throwing your money away but any improvements you make to the apartment (if you're allowed to) are meaningless financially.  If the apartment has crappy kitchen cabinets and the landlord won't change them but you want to have a nice looking kitchen and go through the expense of getting nice kitchen cabinets (inexpensive HomeDepot ones, not over the end for an apartment) but still an expense...guess what?  It means crap when it's time to move, the landlord keeps for FREE.

Own...sure, you are paying the bank interest, which at the moment is not much interest considering how low the rates are.  Nonetheless, a mortgage gives you options.  Most of them do not have prepayment penalty so if you happen to get a big chunk of money you can apply it to the principal.  You have the option of making double payments each month AND if you happen to become a millionaire you can pay it off and just like a car loan, you have a payoff amount (you don't pay the interest due for the remainder of the mortgage).  So sure, till that point you paid interest on the amount borrowed, but a) it's tax deductible (in rent nothing is deductable); b) your equity goes up with time; c) anything you do to the premises increased the value.

About the over inflated real estate market?  Yes, that's a given BUT you can always get foreclosure lists from banks, the State of New York does have auctions for real estate properties... http://www.jer.com/JERrevserv/JERRS-NYC-Overview.asp, also look at the HUD site.  

Sure, realtors don't want you to have this information because the lose out on commission but it is there, just a matter of doing the research.

Good luck in finding a home in Astoria, it's a wonderful neighborhood.

Oh, one more thing....Landlord's (even in a stabilized rent building) can say that the apartment is going to be used for "personal use" and kick you out (of course you have a certain amount of time to move out, etc.) but you are at the Landlords mercy.  Whereby when you own your place, it's up to you and no one else.

I think what DJ is trying to say that a lot of times you can rent a place for a lot less than the interest payments you'd make on a mortgage if you buy the place (in other words, the interest payments alone, excluding any amount you are paying in principal, are way higher than what you'd pay in rent). Consider the following example:

An Astoria House:
To Rent: $2000/mo. Thowing away entire $2000 a month to landlord. Heat, Repairs, Taxes paid for (theoretically).
To buy: $800,000.  Mortgage payment of $4000/mo ($3,000/mo going to interest, $1,000 going to your principal) = Throwing away $3,000/mo to bank, and $1000/mo going into your house equity). Heat, Repairs, and Taxes not paid for. These are additional on your mortgage payments.

In that example it would be a better "investment" to save the extra $1000 a month and just rent. Assuming the housing market continues to grow, at some point (some unknown number of years in the future) your "investment" portion of the house would have paid for itself and the buy option is a good deal.)

If you are living in an apartment where the landlord bought the house in 1950 for $30,000, then you can see why you may be able to get a lot lower rent than mortgage payment for the exact same house as the owner is probably renting it for less than the interest alone you'd pay on a mortgage (the throw-away portion of the mortgage excluding any equity you're building).

Bottom line is that if you want to live in a house for a long time then it's probably never really a bad time to buy (like 10 years or more). But if you're looking for a good "investment" that will earn you a return, you're probably better off putting your money in the bank and renting unless you find a way to get a mortgage where the interest portion of the loan is less than or equal to the amount you'd pay for renting the exact same place.
