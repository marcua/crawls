Economics as a science focuses on studying human action: It is concerned with *what human beings really do*.

Economics investigates and examines the costs, motivations, and consequences of human action (including human rules/social structures) given the nature of humans as valuing and acting individuals with certain abilities and limits operating in the context of the natural world and human society.

Economics as a science does not tell us what humans should or should not do. The role of an economist vis-a-vis public policy set by politicians is to discover the impact of those policies on people's lives.

There is a centuries old research project (broadly speaking, it's called classical liberalism) that has developed and continues to refine our economic understandings. It is now understood that prosperity (people living longer lives with more choices and more wealth), not the theory of prosperity, but the real lived phenomenon, arises consistently when individuals are free to seek their own ends, according to their own individuality and awareness of the specifics of their time and place.

The failure of central planning to produce prosperity has been observed, its problems perceived, documented, studied, and explained. Your haphazard support of minimum wage laws is, to be blunt, mystical. The evolution of the human mind over the course of about one hundred thousand years living as hunters-gatherers certainly increases the likelihood that some of us will naturally feel averse to wealth inequality. Hunters-gatherers were used to living in what game theorists call a zero-sum gain context. There were only so many berries collected, only so much meat. When George the hunter ate more meat than usual, it almost certainly meant that somebody else was eating less meat than usual. But that all changed with our transition to an economy based on producers-consumers. If you plot the progress that human beings have made in the last millennium against the progress made during the first 99,000 years of human civilization, you'll find that human beings AT EVERY INCOME LEVEL are benefiting tremendously from startling improvements in health, longevity, technology, and quality of life made possible by the emergence of ever more sophisticated and inclusive markets for more advanced and more accessible goods and services.

***

Wealth inequality is a boon to human society. Why?

Scientific discovery, technological innovation, the development of advanced manufacturing/distribution/transportation techniques... developing all of this progress involves a continuum of highly risky, speculative, costly endeavors.  The existence of super wealthy people represents a target market for novel inventions, for prototypes of goods and services that are, at least initially, extremely expensive to produce. The absence of such people would represent (1) the absence of an incentive for people to risk capital on the development of cutting edge breakthroughs and (2) the absence of a market-based PROFIT AND LOSS feedback loop for these highly risky projects. Profit and Loss are the ways in which capitalists learn when to launch/discontinue one project or how to make products more useful and appealing to potential investors/customers/consumers.   :mrgreen:

(Actually, all of us make profit/loss decisions every day. You put less energy/time/wealth into relationships, professional endeavors, career choices, romantic involvements  (etc.) with lower rates of return than alternatives. You put more energy/time/wealth into relationships with higher rates of return, so on and so forth. There is no way to operate outside of a profit and loss context. We are always using finite resources in finite contexts with results that can be measured against better or worse possible outcomes. Generally, we try to make the most of what we've got, or, in other words, to use what we have as profitably as possible.)

Automobile, electric power, medical (therapeutic and diagnostic), home theater, and computer technologies are all examples of innovations that were first enjoyed only by a precious few wealthy people. Plasma tvs that now cost under $1000 once cost a minimum of $70,000. Cell phones used to be huge and cost tens of thousands of dollars, now they're tiny and included with sub $30/month subscription plans. It must be noted that although wealth made it possible for a first wave of entrepreneurs to offer products based on these technologies, terrific levels of wealth only accrued to the subset of these entrepreneurs who were able to produce and sell products/services profitably to the masses, not merely to niche markets.    :mrgreen:

Think about that for a moment.

It is a comforting thought to imagine a world filled with people who are trying to develop products and services that you and I would benefit from using at a price you and I can afford. If those people become phenomenally wealthy, it means that a huge number of people valued their products MORE than what they paid for them. We all benefit from entrepreneurs' risk-taking, intelligence, insights, and competence, and they benefit from our choice to use their product instead of their competitors'. For the record, I simply never said that New Liberalism was an invention of Clinton or Roosevelt. I don't think anything I said even suggests that.

Yes, the ideas underlying the Progressive Era in the U.S (typically considered to be from about 1890 through the 1920s, though many agree that Roosevelt's New Deal represented a climactic expression of Progressive Era thinking) took shape during the Enlightenment Period, which itself was ignited by a re-discovery of the ideas of Aristotle and Plato and other philosophers of antiquity. Certainly Rousseau would be considered an intellectual forefather of the progressive era. His views on the important of state-controlled education certainly influenced Dewey. And his notions of freeing individuals from their own self-interests by teaching them from an early age to adopt as their own the general will of society certainly were part of the intellectual assault on liberalism that characterized the Progressive Era.

The Enlightenment's extreme reaction against mysticism would also lead, ironically, to an exaggerated sense of the power of human reason. That was great insofar as it inspired many to seek ever deeper and more sophisticated understandings of human nature and the natural world, and also tragically misguided insofar as it would inspire some to believe that societies might be improved by expert thinkers from above.

New Liberalism is ridiculous to me because the "new" stuff is all about undermining the "liberal" stuff. What exactly is so new about that? Most of the history of humanity is about some human beings holding power over others for no other reason than their own enrichment. Frankly, the warlords who sacked and pillaged never told the people they were plundering that it was for their own good. That was a socialist innovation.   

I know that many of the Progressive Era proponents of "positive rights" did not believe that their proposals posed problems to individual liberty. But they were wrong. Liberty rights are logically incompatible with rights that impose obligations on some at the involuntary expense of others. Once that contradiction is overlooked and laws begin to encroach on liberty, the results are ultimately dismal.

I know that intellectuals inspired by scientific breakthroughs were probably quite well-intentioned when they recommended putting experts in charge of the economy. But they were wrong, too. They mistakenly believed that their successful mastering of the mechanism of nature would translate into improving human society. Hence, during the Progressive Era, there's a great deal of jabber about "fine-tuning" human values, about individuals as "instruments" through which society improves, et cetera. Some people objected that human beings were not "means to ends", but the whole "individuals as ends in themselves" crowd was written off as regurgitating "old-school" thinking. Classical liberalism was on its way out.

So socialist ideas were increasingly in vogue at the beginning of the 20th century. I will grant that many (possibly most) advocates of positive rights and central planning back then were well-intentioned. If they could have known what the consequences of their ideas would end up being, I bet they would not have pursued their agenda of diminishing individual liberty and increasing the role of government in the economy.

But a great deal has happened since then. http://www.hawaii.edu/powerkills/  We simply no longer have any excuse to trust government to serve our interests better than we can. There's no longer a reasonable basis for believing that idea.

We've learned from experience that central planning fails, that its unintended consequences are profoundly harmful and even lethal. Intellectuals have developed theories that accurately describe the problems with central planning. Public choice theorists, Austrian economists, experimental economists (see Vernon Smith). There is an overwhelming data set bolstering the case for more individual freedom and less government interference.

The tragedy of the commons (how the absence of prices leads to shortages, abuse, and violence). The knowledge problem (the impossibility of central planners accounting for human individuality and contextual dynamics in a complex society). Unintended Consequences (there are costs and opportunity costs and unexpected costs with government interventions). Greed in the Public Sector (the keen observation that government employees are human beings just like the rest of us, but that the incentive structures at work within public institutions cause inefficiency and vice). Spontaneous Order (like Evolution, how social complexity arises from simplicity over time, not by design but as a result of human action).   

So, to all Democrats and Republicans excited about intelligently designing human society, I say this:

GIVEN what we've learned about what happens when the most well-intentioned government interferes coercively with the freedom of individuals to make choices, respond to incentives, adapt to their conditions, trade with one another, relocate themselves and pursue their own goals;

IF you care about poverty, if you care about starvation, if you care about the quality of education and health care, if you care about longevity and quality of life, if you care about discovery and innovation, if you care about prosperity;

THEN you have to become a bleeding heart advocate for human liberty and against state power.

You think there is something "extremely debatable" about this position? Bring it on. :mrgreen:
