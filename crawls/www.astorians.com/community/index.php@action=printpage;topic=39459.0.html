Post by: litza on July 25, 2016, 03:30:11 PM
I educated myself a lot about residential property taxes in NYC because I could not fathom how the numbers were calculated for our house, and I wanted to know how much they might increase.


Your situation may be very different, since I believe that the laws are different for multi-family dwellings (more than two units) than for private homes, coops, and condos.


Regardless, you can look up the latest property tax bills for your building here: http://nycserv.nyc.gov/NYCServWeb/NYCSERVMain. That gives you the Borough/Block/Lot numbers for the property, which you can use to look up the latest assessment here: http://nycprop.nyc.gov/nycproperty/nynav/jsp/selectbbl.jsp.


The latest bills may not reflect the latest assessment, however, because there's a limit on how quickly property taxes can increase on a given property. So if the property value increased by 20% in a given year, that increase will be phased in to tax calculations over several years (for single family properties, the cap on the increase is 6% per year, but no more than 20% over 5 years - I'm not sure how it works for multifamily). But the assessment will tell you both the assessed value and the "effective market value," so you can see both what the current taxes are and what they'll be increasing to within a few more years.


We've seen our property taxes nearly double in the 6 years that we've been in our house, and once housing price increases are phased in, they'll probably going to double again. So it's a not-inconsiderable increase, but still buffered to an extraordinary degree by generous city policies. And it's worth noting that by regional and national standards, our property taxes are insanely low.


I hope these tools help you get a handle on how your building's costs have increased! I'd be curious to find out what you learn.

Post by: 28Grand on July 25, 2016, 08:23:05 PM
Yippee, I feel your plight but in the end what diiference does it make if your rent is raised because of taxes or any other reason?

If your landlord is receiving a tax rebate through 421-A, taxes are probably very much reduced compared to similar properties. If your rent is rising I doubt it's because of property taxes.

Is your building covered by rent stabilization laws? If it's a newer building, i.e. built after 1971, then it might not be but often in exchange for 421-A status, landlords have to agree to abide by stabilization laws.

In the end though, if it's not covered, the landlord has the right to charge whatever he/she thinks the market can bear. You have the right not to pay it, but you'll have to move out of course.

I wish I had a solution for you but 'fair' is in the eye of the beholder. The rental situation is increasingly precarious in this city, and in Astoria. It really worries me and I think tbe dismantling of rent stabilization system is really unfair.
