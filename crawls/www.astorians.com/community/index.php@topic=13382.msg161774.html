I put my carrots and beets if I won't be using them right away into the fridge veg. bin. They've lasted without going soft for 'bout 3-4 weeks. And now, I know better than to keep onions and potatoes together as they will interact badly with one another, potatoes will soften, grow roots, etc... I now just keep the potatoes in a plastic bag and they're still with the onions, but at least not completely on top of one another. I know this doesn't help you, but as I have little space for a quantity of root vegetables nor the ability to try to use them up before they do go bad, I avoid getting the big bags of anything. While it might be cheaper (if you'r'e not doing the CSA) to get the bigger bags of potatoes, etc... in the end, it's far less wasteful (and less expensive after all) when I only buy the amount I'm actually going to cook with and that might be 2-3 onions or potatoes every 2-3 weeks.

Re: keeping root vegetables
« Reply #4 on: December 15, 2008, 02:52:23 AM »
Root veg -- beets, carrots, rutabega, parsnip, etc -- I store in large plastic bags with holes in them (just a few holes) in the fridge with the top twist tied.  These guys need to keep some moisture content to avoid rubbery trapped in grossness.

Potato and onion and garlic -- brown paper bags but separate brown paper bags for each sort.  Potato likes it cold and dark.  If exposed to light they will turn green and/or begin to sprout.  Don't eat the green ones.  If they do begin to grow eyes, you can snap the eyes out in the early stages and potato is still more or less edible.  Find the coolest spot you've got in your apartments and tuck the bulk away there (onion, potato, garlic).

Onion needs to be warmer than potato but again, not overly warm.  If they're going soft, dice and freeze.

Cabbage can be kept much the same as root veg in fridge.  Outer leaves will get gross but inner will be fine.

Winter squash that's been properly cured will keep at room temp.  Watch out for bruising and try to rest on soft surfaces.  Eat the bruised ones first.

Apples store in the fridge but separate the varieties as they off gas and may affect one another in flavor/time to decay.  You can also cut up and freeze slices for later use in baked goods.

And if your root veg isn't keeping well -- ferment it.  Lacto fermented veggies are good.  Kimchi is easy and there aren't rules so don't stick to standard cabbage ferments.  Or roast and freeze if ferments are your thing.
