You know, the Simotas supporters on this thread are throwing around all kinds of accusations about "attacks" but ever since I came to this thread, I've seen the majority of the hostility coming from their side. Honestly, just read the nonsense from Sting55. Mcdirk and BRIAN are notable exceptions and I wish we'd hear more from them.

I've thought a lot about this race since it's my district and my representation. I started out trying to be open-minded, despite the overwhelming negativity from one side on this board. But as I've said, I've decided to support Frei-Pearson.

As stevecrawford says, Simotas "seems" like she's on the right side of things. But she's mostly talk from what I can tell. She just doesn't seem to have done much to back up her words. Honestly, I just don't feel Simotas is running on her own merits: she's benefiting from tremendous systemic advantages as the Party pick and the playing field in this election is just incredibly distorted in her favor. Given that the Democratic Primary is the only chance voters in this district will have to choose their representation, I think that's disgraceful.

Astoria's representatives, Mike Gianaris and George Onorato, just voted to cut $775 million dollars from New York's healthcare; taking away indigent care funding for people who can't afford insurance and reducing Medicaid reimbursements for the poor. How can we expect their protege, Simotas, to do any different? I think that's the fundamental question of her candidacy: she's a product of the same Albany system that gives us scandal, corruption, and failure. How on earth is can we expect her to be anything but more of the same?

I suppose what I don't understand is why it's so bad to be part of the establishment. While I do understand the importance of bucking trends and being a strong voice in our dysfunctional government to try and bring about some reform, how does an outsider who failed to capture the endorsement of the major party bigwigs get anything done in the chance they actually do get elected? Wouldn't there be resentment and stonewalling? And wouldn't that only hurt the district?

In 2008, Barack Obama was backed by the established Democratic Party and thrust into the spotlight over candidates with more experience and greater recognition. Was that a bad thing too?

I've been on the fence about who to vote for, but this point I see myself voting for Simotas. holyfrjole, the Party has proved fairly resilient in adapting to challengers--once they win. Danny Dromm ran against a Party pick for his city council seat and now he's welcomed by the Party leaders--because he won. If a candidate has the backing of the voters, the Party adjusts. But the Party always resists outsiders at first.

Jeremiah has broad-based grassroots support in the district--I see it every time I canvass or petition. Many reform-centered campaigns have been launched by candidates who were initially opposed by the Party bosses. As an Assemblyman, Jeremiah will not be afraid to stand up and fight for our district where necessary--he's proven that. But he also has good relationships with many local leaders now and those relationships have survived this campaign. I'm sure they'll become even stronger when he wins.
