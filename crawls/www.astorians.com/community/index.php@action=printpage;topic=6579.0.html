Title: Re: Looking to buy (not rent) in Astoria
Post by: merm on July 02, 2007, 02:29:12 PM
I think what DJ is trying to say that a lot of times you can rent a place for a lot less than the interest payments you'd make on a mortgage if you buy the place (in other words, the interest payments alone, excluding any amount you are paying in principal, are way higher than what you'd pay in rent). Consider the following example:

An Astoria House:
To Rent: $2000/mo. Thowing away entire $2000 a month to landlord. Heat, Repairs, Taxes paid for (theoretically).
To buy: $800,000.  Mortgage payment of $4000/mo ($3,000/mo going to interest, $1,000 going to your principal) = Throwing away $3,000/mo to bank, and $1000/mo going into your house equity). Heat, Repairs, and Taxes not paid for. These are additional on your mortgage payments.

In that example it would be a better "investment" to save the extra $1000 a month and just rent. Assuming the housing market continues to grow, at some point (some unknown number of years in the future) your "investment" portion of the house would have paid for itself and the buy option is a good deal.)

If you are living in an apartment where the landlord bought the house in 1950 for $30,000, then you can see why you may be able to get a lot lower rent than mortgage payment for the exact same house as the owner is probably renting it for less than the interest alone you'd pay on a mortgage (the throw-away portion of the mortgage excluding any equity you're building).

Bottom line is that if you want to live in a house for a long time then it's probably never really a bad time to buy (like 10 years or more). But if you're looking for a good "investment" that will earn you a return, you're probably better off putting your money in the bank and renting unless you find a way to get a mortgage where the interest portion of the loan is less than or equal to the amount you'd pay for renting the exact same place.
