Thanks annie,

You are right, this does represent an interesting risk for people who have domains (at least with eNom) that depend on them for traffic or income. It seems they can pretty easily be convinced to disable your domain without attempting to contact you first.

In my case they bought this person argument for two reasons.

1.) I use a voicemail service out of Seattle, WA. I've had it for years and continue to use it today for business reasons (I give this out publicly to companies to avoid marketing calls). I check this voicemail daily, and haven't ever had problems with it. However, having a site named "astorians" for NY, and a voicemail in Seattle made this person's false claims appear more credible.

2.) I prefer not to put my home address on my domain contact information (for reasons that should now be obvious), so I put my PO BOX, which is located in California and checked regularly by someone legally authorized to collect my mail. But this also gave credibility to this person's claims (that the information was inaccurate).

Even though there's nothing in ICANN's or eNoms agreement or requirements that your address be located in the same geographic area, or that your address has to be your home address, this person was successfully able to convince them to suspend my domain without contacting me (which interestingly their own agreement says they will do before deactivation). This being the case, it took me 2 days to even figure out what the problem was. eNom deactivated the domain last Tuesday.
