Post by: daveed on September 16, 2005, 12:27:30 PM
I'm going to try to resist sounding too academic, but I won't skimp on the cynicism... :twisted:

Politics is about goal attainment within a social sphere. Accordingly, it also provides a medium by which we define/refine those goals. And not just on a governmental level. For instance, when your book club is deciding what to read next, getting everyone to choose involves politics.

But when we talk about Politics as we know it whereby it's become a profession rather than an act of service, it's more about self-serving the practitioners. IOW, the goal is to preserve their power, expand it, take it away from rivals, etc. A politician may say, "the goal is to [insert issue]," but really it's, "the goal is to make sure I stay in office."

(When I say 'office', I don't just mean elected officials. It could apply to all sorts of positions of authority, from school principals to CEOs.)

It gets really messy when politicos start colluding. Then you get political parties, platforms and agendas that creates a power system for its own sake. Self-invested authority will seek to expand that authority -- at the very least preserve it.

Title: Of water, gourds, pyramids and Divine Right
Post by: zeno on October 29, 2005, 08:10:09 AM
After reconnecting with my subconscious mind, a.k.a. sleep, I remembered that there is another way to look at power, or another way to model it, and this model to me (although metaphoric) seems to me to make the most sense.

Imagine if you will... water. Water is the life giver and yet it is also known as the Universal Solvent. Now, imagine if you will a network of gourds and these gourds are filled with water with one gourd that is tipping over filling the next and the second gourd filling the third and so on. Maybe the sixth gourd is not only filling the seventh gourd but also the eighth as well. As the water flows into the gourds imagine that the water picks up speed as it makes its way down, the higher the first gourd the more crushing its effect on the last. The more gourds there are then the bigger the reservoir the first must be.

If the first gourd were to steady itself or to dry up then the consecutive gourds would by logical extention also dry up. If the first gourd in the series were to limit the flow of water by only slightly steadying itself then the remaining gourds would adjust themselves accordingly becoming either more filled or less filled with water. What would happen if the first gourd were cracked? Depending on where the fracture was (and how severe) we could have the first gourd spilling more water into the third gourd while completely bypassing the second!

If power can be likened to water, then power flows down. That is its natural state. And this works itself in well with the pyramidal model of power, where you have one extremely paranoid individual (capstone) at the top and a multiplicity of bricks at the bottom supporting it. Why paranoid? Oh, let me explain.

Apexualis is a man who is afraid of his own shadow so he hires a bodyguard. Can this bodyguard be trusted? In all good conscience Apexualis hires another bodyguard to watch the first. But who will watch the second? Why, a third bodyguard of course! And the third...? Well, there is definitely something shady about him...
This goes on until the amount of potential bodyguards runs out or the resources of the first to control them. Keep also in mind that the bodyguards themselves have become the apex of their own pyramids and also need watching. :-) The higher the pyramid, the greater the resources, id est the greater the power. So now you have this gargantuan structure with everybody watching everybody else but more importantly everyone is watching the one individual at the top from whom they take their cue. If it was Apexualis' original intent to feel more secure then it seems to me that he has alot more to worry about now besides his own shadow.

Back to the gourds. There is another use for this model of aqueducts and reservoirs which has been used historically. This self-same system has also been used to model the flow of divine energy or spiritual enlightenment, even Grace. As the water flowed from one gourd to the next the water got more tainted as a result. The water which supplied the first gourd came from resplendent Heaven and had complete purity. By some twist of word association and post nocturnal grogginess this brings me to the Divine Right of Kings.

Is it a coincidence that there has always been throughout history a dialectic between Church and State, Hierophant and Pharoah, Pope and King? Who gave Charlemagne his crown and who crowned Napolean? Who does George W. talk to anyway when he appears to be muttering to himself when he should be studying with his speechalist? And why was it so fashionable in Egypt and Rome for the supreme rulers to deify themselves? Didn't they have enough power? Perhaps this was only a justification for the power that they already wielded. After all people are less likely to question the dictum of a god than a mere mortal. And why do people seek power to begin with? Could it be that the closer to the top of the pyramid one gets the more likely one would be able to comprehend the inner workings of the Universe, the truer meanings of existence, and a more personal relationship with God to boot? To mortal-ken that have a limited time on earth, that are in fact Doomed to Die, perhaps it wasn't a "relationship" to God that was sought for after all, and given the imperious nature of these individuals who never liked playing second fiddle to anyone, perhaps it was Eternity...

I can’t say that I really agree with the way your analogy of water reflects the nature of power. To me that analogy was indicative to the nature of gravity. So then by going back to your view of the bodyguards you demonstrate that a true pyramid distribution of power is not an effective method of transferring power. How many guards can the guy buy before he runs out of money, and through the whole process he still has to live with the fear of his shadow? A more modern way of looking at the issue would be to hire one guard who is not afraid of shadows to demonstrate to the guy that he does not have to be afraid. Since most of us know that a shadow, by itself will not hurt anyone. Here the nature of power stills lies in the nature of control, but it is a change in what is being controlled. Rather than a person we are controlling it is a fear.

On your last issue you tie power with spirituality. To explore this you have to go back and analyze the root of how God entered into human understanding.  Without going into the definition of God, Which would be the seed, we can conclude that Man has looked to God as the authority in the universe and as such we have subscribed to God the beacon of morality. In part, because over time we recognize our own failings, but also because we have not been able to understand all that transpires in the world or why.  God becomes the convenient escape goat for our failings, and simultaneously the hope by which we imagine we will overcome them. It becomes an intellectual case of denial, followed by a willingness to try to get better. It also embarks upon the beginning of change in the way we look at power. If there is no clear line of communication between the creator and the created, there can be an array of miscalculated conclusions and as such the otherwise given distribution of power could be tampered. We then gain a surer platform by which to attempt to acquire power over our own lives. This leads to the notion that failure is a vital part of our growth, and creates a lesser dependency on God to solve our problems. Eventually you can logically move towards the conclusion that we must develop a moral standard independent of God. Today this is probably the hottest issue regarding the distribution of power.

From here you can move quite easily into the region of Religion, which I will skip. Structured religions are a thing of the past, at least intellectually. They will, like race, be used as a means of acquiring and maintaining power for years to come, until such time as the whole of man recognizes their fallacies. Having said that, the moral vacuum that comes from such a fallout could be devastating.  To move forward into the future of understanding and implementing moral codes without the notion of a God, we get easily lost into the mathematical impossibilities of understanding all the circumstances of a scenario and have to conclude eventually that the best we can do when it comes to making the right decision morally is make a good guess. And then through our weakness we permit ourselves to make that guess work in our advantage, undermining our integrity.

To those who argue that we all know right from wrong, I would strongly disagree. Instinct can prompt us to do any number of things that would be considered abhorrent morally. So if it is not instinct and it is not God, what is it that allows us to make decisions of moral right and wrong? Obviously, those who are in power make the moral codes by which we adhere to as a society.  The strongest debate lies in the priority of the moral power. Does the individual have a right to create his own moral edicts? Does society have the right to independently create their own?  And when there is a conflict which one has the moral high ground?

From here it would be unwise to pass a decision and say that one or the other is more important.  The issue wraps itself around the priority an individual will have in shaping society, and vice versa. Ultimately, there has to be a sharing of power and control.  Where that lies would have to be at the frequency where the needs of society and individuals resonate.
