I personally don't see a big problem using raw eggs, particularly if they're purchased from a reputable supplier, properly stored, and the finished product is then properly stored.  At school we made countless dressings with raw eggs, including more mayonnaise than I care to think about, and never had a problem.  I think it's probably quite rare to get sick from eggs these days in this country, especially if they are stored in a cold enough environment (NEVER on the door of the fridge, always near the back), and that you make sure the egg itself does not come into contact with the outside of the shell.  That said, it's probably best not to use raw eggs in dishes you will serve to people with immunity issues, the elderly, or young children, as those groups of people would be more susceptible to a reaction from bacteria.  It's also possible to buy pasteurized eggs (they're exposed to heat while inside the shell, but are kind of hard to find).  For yourself, for experimentation, I don't think you'll have a problem.  And I also don't think organic versus non-organic eggs would affect their safety, just their overall flavor quality.  I'm all about cage-free eggs, myself.

I, too, would be interested in the difference between ice cream made with cooked custard as opposed to raw egg.  Just use really fresh, well-stored ones.  That's the key to avoiding bacteria, 40 degrees Fahrenheit or below (35, depending on the sanitation book).  Happy cracking.   :-D

Homemade mayonnaise is actually in a slightly different category - once you have made it, you should let it rest at room temperature for an hour or two.  The pH of the mayonnaise (acidic environment) kills any bacteria that might have been present in the raw egg.  This must be done at room temperature, though.  (Thanks, Alton Brown.  :-D )

In Europe, many people never refrigerate eggs.  I have visited friends who keep their eggs in the cupboard.  However, it is important to note that they do not buy large quanities of eggs at one time, and this is generally in more northern countries where it doesn't usually get as hot in the summertime.  I remember posting somewhere about this topic, and I had found all sorts of data on the effects of specific temperatures on how long eggs will last.  I'll try to remember where I posted it.  If I find it, I'll copy or link to it.

In the US, it's my understanding that factory farming has actually increased the incidence of salmonella-contaminated eggs.  It's my opinion, although I've never seen any statistics on the issue, that organic or free range eggs are in fact safer, particularly if you buy them from local farmers (for instance, at the green market). 

Meg - just for reference, my chocolate ginger wasabi ice cream is made with a cooked custard.
