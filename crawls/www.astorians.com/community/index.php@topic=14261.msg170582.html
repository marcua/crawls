That's the increases I usually go for with my tenants--my neighbor ALWAYS takes the absolute maximum rent, chops up his big apartments to rent for two smaller ones to get more rent and--has lots of turnover.

More rent means roommates (more people) using more water, making more noise, more come-and-go, more garbage and more damage to the apartment as greater turnover means people moving in and out, nailing holes in walls, and less goodwill from your neighbors.

That is something to help you in your negotiations with (petty) landlords:

They have to balance the lower increase with the cost and hassle of repainting and fixing up the apartment and the possibility of losing a month's rent until they find another tenant. Ofcourse, I've heard some landlords don't even paint but still...interviewing candidates, dealing with real estate, showing the apartment...leases...keys...returning deposits...

..AND they have to factor in that IF you moved out, how much more or less rent would they expect to make it worth it? (That's where the economy comes in.)

...this is where being a good tenant comes in...and what if the new person(s) turned out to be a real pain? Or bring a dozen relatives? Have a baby or get a dog?

By the way, as soon as a new tenant moves in, they almost always find a ton of things wrong even if they love the apartment because they're focusing on it. Once they've established themselves, they forget to mention stuff that needs fixing until it really cramps their style. As a landlord, you gotta prepare for that too.

It can seem better to some landlords to accept a little less and avoid rocking the boat, if they've got a good tenant and their costs are covered.
