I started doing the coupon thing too in the past few months. At first I felt dorky about it, but it's now become an OBSESSION. Newsday has the best coupons (yes, how much they offer sometimes differs from paper to paper), and the Sunday Newsday is only $1. So if you want to do coupons, get it even if you don't read it.

At the grocery store, I stock up on stuff when it's on sale, as people have mentioned. I eat a lot of chicken, and the family packs of breasts are on sale a lot. I get them home and freeze them into sandwich-sized freezer bags. They defrost quickly if you put the bagged meat in a bowl of cool water (I don't know the physics behind it, but it works!) I hate defrosting them in the microwave, they get all ewwey. I also stock up on cereal - if you just buy individual boxes, they're expensive, but if you keep an eye on sales & coupons, they can be cheap. An example - Smart Start cereal at CVS is something like $4.59 a box. A month or so ago, they had a buy one get one free sale. THEN, I had a crazy coupon (printed from the internet) for $3 off when you buy two boxes of Smart Start. So between the sale and the coupon, I paid about 80 cents a box. If you drive, Target also has good cereal sales. Basically, pay attention to sales everywhere - not just grocery stores - for your food shopping.

I also don't allow myself to get takeout more than once every 2 weeks. It's too easy of a crutch when I'm hungry, it's hot, I need groceries/don't feel like defrosting anything, and I don't feel like heating up the kitchen. All right - it's not very specific, but here are the things you'd need for vegetarian chili a la essen:

vegetable oil
2 cloves garlic, minced
1 medium yellow onion, chopped
1 green pepper, chopped
1 package white mushrooms, cut into wedges, 4 or 6 wedges per mushroom depending on size
1 large can whole peeled tomatoes (whatever the biggest standard size can is)
1 can black beans
1 can red beans
approx 1/4 cup of bulgar wheat
approx 2 TBSP Badia chili powder (I use Badia brand, but you may want less depending on what brand you use)
approx 2 TSP cumin
some ground black pepper
dash of cinnamon

Get out a very large pan or pot. Cut the green pepper first and sauté it in the vegetable oil, and as it's cooking cut up the garlic and onions. The pepper should sauté on low-medium heat for at least 10 minutes before adding the garlic and onions, and those should cook for a few minutes. Before you add anything else, the peppers should be starting to look cooked and the onions should be softening.

While that's cooking, cut up the mushrooms and then add those at the appropriate time and mix it all around. Open up the tomatoes and break them into the mix, including the juice from the can, and then add the two cans of beans. (You may want to drain the bean juice, but I like leaving in some of it. I like the organic beans from Key Food; the juice in the other stuff skeeves me.) Then add the spices. Then add the bulgar wheat. Add as much of the bulgar as you think you want - for me it serves to provide a meaty texture without being "fake meat," but the chili shouldn't be overwhelmed by it, so if I were to measure it I think it's around 1/4 cup.

The wheat will absorb the juice from the tomatoes and bean cans. This should all simmer for at least 20 minutes. Stir it occasionally and taste it to make sure you like the spice level. Add whatever other spices you want; keep in mind that the chili that results from this is not spicy at all, but it is tasty. If you like spicy chili you will need to add your own spicy things. Simmer until it's not so liquidy and the mushrooms have shrunk. I like serving it with rice, and I cook the rice in a method inspired by Chipotle, which is to cook it with fresh cilantro and lime. But that's optional.

Served with rice, I would say this makes 8-10 servings.

My red onion compote goes something like this:

1 tbsp olive oil
1 red onion, finely sliced
½ tsp soft brown sugar
1 tbsp balsamic vinegar
½ lemon, juice only
salt and freshly ground black pepper

Heat the oil in a pan and add the red onion. Fry for 5-8 minutes, or until just softened but not too browned.
Stir in the sugar, vinegar and lemon juice and season, to taste, with salt and freshly ground black pepper.

(From: http://www.bbc.co.uk/food/recipes/database/frenchonionsoupwithc_87385.shtml)

Sometimes I like to trade honey for the sugar--just add to taste.

Enjoy!

salad:
--6 medium redskin potatoes, boiled and cooled, then sliced

--juice of one lemon
--some red wine vinegar. probably about a tablespoon
--1 garlic clove, minced
--1 tablespoon? minced shallot
--1 teaspoon dijon mustard
--one teaspoon minced dill
--maybe a teaspooon of capers
--salt and pepper to taste

All right - it's not very specific, but here are the things you'd need for vegetarian chili a la essen:

vegetable oil
2 cloves garlic, minced
1 medium yellow onion, chopped
1 green pepper, chopped
1 package white mushrooms, cut into wedges, 4 or 6 wedges per mushroom depending on size
1 large can whole peeled tomatoes (whatever the biggest standard size can is)
1 can black beans
1 can red beans
approx 1/4 cup of bulgar wheat
approx 2 TBSP Badia chili powder (I use Badia brand, but you may want less depending on what brand you use)
approx 2 TSP cumin
some ground black pepper
dash of cinnamon

Get out a very large pan or pot. Cut the green pepper first and sauté it in the vegetable oil, and as it's cooking cut up the garlic and onions. The pepper should sauté on low-medium heat for at least 10 minutes before adding the garlic and onions, and those should cook for a few minutes. Before you add anything else, the peppers should be starting to look cooked and the onions should be softening.

While that's cooking, cut up the mushrooms and then add those at the appropriate time and mix it all around. Open up the tomatoes and break them into the mix, including the juice from the can, and then add the two cans of beans. (You may want to drain the bean juice, but I like leaving in some of it. I like the organic beans from Key Food; the juice in the other stuff skeeves me.) Then add the spices. Then add the bulgar wheat. Add as much of the bulgar as you think you want - for me it serves to provide a meaty texture without being "fake meat," but the chili shouldn't be overwhelmed by it, so if I were to measure it I think it's around 1/4 cup.

The wheat will absorb the juice from the tomatoes and bean cans. This should all simmer for at least 20 minutes. Stir it occasionally and taste it to make sure you like the spice level. Add whatever other spices you want; keep in mind that the chili that results from this is not spicy at all, but it is tasty. If you like spicy chili you will need to add your own spicy things. Simmer until it's not so liquidy and the mushrooms have shrunk. I like serving it with rice, and I cook the rice in a method inspired by Chipotle, which is to cook it with fresh cilantro and lime. But that's optional.

Served with rice, I would say this makes 8-10 servings.

This is a recipe I love - it's super cheap, great for cold weather, and need very little attention thanks to the crockpot! It's originally from Slow Cooker Cooking by Lora Brody.

French Onion Soup

3 pounds Vidalia or other sweet onions - peeled and cut into 1/8-1/4" thick slices
1 stick butter
6 cups beef, chicken, or vegetable stock
Salt and pepper, to taste
Bread of choice (I prefer a crusty french baguette)
Cheese of choice (I do a mix of swiss and locatelli cheeses)

Put the onions and butter in the slow cooker for 12-14 hours on low. They should be a deep dark brown mahogany and very soft. Afterwards, drain the onions, reserving the liquid that has accumulated. (After you let the liquid cool, you can skim the congealed butter from the top of the onion broth and use it to flavor another dish). Then combine onions, cooking liquid, and stock in a pot, bring to a simmer over medium-high heat, and season with salt and pepper to taste. Then ladle soup into ovenproof bowls, float a slice of bread in it, top with cheese, and put in the broiler until the cheese melts and starts to bubble. It only takes 1-2 minutes in my broiler. Enjoy!

Since I make my own chicken stock and always have cheese and butter in stock, it usually only costs me around 7 dollars to make a huge potful of soup.
