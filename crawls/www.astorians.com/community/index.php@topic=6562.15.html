I would love to hear more personal stories as to who drives in Manhattan, and why. 

I drive. I work in Queens (out in Jamaica) and drive to work every day. Frequently, I will either have something to attend in Manhattan immediately after work or during work hours and will necessarily have to drive into Manhattan. Or I'll have an early morning doctor's appointment (my doctor is in Manhattan) and then have to drive straight to work. Or I'll just have something to do in Manhattan for the day, but got screwed with alternate side parking and had to move my car by 8:30 a.m. and had nothing else to do with it (because really, why open parking lots when there can be more overpriced "luxury" apartment buildings?). OR because I live here in Astoria, just a stone's throw away from Manhattan, I'm either picking someone who lives in Manhattan up or am dropping them off.

My point is that there are many reasons that one would drive into Manhattan that have nothing to do with daily commuting. Congestion pricing places an undue financial burden on the "occasional" motorist -- the one who, like me, makes fairly frequent, but not regular, trips into Manhattan by car out of necessity.

I think this plan is awful -- not only because it WILL make it more difficult for residents like us to park in our own neighborhood (because really, the 20 minutes I now spend looking for parking isn't nearly long enough!), but because it also places further constraints on the time and wallets of the already overburdened working and middle class.

For the occasional commuter I think its important to keep in mind some of the parameters of the proposed plan.  The charge is in effect from 6a-6p, Mon - Fri.  So a sunday drive through the city does not pick up the charge.  Neither would entering the city after 6p to go to dinner or see a show.  Also tolls you'd have to pay anyways, like through the midtown tunnel and the Lincoln tunnel are deducted from the charge, so if you are cutting through the city to go to Jersey, in effect you'll be paying the same tolls post congestion pricing and pre congestion pricing.  And lastly for the occasional commuter its an occasional charge.  Odds are its not going to put a serious dent in your pocket book, regardless of income level, if you are only driving into the city say once a week. 

What is interesting about Barrister15's story and is typical of many of the auto commuters in Queens is that his daily commute is inside Queens, from Astoria to Jamaica, and therefore is not part of the congestion charge.  What I also find interesting is that Jamaica has decent public transit access and in theory (not knowing exactly where in Jamaica he's headed) Barrister could take the subway to there, but he elects to drive.  Which is why we need to promote deterrents to driving and not, as some groups would promote, incentives to not drive, because people often do respond better to a deterrent than an incentive.  I'm not trying to pick on Barrister15 or tell him to change his commute, but it is a good example.

And Alexa is right as well, while it may seem to benefit only Manhattanites, it really will have global benefits that will spread out as less people drive all over.

Here's my driving story.  I used to drive commercial vans as a production assistant and a carpenter's assistant all the time from Chambers St to all over NYC.  I would spend forever sitting in traffic trying to get from the rental place to downtown and then going back uptown again to our client's location.  For the carpenter this was often on the Upper West Side and the Upper East Side.  We'd use all sorts of routes, the highway, not the highway, through the park, etc., and he had lots of traffic theories, but the fact was most of the time we sat in traffic.  On some days this clearly set us behind and cost him money to pay me to sit in traffic by the hour and to take longer to finish projects.

First off, Barrister is a woman. 

A woman who works very long hours as a public interest attorney representing at risk youth, who often leaves her office (in a not so nice neighborhood), alone, and at night. Now, as to congestion pricing...

$8 may not seem like much, but it adds up, and can become burdensome if done with some degree of frequency. People like their cars. People will still drive. And I'm sure if this goes into effect the mayor will be patting himself on the back if the numbers show a decrease in traffic congestion in Manhattan, but I highly doubt that anyone will be looking at the increase in traffic in surrounding areas.

Sorry for the gender mix up, those little gender signs are small and easy to miss.  Your commute is your commute and you obviously feel strongly about it, however it won't be affected by congestion pricing.  Your occasional trips would, and if you enter the city enough for the price to become burdensome than maybe your occasional trips aren't so occasional after all.

As to congestion pricing, like a lot of opponents say that $8 is too much but at the same time that it won't deter many people from driving anyway.  But how can it realistically be too much for people to pay if it's not going to act as a deterrent?  I think of course that the first part, its too much, is accurate while the second part, it won't work is not.  Yes there are people who love their cars and can afford the $40 a week it would take to drive in and probably will continue to do so, but I think this is going to be a financial pinch for a good chunk of drivers and it will force a lot of people to rethink how they get into the city.  And yes, most of those people who need to rethink their commute are not going to be wealthy.  But congestion pricing isn't meant to fix the harsh realities of economic disparities, its meant to reduce the amount of traffic.  And in exchange the money from people who insist on driving, and who will be largely in the upper tiers of income, will go to public transit, already used mainly by people in the lower tiers of income.  And most of those public transit benefits are going to be seen in Queens, B'land and the Bronx, where public transit is need, initially in the form of BRTs.  This is not some sort of Flintstone's vitamin that tastes like candy and does nothing good for you, this is a nasty tasting pill that will make NYC's traffic better.  Once we swallow it we will feel better, I feel that very strongly.

And like I said before, the city has plans to keep people from park and riding in our neighborhood, and neighborhoods like ours.  First will be residential parking permits, like they use along the river in Jersey and in cities like Boston.  No residential permit, no parky, probably a tow and a steep fine.  No more free parking, and thats something that the Mayor may put through even if congestion pricing fails.  Second, the DoT did one of their models and their model tells them that most drivers will not add more time to their commute to spend looking for parking in an area like Astoria where parking is already a bitch to find.  I don't know how they figure out their models, but I know anecdotally when my now ex-girlfriend had a car I hated having to park it with her, sometimes a 10-15 minute walk away from her apt, on alt side parking days.  This was a big bone of contention between us as I urged her to just get rid of the car, since she never really used it, and she had an emotional attachment to the purple beast.  I can't imagine myself wasting even more time just to avoid an $8 charge if there is transit where I live.  And the charge will enable the MTA to be sure there is transit where people live.

Do you know what the DoT found in one of their traffic studies?  That in order to avoid the tolls on the Midtown Tunnel, a better faster way to midtown, drivers would come through Astoria to take the QBB and avoid the toll.  If drivers know they have to pay a congestion charge anyways, and that the Midtown Tunnel toll will be deducted from that, drivers won't need to avoid that major artery and won't feel the need to cut through neighborhoods to take the free bridge.

So thats it for me, I'll be away from my computer until after the Congestion Pricing Forum tonite.  I hope to see some of you there, the last one was very informative and they did take questions at the end, so if you're on the fence or just don't know what the plans really all about I highly suggest you come.  I'll be the short guy with glasses, brown hair and a scraggly beard if you want to say howdy, but if if I'm now on your enemies list I'm actually a tall muscular Latino gentleman with no hair and a dragon tattoo on my head.
