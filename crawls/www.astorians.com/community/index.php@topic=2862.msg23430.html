A beginner’s guide to Astoria
« on: August 04, 2006, 09:15:39 AM »
For the recent arrivals who have recently discovered and ventured to our fair shores –  Some history about the neighborhood in case you didn’t know…

The ‘smell’
Who the heck needs an alarm clock when you have ‘the smell’ to greet you each morning? The tide goes out and the smell comes in – voila! – eyes a poppin’ and nose a waterin’. I remember a study was done when I was in grade school, some government agency, the EPA or the Port Authority I think, gave out ‘smell-o-meters’ to a few residents so they could measure the smell and the study confirmed what everyone already knew (don’t they always?) – Flushing Bay around the airport was not so flushing anymore. They dredged the muck around the airport and Bowery Bay and the odor abated somewhat. Aside from the unfortunate stretch of stench when the boilers went out at the sewage plant recently, it’s been almost rosy compared to what I remember growing up. Don’t worry -- you’ll get used to the smell, and, after 20-30 years of living here, won’t even notice it that much.
 
Astoria Pool
A melee at Astoria pool? Yeah it’s been 100 degrees for weeks but now I know it’s summer! Let’s go! If you were lucky enough to experience one of these free-for-alls you can proudly claim you are practically native Astorian. There’s at least one, sometimes two to three per summer. Temperatures and tempers flare and a few hundred people baking in the heat do not a picture of tranquillity make. Scary yes, but at least no one was stabbed or shot. Who said quality of life isn’t improving?

Motorcycle racing
Ah boys and their toys. Guys think that making the biggest spectacle of themselves as they can will attract the ladies but the truth is it only attracts other guys…plus hasn’t the thought crossed your minds that the louder the bike means the smaller the penis? They  say it pays to advertise but really, why a guy would want to call attention to himself in this way? Well, again it’s not summer until the bikes come out. Racing/noise has been a problem for as long as I can remember, especially down by the park. Sometimes the cops close off Shore Boulevard to help control the problem but it’s a short-term fix that usually results in the riders moving to other parts of the neighbourhood to annoy the people elsewhere. Complaints have been made; committees have been organized but nothing much is done about it – unless we outlaw motorcycle sales to young male between the ages of 18-24. Turn up the Air con, turn up the TV and tune out (and buy ear plugs).

Book store
I hate to shatter the dream but I feel I must. It’s better you know this now to avoid heartbreak in the future so here it goes: There has never been and probably will never be a decent bookstore in Astoria. Give this thought up right this minute and save yourselves a lot of pain and aggravation. Until recently the population has been mostly working class, largely immigrant, and for the most part non-English as a first language speaking and the demographics just never worked to support one. Where did B&N open their stores when they first expanded into Queens? –Forest Hills and Bayside, neighbourhoods with people with higher incomes and better educations. It’s just the way it is. Accept it and move on.

Architecture
The darkest day in Astoria architectural history was not the day they erected a five story apartment house in the middle of a beautiful run of row houses on 36th street but much longer ago, in the early 70s when the Loews Triboro theatre – the one screen, 3290 seat movie palace, on the corner on 28th Avenue and Steinway – that was demolished and replaced by the most undistinguished, uninspired stretch of shops and apartments were imagined. All I can say to the buildings of Astoria is avert your eyes and learn to love. There’s small apartment building and house on the corner of 33rd street and 31st avenue where, after the initial feelings of revulsion, you just can’t help but love – c’mon the spectacle, the pizzazz, the balls it took to clad your property in pink, grey and white flagstones. Astoria was never really an architectural hotspot but we have (had) our gems. I don’t know what to say but I just wish the esthetics could improve. What does it cost a developer to add some distinction to a simple two- to three-family row house? Peanuts I think. But my beef is at the people who buy them. You get what you pay for. It’s sad when the quite plain row house that I grew up in can be considered downright pretty compared to the monstrosities going up today. Oh well, if you travel to Italy, Greece and dare I say Ireland (I was in Dublin a few months ago) not the pretty parts mind you where the tourist and the well-off live but the areas with the ‘real’ people you’ll find you haven’t left home.

Shops and cafes
What’s the problem? Real stores that sell real things -- not boutiques that sell useless items at ridiculous prices. At least we don’t have empty storefronts which was about to be a problem not so long ago. I know we could do without another .99 store, or a nail salon, or a cell phone store, or a bank – it’s all so boring but they wouldn’t open if people in the neighbourhood didn’t frequent them. At least we don’t have to deal with high-end shops that sell designer-ware for people’s pets or too-cool-for-school cafes filled to the brim with hipsters and wanna-bes, dressed in the same clothes, reading the same things (if they could find a bookstore that is), and droning on about whatever they drone about. Okay, maybe one of those places would be nice but when one opens it’s only too soon before we're overrun. I don’t know, I may not sip my coffee at some of the euro-cafes but a part of me is glad they’re there. 

Come and enjoy Astoria..we have the park, the waterfront, and a beer garden...what else matters?

(Erin Lippincott was a reporter for the Queens Chronicle for a few years: she covered Western Queens and lived off Broadway in the 40s. Written before she moved out west in 2002)

Why I Don’t Really Hate Queens
E.E. Lippincott (NYC)

I hate the borough of Queens or, more accurately, until last week I hated the borough of Queens.

It's an unsightly place, full of the pollution that the rest of New York City won't tolerate being produced in its backyard. It's full of immigrants who may or may not take their new country seriously. It's full of longtime United States citizens, who decided decades ago that with as little learning and bravery and creativity as possible they could just about avoid any progress.

It’s full of large, dirty thoroughfares, two huge airports and a second-rate baseball stadium. It's decorated in cheap wrought iron and gaudy fake-gold appliqué, like a prize out of a 25 cent machine in a grocery store.

Buildings keep going up all over the place as rents in Manhattan become too severe and people move east towards Queens and Long Island. And that construction is of the poorest quality possible because the entire borough has been put up for sale by the smalltime crooks who are elected to run it. Families hand down run-down diner or restaurant dynasties to their offspring.

Newspapers are bought out by the advertisers or the politicians who pay for their printing.

The old ladies, Italian or Greek or German, sit on their front porches and are very sure they know exactly the way life should be and why you are not acceptable. The men make a lot of talk about what they won't tolerate and how they're not afraid to stand up to so and so and then quietly, with harsh lined faces, let themselves be hoodwinked again and again.

It's a place of sit down and shut up and don't you dare talk back you'd better like what's coming to you. It's a place that refers to Manhattan, the next borough over and often plainly visible on the horizon, as "the city."

It's a borough full of losers. It's a borough made to be suckered. I hated it because I felt I was suckered too. No man is an island, so they say, but its very easy to get walled off onto what you think is an island. It's easy to loose sight of the rest of the world, and instead become blinded to everything but the few opportunities in your immediate sphere.

About two years ago I embraced a job as a reporter covering a culture that was different from anything I'd ever seen before. The Queens woman: her hair permed and teased into a ball framing her metallic, rose-tinted glasses. How exotic.

The well-groomed Queens man: clean shaven, often with a mustache, and leaving a scent trail of cologne behind him. The wide 'ahs' in the word "horror" and "radiator" enchanted me.

The long dirty streets with their crazed traffic, the kids from the projects who soup up their cars and race them, one block at a time between stop lights, the manner of the butcher and the shifty eyed behavior of everyone in the traditional Italian bakeries thrilled me to the bone.

I was a stranger in a strange land. I was an anthropologist.

I'm not quite sure how it happened, but somehow I became caught somewhere along the line and enmeshed within the culture I was studying. I was breathing the Queens air, I'd moved to the Queens neighborhood of Astoria. I was faced day in and day out with the conversation of Queensites. I felt I had lost the larger view of life. I guess for a brief time, kicking and screaming, I became a Queensite too. It was horrible. It reached gnashing-teeth level about three weeks ago, as I stood in line at the Steinway Post Office. As I waited I composed the following tome to my disgust.

Post Office On Broadway, Queens

Even the children look stern
at a post office in Queens.
Men, the bags beneath their eyes hard won at 40,
stand silently in the line that always stretches out into the street.

Women from Yugoslavia try to be polite to the tellers
while struggling to get a money order
which will send their American dollars far away.

The tellers, also do not speak English,
behind the scratched plastic walls.

Girls, wherever they were born, waiting primly
neatly put together in cheap imitations
of today’s fashions.

A Hispanic man, his wavy hair immaculately parted
in some 1950s method
attempts to retain his dignity at the window,
his cravat around his neck.

He is reduced to awkward straining
because the Post Office is not a dignified place
and his English is still not so good.

A middle-aged woman, her hair hennaed red, stares
at the girls with an unpleasant face.

When she speaks, her voice is too young
to match her sagging flesh and harsh lines.

Two young boys, their hair and skin look dusty,
are very quiet as they wait for their mother.

They smile but there is no play here
where an old Indian man waits an hour
to buy one 34 cent stamp.

But you can’t sum a whole culture up in disgust—can’t turn your nose up at an entire world.

What’s so bad about Queen anyway?

In terms of New York, it isn’t as decayed and suffering as Harlem. It isn’t as dirty and isolated as parts of Brooklyn. It isn’t racially divided like the Bronx. It isn’t ignored like Staten Island. It isn’t congested with cars and insipid small talk like parts of Manhattan.

Queens is pretty mild compared to a lot of other places in this town. It isn't lovely or inspiring but it isn’t THAT BAD.

I hated Queens because I had gotten stuck in Queens. I had an apartment I’d invested in, I had a car I had to make payments on. I had a job that sucked all the energy out of me so at the end of the day I didn't have the drive to go anywhere. Feeling trapped is an awful feeling. I would have hated heaven if I’d felt trapped there.

So while what I saw at the post office is true, from a slightly different perspective it could have been fascinating rather than damning. The proud Hispanic man may have seemed dapper and touching. The young girls’ mimicry of expensive fashions may have struck me as ingenious. The whole scene could have been proof of the resilience of pop culture or that cultures make small improvements on a concept in order to make it their own. It could have been a very different poem.

I am leaving New York City within the next couple of months to seek out adventure in other parts of the globe. I made the decision last week. I will most definitely miss being a daily part of this crazy, mile-a-minute town, which I truly love. Maybe when I visit again I’ll put some time aside to make a special trip to Queens.

I imagine then, I’ll see something quite different.

We tried. My G-d, we tried. But, the wrecking ball came at 3 in the morning, and by the time we got back to our picketing posts, the building was already demolished.

I cried like I had lost a member of my family. I had. It was such a joy to be inside surrounded by all that grandeur. She had supplied every housewife in the neighborhood with a full set of dishes and glassware and even silverware with serving pieces; every kid with Saturday morning cartoons and a feature film for only 25 cents; every teen a resplendent place to graduate Junior High or High School from; everyone a place to cool off on those brutally hot summer days and nights. With wide comfortable seats, a screen bigger than most neighborhood houses, the Loew's Triboro Theatre will always be missed by those of us who grew up in her awe.

In her place are some miserable stores and a few living spaces above them.

I intentionally avoid going past the intersection of 28th Avenue and Steinway Street. It breaks my heart.
