Title: Re: Help Learning Digital Camera
Post by: Harlan on May 29, 2006, 07:24:04 PM
Good choice! I got the similar Panasonic Lumix DMC-FZ20 (http://www.dpreview.com/reviews/panasonicfz20/)!

What sorts of problems are you having? Focus? Color? Brightness? If you set it on "P" and auto-focus you should get reasonably good pictures, assuming you have good light. The built-in flash is (like all built-in flashes) pretty harsh, and I'd avoid it if you can. The best sunlight is in the last few hours before sunset, not during mid-day.

Oh, one tip is to go into the menus and set the "white balance" to "Cloudy", even if it's sunny. You'll get warmer pictures that way.

Otherwise, use the zoom, make sure the subject of what you're shooting fills the frame, keep the sun behind you, and take lots of pictures!

If you can show particular problems you're having, someone might be able to make specific suggestions.... You might try Flikr (http://www.flikr.com) or Zooomr (http://www.zooomr.com/) as places to host photos for you...

Enjoy the camera!

Hi nycmac.. Read the chapter on White balance. Some cameras are good at compensating for white balance but others just don't do the camera justice. Back in the days of film, you would have to change the film for indoor shots with tungsten lighting. Can you shoot raw with that camera? I suggest shooting raw for a bit, try to figure out which wb setting works best indoors and go with that. Also the more you zoom (longer focal length) the less light that comes in through the lens. 

One more bit.. Try the foil trick. Doesn't always work and it's a bit awkward but it might suit your needs on occation.. take a 5x5 cardbord stock and cover it in aluminun. Use the board to reflect the flash to the cieling. (low ceilings only). This the diffuses the light and gets rid of a lot of harsh shadows.

Title: Re: Help Learning Digital Camera
Post by: Newtownian on July 25, 2006, 09:01:38 AM
Quick White balance summary:  White balance is how the camera accounts for different color temperatures.  When the sun sets the colors look warm.. when you are in the shade the colors look cool or more blue. Anyway.. Most cameras have an auto white balance (AWB), some even give you the option of changing the WB. Take a picture in the shade with no flash.. Then take the same picture by changing the WB from AWB to shade.. Take the same picture.. You will notice that the color with the correct wb will look more natural as you saw it.

Since you will be shooting indoors, and with a flash, you might want to take a couple of test shots to see which one works best in those lighting conditions.

Glad to see you tried the foil trick.. There is another part to that trick that I didn't think you would need but It sounds like it might be important to know. Since the light is taking a detour, going to the cieling then back down to your subject, it takes longer to get there. The camera still thinks the flash is going in a straight line so it's going to open and close the shutter quickly. Too fast unfortunately for all the light to reach the subject, hence the dark picture. So to account for this. adjust the exposure compensation to overexpose by a 1/2 a stop. This should take care of it.

If I completely confused you..Try www.photo.net
