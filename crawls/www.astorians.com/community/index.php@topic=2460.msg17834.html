Nope, not really! Courtesy of the Oxford English Dictionary:

Savory (the plant):

Quote
Ultimately from L. satureia; the form-history is uncertain.
  On the one hand the ME. saverey might descend (with substitution of v for th) from OE. sætherie, a. early OF. *sathereie (later sarrie, whence the dim. sarriette surviving in mod.Fr.) = Pr. sadreia (whence 16th c. Fr. dial. sadriege, Sp. ajedrea, and perh. by metathesis the synon. sagerida):- L. satureiam. On the other hand, Heresbach (De Re Rustica, 1570) cites an It. savoreggia and F. savoreie, and the latter appears in Cotgr. 1611 as savorée. The existence of these forms suggests that the ME. saverey may be an adoption of an unrecorded OF. form which had the v either as a phonetic development in hiatus (cf. F. pouvoir for early OF. pooir) or through the influence of saveur SAVOUR.
  Independent adoption of the Latin word appears in OE. satureye, ME. SATUREIE, MHG. saterje (G. saturei), It. satureja, corruptly santoreggia, Pg. saturagem.

Savory (property of food), from British English "savoury":

Quote
Early ME. savure, app. a. OF. savouré sapid, fragrant, pa. pple. of savourer: see SAVOUR v. In the 14th c. the ending was associated with the native -y, so that the adj. was apprehended as f. SAVOUR n. + -Y.

... and "savour":

Quote
a. OF. savourer, savorer (mod.F. savourer) = Pr. saborar, Sp., Pg. saborear, It. savorare (now in learned form saporare):--late L. saporare, f. sapor- SAVOUR n.

If you didn't follow all that, savory the plant is from Latin satureia, while savory the property of food is from Latin sapor.

This, of course, tells us nothing about how to cook with it...  :|

Well, as Meg said, I found the information and links about summer savory in this week's Weekly E-Share to be very helpful.  From the Weekly E-share:

Quote
Summer savory (Satureja hortensis) is an annual that has a strong, peppery and slightly piney flavor reminiscent of thyme. Native to the Mediterranean, it was much in demand in early Rome as a medicinal treatment (a good treatment for bee stings); it was also considered to be a powerful aphrodisiac. The dried herb formerly came to this country from Austria.

Use summer savory to flavor beans, peas, pork, game and sausages. It is also used to enhance poultry stuffing (see below), soups, stews ( near the end of their cooking time), fish (top chilled, poached fish with a blend of savory, chives, lemon juice, and mayonnaise) , and pizza. It makes a flavorful oil or vinegar. Medicinally, it is used as a tonic and digestive.

Flooz, dried and fresh herbs are always interchangeable.  The only difference is that dried herbs are more potent, so you need more of the fresh to attain the same taste.  (If you think your dried herbs are not as strong, then it's time to throw them out and get fresh dried herbs. ;) )
