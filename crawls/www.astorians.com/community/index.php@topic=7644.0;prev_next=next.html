This information and the back and forth replies have been interesting and illuminating.

The reality of the situation for us, in the middle of it, is that we don't have a lot of options. We tried negotiating with the landlords, asked for a year lease, or any lease, and offered compromises to counter the $50/month increase. They were not at all flexible on either count, citing that having a binding lease could negatively impact their sale and that "everyone does month to month, leases are very uncommon in these private houses" (bullshit). The only thing they agreed to do was put in writing that they would not increase the rent for one full year. by then we hope to be gone anyway.

As much as we would like to fight them on this, and leave the apartment so they'll have to suffer with finding new tenants, it's not possible. We can't afford to move again within a year. And as for putting up a fight, they'll just kick us out so they don't need to deal with us.

Much to my chagrin, we're staying put for now and sucking up the situation.

This is our last straw in Astoria. I have been here 5 yrs, I absolutely love the neighborhood. I would love to stay here, raise a family, continue to be a part of this vibrant community. But with prices for cruddy 2 story houses close to a million, rents soaring towards $2000, and renters having very little peace of mind and security, our next move is going to be out of the city altogether. I would rather afford property of my very own in a small town in NJ, and walk away from this nonsense rather than go into serious debt to stay, and become a part of the yuppie gentrification PROBLEM that's invaded the market.

Ariana –
The reason for the increase in your rent doesn’t really matter. It could be due to increased energy costs as your landlord indicated, or it could be any other reason --  increased insurance, taxes, inflation, whatever – or it could be no reason at all.  When it comes down to it your landlord simply wants $50 more per month and he doesn’t have to justify it. – especially since your apartment isn’t covered by rent regulations.

Only you can decide if your place is worth $1600 per month. Given that heat and hot water are included in most New York rentals, $1600 for a two bedroom in Astoria is not out of line (or so it seems for when I look at current listings).

But to me your problem really is not the increase in rent but that your landlord is selling the property. If it were me, I’d start looking for a new place. It’s a major pain to live through a sale. Potential buyers will want to view your apartment and you’ll have people in and out. And in the end you make end up having to move anyway (given proper and legal notice of course).

As Jtrane suggested, If you prefer to stay there’s nothing stopping you from making a counteroffer. You could say that you’ll stay only at the current rent -- given the inconveniences you’ll have to put up with during the sale, possibly having to move, etc.

If LL still insists on an increase I would still equally insist on a new lease, even if it states that it’s on a month-to-month basis, with the new rent in writing.
