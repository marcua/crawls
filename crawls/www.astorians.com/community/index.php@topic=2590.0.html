Old Maspeth Village:

Destroyed in the first half of the 20th century in order to make way for industry.   

Old Astoria Village:

Destroyed in the last half of the 20th century to allow for cheap, ugly housing.   

Vestiges of both remain, but the Landmarks Preservation Commission would rather landmark firehouses and lamp posts in Queens so as not to stand in the way of the mayor and city council's agenda of promoting overdevelopment of our true historic and architecturally significant sites.   As a lifelong Queens resident, I personally am offended that my hard-earned tax money is being allocated toward preserving the playgrounds of Manhattan elitists while my own neighborhood is being thrown to the dogs!   :x

Well, welcome to the Astorians board!

Perhaps you can help shake up the stale western Queens political culture (Oh Pe-ta! Oh Pe-ta!) where political discourse has not evolved for decades, and most civic leaders are pushing 70.

You are right. Isn’t Old Astoria a black eye for our neighborhood? Right up there with the dirtiest and noisiest streets in Queens, smoky bars, and a generally upscale tone in our nightlife. (Well, we cannot be too negative for there is the exception of Bohemian Hall and Park which the local talent wanted to bulldoze, and thrived only because they let it alone when they fortunately left in a huff.) 

We are the last bastion in NYC where developers are treated as gods, absentee landlords milk their properties with impunity, and community planning resembles running a strip mine. No civic group of consequence exists without the suffocating influence of politicians or the development lobby. 

Someone in Queens Civic Congress last year said Astoria is decades behind even eastern Queens. So how can Astoria catch up to the rest of NYC? You know, down-zoning, putting some limits on developers, letting the community be more than passive observers or scripted figures in a charade?

I love Astoria, and its people. I pin a great deal of hope on this board's community. Help us!

What advice can you give? What are success stories in other communities? How do we open people’s eyes?

You need to form a powerful civic group.  Even if you feel that a lot of Astorians are not involved in neighborhood issues, if you have a strong nucleus of 4-5 residents who do care, their concern and enthusiasm for the neighborhood can spread like wildfire.  You just have to hold your elected officials' feet to the fire, send out press releases, hold press conferences and rallies, and you will see people start to pay attention.  I know it is hard to get the hipsters and yuppies to care about the neighborhood they just invaded, so concentrate on lifers whose families have had a stake in the community for generations.
