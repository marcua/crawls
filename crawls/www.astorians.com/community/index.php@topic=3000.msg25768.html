Actually, the layout makes sense if understand that Astoria was part of Long Island City. Outside of some early planned communities (Old Astoria, Steinway) the area west of about 51st street was planned as a classic small city (very much like found in America's heartland) with zones for discrete types of activity:

Hunters Point and Queens Plaza: transportation and commercial centers
Sunnyside and Astoria: residential precincts.
All connected by mass transportation with clearly identified neighborhood shopping/entertainment districts (Steinway Street, Queens Blvd.)

Even better, the charms of Manhattan was only a few minutes away (even as early as the Civil War) so the area always had an urban edge to it. LIC/Astoria was very different from the rest of Queens, which, when all this was thought out, little more than cornfields. Its hard to believe, but some people from Bayside, for example, brag to this day that they have not been to the city for years.

Even more significant, the merchants, property owners, and community leadership actually lived in the community, so everyting was planned to last.

The problem now is this orderly set-up is breaking down: movie theaters are now placed so foot traffic goes through neighborhoods, skyscrapers are built without local shopping. Poor planning is now the order of the day.

And the community leadership, merchants, and property owners increasingly do not live here. Their horizons are increasingly short term.

Am looking forward to the planned mall over the Sunnyside Yards. You can kiss Steinway Street good-bye when that happens.

I lived my entire life up until a year ago in a neighborhood a lot like Astoria (on a smaller scale), and I can't imagine living anywhere less urban than a place like this. Like my dad (who has also spent his whole life in that 'hood) once said, "I can't imagine not being able to get dinner at a decent restaurant, the newspaper from the corner store, and a drink from a local bar if my car was broken down." Basically, he likes being able to walk and get whatever he needs. Up until the local pharmacy closed, he used to go get his perscriptions too - he was big on supporting independently owned places even if it cost a little more because it seemed like the neighborly thing to do. I know there are always exceptions, but  would there be a market for those types of places to flourish in suburban sprawl? If there is, I haven't seen much.

I actually had an ex who lived in typical "suburbia", and he refused to go to non-chain restaurants. Needless to say, that didn't last too long  :roll:

I think the bad ideas (for me, anyway) about typical suburbia partially come from the fact that people go from their house to their car to work, and then back home again at the end of the day. It seems like they don't really get to know their neighborhoods, or they don't take ownership of or responsibility for their neighborhoods. I have friends who live in really suburban areas (both on Long Island and in CT) who have no idea who their next door neighbors are, and I think that's so sad. I mean, I definitely don't know everyone who lives right around me, but if I see people in the hall or out front or whatever, I make an effort to say hi. These people I know just absolutely don't care to even pretend - its like they have their house on their land, and they don't owe anybody or the neighborhood anything.

I guess it comes down to the fact that I feel that everyone in suburbia is so separated, where people in areas like this tend to be more of a community. But, like I said, this is just what I've observed, I'm SURE there are lots of people who could prove me wrong. :)

Cookie cutter houses/McMansions also really depress me.

I have to chime in here about the knowing your neighbors thing.  It's always been my impression from growing up upstate, and in the movies and tv, that everyone in a smaller city/town (Beacon is considered a city) knows each other.  Growing up, the neighbors had block parties in which they invited everyone on the street, and they still do.   My family knows most of everyone who lives on their block.  You see a moving truck next door, you say hi.  You walk over with baked goods.  And in high school, I couldn't get away with anything, because word travels so quickly.   "I saw her with so and so and she was blah blah blah"  and so forth.   In fact, when I left high school - I went away to the big city of Manhattan to college at NYU - and what did I love about it?  The anominity and the fact that no one knew me, and had their own lives, and were too busy to worry about mine.  I also liked the luxury of never having to see anyone I don't want to see ever again.  And in Beacon, one of the primary refrains I hear about the city (Manhattan) is how no one knows each other.  That said, something drew me to Queens.  And maybe subconsciously, I want my neighbors to know me.  Life is a big circle.  But at least everyone has so much more to do here, so they don't have to make up stuff about my life, because their life is so uninteresting.  Maybe that's the difference - things to do.  There is so much more to do here than in Beacon.  But you know, it's hard to meet people anywhere, and if it wasn't for Astorians - I would know very little people here.

Oh and on my street in Beacon, a short walk away is a Dunkin Donuts, 2 bars, car dealerships, a dollar store, and a laudromat.  And everyone drives to them.  The stuff is a short walk, but yeah, most people drive.
