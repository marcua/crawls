Peter Vallone, Jr. will make an outstanding Queens Borough President.

Speaking as a former Borough President staffer (I worked for Manhattan BP Fields for three years), the position is vestigial.  Before the Charter Revision the Borough Presidents sat on the Board of Estimate, a Senate for NYC, compared the City Council which had 35 members and was much more like the House of Representatives.  The BPs, the Comptroller and the Mayor and the President of the City Council (now the Public Advocate) had control over land use and contracts for the city.  They were very powerful and had major impacts.  These days most land use issues are handled by the City Planning Commission and the City Council Land Use Committee, the BPs still get to weigh in and help direct development, but their influence is much less.

Borough Presidents appoint all 50 members of each of the community boards in their boroughs (half outright and half in "consultation" with the local Councilmembers - which means different things in each borough - some BPs appoint who they want and others really do consult).  With community boards being solely advisory, though somewhat influential, even this power isn't much.

BPs also have a budget which they use to make discretionary grants to various groups and capital grants which they can use to help groups buy property (buildings or equipment) or repair buildings.  It's not a lot of money, especially when compared to the funds that the Council allocates.

BPs have policy staffs and press units, both of which help highlight issues in their borough and serve a soapbox.  BPs have greatly differing approaches so you get policy wonks like Bronx BP Adolfo Carrion who has issued many policy recommendations and publicity clowns like Brooklyn BP Marty Markowitz who spends more money on concerts and promoting the restaurants in his neighborhoods.  Queens BP Helen Marshall has focused on tourism, downzoning and education, with some success.

BPs offices also provide the administrative support that manages community board offices and hold two monthly meetings (usually chaired by the BP), the Borough Service Cabinet where all Boro Commissioners of City Agencies are supposed to come and report what they have done that month in that Borough and be questioned by the BP, BP staff, Community Board leaders and City Councilmembers and the Borough Board, which votes on land use items and EDC (Economic Development Corporation) dispositions, and has a bigger impact on development.

Hope that helps.

BP is a good spot for which to run for higher office and be in the public eye, hence you've seen Bronx BPs Fernando Ferrer and Adolfo Carrion, Manhattan BPs Ruth Messinger, Virginia Fields, Andrew Stein and David Dinkins and Brooklyn BP Marty Markowitz all running for Mayor.
