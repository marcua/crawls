Can't speak about ps84, other than what is on the "inside schools.org" site-- which makes it sound like a pretty good school in general.  I think that some new reports have also made it onto the board of ed site recently, if you haven't checked in the past month or so.

We also have a 4 yr old and are figuring out what we're going to do for prek and beyond.

I teach for the board of ed (but in the special ed district), but have also taught at private schools.  Really, I think that it depends on the school (the school culture, the principal, discipline, how much time is used "for the tests" and how much time is spent on arts, etc) and the teacher that your child gets (which sure can vary!). 

It is really hard to wrap my head around, too.  I'm not in these schools-- though I've spoken to a few teachers who teach in Astoria.  It sounds like in general that the schools are very good and the teachers sound pretty happy.  And happy teachers mean that they feel like they are accomplishing goals, getting students to learn and progress....

Also, not all schools are going to be a good fit for your child or for your family.  I think that my oldest child has done pretty well at his daycare/preschool so far.  When a new concept is introduced, he picks up on it pretty quickly and enjoys the learning process.  I think that he would do well at a traditional school.  Public schools in Astoria are fairly traditional type of schools from what I have heard.

Not that there's anything wrong with that.......

I really like public school, though I understand the appeal of private schools, too.  There are some really fantastic ones in the city....

Good luck in your search!

Quote from: Alexa on July 17, 2008, 06:16:34 PM
...private school teachers make a lot less money than public school teachers...I have often wondered who are the teachers that are accepting of these lower paid positions?

Not trying to start a whole new debate, but I have many friends who are teachers (in fact, everyone I keep in touch with from home is a teacher, which is kind of silly!), and it's VERY hard to get into the public school system, especially if you're not special ed certified. One of my best friends (who frankly, is incredibly smart and amazing with children - she comes from a big family and has worked with kids since before I met her in high school), got her masters, with honors, has glowing recommendations, etc etc etc, and still couldn't find a job in the public schools. I even hired her part-time back at my old job back home (not with kids), and while hiring or living with friends is usually a mistake, I can objectively say that she's a great employee. She is currently a first grade teacher in a Catholic school for half of what she should be making (imho, and compared to other first grade teachers), but it's better than nothing, right? She has been in her position for 4-5 years, and she's constantly looking, but really - it's THAT hard to get in.

Please don't judge a school by how much it can pay its staff. Visit the school, talk to other parents, read up on the place, etc. That's how you should make your decision.

We were debating on whether to put our child in 166 or in private school.  We decided to try it.   Administratively, they can do much better and the PTA needs to improve.  There doesn't seem to be much parent involvement in the PTA given that the school has over 1,100 students, but hopefully this will change.  Our child is in the dual language program and the education she gets seems pretty good.  I feel there's a lot of homework for a kindergartner, but not unmanageable.  New grades came out for the school this year and, if I"m not mistaken, it acheived an "A"  -- a far improvement from the "D" of last year.  It's worth it to take the orientation.  You'll have to ask all the questions, because a poor job is done of giving you all the information you need.
