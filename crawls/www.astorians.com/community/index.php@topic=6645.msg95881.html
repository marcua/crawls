"I agree however that Astoria was not a prime area for a book store a few years back.  It is now, and I would venture to say that we will have something move in here."

Backed up by what stats, Debbie?

If you look at the caliber of stores on Steinway over the past few decades and contrast them today, there is not an upgrade but deterioration. 99 cent stores and nail salons and places with cheap furniture. no store fronts and bins of flipflops. Many of the chains that opened in Astoria have branches in Corona, the Bronx, Jamaica and the like. Only 10 years ago there were three places I could by Florshiem shoes on Steinway. Today, I can shop on that street dress myself like a hip-hop artist. Seamans Furniture moves out, El Mundo Discount moves in.

This is the opposite of trends in shopping in Manhattan or downtown Brooklyn. Even long term residents and boosters of the local shopping that have spent their entire lives on local streets have told me that they are sadly writing them off. I used to be proud of our shopping areas. Now they look like they way that 14th Street used to be.

Take a look at the comments from posters on this board. Are they happy? Thread after thread on wishful thinking and dashed hopes.

As to the cafes being filled with people, from my conversations with business leaders, it seems that a good portion of those patrons are people who do not live in our community. For example, the Greeks, like other ethnic groups, are moving out of Astoria. Being a tight-knit community, many come back here frequently to shop and hang out in those cafes.

The big question is not why those cafes continue to grow (while their local population shrinks) but why the population of young professionals that supposedly are moving in have almost no impact on stores, meeting places, and the cultural life of our community.

This is the kind of thing that B&N looks for.

Interesting points, Gleason. (Never thought I'd say that...!)

It does seem like shopping for clothes, furniture, housewares, etc., is less of an option in the neighborhood than it seems it used to be. On the other hand, the number of coffeeshops, non-Mediterranean restaurants, bakeries, specialty stores, etc., are growing rapidly.

On the first point, I suspect that a lot of this has to do with the growing number of big-box stores farther out in Queens, and peoples' ability to drive to Ikea to get furniture. I kinda doubt that the neighborhood is getting any poorer, looking at recent changes in rents and property values. Instead, people spend their money elsewhere. The Internet might take a little blame too, as people do more shopping on-line.

While shopping can be "outsourced" to other neighborhoods efficiently, I strongly disagree that the quality of "meeting places" is decreasing at all. To the contrary. (Just on Ditmars alone, Freeze Peach, Oleput, Fatty's/Crescent and Vine, Waltz, etc., are all new and great meeting places.) And regarding "a good portion of those patrons are people who do not live in our community", as far as I've seen, this only applies to the Beer Garden. It may be that the old-timers don't recognize us yuppies as "people who live in our community", but there certainly are plenty of yuppies here, and they support both the old-time butcher shops and the new-fangled organic groceries. They just don't buy shoes on Steinway.

To restate, I assert that the changes in shopping patterns in Astoria over recent years are due to both Astoria-specific changes (more immigrants, more yuppies, higher property values), as well as to global patterns of more-efficient but less neighborhood-based shopping for major items. Both of these changes are probably good on balance, although I agree that there are an excessive number of 99 cent stores...
