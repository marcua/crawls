Post by: NYCMacUser on September 16, 2007, 01:28:15 PM
Alexa, I know this is going to sound kind of silly, but here goes anyway. First of all congratulations. Be sure to keep a journal. You will find it a treasure in just a few years.

My first, almost term, pregnancy was around 4 decades ago. I was already showing by 3 months. I was walking my dog in Astoria Park and I was approached by a girl around my own age who was also pregnant. She told me she knew no-one who was pregnant and that her mother had passed away a few years prior and wondered if I could answer a question for her. I couldn't. But, she walked me home to drop off the dog, and then we went to the library together to look up the answer to her question. Before we both got back home, we bumped into 4–5 other pregnant girls and we all exchanged phone numbers. Before the end of the week, we all got together for milk shakes, yes, you read that correctly, milk shakes and a gab fest! We remained friends for many years, even as our children grew and families moved, we always exchanged holiday cards and now emails. There must have been something in the water supply that year because there were 4 sets of twins born on my block. It was wonderful to commiserate with other women going through exactly what was happening to me. When we all got together, we puked, passed gas, ran for the potty as our bladders burst, kvetched about our swollen, hurting breasts and laughed at ourselves and each other's misery. Having shared all those good and rotten memories was a remarkable time for all of us. When I was confined to bed rest (at month 6) up to 10 pregnant women would arrive at my house with pizza and sit on my bed and and we would laugh ourselves silly.

No-one is going to think you are nuts if you stop them on the street and congratulate them on being pregnant and ask to be their friend. You might even find women on your own block who are also pregnant; or in your church; or where you work. Start a little local group of your own. It's also like having built-in friends for your baby. It is also a great baby sitting exchange for weekend dinners out, dentist and doctor appointments and later on even weekend getaways.

I can remember the very first time I had 4 port-a-cribs and 2 carriages set up in my living room for a Saturday night. Two of my friends had weddings to go to and one had an out-of-town funeral and I don't remember the other reasons why I had such a crew—but it was such an experience. I enlisted the help of a couple of single friends . . . perhaps that night was the reason neither of them have had any children! I already had twins, so what's a few more?

Alexa, even if you go on to have another dozen children, this is the one you will always remember, just like the first time you . . . you know! Make it special. Journal it. Take growth pictures. Build memories.

Hi Preggers ladies,
I'm currently on the other side of pregnancy. My son is 15+ months and it's so much fun!
Here are a couple of resources that I wish I'd had when I was preggo...

The Yoga Room in Astoria has a PreNatal yoga class. Having done a fair amount of yoga in my time, I opted to do regular yoga to keep fit while pregnant, but found that I had missed out on joining a tight-knit group of women in the area who are a great support system for eachother.
Here's the site: http://www.the-yoga-room.com/yogaroom_astoria/yoga_classes/yoga.html#gentle

I don't know if any of you are planning to breastfeed, but it really worked well for my son and for me. Part of the reason it was successful for us was that we got off to a great start. Our local chapter of La Leche League (a breastfeeding support system that is worldwide AND local!) has a couple of meetings in the area. Check it out while you're still pregnant so that you'll already have the resources you need when you need them:
http://www.lllusa.org/NYE/Queens/queens.HTM
You can also get online and get support from the comfort of your own home computer.

Another one that was so helpful was the meetup group here, in astoria for moms. It's called the "stay at home moms" meetup, but there are lots of part-and full-time working moms in it. There are playgroups and "moms night out" events. No formal mandatory meeting, just fun and social. I remember wandering around Astoria the first few months of my son's life literally going up and down streets looking for other moms of young babies. How frustrating it was! But getting hooked up with this group will give you lots of moms to get chatty with. Some of them are my best friends now! http://sahm.meetup.com/817/

And, incidentally, I have chosen to focus my health counseling practice on moms-to-be and new moms, so check me out if you have questions on which foods are best for you and your baby right now and ways to make your pregnancy a little more comfortable. http://www.angelsonghealth.com/welcome.html

Congratulations to all and good luck!

Gee, have things changed that much since I gave birth almost 4 decades ago? Sure diapers and air conditioning were needed, but they certainly didn't feel like they were exclusives.

Now let's see if I remember that joyful period . . . ahh, yes . . . lots of caffeine was also needed. If you're lucky enough to fall asleep, the baby passing wind in another room sounds like an approaching tornado and will wake you in a NYC second.

Moms are needed too; yours, his, anyones who can take a few hours to come over and help a bit. A cook. Yeah, someone who not only likes to cook, but has the time and energy to cook some healthy and tasty food to help sustain your life while you try to make it through the baby's first 3 months of trying to communicate with the outside world! A part-time secretary would also be a great asset at that time. Someone needs to get those thank you cards written and posted ASAP and you sure as hell won't have either the time or the inclination. Please, whatever you do, don't forget to have a supply of chocolate in the house before you go into labor. You're going to be routinely craving it after every feeding. Don't be cheap, either. Buy yourself the good quality kind. And, you will also need a good package of flash cards. Yes, that is what I said, flash cards! If you are lucky enough to still have the energy to get out of the house for say, just a walk, you don't want to waste your energy answering all those dumb questions . . . that's where the flash cards come in. "Yes, he does have a lot of hair, doesn't he." "No, I don't think he is cold without a hat." "I actually wasn't thinking Harvard or Yale, I was kind of imagining Princeton." "Yes, the (dog(s)/cat(s) really like the baby and no I am not planning on getting rid of either." Same questions, different annoying people. And if you can arrange it, you will also need a good friend willing to do your mani/pedi so that you can feel almost human again. Try to get yourself one of those Hollywood personal trainers, that come over to your house when you call them, so that you won't have to deal with feeling lazy about trying to lose the weight that helped the baby be born so healthy. Last thing I vividly remember needing . . . someone to tell you every day how beautiful you look, how perfect you and the baby look together; someone who wants nothing more than to hold you in their strong, masculine arms while you nurse, or simply nap; who will wake you with a snack that you didn't have the strength to make for yourself; someone who will protect you from all the incoming calls that you would rather not have to answer; someone who will only allow visitors when they are there to be a buffer between you and the things you know are so much more important than company and the same someone who will photographically document every second of the the entire first year of your baby's life.

You know, diapers and air conditioning is not a bad place to start!
