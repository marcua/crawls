Quote
And this is the crux of my criticism. A hurricane was never approaching NYC.  There is serious debate as to whether a hurricane touched down in NC. Hurricane winds were not recorded in NJ.

That doesn't matter much to me, since it was damaging enough anyway. I also think it's important for people to know that something that isn't even hurricane strength can cause this kind of damage. If they'd said all along that it would only be a tropical storm by the time it got to the our area, a lot of people wouldn't have bothered preparing at all.

I should note that this post wasn't aimed at you only. Some people seem disappointed that there wasn't more destruction in our city. I don't know if that's the media's fault, and I am cynical towards the media. I am aware that ratings are more important to them than any sort of benefit they could potentially offer society. But I don't know why some people, instead of being relieved that the city wasn't badly damaged even while surrounding areas were and maybe having some empathy for those people, are disappointed that there wasn't serious destruction here- like that wasn't enough for them. That response disturbs me more than the media hype does, and I'm not sure the media hype and the MTA shutdown are completely to blame for that sort of reaction- that this was supposed to be an exciting adventure, and all they got was a wet leaf stuck to their window.

Quote
Your solution is to keep listening to "wolf" until it happens.

No, I was saying that it was actually worth getting ready for, even if it wasn't the storm of the century. I know you have said that of course a sensible amount of preparation is good, this was just overblown, etc. The point I think you may be overlooking is that a lot of people don't pay attention to the news and won't respond to it unless a particular story is all over the place. What seems like a ridiculous amount of hype to someone who is doing his own research already (e.g. you), just seems like a story that should be paid attention to to those who may not normally be paying attention to any of this stuff. Those who are intelligent and informed enough to know the difference between hype and reality will always know the difference, whereas some people will only pay attention to hype in terms of how much any sort of news will get them to change their routine, and maybe get off their asses to get an emergency radio and a fire extinguisher.

Hopefully, storms like this won't start coming around so much that we end up having this response to it more often than maybe once every few years. I am not yet concerned that we are heading down a slippery slope in regards to the idea that every weather anomaly will require some government proclamation. For some reason, I don't think Bloomberg would care to go that route either.
