I did the couch to 5k, starting from ZERO running, and in April I ran a half-marathon.  If you stick to it it really does work.

When I started, I had trouble running for the first 60 seconds.  I couldn't run a block.  I found a few runners at approximately my level through this board, and we would meet in the mornings and do the program together.  I also pursued it on my own later.  I worked up to running to the track in Astoria Park and back, and then added distance by adding laps.  Sometimes on Saturdays I do long runs through Astoria, going anywhere I feel like.  If you're running in the streets and want to calculate distance, you can use Google Ped, or if you want real-time data, something like a Garmin Forerunner.

I agree with Frappe about buying good running shoes.  Jack Rabbit Sports is good, or you can go to Super Runners Shop on Lex and 89th.  They also take your time to check your gait and recommend shoes based on that.  The first pair they had me try on turned out to be perfect for me, and I've been using the same brand/model ever since.

There is a running club based out of Astoria Park - Hellgate Road Runners.  The google cache of their site says they sponsor a 5k race every June, but I can't find any information about it on their site, so they may not have done it for the past couple of years.

Otherwise, check out New York Road Runners.  They have the most races, and a lot of 5k races.  Occasionally they have races in Queens, but I'd recommend sucking it up and going to one of their races in Central Park one weekend morning.  It's really not far, and it's so beautiful running through Central Park early in the morning.  No matter how I do in the race, it always makes my day to be running, and then walking, through the park so early before the city wakes up.  It's just beautiful.

I'm still not a fast runner, and I never will be.  But I get the job done, and as I've qualified for the 2009 Marathon, that is my next goal.

Just passed, but every August the Federation of Italian Americans of Queens hosts a 5K run here in Astoria. This was the first year I ran the race and it was a lot of fun. Another way to connect to the Astoria community.

On the topic of why to find a 5k race rather than run the 3.1 miles on your own...community and motivation. Although running in a tight pack of runners takes some getting used to, it's definitely inspiring to run among others that probably started running for similar reasons to get in shape. One of the perks of running is that you can do it on your own and on your own schedule, but if you haven't done so, I strongly suggest throwing an organized race into the mix every now and then. It will help keep your running energized.

Also, if you plan in running outside, it will be difficult to start running during the winter, but not impossible. The colder weather and short days are the main challenges. If you run on a treadmill, you can avoid the weather and light issues, but for some treadmills can be incredibly boring. It's all about knowing yourself and what your "excuses" are, then you can stay motivated and start to really love running.

Best of luck to you!
Bryan

Add me to the group of folks who loves C25k! I HATED and feared running before I started it, but decided to give it a shot b/c I had packed on some extra poundage. I nearly died doing W1D1, but kept at it and it got easier. I finished C25k about 2.5 months ago and am now in the middle of Week 8 of One Hour Runner (ran 4.75 miles on Friday -- that's a big number for me). I f'n LOVE running now and even crave running during my rest days. I feel stronger and healthier and also dropped 20lbs. :D

To anyone out there who is interested in running but thinks its "impossible," I really can't recommend C25k highly enough. It's seriously changed my life.

I'm running my first race on 9/28. To answer Frappe's question... I've been able to run a 5k on my own for months now, but this will be the first time I'll be experiencing running as a "communal" event. I'm really looking forward to feeling the energy of the thousands of participants and spectators that will be there that day. Quite a departure from my usual solitary plod down the street at the butt-crack of dawn. :) They also say that you see the most measurable improvements in your first year of running... I'll be interested to see if I can't kick up my pace time a little bit by then!


EDIT: Oh, and Jackrabbit is great. I bought my shoes there and I've been running pretty comfortably. They're pretty kind to newbies as well... I was nervous they were going to somehow "judge" me when I walked in, but they were very nice.
