The rink is run by the YMCA is Long Island City (on behalf of the City).  The adjacent school (144 I believe) doesn't really have anything to do with it.  But I doubt the Y would allow use of the nets for anything other than league games.

If more people play hockey there, you'd see less soccer being played there.  Trust me, those guys are harmless, and they certainly wouldn't mess with a bunch of guys carrying sticks.  All they want to do is play soccer.  But if the rink was actually being used for hockey, they'd just look to play elsewhere. 

Heck, a few of them may probably end up watching.  When I would play league games there, you'd be surprised at the size of the Latin crowd who just stood around and watched.  Remember, it's basically just soccer with skates and sticks.  Not that difficult to follow along.

The funny part was, since I'm Latin, they would be kinda surprised to see me play as well.  I'd often get some puzzling looks after I took off my skates and walked home.  But then again, as the only Brown person with equpment on, I should have expected them to be a little taken aback by me.

Chappetto Square (or "the Cheesebox", as it's more commonly referred to due to it's triangular shape) was a tremendously popular place in my youth for hockey.  Then again, when I was younger (so many eons ago, ugh), hockey was pretty big in Astoria. 

Hockey in the fall/spring. baseball in the summer...that's how most kids spent thier youth.  Not much basketball and football to really speak of. 

The NYC Parks Department used to provide metal nets for use during the year.  I noticed over the last few years that the nets were locked up, then eventually removed.  Most of the kids today don't play hockey (I blame most mothers nowadays, but that's a different conversation for a different thread) and Cheesebox was then filled with latin immigrants who used the field to play soccer. 

I haven't been around the park in some time.  Why it's completely locked up I do not know. 


Sad commentary on Astoria life if it is though.
