We went over here yesterday and it was...okay. Personally, I think the inside space is pretty great, I like the aesthetics, I love the reclaimed tables / benches (although the benches can get rocky if other people are sitting on them). I thought the covered space out front on the broadway side was a smart addition, since it allows smokers to go do their thing. I didn't venture out back, so I can't say too much about that, but it sounds like the lovely neighbors will take care of policing that area (quite literally it seems).
The beer list was solid, they had something like 15 taps, all of which were unique enough. The bourbon list was pretty solid, but I would say that, aside from their moonshine offerings (I don't like clear corn whiskey) there wasn't much that I haven't had a bottle of here at some point - which isn't saying much, honestly, I love trying new bourbons. The price was fair on all the drinks, which I appreciate. Beers were $6 a pop and bourbon was a little more $7 maybe?
I stopped by the food station, but when I read the offerings - last night the offerings were ribs, chicken, pulled pork and rib eye - nothing stood out at me. I had heard good things about the pastrami, and was bummed they didn't have it, and I could have filled up on some brisket as well. Somehow my wife went and asked and apparently they did have pastrami, so she came back with that, a biscuit, corn bread and creamed spinach. The pastrami was okay, the biscuits and cornbread tasted like they were out of the box, and the creamed spinach was overpoweringly creamy and not spinachy enough. I didn't know about the schlepping of food through hte backyard, although it probably wouldn't have bothered me too much, but based on the smell, I thought there was a pit in the large room. I guess not. It's served butcher style like many BBQ's all over the country, or like Hill Country, John Brown, or Fette Sau for local comparisons. With that in mind, I wasn't expecting table service, but was thrown off because when you come in, there's a hostess, who literally just tells you that the bar you're already looking at is right there, and that you can seat yourself. Overall, I'd rather head to LIC to John Brown or into the city to Hill Country for food (not a fan of Fette Sau).
The last thing to touch on is the music, which is bluegrass for the most part. When they have music on, it's not bad. When the live band started (the band was pretty good, albeit with some schlocky "drink more bourbon" banter) the acoustic shortcomings of the space become apparent. There's nothing to absorb any sound, so the cavernous room tends to sounds like garbage, and the music sounds muddy.
I don't know, overall, I can see myself going back on those nights when I want to get drunk and wish I was in any number of southern cities, but have to make do with an Astoria knock off. I actually came home and kept drinking and put some Gram Parsons on the hi-fi. That said, there's room for improvement, and I hope they do improve, because the space is cool enough, although they slightly force the honkey tonk. With a little attention and some grease in the gears, I feel like this place could go from okay to good or even great, but they've got a good starting point.


To translate for those who tend to judge a place by the following two criteria: Good for "hipsters": Check. Good for "transplants": Check. I feel like that will save some folks some wear and tear on their carpal tunnel.

I think both sides of the argument are right to some degree.  This particular business, and others like it, should recognize how close they are to bedroom windows of nearby homes and apartments and try their best to be considerate.  As I said, I don't see the real need for The Strand to have a backyard that offers nothing more than a space for people sit, talk, smoke, and be a nuisance to the nearby apartments.  They have a really nice and huge indoor space.  And there is a front yard area that should prove to be much less of a disturbance.  So they should cut it there and try to be more neighborhood friendly.  But McNugger is also right.  Astoria is not a sleepy bedroom community.  It's becoming more of a destination year to year.  And 30th, Broadway, Ditmars, 34th, and 21st Street are all becoming rather hot dining and nightlife destinations and they will be busier and noisier as time goes on.  And I, like McNugger, find those changes in Astoria to be exciting and energizing.  If you choose (because where you live is a choice, after all) to live in the heart of a business district, you cannot seriously expect peace and quiet.  I think Astoria offers a very diverse selection of housing options for all wallets and lifestyles.  If you live near a bar, then you can expect all the stuff that goes with it.  But business owners can take some care to be polite to its neighbors without ruining the atmosphere of their business.
