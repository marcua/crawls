On the Robot genre
« Reply #18 on: October 06, 2005, 12:00:05 PM »
Anyone out there who knows better please correct me, for what I am about to say is probably going to be erroneous (yet again).

The Robot genre are any of those movies where the main subject or device is that of a giant robot (if you want to see a really gigantic robot, watch Macross), or that of a human being interfacing with the robot (controlling it). Sometimes there is in fact no human controlling it, but more on this later. There is usually a "good" robot (or group of robots) and a bad one (or set of bad ones) that eventually duke it out in a battle of epic proportions. The act of interfacing with the robot (usually being inside it) usually has profound psychological and sometimes physical implications for the operator, at times there is even a parasitic relationship where the human looses his or her individuality. In many of the movies and shows the origins of the robot(s) are unclear and sometimes came before "time began", vide Big O. The robots could either be distinct individuals in themselves or they could be modeled as a form of armour or a mix of the two, sometimes acting independently of the operator (maybe when the operator became unconscious or was working in contradiction to the prime directive of the robot) while most of the time not.

It is my belief that the Robot Genre is the science fiction equivalent of the  Samurai histories and legends of old. The Samurai dominated Japan for many centuries and the Japanese people seem to love relating their stories via many different media as entertainment.  One of the things that gave them (the Samurai) even more power was their weapons and armour.

There are even legends of "haunted" armour that were posessed by the spirit of a dead Samurai who became revenant in order to pursue some critical goal such as protecting his descendents or dealing retribution.
Since these manifestions were supernatural in nature it wasn't uncommon for the armour to change size, at times becoming gargantuan.
There are paralells in Western literature and legends, "The Castle of Otranto" being one such fine example : http://digital.library.upenn.edu/webbin/gutbook/lookup?num=696
