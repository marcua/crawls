At what point is sidewalk stuff fair game?
« on: August 13, 2009, 05:38:31 PM »
Ok. There is a super nice blue mountain bike outside my apartment, not chained to anything, just standing there on it's kickstand. It is on the sidewalk, next to a lightpole, close to the curb, where we always put the trash, but again...not chained to anything. It's been there for over an hour (going on 2 hours at this point). I live on a busy street, and the only reason it hasn't been stolen/taken is PROBABLY because people are thinking "oh, the owner is probably right there, in the deli, watching it."

I would LOVE LOVE LOVE to have a bike but I am severely underemployed right now, to the point where I'm putting off getting my hair cut.

My question/s: at what point, ethically, can I claim it? How is this that much different from when people leave household items on the curb (my lamp is a trashday curb find)? Obviously, if someone is down there staring at it, it's theirs....but at what point does neglect (oh, I'll just leave my bike out here while I go inside and visit my friend) become surrender, or when can I assume it's fair game?

I do not advocate stealing at all...but this bike seems just so blatantly available. It can't be right, can it? I'm seriously having a real internal debate about this! Can I take the "trash"? Is it even trash, or is it's owner that adorably naive?

I am not opposed to sitting downstairs on a folding chair next to it for a couple hours, as if I own it, to deter other possible takers.

There is no ethical argument.  Unless you know it's been put out for trash (which you didn't), then it's not yours to take.  If someone was dumb enough to leave it out there unlocked, it still doesn't mean you have the right to take it.  If someone is dumb enough to leave their wallet on the subway, does that mean you have the right to take it?  I'm no Holmes or Wolfe, but I'd take a guess and say that you probably already knew that it would have been ethically wrong to take it, but were trying to look for some justification.  That being said, there would have been someone else that would have taken it on the spot and had no qualms about it.  That's a point for you, however, and at least shows that you had some doubts about the situation before you acted.  As far as the haircut goes:  grow it long and look hip, cut it yourself, or I'll send you a check if it will help you out, no quid pro quo.
