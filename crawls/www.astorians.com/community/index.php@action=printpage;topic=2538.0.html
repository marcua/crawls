Title: Re: What's up with the Pistilli properties?
Post by: lanseaux on July 09, 2006, 11:44:42 PM
Just to clear up one thing I've heard mistakenly said before:  the place on Ditmars and 45th is not and never was the Steinway factory.  The Steinway factory is still alive and well in its original Astoria location on Steinway Place, which is the continuation of 38th Street north of 19th Ave. 

The Hell Gate bridge paint is a special paint that was formulated by a German company specifically for the Hell Gate bridge during its refurbishment that was completed in 1996.  Its original colour is closer to purple, but it is officially called Hell Gate Red, and don't try to suggest to any of the people involved in the painting project that it is anything but red (Jim tried).  Like all - or most - red-related pigments, it does fade in the sun, hence its pinker appearance over a number of years.  Before that it was kind of a rusty (or rusted) black.

Bogyos worked in various departments at Steinway and studied drafting at night in anticipation of getting a job at Con Edison-- which also had a concert band. The Depression destroyed that possibility. When World War II came, Steinway began making wooden parts for gliding airplanes, and the young draftsman was moved into the pattern shop, which was then undergoing quite a transformation.

"When I got into engineering we called it the pattern shop. It was in Ditmars [an ancillary factory building in Astoria, since sold], up on the seventh floor, up in heaven, sort of. You were left alone. And the way they worked it, Frank Walsh, who was my boss at the time, he was the chief engineer, he came up like I did in the factory--from an office boy he became plant manager and engineer. He had his own book, and he just wrote down everything he ever saw, how it was done, how it was made. And every foreman had a little book, like littler than yours, in his back pocket, for his department. It was everything more or less verbal--passed down from son to son, as far as the Steinway family was concerned, and from foreman to foreman. There was no such thing as specifications. There were some drawings, and where there weren't drawings, they had patterns. And that's why it was called the pattern shop. The major pattern was hanging on the wall or, if it was small, in some drawer. And if something had to be made, and something happened to the secondary pattern which was in the factory, okay then they came here and we compared, or made a new one from this, et cetera. But there were very little drawings, as we know them today."

Hi.  I was on the search for a co-op/condo earlier this year.  IMO, knowingly accepting shoddy or "cheap" building materials for a small discount (if any) in the moment is a weak trade-off.  In the coming years, repairs and improvements could get costly.  Or if the co-op ends up preferring to keep expenses down, the building will just deteriorate.

Even more worrisome, according to some of the articles: the Eagle Electric co-op's offering plan will make it mandatory for the building to convert from co-op to condo in ten years.  THAT will be expensive.  The co-op is just leasing the land right now.  Before they can convert to a condominium, the co-op shareholders will have to buy the land.  I don't know the numbers, but each unit owner will probably take  on additional six-figure debt from this part of the transaction alone.  And all because the developers couldn't/wouldn't finance this land purchase themselves.   I wonder what's the story there.

Of course, there'll be the usual expenses that come with conversion.  Never mind the substantial legal/professional fees that these things can incur.  Shareholders will have to pay off any mortgage on the building itself.  Usually a very hefty assessment.  They'll have to pay off their co-op share loans and take out new mortgage loans, which means closing costs like title insurance, recording fees, more legal fees, etc. etc.   

Now, doesn't that sound like fun?  :-P   All for the privilege of inhabiting the exact same space, which may or may not be falling apart because of the cheap construction, which, at this point, seems to be an open secret.  My mind is totally boggled. :lol:

Title: Re: What's up with the Pistilli properties?
Post by: igpig on June 26, 2007, 08:13:08 PM
I've seen the columns, the small kitchens, the small fridges, etc., as described in the previous posts.  So far ~40 apartments have been sold out of 184 since Oct. 2006.  So, slowly things are moving along.

There are some areas of concern.  The information below is what I learned during the visit and gleaned from the internet.

The co-op does not own the land, but there is a lease for 49 years.  In 5 years (was 10 years when this was set up) the Sponsor will have an option to buy the land.  After the land is purchased, the Sponsor will swap the land with the co-op for the ownership of the garage and the commercial/retail space.  At this point, the co-op will become a condo-op and the sponsor will own the garage/commercial/retail space and co-op owning the residential part.  With the income from the former going solely to the Sponsor.

The Sponsor will retain 49% of the board vote.

The bank, the First Central Savings Bank, financed the construction and is under sponsor's control (Pistilli brothers are the CEO and the CFO of the bank.) The bank also provides mortgages for individual buyers (I suspect, it would be more difficult to obtain mortgage from other financial institutions since the sponsor has 49% of the voting power in the condo-op.) The sponsor seems to have a lot of power.
