I'm no fan of Guiliani, Josh, but it doesn't look like this was his idea. It's a mistake to just assume this is the case.

Frankly, I have no problem with him receiving money from people who want to pay to hear him talk about 9/11. I don't see what is immoral about it. There are people who really like him, who want to see him run for office, who were impressed by his role in addressing the 9/11 crisis, who want him to rise to national prominence based largely on this. They throw a party for other like-minded people and they pay for his time. He sets a price, if they want to pay it, they do. If they don't, they don't. You would be okay if he went on a speaking tour and turned away donations, or directed them to charities. But what if the people giving the money to him want him to use it for his campaign, or want him to keep it as their way of expressing gratitude for what he did. I just don't see what's immoral about this, sorry.

Reasonable people can both criticize and praise his performance as mayor. I happen to disagree with his stand on the war and I think that his negligence after the first WTC attack in upgrading the intercommunication technology of the police/fire department/emt services is inexcusable. He was also a reassuring presence on 9/11 and during its immediate aftermath.   

In the spirit of tit-for-tat, the Democrats regularly scaremonger about what will happen if the government doesn't feed or house people, what will happen if they don't socialize health care, support the teachers' unions, etc.

Republicans and Democrats are equal opportunity exploiters of baseless scare tactics, mon ami.
