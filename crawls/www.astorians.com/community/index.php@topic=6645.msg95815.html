I wouldn't really agree that there's no place for a B&N or Borders in Astoria.  The former Crystal Palace location, now Club Fitness, would have been quite a good choice, and combining it with a Starbucks or a B&N Cafe would have probably been a fantastic idea, since the nearest Starbucks to that area is all the way up on Steinway.  Another large and long-vacant space is the former Kay-Bee toy store on Steinway Street...two stories and it's been vacant for a while, and it's right in the heart of Steinway. 

I recall for about a year or two there was an independent bookstore on 31st Street right by the Ditmars train station, where Rosario's is now.  Unfortunately it didn't last long...but things have changed in Astoria since then.

I also wouldn't agree that libraries lead to the dearth of bookstores.  Again...there's the issue of inconvenient hours as well as selection...that and some people actually like to own their books.  Libraries and bookstores can totally co-exist in the same area.

I wouldn't agree with the ethnic/demographic "realities" that another poster brought up either.  Astoria has changed a lot in the past decade or so.  If anything, Astoria is much more of a "cultured" (for lack of a better term) place than some other areas in Queens which boast large bookstores, such as College Point or Bayside (Bay Terrace).  As for Forest Hills...at this point I don't think Astoria is far "behind" it in terms of the quality of its establishments or its sophistication.  If anything, Austin Street is just a three or four block stretch of stores...Astoria has many "centers" and in many respects an even livlier atmosphere than Forest Hills does....not to mention, demographically speaking, Astoria and Forest Hills seem to be going on somewhat different tracks.  Astoria has been infused with more people moving in from other parts of the city, and other parts of the country and a lot of younger people, in addition to all the new immigration.  Forest Hills largely is only getting the later...immigration from certain countries without much of a "new" infusion from anywhere else.

I just noticed this posting and I had made the demographics comment in another thread. Sorry if I sound like I'm banging on but my point (and opinion) was not that Astoria is “inferior” to other neighborhoods in Queens, it was that demographically, the population of Astoria traditionally has been comprised of a large number of immigrants or children of immigrants, many non-English speaking or non-native English speaking, which could pose a problem in sales, given that B&N mostly sells many books printed in English.

Also, I looked up average income and found that in 2004 the average salary/income of people listed on tax returns in Astoria was 37K per year (in 11105 – it was lower in the other Astoria zip codes). In the main zip code of Forest Hills it was 63K and in Bayside the average salary was 67K per year, which leads me to believe that people who live in those areas probably also have higher levels of education in addition to higher incomes – two factors that make these areas a highly attractive place for B&N to operate in.

The ‘youthful’, ‘cultured’, and ‘sophisticated’ place you describe is the Astoria of today, to which B&N or Borders may finally notice and open a store to cater to. The Astoria of 10+ years ago, well, as much as I love this neighborhood, sophisticated? – eh not so much.

Yes, Astoria has changed a great deal in the past 10 years.

I would invite you all to do a little market survey.

Walk down Steinway Street and count the number of banks, 99 cent stores, nail salons, and stores selling hip-hop kid stuff and gaudy furniture. Take a look at those other communities that these chains have outlets in. Those are the peer communties for Astoria.

Now contrast this with the remaining mainstream stores. Fewer and fewer each year.

Now walk down Broadway. Look and the people and listen to their languages spoken. Count English speakers. Take a look at how the people are dressed. What stores they patronize.

Now take a look at the communities that have the B&N and compare these (admittedly off the cuff) observations of Astoria with those communities.

Astoria will never have a B&N for the same reasons the Elmhurst or Corona will never that them. Perhaps 10 or 20 years ago, but certainly not today. And as the community continues to be hollowed out with a decline in services and rise in absentee landlords undoubtedly there will be none in the future.

And yes, the old Oyster Bay spot on Broadway would have made a nice store and stuff. But you should get the impression by now that something like that is not going to happen - if it did, it would have been built long ago. Perhaps because those folks that control the real estate (and by extension, our community) really doesn't want that for us. I don't have an answer. But I do know that they tried their damnest a decade or so ago to close Bohemian Hall so that should tell us about what they think is good for our community.

Talk to any of the cultural groups (no I don't mean the Fed of Italian Charities and the like) that are trying to exist in this community - they will tell you it is hard ground with scant support from the political or business community.

The developers do have a big say on what is important around here. All you need to do is ride a bike up our death defining 'Greenway' to discover that.

Just one person's opinion.
