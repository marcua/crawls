Post by: Hell Gate Kid on January 13, 2007, 10:19:19 AM
Older buildings do not have a lifespan, as such, but reflect the way they are maintained by their owners. Europe is full older buildings (where they were not destroyed by war) that are central to their charm. Think Prague, Venice, et al.

Having spent a few summers as a kid working for a builder who custom-built homes one at a time, I can state that the older construction techniques were far more solid than the cheap composites used today. When commenting on the Levittown-type tract housing of the 50s, he would tell me those homes were built to sell, not to live in.

The mentality of the 1800s and early 1900s was to construct homes that would stand the test of time. Homes were built for the owner's children - and their children - to move into.

Today, the world is different. People are more mobile. Like global warming, an exploding population is something that is self-inflicted, and the machinery to housing this problem is designed to put a roof over peoples' heads as quickly, and as cheaply, as possible.

Another problem is the utter failure for the city to encourage owner-occupied housing. You can walk down a block and almost point out the few remaining homes that have the owner on site. A community with absentee property owners is a community in trouble. Not only are services over burdened, but the property is allowed to decay. The housing, once a home, becomes a cash cow.

The new housing will, in short order, become a slum. One only needs to walk around Elmhurst and downtown Flushing to see structures built in the past 40 years ready for the wrecking ball.

Which starts the vicious cycle all over again. An exploding population and shorter lifespan of buildings causes development to run on a faster and faster treadmill.

But I guess that is the entire point, eh?
