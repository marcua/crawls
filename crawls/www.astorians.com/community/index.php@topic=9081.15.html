Yep, the movie, even though it was filmed in 1983, really goes into all aspects of graffiti writing, from crews who paint large scale, well done murals like what you see at 5 Pointz or the Graffiti Hall of Fame in Harlem to taggers and bombers whose only purpose for writing is to get famous, at all costs. 
The reason that I recommend the movie is because, in my estimation, the best way to to prevent something like graffiti is to understand why kids are writing in the first place.  If you can remove the motives for graffiti writing, a kid is less apt to write his name somewhere.
Even if Style Wars doesn't change your mind about whether graffiti is a legitimate artform, I think that it provides insight into the psyche of a writer, which seems more useful when combating graffiti than expecting an understaffed police force to catch every kid with a marker or a spray can.  Additionally, if you really want to deter kids from writing, pressure the property owners whose buildings are being tagged to quickly cover up tags.

I don't necessarily think that graffiti itself can provide a living for a person, but it can certainly develop into a career.  Manipulating letter forms can turn into a career as a type designer.  One of my close friends was a graffiti writer in the town we grew up in, and he took his love of graffiti and turned it into a lucrative, prestigious web design career.  Hell, one of these Astoria kids could be the next Keith Herring.  I think that there are ways of fostering these talents and making them useful and profitable, but there needs to be a nurturing environment.  Someone to tell these kids that, "hey, that's really cool what you're doing there, but what if..."  Someone to give them the recognition that they're so obvious pining for.
Personally, I never did graffiti, I have loved it since I was a little kid and first visited NYC when I was 5, and there were still painted trains and the like, but I never wanted to get arrested, and I couldn't bring myself to paint or draw on someone else's property without their permission.  However, a desire to have my work seen and recognized drove me to designing store windows for a living.  I think that yearning for recognition drives a lot more people than graffiti artists and window designers. 
I personally think that there are lines that a lot of graffiti kids cross that shouldn't be crossed with regard to where they paint.  At the same time I think that the best way to prevent this is by diligence when painting over these illegal pieces, providing a legal space (such as 5 Pointz or the GHOF) for kids to paint, and by addressing the need for these kids to be creative and the desire to be recognized.  Unfortunately, the two things that seem to get cut first in school funding are art and music. 
I don't know I think that this has gone far enough off topic, I'll end this by saying that, to me, relying on the police to eradicate graffiti isn't enough.  If the community wants to see an end to graffiti, there needs to be a proactive movement.  Thte community needs to push property owners to keep up their buildings, lobby for fines to property owners who let graffiti ramain on their buildings for a certain amount of time, or even put together a community group that goes out and paints over graffiti.

Instead of your knee jerk reaction, I would have appreciated had you taken a minute to consider the validity of what I was saying. 
There is a reason we don't see painted trains running through New York City, that's because the MTA cleans all of their trains at the end of the line, they don't let a train run if it's painted either inside or out (which is why scrachitti has gained popularity).  This effectively removes the recognition that a writer gains by having his piece run through the city, even once.  This is what needs to be done to buildings.  If a kid can't brag about how he got up somewhere because he knows that by the time he gets there the next day it'll be gone, his motivation is removed from the equation. 
At the same time if a kid is motivated enough to climb up a billboard to paint his name for the recognition of a very small subculture, wouldn't it it make sense that if this energy was channeled into a different, legal, creative endeavor that would be recognized and appreciated by a much larger audience, the kid could actually be motivated enough to make something of themself. 
At this point, I feel like I've wasted enough bandwidth trying to explain why we, as a community, can't solely rely on the police.
