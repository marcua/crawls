Four Dems Contending for Owens' Crown in the `City of Kings'
By Marie Horrigan
Sep. 06, 2006

Four high-profile Democrats are running in the Sept. 12 primary to
succeed veteran New York Democratic Rep. Major R. Owens in
Brooklyn's 11th Congressional District. And they have set up a race
so tight that even local political insiders are loathe to predict a
winner.
State Sen. Carl Andrews, New York City Council members Yvette Clarke
and David Yassky, and political activist Chris Owens, the
congressman's son, are competing for a seat that could provide the
winner with lifetime job security.
The district, in which three-fifths of the residents are black and a
total of roughly four-fifths are minorities, is a Democratic
stronghold. Three out of four voters consider themselves Democrats:
Party presidential nominee John Kerry received 86 percent of the
district's vote in 2004, and incumbent Owens, in the last of his 12
House victories, took 94 percent and had no Republican opponent.
The four would-be successors in many ways reflect the diversity of
the district itself, which encompasses areas of Brooklyn including
middle- and working-class communities such as Crown Heights,
Flatbush and Brownsville, as well the increasingly "gentrified"
neighborhood of Park Slope:
• Yassky has drawn much attention, and perhaps caused the biggest
stir, as the only white candidate running for a seat that has had
African-American representation since it was claimed in the mid-
1960s by black political pioneer Shirley Chisholm. Despite criticism
from some black activists, Yassky pursues a liberal agenda that he
says should appeal to district voters regardless of race.
• Clarke's political heritage, after succeeding her mother, Una
Clarke, on the City Council, gives her deep ties within the
political community that supplement her voter base in her council
district. Clarke set the stage for this campaign by challenging the
elder Owens in the 2004 primary, holding the incumbent to a 45
percent to 29 percent edge in the four-candidate race.
• Andrews represents half of the congressional district in the state
Senate and is well-connected to top state Democrats, including
Attorney General Eliot Spitzer, the likely Democratic nominee and
general election favorite for governor.
• Chris Owens, a former campaign manager for his father, predicts
his background as a private-sector businessman, his political
activism, and district voters' fondness for the incumbent will
propel him to the front of the pack next Tuesday.
"You've got the interaction of geography and demography. You've got
three black candidates, you've got overlapping districts ... and
you've got the various institutional supports. It's very difficult
to figure out which way the vote's moving," political science
professor Doug Muzzio of Baruch College, a part of the City
University of New York, told CQPolitics.com.
The consensus among analysts, though not an overwhelming one, is
that if there is a top tier in the field, it is composed of Clarke
and Yassky.
Clarke differentiates herself as the only woman in the race. As the
daughter of Caribbean immigrants, she also connects with one of the
two major ethnic groups among black voters in the 11th District. She
succeeded her mother (who challenged Major Owens in the 2000 House
primary) in the 40th District City Council seat in 2001 and was re-
elected to the seat twice, the second time just last year.
Yassky, who last week picked up the endorsement of the New York
Times, is by far the best-funded of the candidates. According to the
candidates' pre-primary filings with the Federal Election
Commission, Yassky had $360,000 in cash on hand as of Aug. 23 out of
$1.4 million raised. Andrews raised the second-most money with
$543,000 in total receipts, but heavy spending left him last in cash
reserves with $32,000. Clarke had $76,000 left in her coffers out of
$398,000 raised, and Owens had $64,000 out of $351,000 raised.
"He's the Bloomberg of the race in terms of money," former New York
City Councilman Kenneth Fisher said of Yassky, comparing his
campaign to the big-spending blowouts by Republican Michael
Bloomberg in his successful 2001 and 2005 bids for mayor of New York.
But, Fisher continued, "If demographics is destiny, Yassky needs to
win a good-sized majority of the white vote and a respectable
percentage of the black vote in order to be able to win. And it's
not clear to me whether he has closed the sale in either of those
categories."
Clarke, meanwhile, is one of several critics who charge that
Yassky's decision to run in the 11th — he moved into the district,
although he already represented part of it in the city council — was
an opportunistic move in which Yassky hoped to take advantage of the
three-way split among the primary's black candidates.
"I've said before that I thought it was a racial calculation, and I
stand by that statement," Clarke said in an e-mailed statement to
CQPolitics.com. "To pick up and move to a district where there were,
at the time, four candidates of color running against one another —
I don't see any other way to look at it, considering his council
district is just about evenly divided between three congressional
districts."
Yassky and Clarke, though viewed by some as the possible front-
runners entering the home stretch, may be hindered by recent
developments. Yassky, who has been portrayed by his opponents as too
cozy with developers, last week canceled a fundraiser with architect
Robert Scarano Jr., whose large-scale designs for buildings in
Brooklyn have caused controversies.
The Yassky campaign has dismissed the flap, saying simply that the
event was cancelled after it was determined to be
inappropriate. "You know a race is being examined under a microscope
when a scheduling mistake makes the newspapers," Yassky spokesman
Evan Thies told CQPolitics.com.
Clarke, meanwhile, faces the re-emergence of reports that in recent
campaigns she inflated her fundraising numbers as well her
educational credentials, implying she graduated from Oberlin College
in Ohio when she did not.
The other two candidates contend they are not out of the running.
Owens said that although his campaign trails in fundraising, voters
respond enthusiastically once they get the chance to meet him.
Voters find meeting him "quite exciting, because they do have a lot
of respect and love for my father, and they're excited by the fact
that I'm running," he told CQPolitics.com.
Owens argues his own experience in the private sector gives him a
leg up over the competition in terms of understanding how to serve
voters. "We talk about how we deliver on better customer service or
constituents, when we talk about some of our plans in the business
world and how government impacts on small businesses, I speak with
the experience that my opponents cannot," he said.
Andrews, who the New York Times slammed in its editorial endorsing
Yassky as too close to the Democratic political machine, dismissed
the label as a typecast. He cited his experience in the state Senate
and in helping run Democrat Hillary Rodham Clinton's Senate campaign
in 2000 and Spitzer's re-election campaign for attorney general in
2004 — all of which, he said, have given him an understanding of
reaching out to voters and also across the aisle.
"I have a proud record of working in the Democratic Party getting
Democrats elected and all those individuals came to me to be a part
of their campaign ... so I think that those endorsements of support
from those individuals speak for themselves," he told CQPolitics.com.
Muzzio stated, though, that recent stumbles could place all the
candidates on the defensive. "It may be rather than who's going to
win the race, it's who's not going to lose it," he said.
The New York Working Families Party, which often endorses Democratic
Party candidates, has not yet done so in the 11th District race.
Attorney and community activist Elon Harpaz is running unopposed on
the party's line Sept. 12, though party officials have been known to
ask their nominee to step aside after a primary in lieu of the
Democratic Party's nominee.
Stephen Finger, a medical doctor and self-described libertarian, is
running on the Republican line. Marianne Blume, an outspoken state
Republican Party committeewoman is running for the Conservative
Party line; she did the same in the neighboring 10th District two
years ago, taking 1 percent of the vote in a contest dominated by
Democratic incumbent Edolphus Towns.
CQ rates the general election race as Safe Democratic. Please visit
CQPolitics.com's Election Forecaster for ratings on all races.

I've been following this race very closely, and even helped moderate a forum for it this month. Again, it's similar to the AG race, four good candidates.  That being said, I have a strong preference in this race, and it's Yvette Clarke.  We need more women in Congress and the candidates have almost identical positions on all issues. 

Major Owens, while a very nice man, and one who had good politics, did absolutely nothing of note in his many years in Congress, hardly a fitting legacy for the seat held by Shirley Chisolm.  His son has the best politics in my opinion, he's a true progressive on all issues and is charming, but I fear he would be a repeat of the father and his continuing insistence that development issues can be addressed by a Congressmember makes me think he doesn't appreciate the areas of focus between local, state and federal levels of government. 

David Yassky did great things in the Council and he would be a great addition to Congress, but to run in this district smacks of political opportunism of the worst kind and I think it's wrong for him to run in this seat.  If elected, he would probably only hold the seat for one or two terms before an African-American consensus candidate emerged and knocked him out of office. 

Carl Andrews is one of the nicest men around and he knows government inside and out.  He would also be a good Congressmember.  His years of work in the district and his connections in city and state government would allow him to access great resources for the district.  He's been a good State Senator and his connections to Eliot Spitzer would serve him well.

I support Yvette because all things being equal, the fact that she is a women and the child of immigrants, she will bring greater diversity to Congress and she is able to bring people from all backgrounds together.  Her record in the City Council is strong - she fought hard to bring greater diversity to the Fire Department and to ensure equal treatment for women.
