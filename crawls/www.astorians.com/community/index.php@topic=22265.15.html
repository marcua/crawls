The problem isn't that the 114 is going after graffiti vandals and pot smokers, the problems is that they FOCUS their energy and resources on going after these low level offenses, while ignoring that more serious crime occurs in this neighborhood.

A person over the age of 16 who gets busted for marijuana or graffiti will spend time in bookings (it's supposed to be less than 24 hours to arraignment, but often it's 24-72 hours), get assigned an attorney at tax payer expense, be granted an ACD and walk out the door. So to grant this ACD, the case takes up the time of a defense attorney and the assistant district attorney, it keeps the police officer off the street and in an office to sign off on paperwork, it takes up the time of the clerks and other administrative personnel in the court system, and it goes before a judge who will see this person for all of 2 minutes before sending them on their way. Btw, there is rarely restitution paid to victims whose property was vandalized with graffiti.

A person under the age of 16 who gets busted for marijuana or graffiti will either spend the night in a juvenile detention facility or be released to their parents at the precinct. They will go to Family Court where they will meet with a probation officer who can decide to adjust their case, meaning they meet with probation once or twice over a 60 day period and never have a case filed against them, or have it referred to the Corporation Counsel's office for filing. If a case gets filed against them, they will also be assigned an attorney at tax payer expense, see a judge who may also ACD the case or set the case down for trial, taking up more limited resources in an already overburdened court system. And again, rarely is there restitution paid.

This isn't just a problem with the 114, though. NYPD has a habit of going after the easy arrest -- the person who falls asleep on the subway and leans over, taking up two seats on an otherwise empty subway car, or the person who walks their dog offleash and doesn't have ID on him, so he ends up going through bookings and spends the night in jail (these are real examples of cases that have come through Queens Criminal Court arraignments in the last week or two). They say that they're going after "quality of life" crimes, but all it's really done is strike fear in the heart of the non-criminal citizen that they may do or say something perceived as out of line that lands them in jail for the night, while the real criminals are left unchecked.
