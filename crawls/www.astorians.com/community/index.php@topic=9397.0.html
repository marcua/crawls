Here's what I do--and I have to be out of the house by 6:10 every morning.

I figure out the ideal time and the absolute latest time that I need to be up and then set three alarms. One for fifteen minutes before the ideal time, one at the ideal time and one for five minutes before absolute latest. I find that staggered alarms mean I'm less likely to shut them all off.

Quote
It's just physically impossible for my alarm to go off and wake up and get out of bed.


 :mrgreen:  The day my alarm wakes up and gets out of bed I will know I have lost.  Or won.   :mrgreen:

I can't get up no matter what I do.  I have brief periods, sometimes lasting weeks, where I manage to get up early and do things or on time and get ready for work in a reasonable manner, but generally, I can snooze my alarm for up to two hours -- or do what I've been doing this week, turning it off entirely.

If I put my clock ahead, I know it's ahead and it doesn't work for me.  If I put it across the room, I sleepwalk and turn it off.  Setting multiple alarms works for me for important things -- like when I have a plane to catch (although I did miss a plane once because I slept completely through the alarm - it was an old-fashioned extra loud bell about six inches from my head with no off button, but I had acclimated to it) -- but if I do it on a regular basis, I just start turning them all off in my sleep.  It works sometimes, though.

Good luck.  I am a complete, hopeless night owl, but even when I do manage to get to bed earlier I still have trouble.  I'm also a very deep sleeper and if it's not time for my body to get up, it fights it every step of the way and can sleep through almost anything (including fire alarms).  I haven't found a good solution yet, although multiple alarms set for different times in different locations is probably the most successful for me.  Maybe change it up -- put them in different places and at different times every few days so your body doesn't have the time to learn the pattern.

Are you sure you're getting enough sleep?  Maybe you suffer from sleep apnea or something that prevents your body from getting the rest it needs.  Do you feel rested when you get out of bed?  What about on weekends?  Does your insurance cover doing a sleep study?  It might tell you why you're not getting up. 

I'm so not a morning person and need at least 20 minutes of psychological snooze time.  When I'm afraid of missing a flight, I set two alarms, stagger the times and out one across the room.  One clock is set 10 minutes early but I find that it just makes me do early morning math in my head.  I think if you set one across the room, once you are up, just force yourself to do anything else besides get back in bed.  Go pee, make coffee, turn on a bunch of lights, splash water on your face -- anything besides getting back in bed. 

Have you looked into one of those services that calls you on the phone?

I set my alarm for 5:50, with the expectation that I'll get up at 6:30; usually I am up between 6:30 and 6:45.  I've been training myself to wake up early (I used to get up at 8!) since I started my job in December, and it's gotten easier as time has progressed.  I also put on the radio as my alarm, fairly loudly, on a news channel (CBS news - sometimes NPR), so that the news engages my mind and I listen to the headlines, weather, sports.   I like the radio better than an alarm, too.  The alarm sound just pisses me off and gives me no incentive to stay awake.

My goal is to be able to get up by 6am so that I can get more exercise.

It's hard, though.  But setting my alarm close to an hour before I need to be up has worked for me over the years.  Good luck!
