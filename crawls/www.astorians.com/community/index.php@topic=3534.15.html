Heh - well, like Alison, my secret weapon is Fitday, and I've been using it for about 3 1/2 years.  They have a completely free online version, and they now have software as well.  I bought the software as soon as it became available (almost the only time I've ever bought software for something extraneous).  As Alison mentioned, both the online version and the software let you enter custom foods, so you calculate everything once, and then if you more or less stick to the basic ingredients, you have at least an easy estimate of what you've eaten.  However, there really isn't an "easy" way.  It does take some dedication.

I lost nearly 45 pounds 10 years ago (and have kept most of it off) by low carbing, and I've essentially maintained a low carb eating pattern since then.  Before anyone jumps in and starts telling me how unhealthy it is, my diet consists of lots of veggies, some fruit, lean meats, fish, chicken, and sometimes vegetarian protein options.  I eat some whole grains (quinoa is a particular favourite, and I also love The Baker flax bread, which is soy-free).  I don't drown things in heavy cream or eat a pound of bacon a day.  I get plenty of fiber.

For me, the key was that it gave me the ability to construct a healthy, satisfying diet without white flour, pasta, sugar, white rice, etc.  I focus primarily on whole foods, and eat very few pre-packaged foods or "low carb substitutes".  A typical meal for me includes a meat and two vegetables, or a meat, a vegetable, and a mixed vegetable and grain.  For instance, last night (even though I wasn't hungry and didn't eat much of what I prepared), we had scallops, quinoa salad with pine nuts, and a CSA "gazpacho" that Jim made - lots of CSA veggies cooked together, blended and chilled, served with a dollop of fat free Fage yogurt.  If I had wanted dessert, I would have had a square or two of high quality bittersweet chocolate.  I am also quite portion-size conscious, and I have learned what I can or cannot eat on a regular basis in order for me to maintain my weight.  In addition, I have a kitchen scale that measures in both ounces and grams, and I use it.  A lot.

As those of you who know me will agree, I don't completely eschew high carb foods - you've all seen me chow down on pizza, burgers, fries, etc.  However, I do limit those things, and I feel better for it.  I no longer have energy dips after lunch, for instance -- no 3:00 lull for me.

Last but not least - exercise is of course important, both cardio and weight training.  Being a gym member is probably the easiest, but there are also lots of exercise options without belonging to a gym.  You can jog most of the year either in the neighbourhood or at the track at Astoria Park.  You can buy a few weights and construct an entire weight routine around them.  Situps, crunches, etc. are easy to do at home.

Are you equating "dieting" with "watching what you eat"?  I definitely watch what I eat, and I sometimes specifically "diet", but I don't consider them the same thing.  By "watching what I eat", I mean that I am aware of my food and what I can get away with.  I think that is true of most people:  there are a lucky few who can eat almost anything and get away with it, but I am not one of those people, and I think most people aren't.

I know what my caloric intake has to be for me to maintain my weight, and I do adjust my daily intake knowing that I will be going out with friends.  Generally, when I plan my meals (which I try to do consistently - of course, it doesn't always work), I make allowances for the fact that I am going to have a slice of pizza or whatever now and then.  (Just saw Alison's post - and I think I'm saying essentially the same thing.  While my diet generally is free from white foods, I haven't completely eliminated things - I still allow myself to eat them from time to time.  I should add that I usually feel like crap after I eat those things, but feeling like crap once in awhile is better than all the time.  ;) )

Generally, a vegetarian diet is more carb-heavy than an omivorous diet, and while some people can handle that physiologically, other people find it more difficult.  (I'm not commenting at all on personal reasons that people have for choosing a vegetarian or an omnivorous diet -- those are completely different and not on topic for this thread.)  For someone who has some insulin sensitivities (not necessarily diabetes), a vegetarian diet has to be very carefully calibrated to contain sufficient protein.  I know vegetarian low carbers, in fact.  I've experimented with higher carb diets (and I don't mean a Standard American Diet, I mean one with less meat and more whole grains, all whole food), and I instantly start to gain weight, become sluggish and sleepy after meals (the effect of insulin), and generally have less energy.  I have a family history of type II diabetes on both sides of my family, and based on personal experience and extensive research, I am convinced that by avoiding or minimising my intake of foods that cause consistent insulin spikes (I also pay attention to the glycemic index/load of foods), I am lowering my own risk of developing it later in life.
