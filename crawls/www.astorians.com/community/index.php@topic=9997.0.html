Restaurant Ethics - a question (advice and perspectives requested)
« on: April 01, 2008, 12:19:49 PM »
Just wanted to get folks to weigh in on a situation myself and a few others had this weekend (without naming names of establishments) and see what the general consensus is.  I fear I'm too close to the situation and am not sure I'm able to be objective.

I'm one of the coordinators of a dining group, we do both brunches and dinners and hold two events monthly - we have for over two years.  I also am the Executive Director of a Chamber of Commerce, so I deal with restaurant owners every day and am in great sympathy with them and try to make sure anything I do works out well for them.  Being far from shy, extremely vocal I guess is the best way to describe me, I tend to sing the praises of any place I think is wonderful and express dismay when I think a place did something shady.  As I'm involved in too many groups for my own good, I tend to circulate information pretty widely.

We held a brunch this weekend in a new space - one that folks had been suggesting for well over a year.  Not a venue I had ever been to, but one I wanted to check out as I'd heard rave reviews, from such an array of sources that I was eager to check it out.  We made our usual arrangements – group of 24-30 for brunch and booked the date three months in advance (we go up to 50 at dinners).  We have folks who drink, and folks who do not, so we always have the general check only apply to food, and not include alcohol – folks always pay for their drinks on their own.  We try to divide checks by table (so folks can share appetizers, if any).  I've dealt with over 30 restaurants in Astoria, some where the staff had problems understanding me, and we never had a problem until now.

When we arrived, the space had been closed for a "private party", even though we did not have enough people to fill the entire space.  That should have been my first tip off that things were not going to go well.  The new deal, which was very different from the deal I had negotiated, was presented to me when almost all of our party had already been seated.  I suspect I should have pushed back at this point or marched folks out of the place – it certainly would have saved us money.

What would folks think of brunch for $11?  That seems reasonable to me, if a bit expensive and usually comes with a drink.  We also got a $20 per person alcohol charge tacked on, for two hours of open bar, which I completely understand makes money for them, but which really penalizes those folks who don't drink, and seriously, who is going to drink $20 of alcohol in two hours, especially at brunch?  We had at least room for another 8 folks and there were spaces at the bar, so I really don't get why they had closed the space.

We certainly had some of the least attentive service we had ever received and when two non-drinkers asked for a waiver of their $20 bill, and three people who had been charged $5 for their bloody marys in addition to his $20 inquired, they got brusquely told "whatever" though it stayed on the bill.  We also had a base 20% tip, which I understand as we were a large party and that is totally acceptable, though we did, I must re-iterate, get really incredibly lousy service.

Is $31 per person for brunch in Astoria out of line (keep in mind, this is before tax and tip)?  I know I personally think it is.  I certainly know I'll never patronize the place again and will strongly recommend against anyone else ever going to it.  I personally ended up putting in $60, and another organizer got smacked with a bill for $120, so I guess the restaurant ended up doing somewhat decently, but it left such a bad taste in so many folks mouths that I think it probably ends up doing them harm in the longer run.

What would folks have done in this situation?  Any constructive advice?  I think I should have spoken up aggressively at the start instead of placating the owner and been more proactive in contesting the charges for the non-drinkers.

It was a very strange situation, and a very uncomfortable one.  It was an odd development and I just do not get the business practice.  Is Dillinger's gay friendly?  We usually do our Out Astoria brunches at Brick, Mundo, Cup, and sometimes at Cafe Triskell.  We even did Grand Cafe, though that was a bit too tight.  We're always interested in new venues - I think we'll add Mojave for a brunch as they are absolutely wonderful folks and if folks have suggestions, we'd love to hear them.

I think the lesson I learned is to put things into writing where I can and not rely on oral arrangements with folks, though that's always worked for me in the past.  I think we should have just gotten up and left as a group, as awkward as it might have been, especially since I'm getting upset emails from many of the attendees.  I'm still debating whether I should call or write the place and say how incredibly disappointed we were in the entire experience.
