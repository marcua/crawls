The signs are not just on the walls but at the pump. Time to switch to human powered vehicles. And if it's DOT spearheading this change on Vernon Blvd., well, about time they got their smarts. I say DOT sees the future. They are into building highways, streets, roads or what have you to accommodate traffic and make it flow. Well, they are just preparing for the coming advent (it's almost here) of more bicycles on the road. I'm happy to see the end of "Build longer, wider and straighter roads, and bigger, heavier and more gas-guzzling V8's and SUVs" mentality. Traffic calming is what we need. Making room for bicycles is the right thing to do to encourage more cycling and lessen our dependence on foreign oil that causes unjustified and costly wars. Besides, who says no cyclists use Vernon Blvd.? Duh?! Most people who go to the parks on the Queens waterfronts use bicycles or go by foot. This has already been proven by a recent Dept. of Parks survey. Duh!

I'm not speaking to anyone specific here but I really have to speak to this bike issue a bit.  I think it's great that people ride bikes for pleasure and even for commuting.  But I think this automatic and overwhelming support for all things bike related is very unfair and misguided.  Personally, I've had more than a couple of very unpleasant run ins with bikes/bikers.  Literrally.  Bikes can actually reach a pretty high speed and it's no fun for a pedestrian to be hit by one.  And it happens A LOT.  There are a lot of bikers out there who think because they're doing this amazingly green thing, that they're entitled to race down the streets and sidewalks as they please.  They run red lights rampantly and then yell and whistle as they're blowing through intersections filled with people who have the Walk signal.  And their disdain of car drivers often leads to aggressively dangerous run ins with cars as well.  A law-abiding driver shouldn't have to slam on breaks because a bike flies in front of them illegally, and worse - deliberately.

Hey - I don't think it's smart for any Astorian to be driving around in a Hummer.  But not all of us can realistically use bikes.  I can't take a bike to my job in the clothes I wear.  I can't take the bike in the rain or snow or 90 degree days.  It's important that I look and smell clean for my job.  So I ride the subway (I know - not always clean and pretty smelling either!).  I would seriously consider a Vespa but that's about it.  I am also not in my 20s anymore and maybe not in the best shape to be biking over bridges and across boroughs.  And you know what?  Maybe I just don't want to.  But don't assume that makes me, and everyone like me, to be these horrible people who just use up gallons and gallons of unnecessary fuel. 

I guess I'm trying to say that it's not fair to think you know both sides.  Because maybe there are aspects not being considered.  So I don't believe that the entire stretch of Vernon should be changed in this way 24/7/365.  I think maybe during rush hours, like HOV lanes, makes sense.  But all the time?  And over the weekend when we have company visiting who need to park - then no.  It's like anything else.  No one likes any group of people imposing their standards on others.  I think a rational result will come if some of the more adamant bikers tone it down a notch.

There are an amazing number of pedestrians who never look out for bikes who are obeying the traffic laws, either.  Bikers are surprisingly invisible to both car drivers and pedestrians.  If you provide a designated space for bikes on the streets, there will be fewer bikes on sidewalks, where some bikers go because they feel the road is too dangerous because there aren't designated lanes.  Their perceived disdain for car drivers are likely as a result of years of battling car drivers who disdain them -- or who go so far as to target them and terrorize them.  I know people this has happened to.  Maybe both sides need to calm down, but from what I've seen, aggressiveness by bikers stems from their desire to stay alive.  There are many cities and countries where bikers and cars drive together harmoniously - the antagonism doesn't need to be there.  But until cars give a little of the road back, it will probably continue.  Both bikes and cars are legally allowed to use the roads - they are not exclusively a domain of cars.

Of course commuting by bike isn't for everyone, but why penalise those for whom it is possible by forcing them to ride in the middle of motorized vehicle traffic?  As for your job - well, I know several people who commute by bike whose jobs require them to wear ties and smell clean.  They seem to have figured out a way to make it work.  They aren't in their 20s, either.

For the record, I don't commute by bike and I don't intend to.  But I fully support safer roads for those who do choose to do so.  In the end, bikers, car drivers, and pedestrians will all benefit.
