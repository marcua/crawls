Quote from: Jtrane on August 20, 2007, 10:17:48 PM
I thought it was illegal to have off duty cops working as security guards.  There are serious issues with that including conflict of interest.  Someone should look into that. (I'm too lazy).

In 2003, the NYC Council passed Resolution 883 (http://webdocs.nyccouncil.info/textfiles/Res%200883-2003.htm?CFID=453426&CFTOKEN=46031848), which noted that with only a couple of exceptions, section 128 of the Alcohol Beverage Control Law prohibited members of the police force from being directly or indirectly interested in the sale of alcoholic beverages, and it called on the state legislature "to amend the Alcohol Beverage Control Law to allow any member of the police force, while off-duty, to provide security at locations where alcohol is used or sold."  Here (http://webdocs.nyccouncil.info/attachments/59338.htm) is the Committee report on the resolution, which "Recogniz[ed] the value of having trained security guards at nightclubs, bars and restaurants."

That said, the current version (on the NY State Legislature website) of ยง 128 and the current version of ยง 128-A do not seem to have been amended to allow it.  I can't link directly to them but from here (http://public.leginfo.state.ny.us/menugetf.cgi?COMMONQUERY=LAWS), click on ABC   Alcoholic Beverage Control, then  Article 8 - (100 - 131) GENERAL PROVISIONS, and then you will see links to  128 - Certain officials not to be interested in manufacture or sale of alcoholic beverages and 128-A - Police officers allowed to work in licensed premises in certain cases.

ยง 128 currently provides in relevant part that "1. Except as otherwise provided in section one hundred twenty-eight-a, it shall be unlawful for any police commissioner, police inspector, captain, sergeant, roundsman, patrolman or other police official or subordinate of any police department in the state, to be either directly or indirectly interested in the manufacture or sale of alcoholic beverages or to offer for sale, or recommend to any licensee any alcoholic beverages."

ยง 128-A provides only that "it shall not be unlawful for a police officer employed in this state, having written permission and consent from his commanding officer, to work in a premises licensed to sell beer at retail for off-premises consumption." [Emphasis mine -- in other words, not a bar.]

If anyone has any further information relating to any amendment (or lack thereof) to these laws, I'd be interested in knowing about it, too.  There appears to be a current proposed amendment in Committee in the State Senate to expand section 128-A to include wine as well as beer, but it doesn't seem to affect the requirement that the sale be for off-premises consumption.  The New York legislature website started acting up, so I haven't been able to confirm whether this proposed amendment is in fact being considered.

Post by: lanseaux on August 21, 2007, 12:00:44 PM
Quote from: lanseaux on August 21, 2007, 12:26:11 AM
There appears to be a current proposed amendment in Committee in the State Senate to expand section 128-A to include wine as well as beer, but it doesn't seem to affect the requirement that the sale be for off-premises consumption.  The New York legislature website started acting up, so I haven't been able to confirm whether this proposed amendment is in fact being considered.

I checked again this morning, and located it.  It's Senate bill No. S3011.  You can access it here (http://public.leginfo.state.ny.us/menugetf.cgi).  Type S3011 into the blank box, and check all the boxes below (Status, Text, Summary and Sponsor's Memo) for complete text and information regarding the amendment.  Basically, the Sponsor's Memo states that this will allow officers to own grocery stores - it has nothing to do with allowing off-duty officers to act as security at a bar or other establishment where consumption of alcoholic beverages is permitted on the premises.

Quote
JUSTIFICATION:  By enacting this proposal, New York State would allow police officers to own premises licensed to sell beer and wine coolers for off-premises consumption. Generally, grocery stores would fall under this category. Since ownership of a grocery store would not compromise a police officer in his or her enforcement duties, and beer and wine coolers are basic products in such stores, this bill would have the effect of allowing police officers the freedom of involvement in an area of business unrelated to their other professional duties.
