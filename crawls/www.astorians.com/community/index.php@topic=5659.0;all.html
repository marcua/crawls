I find the way that Icelandic people deal with last names to be fascinating - they are patronymic or matronymic constructs:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icelandic_name

Typical Icelandic naming

For example, a man named Jn Stefnsson has a son named Fjalar. Fjalar's last name will not be Stefnsson like his father's; it will become Jnsson, literally indicating that Fjalar is the son of Jn (Jns + son).

The same practice is used for daughters. Jn Stefnsson's daughter Katrn would not have the last name Stefnsson; she would have the name Jnsdttir. Again, the last name literally means "Jn's daughter" (Jns + dttir).

In some cases, an individual's last name is derived from his/her parent's middle name instead of first name. For example, if Jn is the son of Hjlmar Arnar Vilhjlmsson he may either be named Jn Hjlmarsson (Jn son of Hjlmar) or Jn Arnarsson (Jn son of Arnar). The reason for this may be that the parent prefers to be called by his/her middle name instead of first, which is fairly common, or that the parent's middle name seems to fit the child's first name better.

In some cases where two people in the same social circle bear the same first name and the same father's name they are socially distinguished by their paternal grandfather's name. E.g. Jn rsson Bjarnarsonar (Jn, son of r, son of Bjarni) and Jn rsson Hallssonar (Jn, son of r, son of Hallur). This method is not common (as middle names are more commonly used), but such tracing of lineages can easily be seen in the Sagas.

Matronymic naming as a choice

The vast majority of Iceland last names carry the name of the father, but in some cases the mother's name is used, for various reasons. Sometimes either the child or legal parent wishes to end social ties with the father. Some women use it as a social statement, and yet others simply choose it as a matter of style and nothing more. In all of these cases, the convention is entirely the same. Fjalar, the son of Brynds, will have the full name of Fjalar Bryndsarson (literally meaning "the son of Brynds"). A notable Icelander with a matronymic name is football player Heiar Helguson

If the tradition were switched up in history, and instead, husband were addressed as Mrs. Brooke Shields or whatever, I'm positive they would have the same issues about losing their identity through marriage.  It's an old-school formality, that's probably lost its meaning over the decades, but it's rooted in the very old-school belief that you are your husband's property once you get married, and your realm is the house and the kids, and the husband's is everything else.  Granted not many people buy into this anymore, thank God, but there are remnants of this still around.   Even your father giving you away on your wedding day, or the fact that you wear a white dress.  Society still hangs on to these traditions, but the traditions do come from something.  And that is why, some women do struggle with the idea of losing their identity through marriage.  And if men had gone through the same thing instead, then they would have the same issue.

Daisy, you hit it right on the head. What's happening these days is people (women) confronting / compromising traditions and mores of the long past.  I will say that my wife who is very progressive and independent made some choices at our wedding that stood out.

1. She did wear a white dress, mostly for family.
2. Did not wear a veil and fought family on that issue.
3. We made sure our vows were mutual and loving with nothing about our relying on god or obeying each other to make our marriage a success.
4. Seemed so happy to have her father and mother walk her down the aisle. Not so much because he was giving her away but for the sheer joy of having them with her (us) at that very important moment.
5. She did unexpectedly take my last name which was a surprise and made me happy on a number of points. Admittedly a few of which were very very old fashioned and did surprise me.

My parents walked me down the aisle and that really made the moment for me.
