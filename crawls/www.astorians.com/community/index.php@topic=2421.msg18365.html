And the rest of the community, that doesn't live in any of these buildings, gets their view cut off by the buildings.Used to be that you could watch the fireworks from anywhere in the neighborhood with an unobstucted view. No more.

I wasn't here last year, but in 2004 Center Blvrd was barricaded by cops. They screened every single person who wanted to enter the park with metal detector wands and made small children open their picnic baskets to check that they weren't smuggling weapons. It goes without saying that they did all of this with the insolent, indifferent slowness that is the hallmark of airport security and immigration officials the world over. By the time the fireworks got going, there was a vast line of people stretching along 50th Avenue. If someone had wanted to detonate a bomb, they could have killed as many poeple right there as in the park. So it is that on America's day of freedom the waterfront of Long Island City is turned into a police state.

The police are brave men and women who usually act in our interest. On this occasion, their actions were heavy handed and pointless. You could find larger concentrations of people in Times Square every day. I would be interested to know what law they invoke to stop and search people using a public highway. You may think it's a trivial point, but on July 4th of all days it is important to remember that the government is our servant, not our master. "Those who would sacrifice liberty for security deserve neither."

Home in my case is the Avalon building where I will have air conditioning, a fridge full of beer and an excellent view from my window, so I'm not concerned for myself. But I will feel a twinge of pity for the huddled masses below who will have to wait for hours in the baking sun. The barricades simply create an arbitrary boundary between two large crowds. Either would serve as material for a July 4th massacre, so the effort of mechanical screening will not make the average spectator any safer. You might as well screen everyone who wants to enter Times Square. My argument is not against the police presence, but against creating an arbitrary inconvenience for the sake of being seen to "do something". You may welcome it, but I see it as a surrender to the terrorists and a step on the road to serfdom.

I wasn't there last year, but here's what I was told this week by one of the state employees from  Gantry Park: There's no preferential treatment for people from Citylights (or any other group) at all. Access to the piers has always been on a first come, first on basis. Once the piers reach capacity--and this can happen early--access is restricted, but it's strictly a safety/crowd control issue. At that point, he said,  anyone on the piers who wanted to leave to use the bathroom or get a drink was issued a pass so that they could  get back on.

Again, I wasn't there myself last year, but this does sound like a reasonable explanation of how a rumor could have gotten started. In past years, we've gotten there as early as 2:00 PM and seen the piers closed by about 5 or 6.  (One year, the folks seated at those cool metal bar stools tables on the pier told us they'd been there before noon.)

If you've never been to Gantry, its always a beautiful spot for a picnic, with or without fireworks...
