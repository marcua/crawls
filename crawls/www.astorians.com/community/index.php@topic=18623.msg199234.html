Who would "your broker" be in this situation?  What service would you provide as a rental broker, aside from showing the apartment to a prospective renter?

I think she probably means the broker who sponsors her license.  In NY State there are two types of real estate licenses - broker and salesperson.  A person with a salesperson's license must be sponsored by a broker (usually an entity like Century 21 of Halvatis Realty) in order to practice in this state.  Once you've held the license for (I believe) a year you can complete the continuing education and apply for a broker's license (which is helpful if you want to go out on your own but opens a whole can of worms from a liability standpoint if you being to sponsor other people).  Since it seems that Marlene has her salesperson's license sponsored by a broker she has to give a portion of every commission to the broker.   

I know this because I work in commercial real estate and my company sponsored my salesperson's license when I got it.

Who would "your broker" be in this situation?  What service would you provide as a rental broker, aside from showing the apartment to a prospective renter?

Hello to all:

My broker is C21.  They get a cut of my commission.  If and when I become an independent broker, I would have to get error and omissions insurance, a website, pay for Multiple Listing Service, and the list of expenses goes on.  Note that all of these expenses are exorbitant.  For the moment as a C21 Realtor, they pay for everything except, calls, emails, and a portion of my MLS dues.  Thus the reason I continue my affiliation with C21.

Regarding rentals: 
I did a few rentals in the beginning of my real estate career and it was quite exhausting, as you do have to show many units, and the compensation is nominal.  It is rare that a person will look at one unit and rent it immediately.  Therefore, I choose to sell co-ops. 

Although, the rental agents get paid in a few weeks after the lease and checks are distributed, I choose to get paid every three to six months, and that is if the buyer is board approved and there is a closing.  And that is indeed my choice.

In addition to the above, there is usually an application which needs to be reviewed by the Realtor prior to giving it to management or a landlord, whichever the case may be.  If the application is not completed accurately, there may be a rejection.  So it is in the best interest to all parties that the Realtor is efficient.  This makes the transaction move smoothly.

Some management companies will do the credit check, some management companies may request that the Realtor do the credit check and submit that document with the application.  If the Realtor does the credit check that may be an expense which gets picked up by the Realtor.

So although there seems to be a sense that not much is involved, it really depends on the Realtor that you work with. 

Broker's
There are many broker's.  I use a broker when I need to secure a mortgage, I use a broker when I need to transfer funds from one account to another, I use a broker when I purchase life insurance, I use a broker when I need to secure health insurance, I use a broker when I need to make travel arrangements, I use a broker when I need to make hotel arrangements, and the list goes on.  So there are many broker's out there in various industries, and they come in all different shapes, types, colors and forms.  And like it or not they all get a commission cut and that money comes right out of your pocket.
