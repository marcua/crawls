The Atkins Schmaltz Diet

Before we start, there are some variations in ingredients because of
the various types of Jewish taste (Polish Litvack and Gallizianer).

Just as we Jews have six seasons of the year (winter, spring, summer,
fall, the slack season, and the busy season), we all focus on a main
ingredient which, unfortunately and undeservedly, has disappeared
from our diet. I'm talking, of course, about SCHMALTZ (chicken fat).

SCHMALTZ has, for centuries, been the prime ingredient in almost every
Jewish dish, and I feel it's time to revive it to its rightful place in
our homes. (I have plans to distribute it in a green glass Gucci bottle
with a label clearly saying: "low fat, no cholesterol, Newman's Choice,
extra virgin SCHMALTZ." (It can't miss!)

Let's start, of course, with the "forshpeiz" (appetizer). Gehockteh leiber
(chopped liver) with SCHMALTZ is always good, but how about something more
exotic for your dear ones, like boiled whitefish in yoyech (soup) which
sets into a jelly form, or "gefilteh miltz" (stuffed spleen), in which
the veins are removed (thank God), and it is fried in (you guessed it)
SCHMALTZ, bread crumbs, eggs, onions, salt and pepper.

Love it! How about stewed lingen (lungs) - very chewy -- or gehenen (brains)
-- very slimy. Am I making your mouth water yet? Then there are (grebenes --
pieces of chicken skin, deep fried in SCHMALTZ, onions and salt until crispy
brown (Jewish bacon). This makes a great appetizer for the next
cardiologist's convention.

Another favorite, and I'm sure your children will love it, is pe'tcha
(jellied calves' feet). Simply chop up some cows' feet with your hockmesser
(handl-chopper), add some meat, onions, lots of garlic, SCHMALTZ again, salt
and pepper, cook for five hours and let it sit overnight. You might want to
serve it with oat bran and bananas for an interesting breakfast (just
joking).

There's also a nice chicken fricassee (stew) using the heart, gorgle (neck),
pipick (a great delicacy, given to the favorite child, usually me), a
fleegle (wing) or two, some ayelech (little premature eggs) and other
various chicken innards, in a broth of SCHMALTZ, water, paprika, etc.

We also have knishes (filled dough) and the eternal question, "Will that be
liver, beef or potatoes, or all three?"

Other time-tested favorites are kishkeh, and its poor cousin, helzel
(chicken or goose neck). Kishkeh is the gut of the cow, bought by the foot
at the kosher butcher. It is turned inside out, scalded and scraped. One end
is sewn up and a mixture of flour, SCHMALTZ, onions, eggs, salt, pepper,
etc., is spooned into the open end and squished down until it is full. The
other end is sewn and the whole thing is boiled. Yummy!

My personal all-time favorite is watching my Zayda (grandpa) munch on boiled
chicken feet. Try that on the kinderlach (children) tomorrow.

For our next course we always had chicken soup with pieces of yellow-white,
rubbery chicken skin floating in a greasy sea of lokshen (noodles), farfel
(broken bits of matzah), arbiss (chickpeas), lima beans, pietrishkeh,
tzibbeles (onions), mondlech (soup nuts), kneidlach (dumplings), kasha
(buckwheat groats), kliskelech and marech (marrow bones).

The main course, as I recall, was either boiled chicken, flanken, kackletten
(hockfleish--chopped meat), and sometimes rib steaks, which were served
either well done, burned or cremated. Occasionally we had barbecued liver
done to a burned and hardened perfection in our own coal furnace.

Since we couldn't have milk with our meat meals, beverages consisted of
cheap soda (Kik, Dominion Dry, seltzer in the spritz bottles) or a glezel
tay (glass of hot tea) served in a yahrzeit (memorial candle) glass and
sucked through a sugar cube held between the incisors.

Desserts were probably the only things not made with SCHMALTZ, so we never
had any. Momma never learned how to make SCHMALTZ Jell-O.

Well, now you know the secret of how I've grown up to be so tall, sinewy,
slim and trim, energetic, extremely clever and modest, and if you want your
children to grow up to be like me, you're in gohnsen meshuggah (completely
nuts)!

ZEIT MIR GEZUNT (go in good health)... and order out Chinese

Hello everyone - I am the Rabbi at Congregation Sons of Israel - 33-21 Crescent - The "Little" Shule.  I have been here 26 years.
A congregant found this web board and asked me to reply to some of your concerns.  There are three synagogues left in LIC and Astoria.  All synagogues in the area were at one time Orthodox except for Astoria Center of Israel.  Mishkan Israel (defunct), Beth-El (defunct), and Beth Jacob (defunct) all fell into Sons of Israel.  The only other synagogue left is Adath Israel which recently sold its building and will be joining Sons of Israel after renovations are complete.  (If any of you have talents in colors and matching flooring and paneling, etc, please get in touch with us).
Our synagogue stopped selling Aliyot (We only did it on the High Holy Days because there were so many people that it was the only way to determine who wanted and respected the honor the most) years ago.  I stopped it because people were offering $10.00 and 18.00 to be called up to the Torah which I thought was embarrassing to the Torah.  Now, Aliyot are given out to the most active people and regular daveners.
We have an interesting mix of Congregants.  More of the congregation is young now than old.  We have Mexican Jews, Morranos, Puerto Rican Jews, Texan, Guatemalan, and Filipinos.  We had a young man from Tenn who just made Aliyah to Neve Tzuf with his Filipino wife.
These are really great people and lots of fun.  We have lunch together every Shabbat and some people stay to schmooze as late as 3:00 - 4:00 in the afternoon.
Dues are $100.00 a year but people pay what they can.  If you cannot pay - no one here will ask you for money or tell you that you cannot daven.  A young lady, who owns ERETZ foods on Columbus and 93rd in Manhattan supplies the food for Shabbat.  We have a kiddush fund and we try to pay her out of that but I think she takes a loss every week.  She is extremely generous.  If you stop by her store - ask for Yaela and you can meet her.
I don't know why the word "Orthodox" scares away so many people.  It's not a dirty word.  We call ourselves traditional.  Everyone is welcome to come.  Last week a young couple came by and wanted to sit together.  We gave them two seats just where the mechitza ends so that they would be right next to each other.  I would say that most of the people in the Synagogue are working towards becoming more observant but have not found their way yet.  A lot of them become more observant as times passes by.  You will find that you do not have to be a Sabbath observer or always eat kosher to be welcome in this Synagogue.
We have a large Sukkah which gets filled pretty much all the time.
Why don't some of you come this Friday night for the last meal in the Sukkah.  Meet the people and enjoy a quiet evening.  Looking forward to seeing you.
If you have any questions, please feel free to contact me.
Rabbi Jay
