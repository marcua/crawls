A shady real estate broker who operated his Astoria firm until 2011 despite being convicted of identity theft is finally in the big house.
Phivos Ioannou, 50, surrendered to the feds on March 1 and was stripped of his real estate license after being charged with bank fraud, according to court papers and his attorney.
The news was welcomed on Wednesday by City Councilman Peter Vallone Jr. (D-Astoria), who said his office has fielded numerous complaints about Ioannou over the years.
“This is a bad guy,” Vallone said. “He’s a piece of trash with nine lives.”
“I think the people of Astoria should probably throw a party,” he added.
The charges against Ioannou included using a credit card terminal at the real estate agency to fraudulently charge an AmEx card belonging to the FBI.
Ioannou pleaded guilty to conspiracy to commit bank fraud in April and was sentenced to two years behind bars and three years parole. He reported to a low-security prison in the Allenwood Federal Correctional Complex in White Deer, Pa.
“By pleading guilty he accepted responsibility and he’s looking forward to moving on with his life,” said Ioannou’s attorney Jeffrey Pittell.
Ioannou was twice featured on Fox 5 News’ “Shame, Shame, Shame” segment. Dozens of commenters on various online forums complained about his shady dealings, including taking deposits without delivering the promised services and using false advertising to lure in clients.
“He tricked my family knowing I had a baby and needed somewhere to live,” wrote one commenter on Yelp.com, a business review site.
Court papers show Ioannou operated Evan’s Corner Realty at the corner of 31st Ave. and 38th St. until 2011.
A staffer interviewed Wednesday at the firm where Ioannou worked after that — Astoria Home Realty, located at the former site of Evan’s Corner Realty — said the business will go on without Ioannou.
“He was a salesperson here — no longer is,” the employee said before asking the Daily News to leave the store.
Pittell asked Judge Deborah Batts for a non-jail sentence, so Ioannou could continue to work and support his wife and two young children.
“While working in the real estate business, Mr. Ioannou met people who engaged in unsavory business practices,” his attorney wrote in a sentencing memorandum to the judge. “They solicited him and taught him how to become involved in various scams.”
This wasn’t Ioannou’s only run-in with the law.
In 2009 he pleaded guilty to a reduced charge of petit larceny after using a client’s identity to rack up $50,000 in debt. He was sentenced to three years probation.
