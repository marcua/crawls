Meditations on American Idol
« on: January 16, 2007, 09:14:27 PM »
So, American Idol is back as of tonight and got me to thinking.  Why do I watch this blaring example of the wasteland that is "American culture"?  I'm a professional musician with a degree in music performance.  What is it about this show that keeps bringing me back year after year to watch this mind-numbing epitome of mediocrity?  The only thing I can conclude is that it's like watching a train wreck.  Utterly disturbed but can't avert my eyes due out of morbid curiosity.  Watching the hordes of misguided wannabes who never had the benefit of someone telling them they have little or no talent.  And the worst part of this show is what happens to good singers.  Seeing singers that truly have potential being molded into consumer products by having any ounce of individual identity sucked dry.  The songs they choose and are forced to sing reduce them to just another sound-alike crappy pop singer.  In the long run, it seams that it would be beneficial to NOT win the show so you aren't pigeon holed into singing the worst example of bubble gum, over the top, ultra overly dramatic crap these assembly-line song factories can come up with.

And to touch on the absolute no talent ego cases...
How do these people get through their entire lives without a single person telling them they can't sing?  And how can they be so tone deaf that they don't know they suck?  And how can they get upset when someone is finally willing to tell them the truth?  Do we now live in a society that is so overly protective of people's feelings that we can't tell them they aren't good at something?  It's good to have goals.  But some goals are just not realistic.  Sometimes it's best to not patronize someone and give them false hope because the fall can be hard for those that are unprepared to hear the truth.

Hahaha.  A girl just sang, "If I were king of the forest" and thinks she should get a shot because she's "unique."  Poor stupid child.

What ever happened to hard work?  As other musicians on this board can attest to, talent isn't a "gift" in most cases.  It takes hard work and dedication.  This is a point lost on most of these singers.  Practice is what makes a musician great.  Not by being "unique."  Uniqueness comes naturally and isn't something that is conciously achieved.

OK.  Enough for now.  Happy new season of American Idol!!!

Quote from: megc on January 17, 2007, 11:08:39 AM
I'd rather watch American Idol than football!  :D

Heh -- I'll take football any day, and I hate football.

But seriously, in terms of wanting to be famous, I find the trend that American Idol is contributing to disturbing.  There's an interesting article in this week's Newsweek (link to article) about American Idol tryout junkies who have tried out close to a dozen times, using all their money to travel all around the country to do so.  The article quotes a recent (unidentified) poll as finding that "31 percent of teenagers believe they will be famous when they grow up."  I don't have time to find hard data that backs up my belief that this number is higher than it used to be, but I found a couple of articles that indicate it might be true.

Here is an article from USA today that cites a Pew telephone poll finding that 51% of American Gen Y-ers want to be famous, and 81% wanted to be rich.

That does contrast with polls from previous decades:

Quote
In an annual survey of college freshmen by the Higher Education Research Institute at the University of California-Los Angeles, 2005 data show that money is much more on their minds than in the past. The percentage who say it is "essential" or "very important" to be "very well off financially" grew from 41.9% in 1967 to 74.5% in 2005; "developing a meaningful philosophy of life" dropped in importance from 85.8% in 1967 to 45% in 2005.

In other words, being rich (or well-off financially) and developing a meaningful philosophy of life have essentially traded places.  I find this disturbing, and I think American Idol is one piece of the culture that is encouraging it.

Re: Meditations on American Idol

Quote from: Jtrane on January 17, 2007, 12:19:51 PM
Things like Myspace, reality TV in general, etc...  I think these are the causes.  It's so easy now to claim your little piece of the pie through social networking websites.  It breeds a greater desire for fame.

Yes, but the Newsweek article certainly posits a link between American Idol and sites like MySpace and YouTube:

Quote
For young people raised in the era of YouTube, bloggers and reality TV, anyone can become a star, and if you're not at least trying to get in the game, you're a loser. According to a new book, "Fame Junkies" by Jake Halpern, 43.4 percent of teenage girls said their No. 1 career goal was celebrity assistantjust being close enough to smell the red carpet has become its own reward. "Anybody can be famous now," says Paula Abdul. "It's like a disease."

The book mentioned in that quote seems to be right on target, so I googled the author.  Here is his website, and here is an excerpt of the book for an article in Psychology Today.  The whole article is interesting, but these two paragraphs stood out:

Quote
What explains the fact that so many American teenagers are so hungry for recognition, fame, and adulation? Oddly enough, part of the blame lies with American educators, who have been embracing self-esteem-building programs since the early 1970s. One popular program, called Magic Circle, requires one child a day to be given a badge that says i'm great. The other children then take turns praising the great child and eventually, these compliments are written up and given to the child for posterity. Programs like this were intended to make young people feel better about themselves, but many educators now concede that they may have overshot the mark and fostered a culture of narcissism among American youth.

According to psychologist Keith Campbell at the University of Georgia , American teenagers exhibit much higher levels of narcissism and entitlement than older Americans and teenagers from other parts of the world. Campbell fears that in Americawith TV talent shows like America Idol and celebrity parades such as Entertainment Tonight making fame seem readily availableteenagers' sense of entitlement and narcissism will grow and fester. Adolescents everywhere may have delusions of grandeur, but in America these fantasies don't seem so farfetched. The problem is that teenagers are encouraged to adopt the self-centered mindset of prima donnasconstantly looking to be admiredbecause they believe their own fame is imminent.

I find it interesting that he traces the genesis of this to the early 1970s.  That fits in with enigmacat's observations that it's been going on longer than MySpace et al. -- which makes sense, of course; American Idol itself is older than MySpace and YouTube!  I definitely intend to take a closer look at his book, as well as his research.

Quote from: megc on January 17, 2007, 12:36:12 PM
Also, the people that were surveyed for the UCLA study are college freshmen, which is age 18.

Yes, but they're comparing like with like.  18 year olds in 1967 had far different aspirations than 18 year olds in 2005.  That is significant and does not require looking at their responses to similar questions when they are older.  And while I don't necessarily disagree with you that it's possible to be rich and famous and have a meaningful philosophy of life at the same time, I think the whole point of this whole thread and the various links demonstrate that many people either don't care or don't think the issue through enough.  The issue here isn't what you think or what I think, it's what young Americans think.

My reference to all of this has nothing to do with individual performances and the easy ability to say "you suck" from the comfort of my living room, btw.  I'm taking a broader view of this issue than that.
