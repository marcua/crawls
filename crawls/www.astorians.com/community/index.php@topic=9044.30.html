Interesting post Gleason – thanks for the serious comment. I’d like to start with a few questions if I may:

First: Who is the “we” you refer to when you state: “we don't quote much from real estate shills.”

--Do you represent a specific organization?
--If not an organization, who empowered you to speak for them?
--Are you the actually the Queens Crapper in disguise?

Though I enjoy the anonymity of a screen name I can say I speak only for myself when I post a comment on this or any other website.


Second: Do you think you’re the only person on astorians.com who also read Astoria- and Queens-centric blogs and websites?

I’m sorry but I agree with Merm. I don’t see the point of simply posting a link without adding a comment as to why you think it’s important for people to click on it and read the content.

Also, if you want to call attention to a something by posting a link fine but I find your attitude that we’re all ignorant and need to be educated very condescending.


Third: When you say “It's about time that Astoria joins in with the rest of the city in this discussion.” Who are exactly the Astoria to which you are refer to? Are you making the argument that most of civic groups in Astoria are pro-development? or that they are uncaring or uninformed? Or what?

Thanks for mentioning Queens Civic Congress. I’m not involved with the organization but I know of them and some of the good things they do. Here’s a link to their website:
http://www.queensciviccongress.org/

From what I can see there aren’t many groups from Astoria represented so maybe that’s what your beef is. If that’s the case it would be helpful if you were more specific instead of painting everyone in the neighborhood with the same brush and labelling them as ignorant, unconcerned, or uncaring.

As requested, in the spirit of generating discussion, the link to QCC’s platform for 2007–2008 is posted: http://www.queensciviccongress.org/platform/CIVIC2030.pdf

People can read and judge the merits of the platform for themselves. As I understand it, QCC is a “big issues” umbrella type of organization. I read through most and here’s what struck me:

Land use and zoning: 56 proposals
Housing and buildings: 44 proposals
Community facilities: 22 proposals
Public safety: 17 proposals
Parks and recreation: 25 proposals
Transportation/Mass Transit: 22 proposals
Transportation/Vehicular: 12 proposals
Aviation: 17 proposals

Blah, blah, blah, blah blah. There are other categories and dozens more proposals and here lies the problem: there are just so many -- how can they all be comprehended let alone accomplished?

If you feel a majority of people are disengaged don’t you think one of reasons is that when they are faced with a barrage of issues like this it forces them to tune out and shut down rather then to connect?


And from questions I move to commentary:

I made the suggestion to post some “pro” links because you represented yourself as a force to educate the people of Astoria and I feel in the interests of a well-rounded education you should present information from both sides of the issues.

Yes, the developer’s websites are often way too funny…in regard to View 59, well, at least everything appears to be spelled correctly in the copy. But their descriptions of Queens Plaza tell me ahelluva lot more of what’s going on and the reality of the problems LIC faces then nearly 33 pages of proposals, written in dense “legalese” text from QCC. 

As I previously said, I read other blogs and websites regularly, particularly the Crapper who I think does a terrific service bringing crap construction to the public’s attention.

Some thoughts:

1. It’s a blog, not a newspaper – i.e., opinion, not fact. I sometimes think people read blogs and mistake them for journalism when it is in fact commentary.

2. Different people interpret what they read in different ways but it seems to me that most of the postings on queenscrap first deal with the way buildings look, and second their impact on the neighborhoods in which they’re built. For example:
http://queenscrap.blogspot.com/2008/01/470-days-of-astoria-crap-in-mere.html
http://queenscrap.blogspot.com/2008/01/fresh-pile-of-crap-meadows.html
http://queenscrap.blogspot.com/2008/01/crap-above-in-brooklyn.html

(and not to single out the Crapper, this applies to most blogs).

You and I differ will have to differ on this point. You put the infrastructure argument in the forefront but to me it’s about erasing character of my hometown first…I’ll worry about the lights going out later.

3. The lack of even a basic editorial policy on queenscrap results in extreme comments, which are often a free-for-all of negativity, ethno-bashing, and racism.

And this poses my biggest problem. I have no problem with a discussion, even a heated one, about what’s being built but all too often the aggressively anti-development crowd veers very dangerously close to, and crosses the line from, criticizing what’s going up to who’s moving in and I don’t like it and I’ll call anyone on it. 


Finally: Does anyone think they’ve actually accomplished anything by posting to a blog? No offense to this website, which I very much enjoy and think is a great resource for the community as well as fun, but I sometimes think that people who post to such sites think they are actually contributing to the civic process when in fact they spending a few minutes/hours gossiping with neighbors. I’m sorry if you want to make an impact you can start by writing a letter to your local representative instead.

A side note: believe it or not, I’m not a priss it’s just that I find the whole PC debate so nauseating from both sides. It’s always framed with choice of words, what can you say or not say, when to me it’s the context that matters. Anyway that’s for another discussion.

Again, yes, I’m well aware of the number of stories that newspapers have picked up from blog POSTINGS, which is not surprising given the cuts to local reporting staff on the papers and the rise of independent bloggers, who have taken their place as leads generators. It may be our definitions of participation differ.

In the online world you get to chat with many more people, which is a plus. The downside is that at the very same time you are connecting to a wider audience you are isolating yourself from them and it sometimes occurs to me is this a good thing? Sitting alone in front a screen instead of outside face-to-face? It’s something that concerns me and one of my “issues” for lack of a better word. (And I count myself as among those people contributing to the problem.)

We always hear about how busy people’s lives have become. Have people convinced themselves they’ve done something because they read a blog post (or a newspaper for that matter) and maybe joined in and made some comments themselves? I still think a letter or better yet a phone call or visit to a local pol, a call to or chat with police, attending a community meeting, i.e., making a face-to-face connection, counts more than making comment to someone’s blog posting.
