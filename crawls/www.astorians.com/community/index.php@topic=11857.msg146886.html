It seems like a big thumbs-up to such refusals, which would endanger the lives of any woman who *dared* to become pregnant, whether she wanted to carry the baby to term or not.

Mer, this is new definition of abortion as proposed by the draft I linked to in the first post:

Quote
any of the various procedures—including the prescription and administration of any drug or the performance of any procedure or any other action—that results in the termination of the life of a human being in utero between conception and natural birth, whether before or after implantation.

Because it specifies that before or after implantation do not matter, many kinds of birth control pills will be subject because it is possible that they can cause a fertilized egg not to implant.

One of the secondary medical effects of combined hormonal pills is that there are endometrial effects that prevent implantation of an embryo in the uterus. Scientists argue about whether or not this secondary is enough to actually stave off pregnancies. Many people, scientists or not, consider it to be a problem with the pills.

Because this is an effect of the pills, they could be considered abortion, should the new definition pass and science or lawmakers decide that the pills possible endomterial effects are pressing enough to warrant inclusion.

From a philosophical standpoint, it depends on whether we think of pharmacists as merchants or as people performing some sort of civil service. If they are considered merchants, just like the convenience store or McDonald's, they are entitled to dispense whatever prescriptions they wish. If they are considered to perform a civil service, they shouldn't have that sort of freedom. It would be like a cop arresting someone for something that they think is wrong but isn't against the law, or a hospital refusing to treat someone they dislike for whatever reason. Since we're dealing with people's health, I tend to think the pharmacist should not have the right to determine what prescriptions they will fill from a moral standpoint.

I've seen the economic standpoint in action when it came to my mom's cancer treatment. None of the big chain pharmacies were willing to fill one prescription she needed for a drug to boost her white blood cell count. This was because it cost $1500. Even though she had a letter from the insurance company guaranteeing coverage, the drugs stores wouldn't do it. It was a struggle for her to find a place that would dispense it. I don't know what she would've done if she couldn't find anyone to fill it. I thought that was wrong, too, by the way.
