I grew up in Beacon, born there raised there, family still there.  The Beacon you see now is not the Beacon I grew up in.  It's going through a major art revolution of sorts, or you could call it gentrification.   For someone whose grown up there, it's kinda funny to watch all the hubbub.  I do wish I had had access to the art when I was in high school, we only had art class, no film no video etc.  It's a beautiful city and the making of it into a tourist art mecca is a positive change, especially as far as image goes.  At the same time, Beacon's always been pretty poor, Main Street wasn't and still isn't always the safest area of town, and jobs are very low-paying.  Real estate has gone up as more and more city people can't afford Westchester, and Beacon's artsy makeover has made it more desirable, so there's a new influx of people (commuters to the city) and $500,000 mcmansions popping up all over.  Most people who grew there and do not work in the city end up living together and find it hard to afford homes or apartments, a one-bedroom can go for $1000 on Main Street and you may only make $10-15/hour at your job.  I wish there was more help given to the community to break the cycle of poverty that is so pervasive there (as in many cities).

I used to say I would never go back.  It was one of those places where you either stay, get married, and pop out the kids right away, or you move for something else.  But going back now once a month to see my family, it's so interesting to see all the changes.  All the nice restaurants and the cute little coffeehouses, all the art everywhere, it's amazing how it's changed so much.  I'm not quite ready to give myself a curfew by having to take Metronorth everyday but I don't mind recommending it to people now, and people actually have heard of the very small city I grew up in :)

Oh and I wouldn't say the train station is mere feet from the Dia.  Prepare to walk or hopefully you get a shuttle.  And there are hills involved to get to Main Street.

Oh, Daisy, allow me a little hyperbole.  ;)  But seriously, as I recall from my visit to Beacon late this summer, the two are very close, and quite walkable.  From the Beacon website, here are the directions:

To walk to Dia:Beacon from the train station, exit the rear of the platform on the river (west) side. Turn left on Red Flynn Drive and follow it over the railroad tracks to Beekman Street. Turn right at Beekman Street and continue to Dia:Beacon which is the next driveway on the right.

S'ok, I kind of understand once I'm reminded!  We stayed at a B&B at the tippytop of High St. (off of Beekman just north of the train station -- and High Street goes up a verrry steep hill), and our hosts looked mildly shocked when we said we'd just walk down (up?) to Main St. for dinner.  Well, we knew we were going to have wine with our meal, and we didn't want to drive.  We found it quite doable, as was most of Main St. before dinner.  We ate at OII (O two) and really enjoyed it (their website says they're currently closed -- I sincerely hope it's seasonal, although that makes me sad, too), because they seemed quite busy when we were there).

To follow up on what Daisy said about Main Street - from the perspective of an outsider, of course -- both ends of Main Street had lots of shops, galleries, etc., although the end towards the river (and train station) seemed to be a little more established (and the other end seemed a little more independent and funky, as well as smaller).  The middle between the two, however, revealed more of the old Beacon, and was nearly deserted, compared to the busy ends.  More run down, comprised of chain drug stores, shabbier shops, social services, low, '60s buildings with no architectural interest (while either end had lots of beautiful older brick buildings).  Jim and I walked through what we think was the beginning of a drug deal.  We didn't feel particularly threatened (as we minded our own business, and I've witnessed far worse in nyc), but it was definitely a different scene from the end towards the river, with all the shops, galleries and restaurants.

I don't want to give a bad impression of Beacon, though.  I'd really love to go back for a weekend.  I think it has a lot to offer.

Quote from: JustDesserts on November 02, 2006, 06:24:14 PM
If you guys were taking a day car trip, what's the one place you would pick out of all the ones mentioned and why?

Even though Beacon and surrounding area would be worth a trip, my top vote would be for the North Fork - heading out on 25 from Riverhead and visiting the vineyards, going through Greenport, out to Orient Point to see the far tip of Long Island and Plum Island, too, then back to Greenport for browsing in shops and maybe for dinner.  There are several good restaurants out there now.  One thing I love about it is that people who aren't from around New York have no idea that Long Island still has rural, or semi-rural and agricultural areas.  North Fork is really different from the rest of the island, and quite beautiful.

As for restaurants, if you want something really special and local, I'd recommend The Fifth Season in Greenport.  (I've eaten at all of the restaurants below except for Eon.)  The Fifth Season has a new menu every week that is dependent on what is local and fresh and in season as much as possible.  Of course, it's easier for them in the spring and summer, and I'm not entirely sure how they deal with the colder months, although right now is still relatively good as far as fall crops are concerned.  They are also a fantastic showcase for local wines. 

In Greenport:

The Frisky Oyster - the first.  Very good food, a little cramped.
Scrimshaw - fabulous dock location, tasty food that's sometimes a bit heavy/out of season, well-meaning but not always on-top-of-it service.
The Fifth Season - see above.  My top pick.

and in Southhold:

North Fork Table and Inn - former Manhattan restauranteurs (Aureole, Claudia Fleming from Gramercy Tavern), impeccable food, but a tiny bit disappointing.
Eon - only one I haven't been to.  Only a few doors away from North Fork Table and Inn.

Um, sorry.  I do always seem to go back to the food.  I had started to write up a whole dissertation on the other restaurants, but I think I'll wait to see if you decide to go out there.  I'm happy to do it, though.

Quote from: megc on November 03, 2006, 06:55:51 AM
What is the name of the place that bills itself as the oldest continuously run restaurant by the same family?

Claudio's.  It's quite the hangout in the summer -- especially popular with the cigarette boat crowd (souped up powerboats that are thunderingly loud on the water and usually driven obnoxiously), so it's not really my scene.  I suspect it's a bit different in the in the shoulder seasons, but I've never been there (although I have been to Greenport in all times of the year).  They are right at the base of the dock, and have a clam bar separate from the restaurant at the end of the dock.  They are closed in winter - December to April.

(Two other nearby seafood restaurants that are very good are Skipper's, a little east of Greenport, and the Seafood Barge, a little west of Greenport in Port of Egypt on the main road.  Almost across the street from the Seafood Barge is a new Cajun restaurant (I think it's called Big Mama's) that I unfortunately do not recommend, except for their dozen local (Pipe's Cove) oysters and a glass of white wine.)
