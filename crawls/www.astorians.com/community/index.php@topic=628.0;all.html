Murphy has to be obedience trained before you should allow him off-leash. You cannot do that in the park. Minimally, he should know sit, down, stay, come and stop. He should know that he has to keep you in sight at all times. Off-leash hours are a privilege that require a responsible owner and a well-trained, well-behaved doggie. If any of those requirements are missing, it may prove to be a disaster waiting to happen. Not all dogs are meant to be off-leash. You and your dog must know what to do, how to react or how to respond to another dog who approaches menacingly. Please enjoy yourself, but be safe and encourage your dog to obey the rules for his own safety.

I have 2 small dogs who do very well off-leash. In fact, one of them hates the leash. But, in the park, they stay leashed. That way I can control any situation with a BIG dog that tries to bully either of them. AND THERE ARE A FEW BULLY DOGS AND OWNERS DURING OFF-LEASH HOURS.

If he is a really bright dog, and a really good learner, you might consider using a clicker with the NILIF (Nothing In Life Is Free) Program. I usually don't wait to find out how bright a dog is before I get right into NILIF. I start a few days after I bring them home. I tether the puppy to me and we go from there.
Quote
The NILIF program is remarkable because it's effective for such a wide variety of problems. A shy, timid dog becomes more relaxed knowing that he has nothing to worry about, his owner is in charge of all things. A dog that's pushing too hard to become "top dog" learns that the position is not available and that his life is far more enjoyable without the title.

It is equally successful with dogs that fall anywhere between those two extremes. The program is not difficult to put into effect and it's not time consuming if the dog already knows a few basic obedience commands. I've never seen this technique fail to bring about a positive change in behavior, however, the change can be more profound in some dogs than others. Most owners use this program in conjunction with other behavior modification techniques such as coping with fear or treatment for aggression. It is a perfectly suitable technique for the dog with no major behavior problems that just needs some fine tuning.

Search for NILIF on Google and see how well it works. You have to be totally consistent, though. If you do decide to try it, feel free to ask any questions. Although NILIF has only been around since the early 1990s, the basic program is what most Police Departments and the US Army had been using for decades.

Quote from: mronda on June 04, 2008, 11:51:04 PM
I am practicing in the house with "come" - we've already mastered "sit," "paw," "make it," and "let's go." But "come" is not coming easy, even with favorite treats! And for me it is the most important of the commands.
What is "paw" and "let's go?' I think I understand "make it." Take less time with these commands. They are not very important, and you can always come back to them at a later date. For right now, Grrly, needs to excel at the basics: sit, stay, come, release and stop. For sit, stay and release use one type of reward treat; for come another and for stop yet another. Remember that the treat does NOT have to be food. It can just be the smell of a food product. And you cannot use it all the time or the dog will never work without it. Your objective is to get your dog to do what you want her to do, when you tell her to do something, and be extremely grateful for a pat on the head and a high-pitched "whatta good doggie." If you are having trouble with the "come" stop using the word, and try using only a hand signal. As soon as Grrly responds, put her into a sit, and then give the reward. Most dogs really want to please their owners, so show her who the boss is and take the stress off her by not allowing her to dominant over you. If she doesn't respond to a word more than twice in a row, go over to her and physically help her. One other thing that might help you when you work her in the house . . . keep her on a 16' training lead. Use the lead to get her attention and also to prevent her from ignoring you. Are you using a clicker?
