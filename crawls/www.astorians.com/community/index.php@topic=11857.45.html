I want to thank everyone for the interesting and very civil discussion going on!

I do not think that anyone in any job should be forced to do something he or she would be uncomfortable with, whether it's because of religious beliefs or otherwise. However, I also do not think that any individual should have the power to enforce his or her religious beliefs on another individual through the withholding or refusing of products or services. So I think pharmacists and cab drivers should be given a choice--either you fill all prescriptions or only work shifts with a pharmacist who will, perhaps taking a pay cut if the pharmacy would not normally have two pharmacists on the clock at the same time. You take passengers and all their belongings, or you don't wait in line at the airport.

I think it's a dangerous precedent that we would allow certain sectors of the community to refuse service/products because they perceive it as a "sin" (thereby judging the consumer). It could allow the creation of a nanny state which decides who gets what and why.

On the other hand, requiring someone to break their religious vows because of their job is also unfair. It could set the precedent for a state of non-religion, which does not jive with freedom of religion. It could turn into a situation where pharmacies will not hire Catholics, and that's discrimination. While I personally prefer that people leave their religion at home, I try to be accomodating as long as it isn't infringing on my life. People can judge, but they can't use their judgment to prevent me from living my life as I see fit.

Basically, it's a really sticky situation and I'm glad I don't have to be the one to solve the problem. If I were ever faced with a pharmacist who refused to sell me birth control pills, I would take my business elsewhere and make sure EVERYONE knew why. Sometimes the simplest solution to problems that affect the public gets voted in by the dollar.

Quote from: Sweeper on August 20, 2008, 08:40:47 AM
Are they really denying access to healthcare, or are they saying "you can't get that here"? Forcing someone to abandon their beliefs because of societal changes seems a bit draconian.
You added the term "procedures" to your list of negligent actions. Are you suggesting that a doctor who opposes abortion should be stripped of his / her license to practice if he / she refused to perform an abortion?

The reason they want to change the definition of "abortion" is because abortion is one of the few things that the medical community does not require a person to perform or assist with if they have moral opposition to it.

The problem with the new definition is that the expansion of the term would lead to many more situations wherein people could refuse to give service. A doctor could refuse to assist anyone who takes birth control under this expanded definition, if they wanted to. A health insurance company could deny antibiotics or chemotherapy to a sick pregnant woman if they wanted to.

I am not so much worried about doctors refusing--I don't think the kind of doctor you want to go to would do that in the first place--but what about health insurance companies using this new expanded definition to allow them to cut corners and deny expensive procedures on women who are pregnant, regardless of their intentions to keep or not keep the baby.

There are already provisions to keep doctors, nurses, anesthesiologists and any kind of health care professional from being forced into participating in surgically opted abortions. I in no way think they should be forced to perform surgical abortions. I think that a medical professional should be able to chose their field and no one is getting sent to put in their 6 hours at the abortion clinic. It doesn't work like that.

The problem is when "abortion" is expanded to include *ANYTHING* that could disrupt the pregnancy, either before or after implantation, it expands to include birth control pills, the morning after pill, possibly diet, smoking and drinking and, if you're pregnant, almost *any* medical treatment for serious illnesses (bacterial infections, broken bones, cancer) runs a risk of terminating the pregnancy. What would healthcare for a pregnant woman look like if her doctor, pharmacist, and, most importantly, insurance company, could deny her almost any kind of services because there is a risk of terminating the pregnancy?

I guess it just seems to me that the idea of changing this definition is a pretty big thumbs up to insurance companies to cut corners on health care and for pharmacists to dictate what prescriptions they will or will not fill.


Also, like purplecurlygirl said, I think it's pretty awesome that we're having this discussion without any whack or immature comments.

The problem with this whole "making everything an abortion" is that it also impinges on medical treatments for conditions other than pregnancy.

Case in point:  In Kansas, for a while, EVERY D&C was reported to the state.  Now, a D&C (dilation and cutterage--scraping out the lining of the uterus) can be used for LOTS of things, including a diagnostic biopsy, to cure uterine polyps, and to contain general bleeding. 

I know, because I'm home recovering from one that I had to get done.  Thank goodness the path reports came back cancer-free but I may need another one for more polyps. 

Now, what if I lived in the ass end of nowhere and some doctor thought that nobody should get one because it MAY be for abortive reasons?  Even if a pregnancy test showed up negative, there have been doctors that have gone on the record saying that ALL midcycle bleeding is psychosomatic (yeah, I guess my anemia from bleeding for almost two months was psychosomatic also, and the polyps they took out).  And no, a D&C is not the kind of flip decision that one makes on a whim, and schedules in between a manicure and pedicure.  It is an invasive procedure that requires follow-up and a trained practitioner. 

IUDs are also frequently perscribed for bleeding due to a number of conditions. So are birth control pills.  Many many women with these issues have to go through a series of staged treatments..ie BC pills, and if that doesn't work then a D&C, and if that doesn't work  an IUD or ablation.

The bottom line is that the anti-choice movement is all about legislating morality and punishing women, whenever possible, for expressing their sexuality.  This extends to limiting all reproductive health options and education--show me one pro-choice pro-abstinence-movement teacher.

NO anti-choice groups ever want to see more contraception.  They just have an abiding and lasting hatred of other people choosing for themselves something that they object to.
