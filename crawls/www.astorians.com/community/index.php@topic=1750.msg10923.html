You Got a Problem With Queens?
« on: March 12, 2006, 11:44:57 AM »
Funny essay on the Metro Opinion page of the Times today, by a woman named Barbra Williams Consentino. In shocking violation of Fair Use, here's the text. If you disapprove, please buy a copy of the Times (and read the fascinating essay in the Magazine about the ethical and legal issue of "wrongful birth"...).

Quote
WHENEVER our friends the Mosses say, "Let's get together soon," my husband and I agree, mentally mapping out the best way to get to their apartment on the Upper West Side. Do we take the car and spend $30 for parking? There's also the F train to the D to the No. 1 uptown. Or, of course, we can always take the R train to 57th Street and walk from there.

Why do we do this? Because after 25 years of living in Queens as geographic undesirables, we know that "let's get together soon" really means "let's get together soon — in Manhattan." Every time the idea of seeing another couple comes up, we can't help but sigh, knowing that we will be the ones trekking into the city to visit our friends.

It's not that we're bad hosts. On those few occasions when intrepid adventurers have actually crossed the East River, we've served them excellent wine and good food. We've entertained them with challenging games of Trivial Pursuit or penny-ante poker. In fact, those brave enough to visit have claimed to have had a good time. So why then are we so often the visitors instead of the ones being visited?

When asked, our city pals offer these explanations:

• "Our trip to Queens is longer than your trip to Manhattan." Is that in miles or kilometers?

• "The street numbers don't make any sense. We're afraid we'll get lost." You have a degree in mathematics from M.I.T., and you can't figure out that 64th Road is in Rego Park and 64th Street is in Woodside?

• "Between Shea Stadium and the tennis center, there's too much traffic." But the theaters, museums and tons of restaurants in Manhattan attract lots of people, most of whom take taxis even though it can take 15 minutes to drive a city block in rush-hour traffic.

• "There's nothing to do in Queens." Then why is there so much traffic? Queens has some of the best ethnic restaurants in the city, historic sites like the Louis Armstrong House and the Queens County Farm Museum, bucolic parks and enough shoe stores and clothing boutiques to keep a shopaholic content for weeks.

• "We came to Queens already." Oh, yeah? Well, we paid taxes last year, and I suspect we will again this year.

• "I grew up in Queens and going back there reminds me of my tortured adolescence." So, you'd rather be reminded of your approaching senescence, sagging derrière and imminent addiction to prune juice?

• "I'm allergic to trees." And the trees in Central Park are hypoallergenic?

As tiresome as the trip into Manhattan gets, there are, however, some good things about leaving our neck of the woods to come into the big city. We have the opportunity to feast our Forest Hills eyes on some urban wonders — grimy skyscrapers, scurrying rats the size of Chihuahuas — and enjoy the cacophony of city sounds: screeching sirens, clanking garbage trucks, pounding jack hammers. We get to eat city food, prepared in restaurants or take-out joints since few Manhattan apartments have kitchens that are large enough to prepare a meal in.

And I guess there's some good things about the fact that people seldom visit us. It lets us keep the great things about Queens a secret. Why let others know that our public schools are stellar and that Queens has the best moussaka in the city?

On that note, I've got a great idea: Let's meet for brunch in Brooklyn.

Barbra Williams Cosentino, a clinical social worker, nurse and psychotherapist, is working on a memoir.

Heehee. :) (Of course, my Brooklyn friends declined to come up for dinner last night...)

Astoria is exactly what is was planned to be.

Many, many years ago, when Astoria started to decline, the Steinway Street BID, the Broadway BID, the 30th Avenue BID and the Ditmars BID worked together in an effort to help control the area to retain the flavor that was originally intended.

Although gentrification had already begun—and YUPPIEs were moving in—had no-one stopped to understand what this community was becomming it would have ended up *just* a cultural stopover for the *better-off* young population. Astoria was a community of families. Not singles. Not even couples. Families. Schools, transportation, decent, affordable rentals, affordable shopping areas were the requisites.

Around 15-20 years ago, when Manhattan rents went sky high, people were relieved to find a small apartment in Astoria for half of what they would be paying in Manhattan. They had other options at that time. Greenpoint. Park Slope. Williamsburgh. They were ripping down some of those garish 6-story walkups, and all those beautiful brownstones were being gutted to accommodate 6-8 units instead of the old way which was a unit per floor.

Astoria used to be called the parking lot of Long Island. We used to be able to park our cars *without* driving around for hours or paying a month's rent for a garage! When the influx of the YUPPIE population showed their willingness to pay more for everything, the neighborhood started it's downfall.

What happened to the times when we knew we had to schlepp to Manhattan to buy books, or go to museums, or to a zoo, or buy ethinic foods? Why does *every* neighborhood.community have to accommodate all the needs of everyone? I really loved that Astoria was a bedroom community. That we had some fairly decent grocery stores; some that even delivered. That there was always somewhere to buy a birthday card, discounted, disposable diapers, a pair of everyday shoes, find a good doctor or dentist, walk to a few great playgrounds and a nice safe park.

I much prefer having a great place to live than having a place that gets crowded with people who don't respect *our* lifestyle.

Just my 2 cents worth on a crappy, depressing rainy Sunday.

BTW, my boys were given partial financial scholarships each year.

So, as I they tell the story now—they had the best of both worlds. They got a really fine education in their Manhattan schools and hanging around with their local friends they learned to strip a Lincoln in 48 seconds!

Well, yes, the schools here aren't the best. The school issue is far and away  the most frustrating issue in what's otherwise a pretty good neighbrohood for families. Overcrowding is a huge issue and the number of immigrant kids with limited English proficiency puts a huge strain on the system here. On the other hand, they're not the worst either. Lorrie is right that the problems with the NYC DOE are not unique to this neighborhood. In fact, Astoria schools are actually better than schools in most areas with comparable prices & rents. They're definitely better than schools in Williamsburg. So, sure, there are better schools in Brooklyn Heights and Bayside and the UWS, but that's really apples and oranges.

Some practical advice: theoretically, there's school choice throughout the system. (Obviously, this doesn't work in practice for every family.) PS 122 (ditmars &21st) consistently does well on standardized tests; Our World Neighborhood Charter School (35th ave and 36 st) is another good option;  PS 78 (in Queens West, in LIC) does have the kinds of parents with enough clout to advocate for things so they have some more innovative programs; a lot of people have found that their huge, ugly ,old local schools have some good teachers and are not so bad if you get involved. Some parents in the neighborhood send their kids to public schools in Manhattan. They either take the subway in or figure that paying for a private bus is still less than private school or manhattan rent.

Oh, but I'm so with you on the bookstore issue, Jennifer
