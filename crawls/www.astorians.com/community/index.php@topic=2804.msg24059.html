I spent 5 years in sales and marketing for a major book publisher and am a marketing/web director.  As mentioned earlier independent booksellers face major competition, declining sales, etc.  The most successful stores focus on a particular area or two.  Depending on the local population/foot traffic and you pretty much have to allow for additional revenue streams.  Coffee sales, gifts, dvds, etc.  You'll find the add-on sale vital I'm sure.  An example I guess would be a book store that opened in Park Slope focusing on childrenâ€™s books and parenting books because of all the yuppie families moving into the area.  Also, successful ind. stores usually develop a few key areas they concentrate on and supplement in store sales with a website.  In fact, for many stores, they sell way more product online then in person.  As far as Astoria, I'm not sure what to suggest, it would depend on the area.  Wifi is bad if you don't offer something for people to buy or require a purchase. People probably won't need to pay for your wifi service because it's likely they can latch on to a nearby store or apartment's open access.  People sitting all day reading/surfing don't necessarily buy anything when the research is done and you're left with worn books and magazine.  Stores like B&N can afford to do this as the huge numbers they attract bring in enough real sales and publishers let them return unsold books (meaning all those books they order knowing they will never sell, but use for display as well as those that are ruined by people using the store as a reference library).

 I don't mean to discourage you, just wanted to point out some things to think about. By the way, many websites, such as yahoo, allow you to set up web stores with as many as several hundred products very inexpensively and easily. You probably already use stores hosted by them and don't even know it.

GenXnyc makes several excellent points, although I will offer a slightly different view for some things.

An ecommerce capable website is probably not critical.  If you are selling used books, simply set up as a seller on half.com (aka ebay) and/or abebooks.com.  This is an inexpensive way of getting huge distribution on the internet.

For new books online, simply realize that you will never be able to compete with amazon or bn.com.  Setting up your own site using yahooshops or a similar service will actually be a huge timesink.  (jacket art, blurbs, navigation, etc)  Now, having a website is critical for advertising purposes, but you want to put as little effort as possible into this.  For a good, easy, cheap option, checkout www.booksite.com.  For the record, I am not affiliated in any way with booksite, so this is not a self-interested plug.

All bookstores have return options with publishers.  This is generally for credit only on future purchases, and may be at a penalty.  The books must also not be shopworn (as Genxnyc describes).  Do not view this as a get out of jail free card if you make bad buying decisions.  You will need to pay freight on incoming shipments, out going shipments (for returns) plus the labor involved in initially receiving the books, then pulling them for return, plus all of the accounting.  Returns are very costly for the bookstore, even if you get full credit.

Be careful on the discounts that you offer to people.  Discounting is a valid way of attracting customers, but each and every penny of discounting comes from your gross profit margin.  You will also need to be very, very careful about your budget in terms of wages.  Go to the American Booksellers Association and get all of their literature.  If I remember correctly, they offer sample guidelines as to what percentage of overall sales you want to spend on rent, utilities, wages, freight in, freight out, credit card fees, etc. 

Community events are a great way to build a loyal following, and hopefully some paying customers.  One thing to watch out for is to not be too obscure in your offerings.  As an example, most bookstores need to carry poetry, or else they loose credibility with their customers.  Overall though, poetry is not a category that sells any books. 

Sidelines.  Do it.  Coffee sales, I Love Astoria T-shirts, greeting cards, whatever.  You will make more money from all of these things than you will make from the books themselves.

Quote from: jennifer on August 08, 2006, 09:43:47 AM

2) Is it possible to sell used books on consignment?  That way you don't have to lay out any money for inventory.  Your "suppliers" come by once every few months to either pick up a check (say 40% of sold) or if they opt for store credit it could be a higher percent. 



Yes, but the recordkeeping makes this terribly annoying & time consuming.  Consider the fact that a book may hang around the store for 6/8/10 months.  The "supplier" comes by to check to see if it has sold, this takes a few minutes each time to check, etc.  Imagine keeping records on 30,000 different books, whoyou got each one from, when it sold, for how much, did the supplier pick up his money....

Most used bookstores seem to only give 10-20% of cover price in any case, selling the book for 50% or so of cover price.  Some stores will give an extra 5% or so if the customer is willing to take it in store credit.

It is far easier to just give cash/credit up front, plus purchasing stuff from estate sales and remainder houses.

Please, go for it! You have no real competition and I think would have lots of interested customers. The used bookstore suggestion is a less appealing to me than a nice, independent bookstore stocking new books, but honestly, I'd be delighted to see any ol' bookstore move in. Also, if you included a small section with non-cheesy cards and a limited selection of decent little luxury items (similar maybe to the vibe of Alphabets or Exit 9 in the East Village) for extra revenue, I think that would be welcome addition. There's nowhere in the area to buy that kind of stuff, at least not that I know of -- I feel like I have to get on the train every time I need to find a gift. But maybe this is off the topic.

Another option, if your space is large enough, is to consider holding events for kids in the mornings. It might not be your thing, but there are a lot of young families nearby, and very few activities for the kiddies, especially in the colder months. This might bring new customers in, and depending on the type of event you might even be able to charge something. Or, why not have a weekly storytime for toddlers during a slow time of day, and push the featured book(s) for sale right afterward? Or maybe organize some kind of book club that older kids can sign up for at the beginning of the season, purchase a specific collection of books up front and then invite the families "in the club" to come to a related little musical event or discussion about each book on scheduled days throughout the season.  I'm not sure how big a children's section you would like to have, but I'm just saying that people pay $20 a pop for silly 45 minute sing-along classes for their little ones, so why not cash in on that market to supplement the book sales?

Good luck, and let us know what you decide to do! Your customers are waiting...
