curious what the lifer astorians think - i grew up in flushing but my dad grew up here - i wonder what's gonna happen here and in LIC with all those luxury towers going up, esp near the plaza, and Queensbridge and Riker's drop off points right there, mingled with people that are gonna pay upwards near a million to live there. could be a dangerous mix.

I'm not a lifer Astorian, but I have lived in NYC and Chicago, long enough to know that there are plenty of examples where people who are so far apart in income live so closely together and exist, for the most part, in relative calm.

In Chicago, the notorious housing project, Cabrini Green was next to luxury townhomes and condos. 

Here in NYC, you can look at Spanish Harlem, where initially middle income professionals were pushed north and now, more wealthy families are moving in.  I have friends who live in the Metro North Houses and the East River Houses and they don't seem to have any issues with their new neighbors, although I can see where there might be some resentment. 

The same thing happened in the Gowanus/Carrol Gardens area.  There are two large housing projects in the middle of multimillion brownstones and $3,000 a month, 1 bedrooms.  In this case, I had friends and acquaintances in that area and I never heard them complain about being harassed by the "locals."

There are also examples of the very poor and the very wealthy living together in other sections of Manhattan.  Take for example, the projects in the back of the Lincoln Center.  They are fairly close to some of the most expensive real estate in NYC and perhaps the nation.  And then there's the Chelsea Houses.  In the last twenty years or so, the area went from seedy adult video stores and dive bars to high end restaurants and lounges.  The one constant has been the large development.  While there is some friction, the last time I hung out in the area, on both sides of the "tracks," people seemed to be getting along.

Of course, it isn't a perfect world.  A friend of mine who moved into a very nice co-op a poor section of Fort Green that was being gentrified.  He told me that several times he felt uncomfortable when he was going home or when he went to the local bodega, but he was never robbed or openly harassed.  And an ex-friend of mine who moved into one of those nice coops in SpaHa told me that a group of young kids followed him home and called him "whitey" and "cracker."  He also told me that the mother of one of the kids came out of nowhere and grabbed her child and made him apologize and then cursed him out in front of his friends.  That doesn't always happen, of course, because often it's the adults who set the bad example, but I thought it was a good story, anyway.

Having associated with both sides of the economic extremes (well, not the really, really wealthy), I think for the most part, people just want to live their lives.  There are bad elements on both sides and if the community is vigilant, the situation can deteriorate, but that's part of our responsibility as residents.

Sorry for the lengthy, perhaps OT nature.  I just thought I would address the previous posters concern.
