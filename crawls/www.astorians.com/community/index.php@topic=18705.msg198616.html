A key fee is actually very illegal (look it up) but a lot of landlords do practice it.  When I was living in a Pistilli property, he took a key fee of $100, meaning you get the fee back when you return the key.  I made them specify that the deposit would be returned to me in my receipt and the lease, and when I moved out, I made sure it was.  I also had to hassle them to get my interest on my rent, and after many phonecalls, this was also returned to me.

If you do give this fee, make sure you have a receipt and a written guarantee it will be returned to you when you move out.  And I doubt it would be $1150, that's just crazy.  I would assume it's $100 or lower.

Yup, it's illegal and high and sketchy, but basically equivalent to what you'd pay a broker. The supers in these buildings wield all the gatekeeping power, and there's no getting the apt. without the fee (they don't list vacancies on CL or with any agents).

For the several people that I know who've moved into these buildings, the fee was very reasonable since they would otherwise be paying a broker the same amount. And frankly, to get a spacious rent-stabilized one-bedroom in an elevator building for under $1200... if you plan to stay in that place for any length of time, it's a reasonable investment.

Other than the fee, it seems like the management (in these particular buildings) is good. I've never heard any complaints from the four people I know who live there.

And unlike the Pistilli arrangement, it's not the management company that gets the money here, but the super. At least you're paying someone that you're actually going to have an ongoing relationship with. Maybe that's more palatable than paying a broker?
