Daisy, I have always like your postings!

I got involved in the first Earth Day back in 1970, and have always read everything I could get my hands when it comes to environmental issues. Concerns on development is an outgrowth of this.

Over time, I have built up quite a network of contacts. The computer is fantastic, and a loose community of concern from around the city (and country) has developed. If you rely on two or three sources, or 50, perspectives on any issue will be different.

The really cool thing is we compare notes with each other. No one believes much of what we read in 'official' sources.   

My opinions are based mostly on conversations with people who actually live in communities that have waterfront parks. They are dumbfounded when they hear of projects like Silvercup. Some don't even believe me.

So, I suggest the good folks on this board urgently reach out to groups that are involved waterfront parks. Talk to them directly. Approach them with open eyes, ears, and minds. Form a group and go out to an event at the Brooklyn Bridge park.

Unlike most of you, I met with Suna face to face. He categorically dismissed all my concerns.

The proposals submitted to the community board, etc. were incomplete and vague to the point of uselessness. Number of stories, etc undefined. It hard to fight something slippery.

This final wrinkle of segregation just makes me gag, as it should anyone.

Yes I know one or two people will make millions. Any infrastructure support will likely take decades of my -- and your -- tax dollars to get into place. Frankly, I have better places to put my hard earned money. Making someone else rich ranks low on my priorities.

Now, on the other hand, lets put a value to one 5 year old spending an afternoon in a waterfront park. Now multiply that by thousands of people over decades. Anyone want to come up with a number?

Regarding your earlier note, I strongly support both waterfront parks and new housing, high-income, low-income, and everything in between. This city is gaining residents very rapidly, and we need more density if anyone aside from the very rich will be able to afford to live here. Of course we need to have waterfront access and waterfront parks, but those parks are not cheap to build and they're not cheap to maintain. I'd rather have a mile of park and a mile of waterfront housing (with a wide public bike and foot path along the water), then two miles of run-down, dirty and dangerous park.

Quote from: Gatorboy on July 16, 2006, 10:45:50 AM
If you want affordable housing, its stupid simply to build more and more (the infrastructure costs negate any marginal savings - its just a red herring to get the middle class to accept more building.) Look at the 70s. No building, plenty of affordable housing. Today, a building frenzy, and rents climb even higher!!!!

In the 1970s, people were leaving the city rapidly as crime increased and the economy stagflated. See this graph. You can't compare that to now, with very low crime and a slowly growing economy. It may be that the way development is going now is bad and needs to be improved. But trying to stop increasing density is not a good idea. Or do you just hate cities?

Quote
Housing becomes affordable only if you lower taxes and find good paying jobs. Taxes make a big part of rent increases, and the taxes in NYC are among the highest in the world. Thirty years ago, you could live on $12,000 in Manhattan cause apartments went for under $200. Jobs have not kept pace with rents.

Do you mean property taxes or income taxes or sales taxes? Again, comparing crime-ridden 1970s NYC with current NYC is not a fair comparison in any way. And just exactly what city services would you slash to allow city property taxes to be reduced? Police? Transit? Parks? Schools?

I believe that NYC's job growth has been significantly better than the country's as a whole for the last couple of decades, and that's to be commended. (The year after 9/11 is an exception, of course.)

Quote
Being cut off from the waterfront for one or two hundred years is a damn long time, folks.

Historically, until rather recently, people with money avoided the waterfront as much as they could. It was dirty, smelly, and filled with dangerous people. I'm as pleased as anyone that NYC is now finally embracing its waterfront, but you shouldn't be misleading about history.

Overall, Gatorboy, I commend your enthusiasm for waterfront access, but I think you are severely misinformed about both the history of this city and current realities, and I don't think you help your cause by making invalid comparisons.

July 19, 2006
Con Ed Urges Customers
in Northwest Queens to
Reduce their Use of Electricity

NEW YORK - Con Edison is urging all customers in the Long Island City, Sunnyside, Woodside, Hunters Point and Astoria neighborhoods of Queens to discontinue their use of non-essential electrical appliances and equipment until problems on electrical cables can be resolved. The utility is experiencing difficulties in this area and customer cooperation will help ensure uninterrupted electric service.

The affected area is bounded by the East River on the west and north, the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway on the east and Newtown Creek on the south.

Company crews are working now to rectify the problem. Con Edison has asked customers in the affected areas not to use appliances such as washers, dryers, air conditioners and other energy-intensive equipment and to turn off lights and televisions when no needed until the feeder cable problems are resolved.

The cable problems in the northwest Queens have no effect on the rest of the Con Edison system. Con Edison will provide updates to affected customers both directly and through the media as the situation warrants. The company is in constant communication with the New York City Office of Emergency Management.

Please contact Chris Olert, assistant director, media relations, at 212-460-4111.
