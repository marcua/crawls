30th Avenue and 14th Street and way, way west in Astoria, near the river.   It is also near the Astoria Houses, a public housing project, that is a high-crime area.  The Astoria houses are actually right on the water at the end of Astoria Blvd.  Vernon Blvd. goes right by north to south. 

It is interesting how they situated the public housing projects in Astoria/Long Island City.   They are "in" Astoria, not "of" Astoria.   The Astoria Houses are actually on a peninsula.   Two of the three sides are surrounded by water (the south & west).   Facing East at the end of Astoria Blvd. is a traffic circle that kind of separates Astoria from the rest of Astoria.  North of the projects is Newtown Ave. (if Newtown is still called Newtown west of 21st) and there is one block of buildings north of the projects, though it is mostly industrial.   There is a baseball field over there with a spectacular view of Manhattan.  There is also a bus depot.   

The Queensbridge Houses, hard by the Queenborough Bridge, are also isolated.   The southern barrior is the bridge, the western barrior is Vernon Blvd. & a park, the western border are industrial buildings, though those "luxury" buildings being built on Queensboro Plaza are creeping also close.  The southern border is more industrial buildings, a few apartments, some over priced stores and empty lots. 

I'm sure the locations of these projects were carefully chosen and the demographics of the projects are very different from the rest of Astoria.   There doesn't appear to be much integration (or interaction) between the people who live in those housing developments and the rest of Astoria.   Those physical barriors that were created are also a psychological barrior.

It is ironic when you think about what they are paying to look at Astoria from the other side of the river and you compare to what they are paying in the Astoria houses.

I've had two jobs in my life where I've had to go into the projects.   The individual apartments are surprisingly well kept.   It is the public areas where there are problems.   Give credit where credit is due because New York City has done a commendable job in maintaining its public housing projects.   Other cities implode their projects.   In New York City there are waiting lists to get a spot in those projects.

The problem is with the common areas.   The elevators reek of urine.   The smell is unbearable.   I don't know what type of human being urinates in an elevator.   And then there is the safety issue.   You take your life into your hands when you go in there.   In one job, they wouldn't let us go into the projects alone.   We had to have an escort.   Safety in numbers I guess.

The Queensbridge Houses actually have been much improved.   I was in there '89 and it was a real pit.  Like much of the city it was out of control.The locks on the outside doors were broken and anyone could move in an out.   I went back in '00 and the city pumped some money in there and cleaned up the grounds and fixed the doors.

I wonder what it is like to live in a place like the Astoria projects where you are part Astoria, but at the same time separated by race, class and geography.  There must be a feeling of separateness and not being part of the greater community.

I'm with Daisy and Duska.

I lived across the street from some projects in Morningside Heights in Manhattan for almost three years, and to be honest, my building might as well have been part of them except for what we were paying in rent (urine in the elevator, broken outside door locks, etc). The guys in front of the bodega WERE watching out for the 'hood, would help us carry heavy things, keep an eye on the kids, even stopped my roommate's car from getting broken into once. It was a little comforting knowing that someone I "knew" was around late at night if I felt unsafe.

The interior parks inside the projects complex were always full of kids playing and mothers watching all the kids, not just their own. They'd have birthday parties and picnics and other get-togethers, and a few times I was invited to join them as I walked by. There was a softball field always full of men and kids playing in the spring and summer, and the benches were always full of people watching them.

Now, it wasn't rosy all the time--I know of at least two murders that happened while I lived nearby, including one that was pretty gruesome (some guy hacked up his girlfriend and her kid, and they weren't found for a while). Drugs were pretty common, and I heard shots a few times. And the motorcycle racing would drive me bananas in the summer. But to make any blanket statement that projects should be built underground or that they lack a sense of community is unfair and uninformed.

I don't think anyone said that it was the idea living condition and that they didn't have issues like crime, drugs, poor living conditions, whether caused by residents or bureaucratic neglect.  Heck, even the rappers from the 'Bridge who made QB famous (NAS, Mobb Deep, Marley Mal, Roxanne Shante) have conflicted feelings about their environment; on one had they are proud of the area, but on the other they long to get out. But then again, isn't that the same story for everyone: at some point, many of us are looking to own a house or some sort of property and want a "better" life. 

I lived with a family in the Martin Luther King Houses in Harlem during the worst of the 1990's and I had many friends who lived in projects in four of the five boroughs. 

The family I stayed with had lived in the projects ever since they came from Puerto Rico in the 50's.  The matriarch was a strong woman who raised three children and tried to support them with menial housekeeping jobs that paid little and offered no benefits.  The patriarch was a salsa musician in Puerto Rico who was relegated to cleaning tables at clubs because black Latinos were not allowed to perform at the popular Latin clubs in NYC. 

The situation was far from idea, but when I needed a place to stay people who were considered the "dregs of society" helped me out.  I relied on their kindness and generosity to see me through some pretty hard times.  In the process, I became part of a community that I did not know existed because of my own prejudices.  I could walk through the courtyard or into other buildings of the development and feel safe and actually welcome.  When I got jumped on my way home from the 116th Street station, a group of kids from my building wanted to know who attacked me because they didn't want anyone messing with me as I had become part of the family. 

Anyway, the story is much longer (I think this is a bit lengthy as it is) and there were a lot of things that I witnessed that someone who didn't grow up in the environment could never imagine, but this idea that "the projects" and those who live there can be judged in a certain way, is simply wrong and it prevents us from understanding our neighbors.
