Money is a symbol of acquisition. We want to acquire things, lots of things. Is it our religion to acquire, or do we need instant reinforcement to maintain a behavior? If you engage in a kind act towards someone and don't get a "thank you" or acknowledgement, what are the odds you will engage in that behavior again, if the only reinforcement is a wonderful afterlife (that you need to wait a lifetime for?) Money is feedback that you are doing something right, though "right for who" is what we may lose track of.l

Acquisitions provide immediate pleasure and reinforcement. Therefore the behavior that was exhibited to acquire the item/thing/t.v, becomes stronger, reinforced, and is given another opportunity to be practiced. Religion, god, are things that require time, waiting, and patience, with NO instant reinforcement, no feedback that you are on the right track. Is it possible that we intrinsically are wired to need immediate reinforcement, rather than to wait. Evolutionarily speaking, you better acquire food while you are hungry or else you will starve. An immediate reinforcement feedback loop in order to survive. Money is the means for us to acquire what we need, but that has shaped into acquiring what we want or rather THINK we need. Money and acquisitions are easier to believe in and to strive for because they are concrete. We get it right away and don't need to wait. This may make "money/acquisition" our goal, not our god...it is what provides immediate reinforcement, that doesn't require a lifetime in order to be reinforced for a target behavior.

I don't know the statistics, but are individuals in a lower SES more religious? I feel like I have heard this but I don't have a citation. Do they follow a god as the money and acquisitions are not forthcoming necessitating another goal, another purpose? Do they find meaning in random acts because 'feedback" is necessary in order to maintain a behavior?

And frankly, where is the role of this in our political life? Do the decisions made by our elected officials that require some feedback loop of approval or disapproval by the people (because it isn't coming from a god that I am aware of) have a role in decision making in stem cell research and gay marriage?

It’s much easier to make my flippant remark like that then to support it.  There have been books written on this :-)

We’re a pretty rational nation, but the one central belief that we hold is that the more money one has, the happier one shall be.  We take this statement as an article of faith because there is no scientific proof which supports such.  What other belief system do we have in this country that is so pervasive? (Commercial consumerism perhaps?)  Our media is constantly extolling this.  This belief guides our decision making.  Example:

Bonded slavery did not end because people thought it was an awful practice.  Bonded slavery ended because it was no longer an economically viable institution.  Presently, wage slavery is an economically viable institution and it openly flourishes. 

True, every society has to make such economic decisions, however, in America we are less encumbered by other belief systems when making such.

I think it’s something we aren’t comfortable with because we feel its too pedestrian to form a core belief system around.  But its not an inherently bad thing and the institution of money doesn’t need to be defended.  It has some positives:  The “–ism’s” shall not end by government decree nor a liberalizing of society.  They shall end when they no longer make economic sense.  Money, more then justice, is blind.  An ideal that we seem to be working towards is that nothing else ought to matter except for one's own abilities.  These abilities are (mostly) translated into money/consumerism.

But it is a belief system, one which is not compatible with some of the other systems around the world.  It prevents us from understanding other cultures and I personally think its gotten us into the hot water we presently are in.

Evangelical Christians attack use of torture by US

Ed Pilkington in New York
Tuesday March 13, 2007
Guardian Unlimited

The uncoupling of American evangelism from the administration of George Bush gathered pace yesterday when one of the largest national umbrella groups of socially conservative Christians issued a statement critical of US policy towards detainees and repudiating torture as a tactic in the war on terror.

The National Association of Evangelicals (NAE), which represents about 45,000 churches across America, endorsed a declaration against torture drafted by 17 evangelical scholars. The authors, who call themselves Evangelicals for Human Rights and campaign for "zero tolerance" on torture, say that the US administration has crossed "boundaries of what is legally and morally permissible" in the treatment of detainees.

"Tragically, documented cases of torture and inhumane and cruel behaviour have occurred at various sites in the war on terror, and current law opens procedural loopholes for more to continue," the NAE said last night.

The strong alliance between Christian evangelicals and Mr Bush, an important key to his electoral successes, has been tested in recent months with the Mark Foley scandal over his attraction towards male teenage pages in Congress, and perceived corruption in parts of the Republican party. One in three white evangelicals voted for Democratic candidates in last November's mid-term elections, a rise on the 2004 presidential elections.

But Christian criticism has tended to come from the right of the Bush administration. Yesterday's statement on torture suggested a new determination on the part of the evangelical churches to detach themselves from the Republican party and stake their independence - even if it is from a position more traditionally associated with the left.

A leading figure within the NAE, Rev Richard Cizik, told the Associated Press that the statement condemning torture was not intended as a criticism of the Bush administration. But he added: "There is a perception out there in the Middle East that we're willing to accept any action in order to fight this war against terrorism. We are the conservatives — let there be no mistake on that — who wholeheartedly support the war against terror, but that does not mean by any means necessary."

The document on human rights quotes the Bible, Pope John Paul II and Elie Wiesel among other authorities. It particularly condemns the indefinite detention of suspects without trial.

"As American Christians, we are above all motivated by a desire that our nation's actions would be consistent with foundational Christian moral norms," the document says. "We believe that a scrupulous commitment to human rights, among which is the right not to be tortured, is one of these Christian moral convictions."
