unwanted mail
« on: November 20, 2007, 09:06:09 AM »
I was just having this conversation with a few friends the other day.  I get tons of unwanted mail.  If it isn't another credit card offer, it's a charity sending me address labels and calendars and all sorts of other things to guilt me into giving them money.  It's so much that I have to set aside hours of my time to go through it all and shred it.  It's incredibly frustrating and annoying.  I'm beginning to recognize the companies just from the return address on the envelope, they send the same things every week!  And charities, why do they have to send me all this stuff, I never asked for address labels, christmas cards, a picture of the baby with the big eyes so I can feel awful for not giving money.  I only donate a few times a year, and only to a few organizations, and not much money at that.  I'm not rich.  And just because you send me one calendar, and I donate $15 doesn't mean you should use that money to make me another calendar to send me, and then ask for more money!!  This is a complete waste of not only my time and theirs, but also a waste of paper, resources,  and money!  I don't get the business sense in this at all.  And how much money is spent by charities mailing out all this crap that doesn't go towards actually helping people?  It makes one not want to donate anything.

My real questions are these: How do I get rid of this unwanted mail?  I have heard that you can opt out of credit card offers by calling some number.  Does this work?  Does it affect your credit report?  How in the world do you get off the charities lists?  How do still donate to those few you want to once a year, and get them to stop mailing you again and again every month, plus selling your name and address to every other Tom, Dick and Harry?  Is there a stop junk mail list I can sign up on, that includes non-profits, charities, and the like?

I'm not against charities, and do donate where I see fit.  I'm against being solicited around the clock as if I'm made of money.  It's ridiculous, wasteful in every way, and incredibly annoying.  And does not make any charity look good.  I assume others have this problem.  Any solutions, beside writing angry letters to them all?

This is a good starting point.

It will take some time, but if you consistently call anyone who sends you junk mail before shredding it and demanding to be taken off their list, they will take you off. It may take a few months because they send their campaigns to fulfillment houses several months in advance, but it will work.

When you make donations to charities, include a note that forbids them from selling, sharing, or giving away your information and ask them not to add you to their mailing lists--this works particularly well if it's a save-the-earth type of charity. You could give them a junk email address and ask them to please direct any correspondence to the email for a greener solution, and then check or ignore that email at your choice.

Some people take their junk mail and stuff it back into prepaid return envelopes, or tape those envelopes to bricks, etc., to help give companies the picture. It's kind of satisfying, but ultimately pointless (it doesn't charge them more in postage, for example). You can also mark all the letters "Return to sender", which might help them get the point. Many companies specifically take letters that come back and remove them from their lists so they're not wasting postage.

As far as credit cards, you can absolutely 100% get off all pre-approved offers with no adverse affect on your credit report by calling 1-800-5-OPT-OUT, which will stop the mail for five years.

It seems like a lot of work, but it's worth it. I did this with catalogs this summer (no clue how I got on Land's End's list, for example) and I'm no longer getting stuff from any company I called then. I need to start now with some other companies who have mysteriously got my info, but it took me only about 10 minutes once per week--I saved all the junk mail to call about and did it on Saturdays, calling their sales lines for catalogs (they pick up those lines faster) and asking to be taken off the list. Then I shredded or tossed the junk and eventually quit getting the mail. Now if I could only stop getting OTHER people's mail addressed to MY address....

I'm going to pretty much reinforce what people have said here...

I work at a non-profit that does a lot of fundraising - that's how we function; it's our bread and butter. Some people totally love getting the calendars and notepads, etc., but some people are very upset at receiving this sort of thing. The key is to email or mail them a letter asking them to remove you from their list. It takes time to get taken off a list when a request comes through this way because it is not an automated process.

Unfortunately the systems that non-profits (and for-profits for that matter) are not perfect, and sometimes there are multiple systems in place. Protocol may also vary from non-profit to non-profit. You may be removed only partially or kept in the system but marked as "do not mail."

One thing I will point out is that if you email an organization via a contact form you have just added yourself to their database. If you did not opt out (most forms will say "Do you want to receive communications from us?") and do not want to receive email solicitations from them you WILL receive emails. If you can, email them at a real email address - they will be less likely to add you to their database (we don't unless we are specifically asked to add them).

I am of the mind that if I want to give to a specific charity I will. Since becoming an online marketer I have found email and direct mail solicitations fascinating, but most do not sway me. A lot of people give to these charities because they want to, so don't feel bad if you don't give to every one that comes your way.

And yes - save paper! It will be your own kind of donation, I think, if you ask them to stop sending you trees.
