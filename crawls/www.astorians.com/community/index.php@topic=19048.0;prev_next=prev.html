If they aren't changing electrical or plumbing they can actually do the drywall work.  At the very least testing the walls for Lead and or Asbestos is required.  If evidence of ACM or lead paint is present they must file for the work.  Most likely there is no ACM in the drywall (not impossible but not likely) but there is a good chance the paint is Lead based and the new rules must be EPA licenced to do any drywall work and if there is evidence of lead they have to file the Lead demolition project.

It is very common for firms not to file interior work that doesn't require major plumbing or electrical work....even licensed contractors.  If you don't feel good about it just skip to the next one.  Why would you bother to undertake the task in calling the city and possibly creating a situation for the contractor and owner (unless you are legitimately considering renting the place).  There is way too much over-policed activity in NYC...My only concern with the work is that the contractor is licenced by the NYC consumer affairs and the US EPA.  Don't get me wrong, if you truly have concerns that the work is done correctly so that it is a safe place to live I understand your position, but otherwise it will be seen as "looking to start some stuff".  Know what I mean?  Good luck in your apartment search... :mrgreen:

My impression is that permits are necessary when the basic structure/layout of a residence is changing. If you're swapping out walls, floors, fixtures, etc., you're fine w/out a permit. Once you're moving the pipes, doors, walls, you need a permit to ensure that all the changes adhere to building code.

A licensed plumber/electrician oversees the work to make sure that no permits are required, but they don't actually have to do the work or even be present when it's going on.

I am no expert -- this is the understanding I've gleaned from talking to architects, plumbers, electricians, and contractors as I do minor renovations on a house. We're having a bathroom redone, walls replaced, and the electrical system upgraded and none of it has required a permit. And we checked with our home inspector to confirm this before we started the work, just to make sure that none of the contractors we were talking to were trying to cut corners.

It sounds like the kind of work that's going on in the apartment you're looking at is probably perfectly legit without filing a permit with the city.
