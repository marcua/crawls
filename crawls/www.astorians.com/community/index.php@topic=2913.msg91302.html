I moved to Astoria in '87 and lived in the top floor of a three-family, owner-occupied home on 32nd between Broadway & 34th.    One piece of advice I'd give anyone, especially if you plan to stay in Astoria, is get a rent stablized place.   I didn't have any protection and back in '01 when the "Times" ran a big article about how wonderful a place Astoria was to live.    The landlords, of course, got greedy and the owner of the place I lived wanted to raise my rent by $800 a month (he was the third owner of the place since I lived there).  Rents went through the roof after that article appeared.   

I had to move and ended up in a rent stablized place on 34th Street between 28th & 30th, but wished I moved here  or at least in a rent stablized place back in '87 when it was easy and cheap (I paid $600 for a two bedroom place and got it the day I looked) to get an apartment in Astoria.

I had some interesting experiences when I looked in '01.   I specified I wanted a big one-bedroom apartment near the subway.    These clowns I went to see showed me places too far from the subway or too small.   Some of the stuff I saw was hilarious.   There was this one place that had this closet.   When I opened the closet I bet it wasn't even 6 inches deep.   I couldn't figure why they even bothered making it a closet and then what you could fit in there.   You couldn't hang clothes and the shut the door.   It was bizarre.

Another guy showed me a place right on 31st Street, a little too close to the subway.   The front window looked out onto the platform of the 30th Street subway station.    I said thanx but no thanx.

Another guy, an old timer on Broadway who got me my first apartment in '87, was still there at the real estate place.   He sent me to this place on 35th Street a block from where I am now and said "You'll sign the lease for x amount, but actually have to pay y amount."   What I would pay would be less than what was on the lease.   It had something to do with rent stabilization laws.

And the firm who got me the apartment was on the north side 31st Avenue between Steinway and 38th.   The broker was actually pretty good and told me he was showing an apartment "in the best building in Astoria and that if that didn't satisfy me then he couldn't help me."  He told me to come back the next day a Sunday when he showed it.    I get there and get in the broker's car and there is this other guy with me who is also looking at the apartment.    It was kind of awkward to say the least because both us were under the impression that we would be alone with the broker and get the apartment if we liked.   To make me feel even worse the guy was looking for a place to live with his father and the father required a building with an elevator.    This building has an elevator.    We get there and I got the apartment, but from what I understand they were looking at nine or 10 people and I "wasn't number one on the list" for the apartment.   I don't know if no. 1 didn't check out when they did the credit check or if they didn't want the apartment, but I ended up getting it.   

And did the woman who works in the real estate market lord over the poor broker.   Did he suck up to her.   She was a little tyrant.

The only way you can get rent controlled is if a family member passes and existing rent controlled unit on to you.  There are roughly 50,000 such units in NYC.

Since that applies to, like, nobody, it is safe to assume that any apartment presented to you as such is actually rent stabilized, which means that 1) your rent can only increase by a certain percentage each time you renew and 2) you have the right to renew the lease if you want, except in very specific circumstances (like the landlord wants to put his family in the unit).  See the Rent Stabilized faq at http://www.housingnyc.com/html/resources/faq/rentstab.html or, more generally, the entire site at http://www.housingnyc.com/ is pretty f-ing awesome.

If you are unsure as to whether a broker is telling you the truth about a building, call 718.739.6400 or 212.961.8930 to find out.  Please note that some buildings are overall stabilized, but individual units within may not be.  So if a broker says "the building is stabilized," make sure to get clarification on whether the unit is.

Quote from: Alexis on September 06, 2007, 02:29:40 PM
The only way you can get rent controlled is if a family member passes and existing rent controlled unit on to you.  There are roughly 50,000 such units in NYC.
Not quite true. In order for you to become the legally recognized tenant of a rent controlled apartment, you must first prove that you are related to the legal (now deceased) renter and that you have lived there continuously for at least six months prior to the death of the original renter.

Trust me, you don't want a rent controlled apartment. These apartments have no leases, the tenants are responsible for defraying the cost of fuel to the whole building, their rents, by State law, increase 7.5% (minimum) every year.

Whereas a rent stabilized apartment has a lease, and only has to deal with 3%–5% increases every two years. Many, many of those estimated 50,000 controlled apartments are now paying more than their rent stabilized next door neighbors who have new stoves and refrigerators and totally refurbished bathrooms.

I moved into my apartment in 1964 when the rent was $92.16. Starting in 1970 I have received annual increases of at least 7.5% and in some years as high as 13%. You do the math. My apartment doesn't need to be registered with DHCR, and they don't have a clue what my rent SHOULD be!

Rent stabilized apartments MUST be registered with DHCR and they will protect your interests if it is not. If you live in a building of less than 6 apartments you are at the mercy of the building owner. Check your lease . . . if it says that your apartment is stabilized . . . verify that with DHCR. Someone in my building did that, and discovered that her apartment hadn't been registered by our unscrupulous landlord. She filed for an overcharge, her rent was formally decreased by 50%, and she was awarded almost $40,000 in overage and damages. She will be living rent-free for the next 5 years!
