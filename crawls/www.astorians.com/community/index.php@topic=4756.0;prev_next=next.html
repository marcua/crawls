Quote from: alexthemusicdude on February 01, 2007, 05:13:25 PM
once you get passed the intial 2-3 weeks of nicotine withdrawl from your body, all it becomes is breaking the habits that you are used to smoking with. driving, smoke breaks, with coffee, etc. the way that i overcame it was simple. when i smoked, my chest was always very tight, it was hard to breathe, i couldn't climb the stairs up broadway to the subway platform without being completely winded and panting. as i quit, i made it a habit of if i wanted a cig, take a deep breath of fresh air and know that if you were smoking, you wouldn't be able to fully enjoy that full breath. i still use that to this day when i get a craving. just take a minute. clear your head and take a few deep breathes, concious that if you smoke you wont feel as good  as it does when you're smoking.

Yeah, I found for me that it was harder to break the habit. I honestly don't think I was physically addicted. I would find myself instinctually leaning toward the door (to go outside for a cigarette), but once I realised what I was doing, I was able to decide not to smoke. (I would go out because I was buddies with the guys who worked out back at the ballpark...honestly, the 5-minute chill breaks where what I missed most). I don't really remember any physical withdrawl symptoms, but I went out to a bar for a work party 2 days after I quit, and this was before the no smoking in bars in CT. So every time I wanted to light up, and EVERY FREAKING PERSON AROUND ME WAS SMOKING, I had another drink. Needless to say, the day after was a bit rough.

Part of the reason I quit was because I noticed that I would kind of wheeze while breathing (ok, it was really more of a squeak, but it was still something that I couldn't control) when I laid on my back.
