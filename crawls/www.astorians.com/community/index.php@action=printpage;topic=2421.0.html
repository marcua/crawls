Discussing beach access is a topic that has been kicking around ever since the first towns were set up on Long Island 350 years ago. Library bookshelves are groaning with dozens of books written about it.

The issue of NYC's waterfront is slightly different. NYC is planning for real parkland on most of the waterfront: not only BOTH sides of the Hudson, but along the East River for the wealthier areas in Brooklyn and Manhattan.

In other words, most of the city.

In direct contrast, it seems like those private development projects that are cutting the public off from the waterfront (and reserving it for the rich) is just planned for a handful of poorer areas: Red Hook, Greenpoint/Williamsburg, and Queens.  

Why? Look it up on zoning studies dating back to the early 1990s.

Is this fair? Ask our elected officials. Ask our community boards. They voted for it

The folks in Greenwich Village (http://www.gvshp.org/) have been doing marvelous work in trying to gain back their waterfront. Check out their work.

Unlike the 70s, funding is found to maintain these places. Go ahead, walk along the existing waterfront parks in Brooklyn and Manhattan. They are stunning!
