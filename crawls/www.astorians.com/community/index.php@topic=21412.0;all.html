As a cyclist, it frustrates me to no end when I see fellow bikers riding on the sidewalks or going down the street in the opposite direction.  As some posters pointed out, the majority of offenders are restaurant delivery men - the taxi drivers of NYC cycling.  I can understand why they behave the way they do in an environment where time is money and they earn their living through tips.  Yet the behaviors of some in this group create hazards for everyone and leave a bad taste in the mouths of many New Yorkers when it comes to any kind of bike transportation. 
 
I'm not sure just how many cyclists are aware of the city's biking rules, but riding on the sidewalks is illegal for anyone 14 years of age and up, as pedestrians have the right of way.  Riding down a one way street in the opposite direction is also illegal, as is failure to obey traffic lights. 
 
Transportation Alternatives, which advocates for safe and appropriate biking and pedestrian traffic in NYC, has been running a campaign since last Fall - Biking Rules - to increase awareness of the city's code.  More info available at bikingrules.org. 
 
There are also several businesses in Astoria and LIC that are signing up to be "bike-friendly" businesses through a related effort being organized by Transportation Alternatives.  Among other things, deliverypeople associated with these businesses SHOULD comply with city rules.  Businesses that have signed up include Queens Kickshaw, Communitea (in LIC) and Aubergine (in Sunnyside) among others.  And the list is growing. 
 
Another way to combat unsafe biking, especially where deliverypeople are concerned, is to take note of offenders and refuse to patronize these businesses.

Avid urban cyclist and sometime bike activist here.


Cyclists definitely need to stop riding on the sidewalks.


When I first started riding in the city, many years ago, I rode on the sidewalks all the time -- out of fear. I'm not proud of it, but more people did it back then, in part because drivers, and people in general, were less aware of our rights to use the streets. I was always extremely deferential to pedestrians, choosing empty sidewalks, slowing down or walking the bike when it got crowded, etc., but whatever, it was still wrong.


I never do it now. You shouldn't either, for the obvious reasons mentioned above, that:


1. It endangers and/or frightens pedestrians.
2. It's illegal, and you can get a ticket (it happens).


But there are other good reasons:


1. It gives us two-wheelers a bad name.
2. It misses the chance to further accustom drivers to seeing us on the streets -- a key to making all cyclists safer.
3. It's actually MORE dangerous for us in some respects, believe it or not. Yeah, you probably won't get flattened from behind by a truck. But I have seen the studies: mile for mile, cyclists riding on the sidewalk are more likely to collide with cars, especially when crossing driveways and streets suddenly, when they tend to get T-boned. Also very unpleasant. Plus hitting a pedestrian can injure the cyclist, too. So can swerving to avoid one. (If sidewalk cyclists seem, anecdotally, to have fewer accidents, it's mainly because they are generally not going very far in that very limited environment. Kinda like, if you drive to the mailbox on the corner, it's pretty safe because you aren't hardly driving, but if you want to go to California, flying is much safer.)
4. You kinda look like a wuss. I'm just saying.


Anyone who wants to ride in the streets generally needs to learn to ride more like a car and less like a kid with training wheels, a concept sometimes called vehicular cycling or effective cycling. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vehicular_cycling. You have to be ready to NOT skulk along the curb or right next to parked cars, but rather to (counterintuitively) assert yourself and ride a little further out into the road or "take the lane" and claim a place in the line of car traffic (which is perfectly legal in NYC). It takes practice, preferably starting on little quiet side streets. But I can't imagine riding any other way these days.


As for bike lanes, some vehicular cycling advocates actually oppose them, because they believe bikes are better integrated with the flow of traffic. I think that separated bike lanes, done right, are appropriate on some busy streets, and painted lanes probably serve as a good general reminder to drivers, though I hope we can all just learn to share most streets naturally over time.


BTW, I think more mass transit and pedestrian improvements are very necessary, and I use both extensively. But for midrange urban/suburban journeys like many commutes, biking has a much lower carbon footprint per person than subways and buses, and the exercise benefits are tremendous in a country that's getting more obese by the year.
