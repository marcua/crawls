To answer your question: Yes, there is barbed wire, gates, glowing towers (smokestacks) and humming noises. But that area is also a decent, safe place to live, close to the park and the subway.

Your apartment will be on a residential side street, surrounded by other houses and/or apartment buildings up until 20 Avenue, which separates Con Ed from the residential area.

It’s not a mixed-use neighborhood like it is around the Big Allis plant in LIC. One side of 20th Ave. is all residential, the other all Con Ed.


If it were me, my biggest concern would be noise but most people adjust after awhile.  Some people would place pollution first but the whole neighborhood area is affected by that and it wouldn’t put me off from living there.

Good luck with your search!

I saw the post late and figured the OP had already made their visit.  I lived in that area for a bit, on Shore Drive right between 20th and 21st Avenue.  Did not notice the powerplant one bit, unless, just like Daisy said, I went over and stared at it.  Nice neighborhood.  Plus, though the powerplant is not a great thing to live next to, the amount of traffic is less over there, and traffic pollution is a bigger concern than power plant pollution, so I'd figure it was a wash.  There is a stinky smell that washes over from Ward's Island sewage treatment plant when the wind it right though, but it is bearable and infrequent.

caroslime, what are you quoting from?  I'd like to see a link.  Since you didn't provide one, I googled part of your quote.  I came up with this summary of recent EMF health assessments assembled by the government of Manitoba, with links.  The studies for the most part agree with this one from the National Academy of Sciences/National Research Council (U.S.), 1997 (link to full study provided in summary):

Quote
Based on a comprehensive evaluation of published studies relating to the effects of power-frequency electric and magnetic fields on cells, tissues, and organisms (including humans), the conclusion of the committee is that the current body of evidence does not show that exposure to these fields presents a human-health hazard. Specifically, no conclusive and consistent evidence shows that exposures to residential electric and magnetic fields produce cancer, adverse neurobehavioral effects, or reproductive and developmental effects… An association between residential wiring configuration (called wore codes…) and childhood leukemia persists in multiple studies, although the causative factor responsible for that statistical association has not been identified. No evidence links contemporary measurements of magnetic-field levels to childhood leukemia.

Bolding mine.

I believe this is the full California report from 2002.  Here is the executive summary of the report; the review did not "conclude that EMFs can cause some degree of increased risk..."  Here is the language from the report:

Quote
To one degree or another, all three of the DHS scientists are inclined to believe that EMFs can cause some degree of increased risk of childhood leukemia, adult brain cancer, Lou Gehrig’s Disease, and miscarriage.

Speaking scientifically, that's pretty vague language and not exactly a conclusion.  Moreover, the executive summary acknowledged that

Quote
the DHS scientists are more inclined to believe that EMF exposure increased the risk of the above health problems than the majority of the members of scientific committees convened to evaluate the scientific literature by the National Institutes of Environmental Health Sciences Working Group (NIEHS) in 1998, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) in 2001, and the British National Radiological Protection Board (NRPB) in 2001.

All bolding mine.

Yeah. I'm a scientist (although in a completely different field (haha)), and the thing about safety and power lines is that nobody can figure out why very low-frequency electromagnetic energy would have any effect on people at all. There's just not enough energy to mutate DNA, cause leukemia or whatever. There might be something else going on, chemical contamination or something, but there's just no obvious mechanism for power lines to be harmful. (Unless you touch them, of course.) It's certainly worth checking, of course, but the fact that there's little to no epidemiological evidence that any effect is real suggests that there's just no safety problem.

Cell phones, on the other hand, are a different matter entirely. The frequencies are high enough and the distances are small enough that there could be real effects. It's fortunate that no strong correlations with brain cancer have been found, but there could have been...

Just so no one is confused: the power plant with Big Allis is the Ravenswood Plant off 36th Avenue. The power plants located off 20th Avenue are not owned by Con - Edison, but Con Ed does own the property and the power distribution equipment. Also, almost all generating stations on the site burn either liquid fuel or natural gas depending on the economic conditions.
