City agencies, state agencies and federal agencies almost always want a letter from the original complainant, pictures if it's something that needs to be repaired or some proof of a violation occurring.  It's easy to just say they should be able to make things get fixed, but you generally have to have some sort of proof else the agencies don't do anything - I'm not sure if they could legally by their internal regulations and the Charter of the City of New York, let alone the laws and regulations at the state and federal level.  I've always found you get the best results when you research what the law is, document the violation or problem and suggest a possible solution.  You don't always get what you ask for, but you usually get them moving at least along the lines you suggest - sometimes you get more or less what you're seeking - it varies a lot by situation.

And the elected official's office is the one responsible for collecting the proof.  They are the one looking out for the neighborhood.  At least that's how it's supposed to be.  The pols have special intergovernmental numbers - they can make one phone call, give the location and get the problem fixed.  I had this very experience last week.

Sometimes - not always.  When I served as Chief of Staff to Councilmember Gerson, we'd go the intergovernmental route to seek redress for constituents, and when it works, it's great, but it doesn't always work and oftentimes you have to change the laws or really make a stink - push the agency hard to make them do the right thing for constituents. 

I wish elected officials had enough resources and staff to gather all the proof - that would make for a much better government.  My 7 years in government make me think differently though - you're almost always drowning in paperwork and requests and trying to do right by every single one of them, but the ones that come with the most back-up and clear goals/priorities always seem to get solved more quickly, not that the complex ones don't get addressed, they usually do, it's just a longer process.  I know when I was fighting a noise-polluting establishment here on Broadway in Astoria, Councilmember Vallone's office tried to help me for over two years and only when I reached out to the Department of Consumer Affairs were we able to get the break-though we needed (and then Vallone's office was able to use the DCA finding to really achieve results, letting me and my neighbors sleep again for a change).
