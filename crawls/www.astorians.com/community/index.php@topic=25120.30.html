It's actually a good suggestion. In a lawsuit, both sides always have their own expert, so I suspect if you look hard enough, there's probably a noise expert that can be hired for litigation purposes. I've not checked these boards for a while, but it's like nothing changed and Strand is still a major headache for nearby residents. Perhaps if the people in some of the nearby buildings organized and took up a collection, each contributing a nominal amount, they can pay for an expert who would visit the location at the time the noise is at its worst. At a minimum, those findings could be presented to the city to get them to enforce noise laws, but the findings can also be used to sue the owner.


As for the person who opposed the "gentrification" of the neighborhood, I couldn't help but laugh. I was born and raised here (I'm 43 years old) and the neighborhood has always been gentrified. It seems that a lot of native Astorians have opposed the shifting demographic of the neighborhood - there's a group on Facebook, "Growing up,Astoria" and there's a lot of grousing about the very same thing. What people don't want to realize is that's times change. Very few old time mom & pop businesses stick around because the owners die or retire and move to Boca. New businesses come in and cater to the neighborhood's needs. I used to go to Joe's, the newsstand that stood where the Brick is now. I'd ride my bike to the corner and get a YooHoo and candy. Joe sat inside his dimly lit store that smelled like stale cigars, leaning up against his counter - a grocery store shopping cart - and made change out of a cigar box and coffee can. That business would never survive in 2013 Astoria. It's a fond childhood memory, but as an adult, I'd rather head to Brick for brunch or dinner. And some of the businesses that people complain about have been here forever, but changed their business model. Athens Cafe on the triangle on 30th Ave has been there for ages - probably over 20 years. The expansion to take up the whole block, the outdoor seating, and the full menu are more recent developments. Much like Omonia on Broadway, it started out as a bakery, but turned into a cafe/restaurant. Both businesses are thriving now - if they were still just mediocre Greek bakeries in a neighborhood with a ton of competition, they'd have closed up years ago. Sometimes change is good. Strand Smokehouse may be noisy, but it's better than boarded up, closed storefronts that make the area look economically depressed. If it closed down and stayed empty, left to fall in disrepair and a target for vandals, people would complain about that too,
