Title: Re: Too Many Sushi Places
Post by: jenriet on November 29, 2007, 09:47:57 PM
Quote from: neojapanese on November 28, 2007, 09:24:48 PM
Remember sushi is raw fish and if it is not prepared right you can get really sick. Most of these guys leave the raw fish out in the open for a long time and use coloring or  MSG.
fake = bad

First, not all sushi contains raw fish. If one is squeamish, simply go the salmon route. It is never supposed to be served raw because it is too unsafe to do so (so it is a major flag if you see raw salmon on a menu in a restaurant), but is instead smoked, grilled, etc. Unagi is usually barbecued as well. Then there are, of course, all of the vegetarian rolls around.

As far as coloring, that is true. You have to be careful of places where the tuna is bright red - that usually indicates that it has been dyed, which means that the restaurant can get away with using older fish, which is not as safe, and definitely not as tasty. Also, given the amount of people in the US with allergies to MSG, it's getting more and more rare to find restaurants that use it in their food.

Proper sushi chefs know how to handle fish properly. If one purchases sushi grade seafood, stores it properly, and uses sanitized utensils that don't commingle with cookware used for other dishes in the restaurant, than there is no reason why the sushi should be unsafe. Granted it takes many, many years to become a properly trained sushi chef, but that's why you have to pay what you do to get the good sushi.  Watch the sushi chefs at the sushi bar - the chefs should only be touching the ingredients, knives, and a towel. Nothing else (unless of course they have a running tap back there to keep the chefs hands moist so the rice does not stick to them).

So all in all, yes, fake is bad. However, good sushi is not fake and that's what one should search for anyway. (Can you tell I'm a sushi fan?  :lol:)

Carbon monoxide preserves only the color of the fish, not its quality. Suppliers and retailers who use the treated fish say the process allows them to sell high-quality, flash-frozen fish that still looks good enough to eat. . . . But for most consumers around the world, vendors say, lollipop-red flesh signals freshness and quality. But "you could put it in the trunk of your car for a year, and it wouldn't turn brown."

When fish are farmed (as most salmon found in sushi restaurants is), they can manipulate the colour of the flesh by the colour of the feed.  For instance, that is why salmon are pink or orange - in wild salmon, it is the colour of the krill they eat, and in farmed salmon it is a result of dye they add to the food (but not to the flesh).  Here (http://oregonstate.edu/dept/IIFET/2000/papers/andersons.pdf) (PDF) is a very technical paper on the colour of salmon.

Quote
Carotenoids are used in aquaculture feeds to provide the color associated with consumer products, such as the bright vibrant colors of ornamental fish. The same carotenoid, astaxanthin, found in wild salmon is used in aquafeeds to impart this natural, pink-red color to farmed salmon fillets. Color matters, particularly in regards to consumer preference for aquaculture products. . . . . In the wild, salmonids absorb astaxanthin from the crustaceans they eat.
