When the stock market crashed in 1986, the real estate market quickly followed mostly due to massive layoffs in the financial sector. Existing residents didn’t have the money to buy and with a shrinking job market, people were deterred from moving to the New York. No jobs, or no new jobs simply meant less demand for housing.

Prices fell about 30% on average from their peak with the market bottoming out sometime in late 1991 to early 1992.

Real estate prices will fall when people start losing jobs. Regardless of how bad the economy is currently perceived to be, people still have money and will continue to buy. Layoffs seem to be starting now but it’s too soon to tell. I pray for everyone because this credit crisis has the potential to get real ugly.

How ugly remains to be seem. Unlike 1986, the market is affected by overseas investors from Europe, Russia and South America. So even if domestic demand drops there’s increased demand from overseas buyers because of the low value of the dollar American properties are seen as attractive. (A million dollar apartment in Manhattan seems a bargain compared to the price of a flat in central London.)

In the end, does it really matter for the average Astorian? A 10-20-30, even a 40% drop doesn't faze me -- I know I still wouldn’t be able to afford to buy a house !

I see a few problems with the housing chart by Dr. Shiller, and while I’m no economist (or real estate developer), these seem to be vary basic flaws in the though process that we would return to the 100% bench mark in NYC on the chart.

1.   Population, world population has grown, and NYC has at least kept up.  Last time I tried to look up what it was in NYC, it was around 8 million and projected to be 9 million by 2030.  Larger population, means that the demand isn’t going to drop back down to this “mean”
2.   Globalization, NYC’s market supposedly is being propped up partially by foreign investors (who are prob. also buying up Astoria but I really have no data) largely due to the value of the dollar (as someone earlier noted, $1.0 million might seem like a bargain for someone looking to buy in London).
3.   Single Family Home, according to Wikipedia (ok not the best source I know) Dr. Shiller’s data is based on single family homes, which there are relatively few in Astoria.

I think the chart is interesting, but it is also important to note that we haven’t dropped back to the 100% line since the mid 1940’s, and the housing market is drastically different now than it was back then.  Home Buying is open to more people, which will drive up the price higher than the inflation-adjusted value of the 1890’s.

I think there is a larger correction in the market place coming, and home values will drop over the next year, but ultimately, due to Astoria’s proximity to Manhattan, I think the drop will not be as bad as it is (and has been) in the rest of the country (worst case scenario Detroit).

The question here isn't whether or not some sacrifice is necessary to buy a house.  There always was.  The question is:  in the current economic environment, even with sacrificing, how difficult is it to buy a house in this area?

Taking into account the accelerated price of homes in the area, along with the now tigher lending requirements of banks AND the overall decrease in the value of one's net salary (as compared to the inflationary increase in the goods and services we purchase like food, gas and insurance), buying a home right now is extraordinarily difficult...especially in a desireable neighborhood like ours.

So the truth now for most people is that even if you forgot the movies and Netflix, don't get high speed internet, never go on vacation, cut the cord on Cable TV, eat in everyday, get your clothes from goodwill, don't drive, wash your clothes by hand, don't have a wedding and forgo having any kids...you still won't be able to buy a house (or in some cases, a condo or co-op) in this neghborhood unless you make more than $150,000.  And that's assuming that you don't have any prior student loans; which is probably unlikely since most in that salary range are those who hold at least a college degree, but more likely a professional degree like an MBA or JD. 

And even if you did do all those things, would it be worth advocating to anyone that sacrificing having a life is worthwhile for owning property??  Nah....at least I don't think so.

Then the issue would be not having a life once you own the home.  Home ownership bears a lot of cost (insurance, taxes, maintenance, improvements, etc.).  So even if you could withhold much of your expenses to get the 20-percent down payment, how much would you need to sacrifice in order to pay the mortgage, and maybe even the additional maintenance cost?  For a some homes in Astoria going for $600,000+, that's a large chunk of sacrifice.

And for most people, that much sacrifice has not been worth it.  Many of the homes now are valued less than what they originaly sold for.  So now people, even in Astoria, are still paying for more in thier mortgage than what thier home is worth.  And with the amount of construction going on, that means there will be housing available; i.e. increased inventory.  So with inventory available and people having less money, guess what?  That means home prices have to go DOWN, not up.  So that investment may not be worth all that sacrifice unless you hold it for 10 more years!!

My opinion: Prices have to lower a bit and likely will over the next few years.  But housing should still only be part of the overall cost of daily living, not the majority of it.  You still should be able to pay your bills, enjoy yourself and be able to SAVE some money every paycheck while still being able to put a roof over your head.  If all your paying for is your roof, that roof better be made of gold.
