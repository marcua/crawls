I'm sure we're all trying to pinch our pennies right now. I wanted to open up a thread to share tips, resources, etc etc. I did try "buy nothing new" for a month or so last year and that was cool - but my biggest money suck is not new stuff (clothes, books, CDs, etc). It's eating out.

What I'm doing right now:

- trying to keep my monthly expenses below a certain amount
- brown-bagging it, eating out less often
- I joined paperbackswap.com and swapacd.com so that I can have "new" books and CDs for about the price of postage and pass my used things along to people who will enjoy them
- not using credit cards
- eliminating debt
- looking for free events on clubfreetime.com and the river to river website

Some blogs I read:
My Open Wallet
Get Rich Slowly

So, fellow astorians, how are you squeezing the lincolns? got any resources to share?

I am not sure I actually save money with these after buying all the bran - I probably do.

Bran Muffins
I make big, hearty whole-grain muffins and add nuts, dried fruit or, um, chocolate chips to make them a little more interesting. Sometimes I even add cocoa powder to the muffin mix.

I've also tried the famous "no-knead bread" and have been pretty happy with it.

I started doing the coupon thing too in the past few months. At first I felt dorky about it, but it's now become an OBSESSION. Newsday has the best coupons (yes, how much they offer sometimes differs from paper to paper), and the Sunday Newsday is only $1. So if you want to do coupons, get it even if you don't read it.

At the grocery store, I stock up on stuff when it's on sale, as people have mentioned. I eat a lot of chicken, and the family packs of breasts are on sale a lot. I get them home and freeze them into sandwich-sized freezer bags. They defrost quickly if you put the bagged meat in a bowl of cool water (I don't know the physics behind it, but it works!) I hate defrosting them in the microwave, they get all ewwey. I also stock up on cereal - if you just buy individual boxes, they're expensive, but if you keep an eye on sales & coupons, they can be cheap. An example - Smart Start cereal at CVS is something like $4.59 a box. A month or so ago, they had a buy one get one free sale. THEN, I had a crazy coupon (printed from the internet) for $3 off when you buy two boxes of Smart Start. So between the sale and the coupon, I paid about 80 cents a box. If you drive, Target also has good cereal sales. Basically, pay attention to sales everywhere - not just grocery stores - for your food shopping.

I also don't allow myself to get takeout more than once every 2 weeks. It's too easy of a crutch when I'm hungry, it's hot, I need groceries/don't feel like defrosting anything, and I don't feel like heating up the kitchen. Just last night around 9pm, I was starving but I didn't want to order anything. Ended up having cereal. Kind of lame, but it's what I had in the house, and I didn't feel like actually *cooking*. I keep track of this because my cell phone logs ALL my calls, so I'll scroll back in the last couple weeks of calls and see when the last time I ate delivery was (yes, I have my fav delivery places in my contacts :) ).

Quote from: firelight79 on August 05, 2008, 12:35:20 PM
I'm debating whether or not an actual selzer maker would be a better investment.

I don't think a seltzer maker would save much money compared to getting cases from Costco, but I do think the seltzer stays fizzy longer, plus it's fun to make. My mom has a seltzer maker and she has to put a new co2 cartridge in for each liter, and those cartridges aren't that cheap.

For me, the biggest things so far to save money (and I'm unemployed, so it's not just about the state of the economy for me) have been to start making my own coffee and iced coffee instead of getting it to go, and cooking food instead of getting takeout. A long time ago I started saving lids from takeout iced coffee cups, which was mainly because I didn't like the iced coffee in the area and thought I could make it better myself. Now I'm especially glad I did that. Those corn plastic cups and lids work especially well for reusing several times.

For food cooking, I try to make huge batches of healthy foods that I can eat for two or three meals in a week and then freeze, like vegetarian chili which I must say I make quite well. Organic pasta, cereal, and rice are cheap when you think about how many meals you get out of them and how filling they are. Decent vegetables are pretty easy to get on the cheap, but meat has proven to be more difficult. I'm trying to get the highest quality stuff I can without breaking the bank, like I really want organic chicken, but that's really expensive. I don't know if I want to eat more non-organic chicken to save money or just make chicken a rarity. Same with other meats and especially fish. I've moved to cheaper but not necessarily unhealthy sources of protein that are easier to keep around like sardines, eggs, and organic jerky (turkey, tuna, beef), and more vegetarian sources like tofu and beans. Beans are great because they are so filling and so cheap, with the obvious unfortunate side effects, so I don't eat them ALL the time. ;) I made a bean salad a couple weeks ago that was great for a snack and lasted for a whole week, with only one can of organic salad beans from Key Food, half a green pepper, one seedless cucumber, and some Italian dressing. Hummus on whole wheat crackers is also a filling, healthy, and inexpensive snack.

Peanut butter and jelly sandwiches shouldn't be relegated to being kids' food, as I've found a very delicious and healthy PBJ can be made if you buy the right bread (which should be toasted for a classier sandwich), organic or at least all-natural peanut butter, and jelly with no sugar added - like Sorrell Ridge brand, which is sweetened with fruit juice and relatively inexpensive. It probably won't impress your spouse, but for those of us without spouses it's pretty damn good.

Also, probably most importantly, I'm trying to make the most out of things I already have, or things I can get for free, instead of just buying more things that I'd like. Figure I'll buy unnecessary things again when I'm making money. It's too bad the economy relies so much on consumption to survive, because it actually makes me feel a little guilty to not be spending money on the things I used to spend money on and supporting the companies I like, which is kind of ridiculous.

When I started reading the Astorians site I used to think that all Astorians were having trouble paying their rent because they all must eat out every night.  Everybody seemed to be trying every new place.  I'm glad to see some people are saving.  Cutting back on eating out is probably the biggest money saver.  Here is my list.

Eating out less often.  Sometimes if you really want the food take it to go so you save on the beverages and tips.

The cost of going to bars adds up really fast.  Do it less often or invite friends over and buy the beer and wine from the Beer Distributor or liquor store. 

I drive to work so I bring twelve packs of soda and snacks.  The cost is about 1/3 or 1/4 of the vending machines.  I also get up 15-20 minutes earlier and eat cereal or yogurt for breakfast rather than buying it at or on the way to work.

Use your Tivo or DVR instead of renting movies.

Never pay for music for your I-pod.  I borrow and lend Cd's with my friends and coworkers.  If no one has a CD, movie or book I really want I buy it used on Amazon.
