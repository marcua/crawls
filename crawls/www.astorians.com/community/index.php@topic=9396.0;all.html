I quit smoking back in June 01, I was 23 and had been smoking since I was 15. I don't remember exactly why I quit, but when I was driving to work (I had a 2 mile, 8 minute commute, aaaah the days), I thought to myself, "I wonder what would happen if I didn't have a cigarette on the way to work." It felt really wierd but it was ok. Then it was kind of like a game with myself, "I wonder if I can make it to lunch without one, Let's see what happens if I go til 3:30 without one." Kind of dorky, but it got me through the first few days.

After that, it was hard to break the routine of taking little breaks throughout the day to go smoke - I used to go out back by receiving for the building and BS with the guys down there, and I really did miss going, sitting outside, all of that. That's the part that really stunk for me.

Oh, this is kind of silly too, but sign up on quitnet.com. You put in how many cigarettes you smoked each day, the day you quit, and how much you paid per pack. It'll keep a running tally of cigarettes not smoked and money saved.  I just logged in (I haven't been on there in AGES), and its kind of ridiculous what my numbers are up to. But it was definitely useful when I first quit - again, I'd play games like "ok, I'm gonna make it to $100 saved, or 250 cigarettes not smoked," etc.

Right on, paratactical -- go go go!!!  :mrgreen:

It CAN be done.

Next week will be 4 years without a ciggie for yours truly -- and I'd been smoking since '91. I think it was on quitnet.com where I got this great piece of info: when you quit, you're going to feel sick, so treat it like you are sick: take a few days off if you can, lounge around eating grilled cheese and tomato soup, watch lots old movies. Take. It. Easy.

As it turned out, the weekend I decided to quit was also when I had a horrible cold; so I couldn't tell the difference anyway between the withdrawal and real illness...

Not saying you should wait to get a cold, though!  :lol:

Few other things I learned that helped for me:

- lots of liquids (for me it was water and fresh juice I was making for myself)
- less salty foods
- remind myself that I'm not going to have a cigarette now; kept it in the moment.
- accepting that there's no way I can be an occasional smoker; I tried having only 1-2 cigarettes a week and within 2 weeks I was back up to a pack a day.
- nicotine stays in the body for about 3 hours; it's metabolic derivatives only about 7 days
- for most people, it takes 30-45 days to form -- and break -- a habit

I also learned that my other attempts to quit didn't work not because of the craving, but because I feared getting a craving. It was an important distinction for me to make, and it helped me see beyond the first pulses of anxiety, then deal with whatever withdrawal I was feeling by drinking some water or juice...

One last note: I tried the nicotine gum before, but that didn't work. It just prolonged my nicotine dependence.

GOOD LUCK!!!

A couple of things that helped me...

1.) Keeping some things around to snack on since my appetite increased when I quit.  Sunflower seeds were key since they were healthier than candy and helped with the whole oral fixation thing.

2.) Taking deep breaths when I would inevitably get the urge to smoke.

3.) Toothpicks.  I carried around a bunch of round-center toothpicks to put in my mouth/play with during those times when I would have normally been doing that with a cigarette like after eating, on the way to and from work, and at the bar when you could still smoke there. 
Note: Chewing them until they splinter in your mouth does not release any hidden nicotine for some reason, but it did help me to quit. :mrgreen:

I've a serious nicotine addiction. I tried Zyban- it helped b/c if I had a smoke it made me nauseous, but after a few months I went back. I did cold turkey and lasted about a year. Et cetera.
Then I called nyc 311- they sent a generic version of the patch, free! (They also did an intake over the phone that made me realize I have been smoking twenty years!)
The great part about getting the patch was realizing that I was not demonic from withdrawal, however, the adhesive is terrible (it's free and generic), so, I started spending the money I used to spend on smokes on a name brand patch.
The adhesive was much better- very important to me as I am a swimmer, jogger, and the generic patch would either fall off during the after-exercise shower, or while I was on treadmill from  sweat.
I still messed up many times. The day I go by is the first day of last summer, by then I was much less tempted. I had absolutely zero alcohol for three months, because the people around me who helped noted I was most vulnerable when under the influence of alcohol.
I also outlawed smoking in my kitchen; even the second hand smoke was enough to reawaken my addiction.
It has been about nine months now. I've had a few setbacks, always while under the influence of alcohol.
The other night I went out and overdid it to the point where I don't recall walking home and I woke up panicked wondering if I had smoked. My clothes didn't smell like smoke, so I concluded I hadn't. (yeah, I know that's not the worst thing about not remembering ten minutes of one's life, but, whatever)
Some of my closest friends smoke, so I try and go places where smoking isn't allowed. I haven't been to the cafes in a while- too tempting.
Carrying a pen and paper is my substitute. If i feel nervous (one of my triggers) I just start writing- I write about how i want to smoke but i will not.
That's they key, i think; know what your triggers are and have a strategy for substitute behavior (like pounding madly away on a keyboard). And don't kick your ass when you falter-it's too easy to go on a binge.
Besides, why give those dirtbags your hard earned money- since I stopped the patch I have set aside the money I used to spend. I spend it on concert tix these days :-)
