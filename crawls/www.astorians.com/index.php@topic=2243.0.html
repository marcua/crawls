I only know it didn't cost *me* anything; and ING got only 15% of my liquid assets; I got a $50 bonus upon sign-up plus additional interest points that someone who didn't sign up with an ING advisor. What made Winston Tsang so special (at least for my purposes) was that he wasn't sleeping with ING. He is a Certified Financial Planner, which means that he is obligated, by law, to be more concerned where and how my money is invested than his obligation to ING.

Before you even consider using a Financial Planner, I would suggest getting a copy of this Free Financial Planning Resource Kit.

As I understand it there are 2 ways they make money. Fee-Only financial planning means that they are compensated solely by their clients, and do not accept commissions or compensation of any kind based on the products that they recommend. As far as I was concerned, there's no better way to be confident that your FP is working in your interest, and solely in your interest. The other way is, of course, commissions from funds or products where you place your money.

When you interview someone to be *your* Financial Advisor/Planner here is a pretty good Checklist for Interviewing a Financial Planner.

First order of business is to get out of debt. Anyone who has debt that is not attached to a fixed asset like a home should not be investing. Your rate of return on investments will never match credit card interest and is never guaranteed to exceed the level of interest on things like student loans. Better to play it safe and get rid of high interest credit card debt first, then pay off student loans next.

Keep three to six months of salary in cash reserve once the credit card debt is extinguished to cover job changes or life emergencies.

The only investing you should really consider otherwise is to put money into a matching 401k or other similar retirement plan whenever possible. If you have high credit card debt, then keep the percentage low (say 1%) so you maintain the habit and get the value of any match.

It may seem like you are missing out by not putting money away in an investment, but the numbers are better for you if you pay off the debt first.
