Thanks for your frank comments.

There are a couple of things going on here, I believe.

First, is the general decline in public manners. Years ago, people would wear suits to the ball park and the beach for heavens sake! Today, it F*@#%% and C#$#$%^ that on prime time TV.

It is no surprise that a growing sense in the world community is that the western society which gave Cole Porter and Gershwin and Model-Ts now exports McDonalds trash and Paris Hilton. It is little surprise that we are despised.

But these are things outside of our control.

The second point is in our control. People are customers, and if its shopping for clothes, or a place to live, they will shop around and go to those places that welcome them.

Queens is marketed as a place with an 'international' flavor. People consequently come here from all around the world. No problem with that. But we must realize that standards of cleanliness differ in differing societies.

Even within our 'society' (what ever that may or may not be) standards differ, often widely. If people are placed in close together, the problem is glaring.

Throw money at the problem? Politically it is a tough sell to move cash from social services to quality of life programs.

The situation was the same 100 years ago, but managed to resolve itself quite nicely.

How? Rather than spending money (government was tiny then) certain public policies were encouraged.

Owner occupied housing made sure the stoop was frequently swept (of punks and trash), long term relationships cultivated (merchants contributed freely to the community). Today its absentee landlords, developers and merchants from outside the community.

Then, development was planned and thoughtful (think Boulevard Gardens.) Today 'if a developer builds as of right, there is nothing you can do' ... and half the block moves out.

Then, codes were strictly enforced. After the horrors of the Genl Slocum and Triangle Shirt Waste, building and sanitation legislation were given teeth. Today, dangerous illegal conversions are the order of the day. There is an old NY adage that you can tell the number of people living in a house by the mountain of trash in front of it ... something lost on a lot of building inspectors.

Then, noxious polluters were shifted to distant quarters. Today, by building and building and not really giving public transportation a chance to catch up, we encourage more cars, the greatest polluters in our cities.

Then, waterfront and neighborhood parks were designed to encourage light and fresh air. Today its cramming more in the same space, whether its an empty corner, or a schoolyard. But the old sanitation trucks still follow schedules first created in the 60s.

Interesting questions.
