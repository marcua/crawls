Since I am minimally inclined when it comes to technology and cannot figure out how to split up quotes, I will simply quote on my own when needed in this response.

First, as initial disclaimer, I will admit that my previous post was "condescending" and sarcastic and probably not in good faith with regard to genuine dialogue.  We should not forget, however, that Habermasian genuine discussion almost always presupposes a certain privilege and lack of urgency on the part of those discussing.  Therefore a fetishization of discussion as such is an inherently conservative and exclusive practice.  An obvious and immediate example would be this forum and the socio-ecinomico-temporal conditions that presuppose membership to the forum regardless of its technically being "open to all."  Nevertheless, I don't intend to be abusive towards those who may disagree with me, and I apologize if I came off that way. 

In response to my assertion that gentrification dislocates the underprivileged in defernece to the middle class Telesphoros wrote, "This of course assumes that by having wealthier people moving into Astoria there is a displacement of poor people out.  This is an erroneous assumption.  Just because tennant A leaves an apartment and tenant B moves in that tenant A must by necessity be poor and B is rich and therefore tenant B is displacing tenant A.  There is no force at work here removing poor people.  It is just market forces at work."

As for the first two sentences.  What gentrification is is exactly this.  Perhaps the dichotomous positing of rich and poor is too absolute.  Still, gentrification is only gentrification if it involes the dislocation of some people, and these people are inevitably the less privileged.  In the third sentence there are a number of grammatical disjunctions.  I don't mean this as an insult, I only mean this to say that I can't respond to it because I don't understand what is being said.  The most interesting part of the comment perhaps are the last two sentences which at once deny the presence of "force" and at the same time place the agency for dislocation at the feet of "market forces."  The linguistic irony of the juxtaposition of these sentences is obvious and I won't go into it here.  What I will go into is the usage of the word "just" in front of "market forces at work".  Saying it is "just market forces at work" seems to apply something like a natural and/or organic quality to market forces, as if they existed of themselves outside and above human decision and moral consideration.  This is nothing but a cop out frequently used to justify the most egregious structural violence in the name of some neo-Darwinian economics that does not and never has existed.  Of course to take this line of thought to its logical conclusion would be not only to criticize gentrification, but to criticize the specific social and economic constructions that allow the word gentrification to have practical meaning.

Now Ms. Cats wrote "Like anything else, "gentrification" must be done with care and thought (now, had you argued a lack of that, I'd be on your side ...). It's the difference between Roosevelt's "New Deal" (gentrification of a nation) and what Robert Moses did to this city in the name of "progress" by destroying dozens of neighborhoods and thousands of lives. Creating new homes and renovating brownstones--even building condos--may seem different from those two things, and they are--to the extent that they are smaller scale--but their essence is similar. All of those things share the idea (the result may be otherwise) of community building."

This, in my opinion, exhibits all the worst qualities of self-styled liberals.  First there is the assumption that the general structure of things is ok, it is simply certain aspects of society that need tinkering in order to make things more just.  I believe this to be completely wrong.  Justly addressing the situation of the poor does/should not mean figuring out the best way to assimilate them into the ranks of the middle class.  In fact, this is not even possible.  The very structuring of the neo-liberal economic model does not experience poverty as something it needs to rectify within itself, but as something that is vital to its existence.  Therefore any serious consideration of poverty seeks not to reform, but to abolish those structures that require poverty.  Second, the entire tone of the paragraph is paternalistic and quasi-colonial.  Its point of reference is the New Deal.  It assumes that solutions are always things that come from the top; things that "we" (the privileged) have to figure out for "them" (the under privileged).  This is not even mentioning the fact that perhaps the greatest accomplishment of the New Deal was not the betterment of society, but the re-codification of the capitalist economic model and the cooptation of aspects of the socialist project in order to prevent real structural change.

So it is interesting when we (I must judge myself guilty of this at times too) talk about "studies" that show how gentrification has had "beneficial" (this in quotes because the word implies, but does not in fact lend itself to objective criteria) effects on the poor, while ignoring from whence these studies come and even the content of knowledges that allow us to deem something "beneficial", and ignore the fact that in almost every circumstance where gentrification is taking place we find active resistance coming from those who are being dislocated.  And not only this, when we talk about things being done with care and compassion, we are talking about these things coming from on high, when in fact what is retroactively fitted as compassion and thought of leaders was always them grudgingly giving in to struggles from below.

I don't have any more time right now, I would gladly continue this discussion at another time.

Quote from: Telesphoros on March 22, 2006, 08:21:50 PM
I do have to strongly disagree that market forces are not a factor and that they are not organic.  They are highly organic and volatile.  They can be influenced by policy, but they cannot be manipulated with any accuracy by any person or group.

I actually agree and disagree with parts of this.  Perhaps I wasn't clear when I tried to problematize the concept of market forces.  I'll try to clarify.  I know I'll still, in part, be addressing things that you have said,  but my own thoughts are also included.  I hope this counts :-).  And I don't mean to seem angry about this, just passionate.  I hope you don't hate  me Telesphoros.  I'm new here and don't want enemies just yet.

Anyway, I in no way meant to imply that market forces aren't at work with regard to gentrification and other market related phenomenons.  What I did mean to imply is that the  concept "market forces" is often taken as a given, unitary, and natural discourse.  I am not saying that any individual or group can specifically plan an economy.  I am saying that "the market" is not something natural and outside and above human decision and morality, so we must not simply regard market forces as things that occur and cannot be helped.

At the outset, the existence of any particular market that exists is a result of political decisions. I don't even mean government regulation at this point.  I am speaking of the most basic existence of our version of a capitalist economy.  We must not forget that this is not something that exists by itself, but is something that has been codified in law and repeated over time and only as a result of this process can it seem to us natural.

Next, even policy makers and economists no longer subscribe to an understanding of "market forces" as such.  The notion of the liberal and laissez faire capitalist economy is one that had a relatively short life.  The advent of the neo-liberal economic model is the current fave of market folks.  Now this is often associated with free trade internationally, but what it has been, in fact, is an admission of the faults of liberal economics and a recognition of the need for direct interference in the economy. This is proved from government involvement in sectors from pharmaceuticals to the so-called "defense" industry to the creation of the welfare state.  This is not to say that we have a planned economy, but that we have an economy that is largely influenced and guided by government action, so to speak of market forces of themselves is highly problematic.

The reason I bring these things up is that I want to reject a mentality that sees human existence as contingent on the workings of a mythical market that is beyond our influence.  Any form of market from capitalist to socialist to laissez fair to planned to communal exists as a result of human decision.  This is important to remember, in my view, because it is the necessary starting point in addressing the inequalities and injustices of "the market"

I have to go now.  But I love you Telesphoros.  Let's not be on bad terms.
