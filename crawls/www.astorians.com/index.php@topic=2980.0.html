Welcome :).  I was in a similar position about a year ago myself.

The N/W runs along 31st St., which is also home to many shops, etc.  Basically 31st and whatever road the subway stop is named for are both going to be populated with shops/restaurants/etc. so can use proximity to those as an idea.  Really though there tends to be a lot of everything within 2-3 blocks of 31st no matter where you are.  There tends to be more stuff from 34th ave and north (down? up? I have no idea how to refer to direction in Astoria).  Steinway is also a major throughfare with shops and restaurants galore.

36th down (up?) to Queens Plaza is comparatively devoid of goods and services and also tends to be sketchier at night.  Basically the closer you are to Queensboro/Queens Plazas the sketchier it is.  I've never felt unsafe wandering around Astoria at night myself but I have also only been in the 'hood for a year.

Pretty much all of Astoria is great.  If you're looking for trees and grass, the Ditmars area near the last stop of the NW and the Astoria park are great for renting out part of a house, and getting that country suburbia feel, but still being close to everything you need.  Ditmars Blvd is pretty busy, lots of great food and restaurants.  30 Ave is cheaper restaurant wise than Ditmars, and a good area to live.  Broadway is the busiest, more bars and clubs, where everyone hangs out.   Steinway is good for shopping.  Along the R line 48 St is great for being close to Stop and Shop grocery, Old Navy, Natiional Wholesale Liquidators, etc.  The R line is also an easy commute to Target and the mall. 

Overall, most areas are safe.  The NW train is always crowded late nights.  Queens Plaza and the blgs towards the water can be iffy.  36 Avenue seems desolate at night, but is up and coming and you may find cheap apartments more here.  48 st and Broadway may also be a little cheaper.  Not as pretty but safe and close to stuff.  Lived in the area a long time.  Try and get a good landlord - this often helps your experience be better :)

Woodside and Sunnyside would also be highly recommended, as would be Jackson Hts and Elmhurst.  All pretty good commutes and nice areas.

Good luck!

I definitely recommend the area around Broadway, as far as 30th Ave and 35th Ave or so, and somewhere in between the two train lines n/w and r/v (and e at night).  At least, it depends on your lifestyle.  If you go out a lot during the work week in Manhattan, or even on weekends for that matter.  You'll love being able to take whatever train comes first on the n/w/r or be able to take the e/v home if you are near those train lines.  Stick (if you can) with the side streets to avoid the noise of the main streets.  Having said that, I don't recommend being much past 35th ave, if only because there are less people around, it's darker at night, etc.  Most of the rest of Astoria has people around all the time, and on Broadway in particular, a lot of outdoor cafes open to 4AM.

Again, this topic area might answer some of those questions
http://www.astorians.com/index.php?topic=2527.0

But to try and address your questions somewhat -
Craigslist is not the best for getting an apartment, there are lots of scams on it, owners trying to get exorbitant amounts, and brokers doing bait and switch - meaning they post a great ad - you call and it's gone and they have something more expensive.   That said, my friend found an apartment in Sunnyside last October for $950 rent stab thru a broker on Craigslist.   A broker may be your best option and many are recommended in the above linked thread.

I also have 2 friends with 1 bedrooms under $1000 in Astoria, and they found them last year as well.  They do exist, but are very hard to find.   A good broker is key, or knowing someone can go a long way.  Brokers have in the past negotiated a lower rent for me, but it depends on the landlord.  But it does happen, especially if your credit is good.

As far as rent stab vs non, it's really a crap shoot.  Non - I think it depends on the landlord.  If they care about their house and like the tenant, they might not raise your rent much at all.  But it can also swing the other direction, and they'll raise your rent $200 and be jerks about it.  Find a good landlord, it can make all the difference in the world.

Don't be afraid to state your limit of how much you can pay - if it's $1000, say it and be firm.  A broker will hem and haw, but in the end, they'll either work with you or they won't.  Find another one.  You may have to compromise by getting a studio, or be one of the lucky ones to land a $950 rent-stab apt that's totally new on the market cuz some old lady just died.   :-) :-(
